		<h1 class="article-title wp-block-post-title">India: Human Rights Defenders Targeted by a Coordinated Spyware Operation</h1>
	</header>
	<article class="wp-block-group article-content is-layout-flow wp-block-group-is-layout-flow">
		<div class="entry-content wp-block-post-content is-layout-flow wp-block-post-content-is-layout-flow"><h4 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-nine-human-rights-defenders-most-of-whom-have-been-fighting-for-the-release-of-the-bhima-koregaon-11-through-litigation-research-or-activism-were-unlawfully-targeted-with-a-spyware-attack"><strong>Nine human rights defenders, most of whom have been fighting for the release of the Bhima Koregaon 11 through litigation, research, or activism, were unlawfully targeted with a spyware attack</strong></h4><p>This blog post is jointly written by Amnesty International and Citizen Lab. <span><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Citizen Lab</a></span> is an interdisciplinary laboratory based at the Munk School of Global Affairs &amp; Public Policy at the University of Toronto.</p><h1 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-summary">Summary</h1><ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Amnesty International and the Citizen Lab have uncovered a coordinated spyware campaign targeting at least nine human rights defenders (HRDs) in India. Eight of the nine HRDs have been calling for the release of other prominent activists, popularly known as the Bhima Koregaon 11, most of whom have been imprisoned in Maharashtra, India since 2018.</li></ul><ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Between January and October 2019, the HRDs were targeted with emails containing malicious links. If these links were clicked, a form of commercially-manufactured Windows spyware would have been deployed, compromising the target’s Windows computers, <span>in order to monitor their actions and communications. This is a violation of their rights to freedom of expression and privacy.</span></li></ul><ul class="wp-block-list"><li>At least three of the nine HRDs were also targeted with NSO Group’s Pegasus spyware in 2019.</li></ul><h1 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-introduction"><span>Introduction </span></h1><p>Amnesty International and the Citizen Lab have uncovered a coordinated spyware campaign targeting at least <strong>nine </strong>human rights defenders (HRDs) in India. These targets include activists, lawyers, academics, and journalists.</p><p>Between January and October 2019, each of the targets were sent spearphishing emails containing malicious links that, if opened, would have installed NetWire, a commercially available spyware. A spearphishing attack is a targeted attempt to install a spyware (a malicious software) on the victim’s computer or smartphone. Spearphishing is generally performed by sending very carefully crafted and personalized emails to the target, often impersonating colleagues or loved ones.</p><p>While NetWire is known to be used in <span><a href="https://www.fortinet.com/blog/threat-research/new-netwire-rat-variant-spread-by-phishing" target="_blank" rel="noopener">cybercrime</a></span> and <span><a href="https://www.fireeye.com/blog/threat-research/2017/09/apt33-insights-into-iranian-cyber-espionage.html">corporate </a><a href="https://www.fireeye.com/blog/threat-research/2017/09/apt33-insights-into-iranian-cyber-espionage.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">espionage</a></span>, Amnesty International and the Citizen Lab believe that in this case it was used to target the HRDs because of their human rights work.</p><p>Surveillance of people based solely on their human rights work amounts to an arbitrary and unlawful attack on their privacy and violates their right to freedom of expression and other rights that are enshrined in the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights, to which India is a state party.</p><h1 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-context">Context</h1><p>The targeted HRDs have been openly speaking out about human rights violations in the country. Recently, eight called for the release of 11 prominent activists arrested two years ago in relation to the protests and violence at Bhima Koregaon in Maharashtra, a state in south-west India. One of the targets is not directly linked to this case, but has been vocal in calling for the release of GN Saibaba, a disabled academic jailed in Maharashtra.</p><section id="section-307114b0" class="section section--tinted section--small" style=""><div id="307114b0" class="container">
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"></h3><p><strong>The Bhima Koregaon Case</strong></p>

<p>On 31 December 2017, activists organized a public event in Bhima Koregaon, Maharashtra. The following day, violence erupted between <a href="https://www.indiatoday.in/india/story/what-happened-at-bhima-koregaon-1326175-2018-08-29" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Dalits and Hindu nationalists</a>. Police claim that activists at the event allegedly instigated the violence through inflammatory speeches.The police allegedly found evidence of other criminal activities as well. In 2018, the Maharashtra Police arrested nine activists including Sudha Bharadwaj, Shoma Sen, Surendra Gadling, Mahesh Raut, Arun Ferreira, Sudhir Dhawale, Rona Wilson, Vernon Gonsalves and Varavara Rao. The subsequent charge sheets filed by the police accuse the HRDs of terror-related activities. In February 2020, the National Investigation Agency (NIA) took over the case from the Maharashtra police after the newly-elected Maharashtra Government raised doubts about the police investigation and signalled a probe against the officials. In March 2020, the Supreme Court of India denied anticipatory bail applications of two other activists, Gautam Navlakha and Anand Teltumbde, who were also charged in the same case. They were both arrested on 14 April 2020. The case relies almost entirely on digital evidence obtained from the arrested activists’ devices. In a breach of due process, some materials found on their devices <a href="https://www.firstpost.com/india/bhima-koregaon-arrests-police-releases-letters-linked-to-activists-claims-evidence-proves-maoist-connection-5089971.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">were also released</a> to the media in an effort to smear the activists.</p>
</div></section><p>The arrest of the eleven HRDs is an egregious example of how Indian authorities are clamping down on dissent and activism. These activists have been charged under various penal provisions and the draconian Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA), an anti-terror law that violates several international human rights standards and circumvents fair trial guarantees. It is also routinely used to intimidate HRDs, journalists, activists and students through arbitrary arrests and prolonged detention. These 11 activists are currently imprisoned and rights groups, including Amnesty International India, have <span><a href="https://amnesty.org.in/take-action/urgent-demand-the-release-of-the-bhima-koregaon-9-now/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">demanded</a></span> their release.</p><p>The attempts at unlawful surveillance outlined in this blog are not the first time that activists and HRDs have been targeted with malware in India. In October 2019, Facebook’s WhatsApp <span><a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/context/read-the-whatsapp-complaint-against-nso-group/abc0fb24-8090-447f-8493-1e05b2fc1156/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">revealed</a></span> that NSO Group, a surveillance tool vendor, had exploited a zero-day vulnerability on their platform to target 1400 individuals earlier in the year. A zero-day vulnerability is a security flaw in software which is unknown to the vendor or developer. In collaboration with Citizen Lab, WhatsApp<span> <a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2019/10/nso-q-cyber-technologies-100-new-abuse-cases/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">revealed</a></span> that more than 100 of those targeted were HRDs, activists, journalists, across numerous countries and notified them of the breach. Subsequent <span><a href="https://scroll.in/latest/942218/nagpur-lawyer-notified-by-whatsapp-of-surveillance-says-bhima-koregaon-accused-were-also-targetted" target="_blank" rel="noopener">report</a><a href="https://scroll.in/latest/942218/nagpur-lawyer-notified-by-whatsapp-of-surveillance-says-bhima-koregaon-accused-were-also-targetted">s</a> </span>revealed that at least 22 of the 100 were activists, lawyers, and scholars, including many HRDs who have been involved in advocating for the release of the 11 activists. <span><a href="https://www.nsogroup.com/governance/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">NSO Group</a> says </span>that it sells its products only to “government intelligence and law enforcement agencies”. </p><h1 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-targeted-campaign-against-hrds-demanding-the-release-of-the-bhima-koregaon-11">Targeted Campaign against HRDs demanding the release of the Bhima Koregaon 11</h1><p>The spyware campaign revealed in this blog targeted lawyers and activists <strong>Nihalsing</strong><strong> B Rathod, Degree Prasad Chouhan, Yug Mohit Choudhary, and Ragini Ahuja; </strong>academics<strong> Partho Sarothi Ray </strong>and<strong> PK Vijayan,</strong> a journalist who prefers to stay anonymous<strong>, </strong>and a human rights collective –<strong> Jagdalpur Legal Aid Group (JAGLAG)</strong><strong>, </strong>received malicious e-mails on the group’s official ID, which is accessed by all of its members, including lawyer <strong>Shalini Gera</strong>. Another JAGLAG member, <strong>Isha Khandelwal </strong>also received malicious emails on her personal account. All the people mentioned consented to be named in this blog.</p><ul class="wp-block-list"><li><strong>Nihalsing B Rathod</strong> is a human rights lawyer based in Maharashtra. He has worked closely with the imprisoned lawyer Surendra Gadling as a junior lawyer. Crucially, he is one of the leading lawyers representing one of the 11 imprisoned HRDs in their legal proceedings.</li>

<li><strong>Isha Khandelwal</strong> is a lawyer associated with <strong>JAGLAG</strong>, a Chattisgarh-based lawyers collective which provides legal aid to the Adivasi/indigenous and other marginalised communities. The group’s primary email, which was targeted, is also accessed by lawyer <strong>Shalini Gera.</strong> They are also involved in the legal defense of the HRDs in the same case.</li>

<li><strong>Degree Prasad Chouhan</strong> is a Dalit HRD who has worked closely with Sudha Bharadwaj in the past. Degree has been documenting and campaigning against land dispossession and forced evictions of indigenous communities in India, which have been carried out by coal companies and governments.</li>

<li><strong>Partho Sarothi Ray</strong> is a Kolkata-based activist and academic, who has been a vocal critic of rights violations in the country. He has also been a member of a collective called Persecuted Prisoners’ Solidarity Committee, and has spoken out openly against the imprisonment of these 11 activists.</li>

<li><strong>Yug Mohit Chaudhry</strong> and <strong>Ragini Ahuja </strong>are criminal lawyers based in Mumbai. Their main area of work include litigating death penalty and civil liberties cases. They represent two of the 11 imprisoned activists in the legal proceedings.</li>

<li>A journalist based in Maharashtra, who wishes to remain anonymous was also targeted. The journalist has been closely reporting on the Bhima Koregaon case.</li>

<li>Finally, <strong>PK Vijayan</strong> is a Delhi-based academic. He is not directly linked to the campaign for the release of the 11 HRDs, but is known to have campaigned for the release of GN Saibaba, a disabled academic who remains imprisoned in Maharashtra. Saibaba has been convicted under the draconian UAPA.</li></ul><p>While the spyware campaign detailed in this blog has no known links to NSO Group, three of the nine HRDs targeted – Shalini Gera (from JAGLAG), Nihal Singh Rathod, and Degree Prasad Chouhan- were <span><a href="https://scroll.in/latest/942218/nagpur-lawyer-notified-by-whatsapp-of-surveillance-says-bhima-koregaon-accused-were-also-targetted" target="_blank" rel="noopener">targeted</a></span> using NSO Group’s surveillance tools. Anand Teltumbde, who is one of the 11 charged and imprisoned in the Bhima Koregaon incident, was also <span><a href="https://scroll.in/latest/942218/nagpur-lawyer-notified-by-whatsapp-of-surveillance-says-bhima-koregaon-accused-were-also-targetted" target="_blank" rel="noopener">targeted</a></span> using NSO Group’s tools. That some of these individuals were targeted multiple times shows that there is a disturbing pattern of spyware attacks against HRDs involved in the Bhima Koregaon case.</p><h1 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-a-campaign-of-malicious-emails">A Campaign of Malicious Emails</h1><p>During this investigation, we identified 12 spearphishing emails sent between January and October 2019 targeting the nine activists.</p><p>A spearphishing attack is an attempt to install spyware (a malicious software) on the victim’s computer or smartphone by sending very carefully crafted and personalized emails to the target, often impersonating colleagues or loved ones. In a successful attack, computers or mobile devices may, in essence, become wiretaps, revealing confidential and intimate conversations and interactions but nullifying the possibility of privacy or confidentiality. Besides this direct effect, the secretive and ubiquitous nature of these attacks means that the victims never know for certain if they are being targeted or have unwittingly downloaded some kind of spyware. The consequence is that they begin to fear that every communication poses a threat, which can be <span><a href="https://targetedthreats.net/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">highly disruptive to trust and collaboration</a></span>.</p><h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-spearphishing-emails">Spearphishing Emails</h2><p>One of the spearphishing emails was sent from an email ID impersonating the name of an activist that may be known by the targets. Other spearphishing emails came from the e-mail IDs pretending to be journalists or masquerading as officials from local courts.</p><figure class="aimc-ignore wp-block-image aligncenter Additionally, this attack takes advantage of numerous other obfuscation techniques often abused by the surveillance industry to make NetWire more challenging to find and analyze (see: <strong>Appendix 1</strong> for more information).</p><h1 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-conclusion">Conclusion</h1><p><span>A coordinated spyware campaign targeted prominent HRDs, most of whom were vocal against the arbitrary and prolonged imprisonment of the Bhima Koregaon 11. The spearphishing emails and spyware suggest that this is not a cyber-crime attack, but a spyware campaign trying to compromise devices of HRDs. If successful it would have enabled the attackers, to monitor the HRDs actions and communications and is therefore a violation of their rights to freedom of expression and privacy. This spyware campaign is very concerning in the context of an already perilous situation for HRDs in India where surveillance is used along with threats, imprisonment and smear campaigns against activists to shrink the space for civil society.</span></p><p><span>Our investigation was not able to conclusively attribute the attack to a particular group with high confidence. However, it is not the first time that activists and journalists in India have been targeted using malware intended to put them under surveillance. Three of the HRDs in this incident were targeted earlier in 2019 with NSO Group’s Pegasus spyware, a commercial product only sold to government entities. This new campaign confirms that there is a pattern of digital attacks against HRDs supporting the imprisoned Bhima Koregaon activists. This pattern underscores the necessity of India fulfilling its obligation to provide a remedy for these abuses by conducting a full, independent and impartial investigation into these attacks, including by determining whether there are links between this spyware campaign and specific government agencies.</span></p><p><span>Targeting people solely for exercising their right to peaceful dissent amounts to an arbitrary or unlawful attack on their privacy and violates their right to freedom of expression. States have an obligation to protect human rights by ensuring that HRDs are protected from unlawful surveillance.</span></p><h1 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-recommendations"><span>Recommendations</span></h1><p><strong><span>To Indian Authorities:</span></strong></p><ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Conduct an independent, impartial, and transparent investigation into the unlawful targeted surveillance of the nine human rights defenders, including determining whether there are links between this spyware campaign and any specific government agencies</li></ul><ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Ensure that all surveillance meets the tests of legality, necessity, and proportionality as enshrined in international human rights standards and affirmed in the Supreme Court of India’s landmark judgement of <span><a href="https://indiankanoon.org/doc/127517806/?__cf_chl_jschl_tk__=64cf11b529f2cbd5837eb88d1620f1075f93a11e-1591978045-0-AfI46vIsktknj-pW2tT-fdtQinE8MmZQsZJYhS9gisa71yaPhLeoJ0-l7sNjAdGe676SZiHdSNnAG9sykmx4VTLNmYq5AVE9esxlbyNaiQVmv6v8dWYCOy6ft2e6zUYtfISlONNw05bPxM7SOKZLU6_mipnATxa5EHb2L80Qh6UQEB--SCHNSh9LIxW2676Gzm-_CA-rSfaNK3ArvXkVLT-izsienQsdLsQ4pE42_5hLuEOGBPAhoxcSx3nAI3pwPuh_y1ROH4aAy5qo0bDZk6hBUpOtpKHKktUTz_kbAaTgLZZ8cOR1DDNN-bxbScJCHsQqBys5qSWEdVxIFDDgpVbZNvqJqmPXjXoLxgOF23ro"><em>KS Putta</em></a><a href="https://indiankanoon.org/doc/127517806/?__cf_chl_jschl_tk__=64cf11b529f2cbd5837eb88d1620f1075f93a11e-1591978045-0-AfI46vIsktknj-pW2tT-fdtQinE8MmZQsZJYhS9gisa71yaPhLeoJ0-l7sNjAdGe676SZiHdSNnAG9sykmx4VTLNmYq5AVE9esxlbyNaiQVmv6v8dWYCOy6ft2e6zUYtfISlONNw05bPxM7SOKZLU6_mipnATxa5EHb2L80Qh6UQEB--SCHNSh9LIxW2676Gzm-_CA-rSfaNK3ArvXkVLT-izsienQsdLsQ4pE42_5hLuEOGBPAhoxcSx3nAI3pwPuh_y1ROH4aAy5qo0bDZk6hBUpOtpKHKktUTz_kbAaTgLZZ8cOR1DDNN-bxbScJCHsQqBys5qSWEdVxIFDDgpVbZNvqJqmPXjXoLxgOF23ro"><em>swamy v. Launches decoy PDF.</td></tr></tbody></table></figure><figure class="aimc-ignore wp-block-image aligncenter size-full"><div style="position:relative"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="637" height="707" src="https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/india-5.png" alt="" class="aiic-ignore wp-image-91375" srcset="https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/india-5.png 637w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/india-5-270x300.png 270w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/india-5-541x600.png 541w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/india-5-65x72.png 65w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/india-5-130x144.png 130w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/india-5-54x60.png 54w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/india-5-108x120.png 108w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/india-5-180x200.png 180w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/india-5-360x400.png 360w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/india-5-234x260.png 234w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/india-5-469x520.png 469w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/india-5-293x325.png 293w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/india-5-586x650.png 586w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/india-5-600x666.png 600w" sizes="(max-width: 637px) 100vw, 637px"><div class="image-metadata is-image"><span class="image-metadataItem image-copyright" aria-hidden="true">Amnesty Tech</span></div></div><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Delivery of the second payload variant</figcaption></figure><p>The Stage 1 SFX archive contains random README files, GPL licenses and even RFCs. Launches both the payloads contained.</p><p>The 1st level and 2nd level NetWire droppers all launch the same payload, either by launching and hollowing dllhost.exe, or launching and hollowing rundll32.exe, or by launching and injecting a copy of themselves.</p><h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-bypassing-security-protections-with-large-files"><span>Bypassing Security Protections With Large Files</span></h3><p><span>It is the first time we have observed large files being used as a trick to bypass security protections. As described earlier, the first dropper is a Self-Extracting RAR archive that extracts several files, including either the dropper or another self-extracting RAR archive running the final dropper. In almost all cases analyzed here, the droppers were very large files, between 50MB and 300MB.</span></p><p><span>These files mostly contain the byte 00 that is efficiently compressed by the RAR format, making the initial file quite small. As many security solutions include a file size limitation to avoid overloading the detection system with heavy files, it is likely that this trick would prevent some solutions from detecting these files as malicious.</span></p><h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-digital-signatures"><span>Digital Signatures </span></h3><p>We identified five samples with valid digital signatures for three different UK-registered companies. We contacted the owner of one of these companies who told us that he was never involved in signing any software. Further investigation led us to question the involvement of the companies for which they were issued. It appears that this may be part of a pattern of identity theft of small companies in order to issue signatures for malicious software. <span><a href="https://arxiv.org/pdf/1803.02931.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">A recent study</a></span> has shown that since 2017, this underground market for code-signing certificates has substantially increased.</p><h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-final-payload-netwire"><span>Final Payload: NetWire</span></h3><p>The final payload launched in memory by these samples is a NetWire sample communicating with the dynamic DNS domain researchplanet.zapto[.]org.</p><p>Netwire is a commercial malware known since 2012, that has been analyzed in depth <span><a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/new-release-decrypting-netwire-c2-traffic/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">several</a></span><span><a href="https://www.circl.lu/pub/tr-23/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">times</a></span>. It has been used mostly <span><a href="https://www.secureworks.com/blog/netwire-rat-steals-payment-card-data" target="_blank" rel="noopener">in cyber-criminal activities</a></span>, but has also been used several times in cyber-espionage operations for instance by the <span><a href="https://www.fireeye.com/blog/threat-research/2017/09/apt33-insights-into-iranian-cyber-espionage.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Iranian attributed group APT33 in 2017</a></span>. It is today sold online for $15 a month by a company called World Wired Labs.</p><h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-iii-network-analysis"><span>III. Infrastructure</span></h2><p>All the samples identified in this attack used the domain <em>researchplanet.zapto[.]org</em> on port 1810 as Command &amp; Control server. This domain is a dynamic DNS domain managed by the company <span><a href="https://www.noip.com/">NoIP</a></span>, and was first seen as used by RiskIQ in mid-December 2018. This domain has used multiple IPs over this year according to several passive DNS databases, we have confirmed the utilisation of the IP addresses 185.117.66<span>[.]</span>188 (Edelaraudtee Infrastruktuuri AS) and 185.82.202<span>[.]</span>155 (Host Sailors) as NetWire C2 servers receiving connections from compromised systems.</p><figure class="aimc-ignore wp-block-image aligncenter
