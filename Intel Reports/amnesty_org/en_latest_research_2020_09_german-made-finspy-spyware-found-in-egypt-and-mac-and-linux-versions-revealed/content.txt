Since 2011 researchers have documented numerous cases of targeting of Human Rights Defenders (HRDs) - including activists, journalists, and dissidents with the use of FinSpy in many countries, including Bahrain, Ethiopia, UAE, and more. Because of this, Amnesty International’s Security Lab tracks FinSpy usage and development as part of our continuous monitoring of digital threats to Human Rights Defenders. • Amnesty International published a report in March 2019 describing phishing attacks targeting Egyptian human rights defenders and media and civil society organizations staff carried out by an attacker group known as “NilePhish”. While continuing research into this group’s activity, we discovered it has distributed samples of FinSpy for Microsoft Windows through a fake Adobe Flash Player download website. Amnesty International has not documented human rights violations by NilePhish directly linked to FinFisher products. • Through additional technical investigations into this most recent variant, Amnesty’s Security Lab also discovered, exposed online by an unknown actor, new samples of FinSpy for Windows, Android, and previously undisclosed versions for Linux and MacOS computers. • This report provides technical information on these recent FinSpy samples in order to aid the cybersecurity research community in further investigations, enable cybersecurity vendors implement protection mechanisms against these newly discovered variants, and to raise awareness among HRDs of evolving digital attack techniques." />

		<h1 class="article-title wp-block-post-title">German-made FinSpy spyware found in Egypt, and Mac and Linux versions revealed</h1>
	</header>
	<article class="wp-block-group article-content is-layout-flow wp-block-group-is-layout-flow">
		<div class="entry-content wp-block-post-content is-layout-flow wp-block-post-content-is-layout-flow"><h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-summary"><span>Summary:</span></h2><ul class="wp-block-list"><li><span>FinSpy is a commercial spyware suite produced by the Munich-based company FinFisher Gmbh. Since 2011 researchers have documented numerous cases of targeting of Human Rights Defenders (HRDs) – including activists, journalists, and dissidents with the use of FinSpy in many countries, including <a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2012/07/from-bahrain-with-love-finfishers-spy-kit-exposed/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Bahrain</a>, <a href="https://privacyinternational.org/blog/1199/surveillance-follows-ethiopian-political-refugee-uk" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Ethiopia</a>, UAE, and more. Because of this, Amnesty International’s Security Lab tracks FinSpy usage and development as part of our continuous monitoring of digital threats to Human Rights Defenders.</span></li>

<li><span>Amnesty International <a href="https://www.amnesty.org/en/latest/research/2019/03/phishing-attacks-using-third-party-applications-against-egyptian-civil-society-organizations/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">published a report</a> in March 2019 describing phishing attacks targeting Egyptian human rights defenders and media and civil society organizations staff carried out by an attacker group known as “NilePhish”. While continuing research into this group’s activity, we discovered it has distributed samples of FinSpy for Microsoft Windows through a fake Adobe Flash Player download website. Amnesty International has not documented human rights violations by NilePhish directly linked to FinFisher products.</span></li>

<li><span>Through additional technical investigations into this most recent variant, Amnesty’s Security Lab also discovered, exposed online by an unknown actor, new samples of FinSpy for Windows, Android, and previously undisclosed versions for Linux and MacOS computers.</span></li>

<li><span>This report provides technical information on these recent FinSpy samples in order to aid the cybersecurity research community in further investigations, enable cybersecurity vendors implement protection mechanisms against these newly discovered variants, and to raise awareness among HRDs of evolving digital attack techniques.</span></li></ul><h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-introduction"><span>Introduction</span></h2><p><span>FinSpy is a full-fledged surveillance software suite, capable of intercepting communications, accessing private data, and recording audio and video, from the computer or mobile devices it is silently installed on. FinSpy is produced by Munich-based company FinFisher Gmbh and sold to law enforcement and government agencies around the world. </span>According to media reports, when<span> Egyptian protesters broke into the offices of </span>the now dissolved State Security Investigations Service, an intelligence body responsible for investigating security threats and notorious for committing grave human rights violations during Hosni Mubarak’s decades’ long rule, in 2011, they<span> discovered contracts for <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2011/apr/28/egypt-spying-software-gamma-finfisher" target="_blank" rel="noopener">the sale of FinSpy to Egyptian authorities</a>. Since then, research groups such as Citizen Lab, at the University of Toronto, and Privacy International have discovered FinSpy being used to target HRDs and civil society in many countries, including <a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2012/07/from-bahrain-with-love-finfishers-spy-kit-exposed/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Bahrain</a>, <a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2018/03/bad-traffic-sandvines-packetlogic-devices-deploy-government-spyware-turkey-syria/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Turkey</a> and <a href="https://privacyinternational.org/blog/1199/surveillance-follows-ethiopian-political-refugee-uk" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Ethiopia</a>. Because of this, Amnesty International’s Security Lab tracks FinSpy usage and development as part of our continuous monitoring of digital threats to HRDs.</span></p><p><span>In September 2019, Amnesty International discovered samples of FinFisher’s spyware distributed by malicious infrastructure tied to the attacker group commonly known as NilePhish. </span>likely to be state sponsored. These attacks took place amid an unprecedented crackdown on independent civil society and <span><a href="https://www.amnesty.org/en/documents/mde12/9107/2018/en/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">any critical voices</a></span>. <span>Over the years, numerous <a href="https://citizenlab.org/2017/02/nilephish-report/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">research</a> <a href="https://research.checkpoint.com/2019/the-eye-on-the-nile/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">reports</a>, including by <a href="https://www.amnesty.org/en/latest/research/2019/03/phishing-attacks-using-third-party-applications-against-egyptian-civil-society-organizations/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Amnesty International</a>, detailed NilePhish’s campaigns of targeting of Egyptian civil society organizations. Further technical investigation by Amnesty’s Security Lab led to the discovery of additional previously unknown samples for Linux and Mac OS computers, provided with extensive interception capabilities.</span></p><p><span>With this report, Amnesty’s Security Lab shares new insights into the capabilities of the NilePhish attacker group, as well as provides detailed analysis of newly discovered variants of FinSpy in order to enable cybersecurity researchers to further investigate and develop protection mechanisms. In addition, we hope to raise awareness among HRDs on the evolution of digital attack techniques and help address common misconceptions that Linux and Mac computers are safer against spyware attacks.</span></p><h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-nilephish-fake-flash-player-update-deliver-finfisher-s-finspy"><span>NilePhish Fake Flash Player Update deliver FinFisher’s FinSpy</span></h2><p><span>In March 2019 Amnesty International’s Security Lab warned Egyptian civil society organizations of a widespread <a href="https://www.amnesty.org/en/latest/research/2019/03/phishing-attacks-using-third-party-applications-against-egyptian-civil-society-organizations/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">campaign of phishing attacks</a> targeting human rights defenders, conducted by the so-called NilePhish attacker group. Following the publication, we continued monitoring the malicious infrastructure operated by this group to identify any new attack campaigns.</span></p><p><span>By monitoring the group’s tools, techniques and attack infrastructure, in September 2019, Amnesty’s Security Lab identified the malicious website <strong>flash.browserupdate[.]download</strong> connected to NilePhish. The website pretended to be a warning by Adobe Flash Player recommending installing an update. Clicking anywhere on the page would download a recent version of Flash Player backdoored with FinSpy.</span></p><figure class="aimc-ignore wp-block-image aligncenter size-full"><div style="position:relative"><img fetchpriority="high" decoding="async" width="897" height="669" src="https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1.png" alt="" class="aiic-ignore wp-image-91342" srcset="https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1.png 897w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1-300x224.png 300w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1-768x573.png 768w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1-804x600.png 804w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1-97x72.png 97w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1-193x144.png 193w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1-60x45.png 60w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1-120x89.png 120w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1-200x149.png 200w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1-400x298.png 400w, https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ff-1-600x447.png 600w" sizes="(max-width: 897px) 100vw, 897px"><div class="image-metadata is-image"><span class="image-metadataItem image-copyright" aria-hidden="true">Amnesty International </span></div></div><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">flash.browserupdate[.]download as displayed in September 2019</figcaption></figure><p><span>When we analyzed this Flash Player installer, we found it to be a FinSpy dropper (with hash f960144126748b971386731d35e41288336ad72a9da0c6b942287f397d57c600), designed to retrieve the final payload from the server <strong>https://172.241.27[.]171/support/personal.asp</strong>. This payload would then be loaded into memory and executed. The server was offline at the time of analysis and we therefore did not manage to retrieve any additional payload.</span></p><p><span>The version of Flash Player (32.0.0.269) used to bundle the FinSpy downloader was released by Adobe in September 2019. The file had a creation date of 20th September 2019. This suggests that backdoored binary was newly created around the time period in September 2019 when it was first uploaded to browserupdate[.]download.</span></p><p><span>It is worth noting that in 2017, ESET, a Slovak internet security company <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2017/09/21/new-finfisher-surveillance-campaigns/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">reported that FinFisher spyware </a>was being delivered using network injection attacks in two (unnamed) countries. The research group Citizen Lab, located at the University of Toronto, later discovered evidence of equipment used for similar <a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2018/03/bad-traffic-sandvines-packetlogic-devices-deploy-government-spyware-turkey-syria/" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span>network injection attacks</span> </a>in Egypt, suggesting it might have also been used for the distribution of FinFisher’s FinSpy. We cannot exclude that targets of this campaign were redirected to the browserupdate[.]download page through network injection, as similar backdoored software have been used in network injection attacks <a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2018/03/bad-traffic-sandvines-packetlogic-devices-deploy-government-spyware-turkey-syria/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">in the past</a>.</span></p><h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-connections-with-nilephish"><span>Connections with NilePhish</span></h2><p><span>The operators of this fake Flash Player download page created several other droppers which would download payloads from browserupdate[.]com. These included malicious Word documents containing macros, and a .NET program named <em>clean.downloader.exe</em> (with hash <a href="https://www.virustotal.com/gui/file/14658327efaa15275fb8718956ee97ebcad5bc80312a4f3182a3b10cd3dcf257/details" target="_blank" rel="noopener">14658327efaa15275fb8718956ee97ebcad5bc80312a4f3182a3b10cd3dcf257</a>), uploaded to the malware scanning service VirusTotal on 8th October 2019. The following attributes can be reconfigured:</p><ul class="wp-block-list"><li>TlvTypeConfigTargetPort: port number for the C&amp;C proxy.</li>

<li>TlvTypeMobileTrojanID: unknown purpose.</li>

<li>TlvTypeMobileTrojanUID: unknown purpose.</li>

<li>TlvTypeUserID: unknown purpose.</li>

<li>TlvTypeTrojanMaxInfections: unknown purpose.</li>

<li>TlvTypeConfigMobileAutoRemovalDateTime: implant self-destruction time.</li>

<li>TlvTypeConfigAutoRemovalIfNoProxy: implant self-destruct if C&amp;C proxy is unavailable</li>

<li>TlvTypeMobileTargetHeartbeatRestrictions: conditions to avoid callbacks</li>
