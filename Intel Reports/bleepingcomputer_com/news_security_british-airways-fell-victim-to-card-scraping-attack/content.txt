          <h1>British Airways Fell Victim To Card Scraping Attack</h1>
          <div class="cz-news-story-title-section">
            <div class="cz-news-title-left-area">
                            By <h6><a rel="author" href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/author/ionut-ilascu/" class="author"><span itemprop="author" itemscope itemtype="https://schema.org/Person"><span itemprop="name">Ionut Ilascu</span></span></a></h6>
            </div>
	    <meta itemprop="articleSection" content="Security"><div class="cz-news-title-right-area">
              <ul><li class="cz-news-date">September 11, 2018</li>
                <li class="cz-news-time">04:54 AM</li>
                
                <li class="cz-news-comment"><a href="#comment_form">0</a></li>
              </ul></div>
          </div>
	  <div class="articleBody">
	   
<p style="text-align:center"><img alt="" height="449" src="https://www.bleepstatic.com/content/posts/2018/09/11/BAcard_scraper.png" width="1280"></p>

<p>The recent <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/british-airways-loses-customer-payment-card-data-in-breach/" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">British Airways data breach</a>&nbsp;affecting 380,000 individuals appears to be the work of a known adversary that infects websites with a script designed to collect payment card data.</p>

<p>The name of the group is MageCart, and the scripts it uses have the same effect as the physical card skimming devices used by cybercriiminals at ATMs. In a typical attack, the group casts a wide net by compromising commonly used third-party functionality that allows access to hundreds of websites.</p>

<h2>British Airways was targeted</h2>

<p>Digital threat management company RiskIQ tracks the activity of MageCart group and reported their use of web-based card skimmers since 2016. They are familiar with the threat actor and their skimmer-code and detect it almost on an hourly basis.</p>

<p>With British Airways, though, MageCart took a targeted approach and customized the script so that did not ring any alarm bells.</p>

<p>"This particular skimmer is very much attuned to how British Airway&rsquo;s payment page is set up, which tells us that the attackers carefully considered how to target this site instead of blindly injecting the regular<br>
Magecart skimmer," RiskIQ says in a <a href="https://www.riskiq.com/blog/labs/magecart-british-airways-breach/" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">report</a>&nbsp;shared with BleepingComputer in advance.</p>

<p>For this investigation, the researchers identified all the scripts loaded by the air carrier's website and searched for recent changes.</p>

<p>The researchers noticed that the Modernizr JavaScript library had been modified with 22 new lines of code at the bottom, a tactic often used by attackers to make sure they don't break the functionality of the script.</p>

<p style="text-align:center"><img alt="" height="500" src="https://www.bleepstatic.com/images/news/u/1100723/Modernizr_malicious_tag.png" width="819"></p>

<p>British Airways website loaded the library from the baggage claim information page, and the change made by MageCart threat actor allowed Modernizr to send payment information from the customer to the attacker's server.</p>

<p>The compromised code reacted the same whether the website launched on a computer screen or from the mobile app, since in both cases the resources for for searching, booking or managing flights were the same.</p>

<p>The change in the JavaScript library was confirmed by the headers sent by the British Airways server, which indicated August 21, 20:49 GMT as the time and date of the last modification in Modernizr.</p>

<p>In the statement on the data breach, the airline said the theft occurred between August 21, 22:58 BST, one hour after MageCart made the change in Modernizr.</p>

<h2>Attackers use SSL certificate from Comodo</h2>

<p>More evidence that MageCart prepared for this attack and aimed to keep it active for as long a&nbsp;period as possible is found in the infrastructure used for exfiltrating the payment card details.</p>

<p>The compromised Modernizr script delivered all the data to baways[.]com, which resembles the legitimate domain used by British Airways, and would likely not raise suspicions during a cursory look at the modified library.</p>

<p>RiskIQ also discovered that MageCart purchased an SSL certificate from Comodo, instead of going with the free choice from Let's Encrypt. The reason for this is that a paid certificate is less likely to attract attention.</p>

<p style="text-align:center"><img alt="" height="500" src="https://www.bleepstatic.com/images/news/u/1100723/bawaysComodoSSLCert.png" width="632"></p>

<p>With this attack, MageCart threat actor has stepped up the ladder and showed they are capable of refining its operations, blending in with the targeted website to maintain their presence.</p>

<p>It is unclear how MageCart managed to compromise the British Airways website, but RiskIQ says that being able "to modify a resource for the site tells us the access was substantial."</p>

<p><strong>Update: </strong>Following BleepingComputer's report, Comodo&nbsp;released a statement saying that it revoked the SSL certificate issued to baways[.]com, the domain used for exfiltrating&nbsp;payment data.</p>

<p>"Comodo CA had issued the DV certificate in mid-August, 2018, after following all industry standards and Baseline Requirements from the CA/Browser Forum," reads the statement.</p>

<p>"While Certificate Authorities (CAs) can and must authenticate certificate requesters according to their validation level (EV, OV, or DV), they are not able to discern the intention of the certificate requester in advance of real-world use," the statement continues.</p>

function cz_strip_tags(input, allowed) {
  allowed = (((allowed || '') + '')
    .toLowerCase()
    .match(/<[a-z][a-z0-9]*>/g) || [])
    .join(''); // making sure the allowed arg is a string containing only tags in lowercase (<a><b><c>)
  var tags = /<\/?([a-z][a-z0-9]*)\b[^>]*>/gi,
    commentsAndPhpTags = /<!--[\s\S]*?-->|<\?(?:php)?[\s\S]*?\?>/gi;
  return input.replace(commentsAndPhpTags, '')
    .replace(tags, function($0, $1) {
      return allowed.indexOf('<' + $1.toLowerCase() + '>') > -1 ? $0 : '';
    });
}
function cz_br2nl(str) {
	var regex = /<br\s*[\/]?>/gi;
    //var pure_str = str.replace(regex,"\n");
    var pure_str = str.replace(regex,"");
    return cz_strip_tags(pure_str,'');
}
