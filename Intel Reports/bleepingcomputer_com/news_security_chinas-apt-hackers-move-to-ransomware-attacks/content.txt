<article><div class="article_section">
          <h1>China's APT hackers move to ransomware attacks</h1>
          <div class="cz-news-story-title-section">
            <div class="cz-news-title-left-area">
                            By <h6><a rel="author" href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/author/ionut-ilascu/" class="author"><span itemprop="author" itemscope itemtype="https://schema.org/Person"><span itemprop="name">Ionut Ilascu</span></span></a></h6>
            </div>
	    <meta itemprop="articleSection" content="Security"><div class="cz-news-title-right-area">
              <ul><li class="cz-news-date">January 4, 2021</li>
                <li class="cz-news-time">09:36 AM</li>
                
                <li class="cz-news-comment"><a href="#comments">1</a></li>
              </ul></div>
          </div>
	  <div class="articleBody">
	   
<p style="text-align:center"><img alt="" height="600" src="https://www.bleepstatic.com/content/posts/2021/01/04/hacker.jpg" width="1400"></p>


<p>Security researchers investigating a set of ransomware incidents at multiple companies discovered malware indicating that the attacks may be the work of a hacker group believed to operate on behalf of China.</p>


<p>Although the attacks lack the sophistication normally seen with advanced threat actors, there is strong evidence linking them to APT27, a group normally involved in cyber espionage campaigns, also known as TG-3390, Emissary Panda, BRONZE UNION, Iron Tiger, and LuckyMouse.</p>


<h3>Abusing Windows BitLocker</h3>

<p>The attacks happened in 2020 and directly targeted at least five companies in the online gambling sector that operate globally and successfully encrypted several core servers.</p>

<div align="center" style="width:98%; margin:0 auto; text-align:center; padding:4px; background:#f0f0f0; border:1px solid #ccc; border-radius:6px;">
 <a href="https://www.wiz.io/lp/securing-ai-agents-101?utm_source=bleepingcomputer&amp;utm_medium=display&amp;utm_campaign=FY26Q3_INB_FORM_Securing-AI-Agents-101&amp;sfcid=701Py00000RTEWMIA5&amp;utm_term=FY27-bleepingcomputer-article-970x250&amp;utm_content=AI-Agents-101" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank"><img src="https://www.bleepstatic.com/c/w/Securing-AI-Agents-970x250.png" alt="Wiz" style="margin-top: 0px;"></a>
</div>


<p>While these were ransomware incidents in earnest, the threat actor relied on BitLocker, the drive encryption tool in Windows, to lock the servers.</p>


<p>The researchers from cybersecurity firms Profero and Security Joes responded to these incidents and found that the hackers reached their targets through a third-party service provider, which had been infected through another third-party provider.</p>


<p>Analyzing the attacks revealed malware samples linked to <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/drbcontrol-espionage-operation-hits-gambling-betting-companies/" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">DRBControl</a>, a campaign described earlier this year in a&nbsp;report from Trend Micro&nbsp;and attributed to APT27 and Winnti, both groups active since at least 2010 and associate with Chinese hackers. If APT27 focuses on cyberespionage, Winnti is known for its financial motivation.</p>


<p>In a <a href="https://medium.com/proferosec-osm/apt27-turns-to-ransomware-77aaba41ef1e" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">joint report</a> shared with BleepingComputer, Profero and Security Joes share evidence pointing to these two groups saying that they found a sample of the Clambling backdoor similar to the one used in the DRBControl campaign.</p>


<p>They also uncovered the ASPXSpy webshell. A modified version of this malware has been seen previously in attacks attributed to APT27.</p>


<p>Other malware found on infected computers includes the PlugX remote access trojan, regularly mentioned in cybersecurity reports about campaigns linked to China.</p>


<p>"With regards to who is behind this specific infection chain, there are extremely strong links to APT27/Emissary Panda, in terms of code similarities, and TTPs [tactics, techniques, and procedures]," the report reads.</p>


<p>Although a cyberespionage group engaging in a financially-motivated campaign is unusual, this attack would not be the first time APT27 deploys ransomware on victim systems.</p>


<p>Researchers at Positive Technologies attributed a Polar ransomware attack from April 2020 to APT27, based on the use of malware normally used by this group.</p>


<p style="text-align:center"><img alt="" height="654" src="https://www.bleepstatic.com/images/news/u/1100723/FinMotivatedPandas.jpg" width="799"></p>


<p>The attacks against the five companies in the gambling sector were not particularly sophisticated and relied on known methods to evade detection and move laterally.</p>


<p>The report from <a href="https://profero.io/" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Profero</a> and <a href="https://www.securityjoes.com/" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Security Joes</a> says that the actor deployed PlugX and Clambling malware in the system memory using an older Google Updater executable that was vulnerable to DLL side-loading.</p>


<div class="cat_quote" style="background:#eeeeee; border:1px solid #cccccc; padding:5px 10px">
<p>"For each of the two samples, there was a legitimate executable, a malicious DLL, and a binary file consisting of shellcode responsible for extracting the payload from itself and running it in memory. Both samples used the signed Google Updater, and both DLLs were labeled goopdate.dll, however the PlugX binary file was named license.rtf, and the Clambling binary file was named English.rtf."</p>

</div>

<p>Additionally, the attacker leveraged a vulnerability from 2017 (<a href="https://msrc.microsoft.com/update-guide/en-US/vulnerability/CVE-2017-0213" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">CVE-2017-0213</a>) to escalate privileges on the machine. Exploit code for this bug is publicly available.</p>


<p>Daniel Bunce, Principal Security Analyst at Security Joes, told BleepingComputer that the key takeaway from these attacks is the involvement of a cyberespionage group in a financially-driven campaign.</p>


<p>Omri Segev Moyal, CEO at Profero, says that the audacity of such a malicious group is another signal that governments should have a unified approach in the fight against these threats.</p>


<p>Recently, another hacker group believed to work for a government has been linked to ransomware attacks. According to ClearSky, the Iranian-backed hacking group Fox Kitten has been involved in <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/iranian-nation-state-hackers-linked-to-pay2key-ransomware/" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Pay2Key ransomware operations</a> against organizations in Israel and Brazil.</p>

function cz_strip_tags(input, allowed) {
  allowed = (((allowed || '') + '')
    .toLowerCase()
    .match(/<[a-z][a-z0-9]*>/g) || [])
    .join(''); // making sure the allowed arg is a string containing only tags in lowercase (<a><b><c>)
  var tags = /<\/?([a-z][a-z0-9]*)\b[^>]*>/gi,
    commentsAndPhpTags = /<!--[\s\S]*?-->|<\?(?:php)?[\s\S]*?\?>/gi;
  return input.replace(commentsAndPhpTags, '')
    .replace(tags, function($0, $1) {
      return allowed.indexOf('<' + $1.toLowerCase() + '>') > -1 ? $0 : '';
    });
}
function cz_br2nl(str) {
	var regex = /<br\s*[\/]?>/gi;
    //var pure_str = str.replace(regex,"\n");
    var pure_str = str.replace(regex,"");
    return cz_strip_tags(pure_str,'');
}
