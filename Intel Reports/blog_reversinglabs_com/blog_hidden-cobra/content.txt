<div id="hs_cos_wrapper_module_1577203992502740" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_widget hs_cos_wrapper_type_module" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="widget" data-hs-cos-type="module">
<h2 class="post-header__secondary-head blog__post-secondary-h2">Enriching public threat intelligence </h2>

<span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><p><img src="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden-Cobra-Blog.jpg?width=700&amp;name=Hidden-Cobra-Blog.jpg" alt="Hidden Cobra" style="width: 700px;" width="700" srcset="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden-Cobra-Blog.jpg?width=350&amp;name=Hidden-Cobra-Blog.jpg 350w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden-Cobra-Blog.jpg?width=700&amp;name=Hidden-Cobra-Blog.jpg 700w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden-Cobra-Blog.jpg?width=1050&amp;name=Hidden-Cobra-Blog.jpg 1050w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden-Cobra-Blog.jpg?width=1400&amp;name=Hidden-Cobra-Blog.jpg 1400w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden-Cobra-Blog.jpg?width=1750&amp;name=Hidden-Cobra-Blog.jpg 1750w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden-Cobra-Blog.jpg?width=2100&amp;name=Hidden-Cobra-Blog.jpg 2100w" sizes="(max-width: 700px) 100vw, 700px"></p>
<h6><strong>Introduction</strong></h6>
<!--more-->
<p>Large malware campaigns with the potential to cause severe damage and operational problems on a nationwide scale often attract the attention of government institutions like the FBI, DHS and similar organizations. To help prevent further malicious activity and reduce the malware’s damage potential, these institutions produce technical reports that describe the malware in detail.</p>
<p><br>While those threat reports can have varying levels of detail and technical complexity, they almost always have a list of IOCs (Indicators of Compromise) with information about file samples and infrastructure used in malicious campaigns. Those IOCs are then used by defenders to detect malicious activity in their networks. Even though using the IOC lists is a good way to strengthen the company network security, it is hard to tell how comprehensive they are and how many sample and infrastructure variants they cover. <br><br>If a malicious campaign is highly targeted, the malware samples will most likely be adapted and configured to use different infrastructure for different targets. In those cases, there is a big chance that the company network will get infected by a malware sample that isn’t detectable based on the IOC list provided in the related report. Even if such modifications slightly change the program logic, there are a few ways to detect similar malware samples based on metadata extracted during static analysis. <br><br>This blog will demonstrate how our <a href="https://www.reversinglabs.com/products/malware-analysis-platform" rel=" noopener">Titanium Platform</a> can be used for that purpose on a real-world scenario of the Hidden Cobra APT group. You will learn how to find similar malware samples targeting your organization that aren’t covered by released IOC lists.</p>
<h6><strong>Hidden Cobra</strong></h6>
<p>Hidden Cobra (often referred to as <i>Lazarus</i>) is an APT group linked to North Korea. Most researchers estimate it has been active for the last 10 years. During that time, this group has conducted several malicious campaigns and used different toolsets in most of them. Their malicious activity was continuously investigated by the US government institutions. As a result of those investigations, several <a href="https://www.us-cert.gov/northkorea" rel="noopener" target="_blank">malware analysis reports</a> were released, containing IOC lists that can be used to detect malicious activity. On May 12th, 2020, three new reports were released describing the <a href="https://www.us-cert.gov/ncas/analysis-reports/ar20-133a" rel="noopener" target="_blank">COPPERHEDGE</a>, <a href="https://www.us-cert.gov/ncas/analysis-reports/ar20-133b" rel="noopener" target="_blank">TAINTEDSCRIBE</a> and <a href="https://www.us-cert.gov/ncas/analysis-reports/ar20-133c" rel="noopener" target="_blank">PEBBLEDASH</a> threats. We selected them for demonstration purposes, since they describe ongoing campaigns.&nbsp;</p>
<h6><strong>Copperhedge</strong></h6>
<p>The first malware tool we will analyze is Copperhedge. As described in the threat report, this tool is a full-featured RAT that comes in six variants. Each variant is accompanied by a list of IOCs, which we will use as a starting point. We will try to find more samples that are not mentioned in the report, extract their configurations, and add them to the IOC list. Technical descriptions of all variants are provided in the report, so there’s no need to go into more detail here.&nbsp;</p>
<p>There are three things to look for during sample analysis:</p>
<ul>
<li>Import decryption and loading at execution startup</li>
<li>Commands supported by the RAT</li>
<li>C2 domains</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Variant A</strong></p>
<p>The two samples mentioned in the report will be used as the starting point for our threat search. Samples from this variant load RC4-encrypted imports at the beginning of the execution. Supported commands start at the <i>0x123459</i> index. The three domains stored in plaintext are used for C2 communication.</p>
<p>Titanium Platform can group processed files based on the RHA1 <a href="https://www.reversinglabs.com/technology/reversinglabs-hash-algorithm" rel=" noopener">file similarity algorithm</a>. During file processing, Titanium Platform also performs metadata extraction. Extracted metadata fields can be used to find related samples that aren’t similar enough to be put into the same RHA1 group, but still have something in common.</p>
<p>Processing these two samples with Titanium Platform and looking at their RHA1 buckets reveals 3 new samples that weren’t mentioned in the report.</p>
<a href="https://blog.reversinglabs.com/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%201.png" class="lightbox-image"><img src="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%201.png?width=1400&amp;name=Figure%201.png" alt="Similar files grouped by RHA1 algorithm" style="width: 1400px;" width="1400" srcset="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%201.png?width=700&amp;name=Figure%201.png 700w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%201.png?width=1400&amp;name=Figure%201.png 1400w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%201.png?width=2100&amp;name=Figure%201.png 2100w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%201.png?width=2800&amp;name=Figure%201.png 2800w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%201.png?width=3500&amp;name=Figure%201.png 3500w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%201.png?width=4200&amp;name=Figure%201.png 4200w" sizes="(max-width: 1400px) 100vw, 1400px"></a>
<p style="text-align: center;"><strong><br><span style="font-size: 16px;">Similar files grouped by RHA1 algorithm</span></strong></p>
<p>The metadata extracted from these new samples uncovers that they use different C2 domains also not mentioned in the initial report.</p>
<a href="https://blog.reversinglabs.com/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%202.png" class="lightbox-image"><img src="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%202.png?width=1400&amp;name=Figure%202.png" alt="Example of a C2 domain list contained in one of the found samples" style="width: 1400px;" width="1400" srcset="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%202.png?width=700&amp;name=Figure%202.png 700w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%202.png?width=1400&amp;name=Figure%202.png 1400w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%202.png?width=2100&amp;name=Figure%202.png 2100w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%202.png?width=2800&amp;name=Figure%202.png 2800w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%202.png?width=3500&amp;name=Figure%202.png 3500w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%202.png?width=4200&amp;name=Figure%202.png 4200w" sizes="(max-width: 1400px) 100vw, 1400px"></a>
<p style="text-align: center;"><strong><br><span style="font-size: 16px;">Example of a C2 domain list contained in one of the found samples</span></strong></p>
<p>Besides RHA1 pivoting, different metadata fields can be used for discovering similar files. For PE files, you can use the following fields:</p>
<ul>
<li>Imphash</li>
<li>Compile timestamp</li>
<li>Section hashes</li>
<li>Resource hashes</li>
<li>...&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<p style="font-size: 16px; text-align: center;"><strong>Search based on section hash</strong></p>
<p>Whenever a new sample is found, the whole search process is repeated. Think of it as a ball of tangled ropes where you need to pull the leads until you completely untangle it.</p>
<p>If you don’t have any samples to start with, you can use <a href="https://www.reversinglabs.com/products/malware-threat-hunting-and-investigations" rel=" noopener">YARA retro hunt</a> as a starting point. Threat warnings often provide some YARA rules as a way to detect samples. Sometimes, those rules will be very specific, and will provide precise hunting results. Other times they will be too generic, and will get triggered on unrelated samples. The biggest problem with YARA rules provided for Hidden Cobra tools is that a lot of those tools reuse the same codebase, and it is often hard to clearly distinguish between different variants used in the past.</p>
<p>In our example, running a YARA retro hunt with the provided rules results in new samples, and disassembler analysis relates them to Hidden Cobra activities. Some of them were already seen in previous threat reports about a variant named BANKSHOT.</p>
<p>The next step is to analyze the results and filter the ones similar enough to be marked as variant A. Filtered results are then used as a starting point for the pivoting process already demonstrated on the initial samples. For example, the provided YARA rule matched the <i>588a298b51921f4ee8f6fb7ec837f80039328afe </i>sample. Searching the imphash extracted from that sample gives 2 new samples, and analyzing them reveals more previously unknown C2 domains. The process is then repeated again until there are no more leads to follow.&nbsp;</p>

<h6><strong>Taintedscribe</strong></h6>
<p>The second malware tool we are going to analyze is Taintedscribe. These samples use two new IP addresses - <strong>221.161.45.202</strong> and <strong>61.106.174.191</strong>. Sample <i>976553cafd72f8e1908f81f297fbc7dbc04c90cd </i>is identical to the <i>9ff4836ff1670816995297234cb5f6e326c16d26</i>, but it has some additional data in the overlay.</p>
<a href="https://blog.reversinglabs.com/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2023.png" class="lightbox-image"><img src="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2023.png?width=1400&amp;name=Figure%2023.png" alt="Search based on original file name" style="width: 1400px;" width="1400" srcset="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2023.png?width=700&amp;name=Figure%2023.png 700w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2023.png?width=1400&amp;name=Figure%2023.png 1400w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2023.png?width=2100&amp;name=Figure%2023.png 2100w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2023.png?width=2800&amp;name=Figure%2023.png 2800w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2023.png?width=3500&amp;name=Figure%2023.png 3500w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2023.png?width=4200&amp;name=Figure%2023.png 4200w" sizes="(max-width: 1400px) 100vw, 1400px"></a>
<p style="text-align: center;"><strong><br><span style="font-size: 16px;">Search based on original file name</span></strong></p>
<p>Unfortunately, pivoting on previously described metadata fields didn’t return any new samples for neither the downloader module nor the command execution module. This looks like a dead end, but is it really? The threat report mentions a list of domains extracted from the downloader that are used for TLS communication.</p>
<a href="https://blog.reversinglabs.com/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2024.png" class="lightbox-image"><img src="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2024.png?width=1400&amp;name=Figure%2024.png" alt="List of the domains used for TLS communication" style="width: 1400px;" width="1400" srcset="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2024.png?width=700&amp;name=Figure%2024.png 700w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2024.png?width=1400&amp;name=Figure%2024.png 1400w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2024.png?width=2100&amp;name=Figure%2024.png 2100w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2024.png?width=2800&amp;name=Figure%2024.png 2800w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2024.png?width=3500&amp;name=Figure%2024.png 3500w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2024.png?width=4200&amp;name=Figure%2024.png 4200w" sizes="(max-width: 1400px) 100vw, 1400px"></a>
<p style="text-align: center;"><strong><br><span style="font-size: 16px;">List of the domains used for TLS communication</span></strong></p>
<p>This same list can be found in the samples grouped by the RHA1 algorithm. There are a few somewhat uncommon URIs in the list, like <i>world.linkedin.com </i>or<i> vk.com</i>. Search results based on uncommon URIs uncover even more samples and their C2 IP addresses.</p>
<a href="https://blog.reversinglabs.com/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2025.png" class="lightbox-image"><img src="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2025.png?width=1400&amp;name=Figure%2025.png" alt="Search based on the extracted URI" style="width: 1400px;" width="1400" srcset="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2025.png?width=700&amp;name=Figure%2025.png 700w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2025.png?width=1400&amp;name=Figure%2025.png 1400w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2025.png?width=2100&amp;name=Figure%2025.png 2100w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2025.png?width=2800&amp;name=Figure%2025.png 2800w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2025.png?width=3500&amp;name=Figure%2025.png 3500w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2025.png?width=4200&amp;name=Figure%2025.png 4200w" sizes="(max-width: 1400px) 100vw, 1400px"></a>
<p style="text-align: center;"><strong><br><span style="font-size: 16px;">Search based on the extracted URI</span></strong></p>
<p>A detailed examination shows that these new 64-bit DLL samples are command execution modules. They execute the same commands described in the report, and the segment of program code responsible for command dispatching is almost identical to the one in the input samples. The sample <i>78925505b266e973ad7b5ec5b28c0f77cd65a628</i> is a PE/EXE file very similar to the main module. The main difference is that the C2 IPs in this sample are encrypted using a simple encryption. It also uses the same “<i>FakeTLS” </i>scheme and the same byte sequences (<i>0x11223344</i> and <i>0x11223345</i>) described in the report.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Repeating the RHA1 pivoting technique on the samples found using URI search gives a few more related samples. This completes the Taintedscribe tool analysis.</p>
<h6><strong>Pebbledash</strong></h6>
<p>Pebbledash is the third malware tool we are going to analyze. It is also described as a <i>full-featured beaconing implant,</i> but doesn’t use additional command execution modules like Taintedscribe. Only one sample hash is provided in the supplied threat report. As always, the first thing to check is RHA1 file similarity grouping. It gave us three more samples.</p>
<a href="https://blog.reversinglabs.com/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2026.png" class="lightbox-image"><img src="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2026.png?width=1400&amp;name=Figure%2026.png" alt="Similar files grouped by RHA1 algorithm" style="width: 1400px;" width="1400" srcset="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2026.png?width=700&amp;name=Figure%2026.png 700w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2026.png?width=1400&amp;name=Figure%2026.png 1400w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2026.png?width=2100&amp;name=Figure%2026.png 2100w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2026.png?width=2800&amp;name=Figure%2026.png 2800w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2026.png?width=3500&amp;name=Figure%2026.png 3500w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2026.png?width=4200&amp;name=Figure%2026.png 4200w" sizes="(max-width: 1400px) 100vw, 1400px"></a>
<p style="text-align: center;"><strong><br><span style="font-size: 16px;">Similar files grouped by RHA1 algorithm</span></strong></p>
<p>Unfortunately, the usual pivoting fields didn't return any new results. However, looking at the resources embedded in the <i>2c879a1d4b6334c59ac5f11c2038d273d334befe </i>sample, one item stands out: a bitmap resource with the language field set to Korean.</p>
<a href="https://blog.reversinglabs.com/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2027.png" class="lightbox-image"><img src="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2027.png?width=1400&amp;name=Figure%2027.png" alt="Bitmap resource from the 2c879a1d4b6334c59ac5f11c2038d273d334befe sample" style="width: 1400px;" width="1400" srcset="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2027.png?width=700&amp;name=Figure%2027.png 700w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2027.png?width=1400&amp;name=Figure%2027.png 1400w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2027.png?width=2100&amp;name=Figure%2027.png 2100w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2027.png?width=2800&amp;name=Figure%2027.png 2800w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2027.png?width=3500&amp;name=Figure%2027.png 3500w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2027.png?width=4200&amp;name=Figure%2027.png 4200w" sizes="(max-width: 1400px) 100vw, 1400px"></a>
<p style="text-align: center;"><strong><br><span style="font-size: 16px;">Bitmap resource from the </span></strong><span style="font-size: 16px;"><strong><i>2c879a1d4b6334c59ac5f11c2038d273d334befe </i></strong><strong>sample&nbsp;</strong></span></p>
<p>Resources can sometimes be excellent targets for pivoting purposes. Samples can come with unusual bitmaps and icons that don’t even have a logical visual representation, but are instead used as a data container. Malware samples occasionally contain specific binary resources required for some of their functionalities to work properly. For example, this could be some kind of a decryption key, or a certificate used for authenticating on a C2 server.&nbsp;</p>
<p>The Titanium Platform Advanced Search feature supports searching based on resource hashes. Search results for the bitmap resource hash contain the three already mentioned samples, but also an additional one not found in the previous search queries.</p>
<a href="https://blog.reversinglabs.com/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2028.png" class="lightbox-image"><img src="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2028.png?width=1400&amp;name=Figure%2028.png" alt="Search based on resource hash" style="width: 1400px;" width="1400" srcset="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2028.png?width=700&amp;name=Figure%2028.png 700w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2028.png?width=1400&amp;name=Figure%2028.png 1400w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2028.png?width=2100&amp;name=Figure%2028.png 2100w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2028.png?width=2800&amp;name=Figure%2028.png 2800w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2028.png?width=3500&amp;name=Figure%2028.png 3500w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Hidden%20Cobra/Figure%2028.png?width=4200&amp;name=Figure%2028.png 4200w" sizes="(max-width: 1400px) 100vw, 1400px"></a>
<p style="text-align: center;"><strong><br><span style="font-size: 16px;">Search based on resource hash</span></strong></p>
<p>Further pivoting on found samples didn’t result in anything new. YARA retro hunt with the YARA rule provided in the report did not return any new samples either, apart from the ones already found.</p>
<h6><strong>Conclusion</strong></h6>
<p>Publicly released threat reports are a great source of information that organizations can use to improve their threat detection, primarily through IOC lists. Those lists often provide only a small number of samples that the researchers had at their disposal, and should be enriched by more detailed threat hunting research.</p>
<p>In this case we were able to find 90 additional file samples, 38 domains, and 9 IP addresses. The research also uncovered samples that target Mac and Linux platforms, which wasn’t mentioned in the original reports.&nbsp;</p>
<p>The appearance of the discovered samples in ReversingLabs TitaniumCloud, compared to the samples mentioned in the related threat reports, ranges from a month earlier to even four years earlier in some cases.</p>
<p>Most of the found samples were first seen in ReversingLabs TitaniumCloud in the period between 2018 - 2020, but there are also some samples that date all the way back to June 2016. Finding related samples can be used to discover security incidents that happened a long time ago but were not detected at that moment. A few of the found samples date back to 2014 and it is very likely that they were used in some of the previous operations like <i>Bankshot, </i>but they were mentioned in the blog to explain how Titanium Platform can assist you at discovering potentially undetected relations between different campaigns observed in the past.</p>
<p>This blog post demonstrated how Titanium Platform can help you take advantage of one such threat report on the actively used Hidden Cobra tools. Titanium Platform makes it easy to discover additional threat samples, even without reverse engineering expertise. Repeating simple search queries based on just a few metadata fields can provide valuable results. The researcher doesn’t even need to understand the details of the underlying file format - it is enough to know which metadata fields can be useful.</p>
