<div id="hs_cos_wrapper_module_1577203992502740" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_widget hs_cos_wrapper_type_module" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="widget" data-hs-cos-type="module">

<div class="blog__post-content html-content">
<span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><h2><strong><img src="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Blog-Unpacking-Kwampirs-RAT.jpg?width=700&amp;name=Blog-Unpacking-Kwampirs-RAT.jpg" alt="Unpacking Kwampirs RAT" width="700" style="width: 700px;" srcset="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Blog-Unpacking-Kwampirs-RAT.jpg?width=350&amp;name=Blog-Unpacking-Kwampirs-RAT.jpg 350w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Blog-Unpacking-Kwampirs-RAT.jpg?width=700&amp;name=Blog-Unpacking-Kwampirs-RAT.jpg 700w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Blog-Unpacking-Kwampirs-RAT.jpg?width=1050&amp;name=Blog-Unpacking-Kwampirs-RAT.jpg 1050w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Blog-Unpacking-Kwampirs-RAT.jpg?width=1400&amp;name=Blog-Unpacking-Kwampirs-RAT.jpg 1400w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Blog-Unpacking-Kwampirs-RAT.jpg?width=1750&amp;name=Blog-Unpacking-Kwampirs-RAT.jpg 1750w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Blog-Unpacking-Kwampirs-RAT.jpg?width=2100&amp;name=Blog-Unpacking-Kwampirs-RAT.jpg 2100w" sizes="(max-width: 700px) 100vw, 700px"></strong></h2>
<h2><strong>Introduction</strong></h2>
<p>Over the past few years, malware threats have increasingly started targeting the industrial control systems. These threats are becoming so concerning that the FBI recently had to issue a public warning about one in particular. As <a href="https://www.zdnet.com/article/fbi-warns-about-ongoing-attacks-against-software-supply-chain-companies/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">ZDNET</a> reported, the US private sector was warned about a malware campaign that targets supply chain software providers. The malware referenced by this report was Kwampirs RAT - the malicious tool of choice from the Orangeworm group.<br><br>Given the possible ramifications this campaign might have, we've decided to leverage the Titanium platform for research into its inner workings. From the threat analysis viewpoint, the most important part of this malware is its configuration (control servers, mutex it uses, registry keys it creates…), since it's essentially a remote access trojan (RAT).<br><br>Following the breadcrumbs left in the network configuration, malware evolution can be mapped to the campaigns carried out by the group. By investigating the connections of this malware to the reports of new malware, its activity can be independently corroborated. But more importantly, documenting the malware network infrastructure can help the defenders protect their organizations from the ongoing attack more efficiently.</p>
<!--more-->
<h2><strong>Expanding the picture</strong></h2>
<p>Every research builds upon what’s already been disclosed, and so we started ours by finding previous publications. As the referenced ZDNET article reports, this group has used the malware since 2015, so it is safe to assume that a technical analysis of the threat already exists. Two such technical reports were referenced in the article: the first one from Symantec, and the second from Lab52. Symantec's <a href="https://www.symantec.com/blogs/threat-intelligence/orangeworm-targets-healthcare-us-europe-asia" rel="noopener" target="_blank">report</a> was older and provided some technical details, but it mostly focused on IOCs. On the other hand, Lab52’s <a href="https://lab52.io/blog/orangeworm-group-kwampirs-analysis-update/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">report</a> provided a more in-depth technical analysis of the malware, listed used modules, and described the dropping and infection phases in detail. This report also included a link to a <a href="https://twitter.com/pancak3lullz/status/1225536379834290177" rel="noopener" target="_blank">tweet</a> containing YARA rules that can be used for detecting Kwampirs. <br><br>The next logical step in our research was to acquire the samples. Publicly available YARA rules are a perfect starting point, as they are easily deployed to the ReversingLabs A1000 threat analysis platform. The Retro Hunt feature offers a quick way to match those YARA rules against all samples seen by the Titanium Platform in the last 90 days. By Retro Hunting with those rules, we found the following samples.</p>
<a href="https://blog.reversinglabs.com/hubfs/Blog/Retro-Hunt-results-on-ReversingLabs-A1000.jpg" class="lightbox-image"><img src="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Retro-Hunt-results-on-ReversingLabs-A1000.jpg?width=1400&amp;name=Retro-Hunt-results-on-ReversingLabs-A1000.jpg" alt="Retro Hunt results on ReversingLabs A1000" width="1400" style="width: 1400px;" srcset="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Retro-Hunt-results-on-ReversingLabs-A1000.jpg?width=700&amp;name=Retro-Hunt-results-on-ReversingLabs-A1000.jpg 700w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Retro-Hunt-results-on-ReversingLabs-A1000.jpg?width=1400&amp;name=Retro-Hunt-results-on-ReversingLabs-A1000.jpg 1400w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Retro-Hunt-results-on-ReversingLabs-A1000.jpg?width=2100&amp;name=Retro-Hunt-results-on-ReversingLabs-A1000.jpg 2100w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Retro-Hunt-results-on-ReversingLabs-A1000.jpg?width=2800&amp;name=Retro-Hunt-results-on-ReversingLabs-A1000.jpg 2800w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Retro-Hunt-results-on-ReversingLabs-A1000.jpg?width=3500&amp;name=Retro-Hunt-results-on-ReversingLabs-A1000.jpg 3500w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Retro-Hunt-results-on-ReversingLabs-A1000.jpg?width=4200&amp;name=Retro-Hunt-results-on-ReversingLabs-A1000.jpg 4200w" sizes="(max-width: 1400px) 100vw, 1400px"></a>
<p style="text-align: center;"><br><span style="font-size: 16px;"><strong>Retro Hunt results on ReversingLabs A1000</strong></span></p>
<p>As the previously mentioned Lab52 technical analysis describes, the main RAT functionality can be found in the DLL payload of the installer.</p>
<h2><strong>Payload analysis</strong></h2>
<a href="https://blog.reversinglabs.com/hubfs/Blog/Functions-Exported-by-the-RAT-DLL.jpg" class="lightbox-image"><img src="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Functions-Exported-by-the-RAT-DLL.jpg?width=1400&amp;name=Functions-Exported-by-the-RAT-DLL.jpg" alt="Functions Exported by the RAT DLL" width="1400" style="width: 1400px;" srcset="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Functions-Exported-by-the-RAT-DLL.jpg?width=700&amp;name=Functions-Exported-by-the-RAT-DLL.jpg 700w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Functions-Exported-by-the-RAT-DLL.jpg?width=1400&amp;name=Functions-Exported-by-the-RAT-DLL.jpg 1400w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Functions-Exported-by-the-RAT-DLL.jpg?width=2100&amp;name=Functions-Exported-by-the-RAT-DLL.jpg 2100w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Functions-Exported-by-the-RAT-DLL.jpg?width=2800&amp;name=Functions-Exported-by-the-RAT-DLL.jpg 2800w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Functions-Exported-by-the-RAT-DLL.jpg?width=3500&amp;name=Functions-Exported-by-the-RAT-DLL.jpg 3500w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Functions-Exported-by-the-RAT-DLL.jpg?width=4200&amp;name=Functions-Exported-by-the-RAT-DLL.jpg 4200w" sizes="(max-width: 1400px) 100vw, 1400px"></a>
<p style="text-align: center;"><br><span style="font-size: 16px;"><strong>Functions Exported by the RAT DLL</strong></span></p>
<p>The Titanium platform analysis matched the facts described in the referenced technical analysis. The DLL exports only one function named <strong>ControlTrace</strong>. Furthermore, the similarity analysis also showed that this sample shares the same functionality with 726 other samples within ReversingLabs TitaniumCloud, grouped by the RHA1 similarity algorithm. These findings significantly expanded the analysis sample set.</p>

<p>Collecting the samples is more than a data hoarding exercise. It's necessary for writing a reliable malware configuration parser that extracts network configurations from collected samples - primarily the C2 URLs. These URLs are interesting because of the way this RAT finds active C2 servers. Every sample comes with a hardcoded list of 200 URLs that it tries to access in the sequential order. The C2 locations are either in the form of domain names or IP addresses. The malware uses the first active URL it finds as the C2 server. <br><br>Since the malware configuration is hidden away in the installer that drops the DLL onto the system, an unpacker needs to be created alongside the parser. This unpacker decomposes the installation component and extracts the DLL, allowing the parser to collect the necessary C2 information. <br><br>Using this combination of extraction and parsing, roughly 1600 URLs were collected. There was some duplication in the list, as some URLs were found within multiple samples. When this data was deduplicated, the number of URLs decreased to 1586 URLs. <br><br>Analyzing the results of the extraction revealed that some of the droppers used the same payload, even though their hashes were different. The only difference between those samples was a 64-byte string used for random file name generation as a part of its execution logic. This indicates that the new dropper samples recently seen in the cloud are, in fact, freshly compiled, even though they use the old DLL payloads.</p>
<h2><strong>Grouping samples into campaigns</strong></h2>
<p>Malicious operations are usually carried out in waves (or campaigns) that typically share the same control server infrastructure. Each of the Kwampirs samples we collected came with a set of 200 control server URLs. Since 1586 of those URLs were unique, it is safe to assume they were remnants of multiple campaigns. As the number of extracted URLs is not a multiple of 200, it is likely that some parts of the network infrastructure were reused by multiple campaigns. <br><br>Grouping samples into campaigns is a challenge. One way to split the dataset is by the time of discovery, but it might not be the best way. In this case, some samples were already nicely grouped together by the combination of their size and their discovery time. However, that still left a large set of 600+ samples of files 255 KB in size.<br><br>Since this collection of samples was too big for manual inspection, we relied on static analysis for assistance. Processing the samples with our Titanium platform and plugging the results into the ELK stack could help us find suitable grouping criteria.<br><br>Right away, two of the metadata fields struck us as the most convenient - Rich header information and the file compilation timestamp. Rich header is a structure that often appears in PE files just before the PE signature. It contains information about the compiling and linking processes, such as the toolchain version artifacts. In this case, Rich header revealed that all samples were compiled with Visual Studio 2010. Their timestamps did not correlate with their first appearance in our cloud, which was in May 2015 and later. In fact, they all appeared as if they had been compiled a few years before their appearance in the cloud. Given what’s known about the operations of this group, it probably means that the samples were compiled in a virtual machine with deliberately inaccurate time.</p>

<h2><strong>Conclusion</strong></h2>
<p>Protection from supply chain attacks is two-fold. Organizations must protect their development environment and ensure their suppliers are not compromised. Kwampirs RAT represents a targeted attack against the secure software supply chain, and needs to be closely monitored for new activity.<br><br>Warnings issued by the FBI are corroborated by our research presented here. The attackers are still using the same methods of infection, tools, and network infrastructure, which indicates that their activity is constant.<br>Converting open source threat intelligence into actionable data is a difficult task made easy with the ReversingLabs Titanium Platform. Pulling from a vast data repository, it enables the defenders to collect necessary samples and extract valuable IOCs that can be used to protect the organisation from past and ongoing attacks.</p>
