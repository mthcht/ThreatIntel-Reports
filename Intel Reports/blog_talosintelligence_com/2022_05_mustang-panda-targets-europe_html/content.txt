    <h1 class="text-center">Mustang Panda deploys a new wave of malware targeting Europe</h1>

    <div class="text-center m-1">
        <div class="post-author">
            <span>By </span>
                <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/author/asheer-malhotra/">Asheer Malhotra</a>, 
                <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/author/jungsoo/">Jungsoo An</a>, 
                <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/author/kendall/">Kendall McKay</a>
        </div>

        <br/>
        <time class="post-datetime" datetime="May 5, 2022 08:01">
            Thursday, May 5, 2022 08:01
        </time>

        <div class="m-3">
                        <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/category/threat-advisory/" class="category primary-category">
                            Threat Advisory
                        </a>
                            </div>
    </div>

    <section class="post-content-wrapper mt-5">
        <div class="post-content">
            <ul><li>In February 2022, corresponding roughly with the start of the Russian Invasion of Ukraine, Cisco Talos began observing the China-based threat actor <a href="https://www.crowdstrike.com/blog/meet-crowdstrikes-adversary-of-the-month-for-june-mustang-panda/">Mustang Panda</a> conducting phishing campaigns against European entities, including Russian organizations. Some phishing messages contain malicious lures masquerading as official European Union reports on the conflict in Ukraine and its effects on NATO countries. Other phishing emails deliver fake "official" Ukrainian government reports, both of which download malware onto compromised machines.</li><li>Mustang Panda has been known to use themed lures relating to various current-day events and issues, including the COVID-19 pandemic, international summits and various political topics.</li><li>While the Ukraine-related Mustang Panda developments have been reported by at least one other security firm, we identified additional samples that have not been cited in open-source reporting.</li><li>Apart from targeting European countries, Mustang Panda has also targeted organizations in the U.S. and Asia.</li><li>In these campaigns, we've observed the deployment of Mustang Panda's PlugX implant,  custom stagers and reverse shells and meterpreter-based shellcode, all used to establish long-term persistence on infected endpoints with the intention of conducting espionage.</li></ul><h2 id="threat-actor-profile">Threat actor profile</h2><p>MustangPanda, also known as "RedDelta" or "Bronze President," is a China-based threat actor that has targeted entities all over the world since at least 2012, including <a href="https://www.cisa.gov/uscert/ncas/alerts/TA17-117A">American</a> and <a href="https://blog.google/threat-analysis-group/update-threat-landscape-ukraine/">European</a> entities such as government organizations, think tanks, <a href="https://www.crowdstrike.com/blog/meet-crowdstrikes-adversary-of-the-month-for-june-mustang-panda/">NGOs</a>, and even <a href="https://go.recordedfuture.com/hubfs/reports/cta-2020-0728.pdf">Catholic organizations</a> at the Vatican.</p><p>We've also observed extensive targeting of Asian countries as well, such as the <a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/unit42-tropic-trooper-targets-taiwanese-government-and-fossil-fuel-provider-with-poison-ivy/">Taiwanese government</a>, <a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/unit42-new-poison-ivy-rat-variant-targets-hong-kong-pro-democracy-activists/">activists in Hong Kong</a>, <a href="https://www.secureworks.com/research/bronze-president-targets-ngos">NGOs</a> in Mongolia and Tibet, <a href="https://www.qurium.org/alerts/targeted-malware-against-crph/">Myanmar</a> and even <a href="https://go.recordedfuture.com/hubfs/reports/cta-2020-0728.pdf">Afghan</a> and <a href="https://www.mcafee.com/enterprise/en-us/assets/reports/rp-operation-dianxun.pdf">Indian</a> telecommunication firms.</p><p>The threat actor heavily relies on sending lures via phishing emails to achieve initial infection. These lures often masquerade as legitimate documents of national and organizational interest to the targets. These infection vectors deploy malware predominantly consisting of the PlugX remote access trojan (RAT) with custom stagers, reverse shells, <a href="https://twitter.com/AvastThreatLabs/status/1404864966679678981">meterpreter</a> and Cobalt Strike, which act as another mechanism for achieving long term access into their targets. One thing remains consistent across all these campaigns — Mustang Panda is clearly looking to conduct espionage campaigns.</p><h2 id="threat-actor-ttps">Threat actor TTPs</h2><p>Mustang Panda's recent activity targets European entities, including Russian targets, and uses political themes to deliver the PlugX family of malware implants.</p><p>Typical infection chains employed by Mustang Panda consist of three key components:</p><ul><li><strong>Benign executable</strong>: Used to side-load a malicious DLL.</li><li><strong>Malicious DLL (loader)</strong>: The malicious DLL accompanying the executable is usually a loader for the PlugX implant, typically an encrypted or encoded blob of data deployed by the loader DLL.</li><li><strong>PlugX implant</strong>: A RAT implant used extensively by Mustang Panda. It consists of a malicious DLL that can perform a variety of actions on the infected endpoint including downloading and deploying new modules/plugins.</li><li><strong>Stagers and reverse shells</strong>: Instead of using PlugX, the attackers will sometimes use DLLs acting as custom developed stagers, meterpreter-based shellcode downloaders and even custom reverse shells.</li></ul><p>Infection chains utilized by the APT group typically consist of:</p><ul><li><strong>Executable downloaders</strong>: These downloaders are delivered packaged in an archive. The downloaders are responsible for fetching and instrumenting various infection artifacts, resulting in the deployment of the PlugX implant on the infected endpoint.</li><li><strong>Archive based infections</strong>: Malicious archives delivered to targets typically consist of a benign executable with names meant to trick victims into executing them. The executable will load a malicious DLL which can either be the loader for the PlugX implant or a reverse shell or meterpreter-based shellcode downloader.</li><li><strong>Shortcut files</strong>: Shortcuts (LNK files) delivered to victims consist of all the infection components embedded in the LNK files. These consist of intermediate components like BAT files that are meant to load the malicious DLLs which may be PlugX loaders or stagers.</li><li><strong>Maldocs</strong>: We've also observed limited use of maldocs to target entities in Asia with the stagers and meterpreter payloads to execute the next stage of shellcode payloads.</li></ul><h2 id="targets-across-the-world">Targets across the world</h2><h3 id="european-political-lures">European political lures</h3><p>This attacker started attacks earlier this year  where a vast majority of the lures and decoys consisted of themes related to the European Union (EU). For example, in early January 2022, we saw the attackers employ a lure that consisted of a European Commision report on state aid to Greece between 2022 and 2027. Toward the end of January, the attackers started using a press release from the EU regarding the union's human rights priorities in 2022.</p><p>The attackers also started taking advantage of publications and documents related to the degrading relations between Ukraine and Russia. In late January, the group started spreading a lure containing PlugX that disguised itself as a report from the EU's general secretary.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/img/b/R29vZ2xl/AVvXsEiACe1-Tmlrjc7kZtPty71mPVpHlPXk88Vb4nWjo4mtuRlCZ8u_v9Fjle97t6n1eA8Q3iGdyTNPmT_tJKYFw8lh2aMqykzKmCQ3y__JBWhE5VR2vM-z8oKLrYCdg634jWvsz_DoSpDUhRWRIBRgtuNdGk4tkVVDJizETEbaW8v4otUnPH_nw_gfZv8b1Q/s2000/image18.png" class="kg-image" alt loading="lazy"></figure><p>When Russia invaded Ukraine on Feb. 24, 2022, the attackers started using related documents to infect their targets. A lure from Feb. 28 was disguised as a report on the situation along European borders with <strong><em>Ukraine</em></strong>, while another one in March consisted of a report on the situation along the European borders with <strong><em>Belarus</em></strong>.</p><p>While the threat actors continued the use of regional and topical events in Eastern Europe, they also used other topics of interest to infect their victims. In March, we observed the use of a lure targeting Russian agencies, a malicious executable delivering the PlugX implant, named "Благовещенск - Благовещенский Пограничный Отряд.exe" roughly translating to "Blagoveshchensk - Blagoveshchensk Border Guard Detachment.exe," a report on the border detachment to Blagoveshchensk, a town of strategic importance to Russia, located on the Sino-Russian border.</p><h3 id="american-themed-political-lures">American-themed political lures</h3><p>Since at least May 2016, Mustang Panda has operated campaigns <a href="https://www.cisa.gov/uscert/ncas/alerts/TA17-117A">targeting multiple entities</a> in the United States. Additionally, the APT has frequently used overlapping topics of interest to multiple entities across the globe. Some of their lures such as "U.S. Asst Secretary of State Visit to ASEAN Countries.rar" from December 2021 and "Biden's attitude towards the situation in Myanmar.zip" from February 2021 reaffirm this trend of targeting two birds with one stone. In all these instances, we observed the use of stagers as the final payloads in the infection chains instead of a direct deployment of PlugX.</p><h3 id="asian-themed-lures">Asian-themed lures</h3><p>Mustang Panda has been extremely prolific in targeting various government entities in Asian countries over the past few years such as those in Myanmar, Hong Kong, Japan and Taiwan.</p><p>The threat actor has aggressively targeted the government of Myanmar since <a href="https://twitter.com/ESETresearch/status/1400165767488970764">2019</a>, even <a href="https://twitter.com/AvastThreatLabs/status/1404864966679678981">breaching their websites</a> on multiple occasions to host malware payloads. This targeting continued into 2021 with lures related to the National Unity Government of Myanmar and its People's Defence Force. All these attacks resulted in the deployment of an implant executing meterpreter HTTP shellcode.</p><p>Mustang Panda has frequently used the <a href="https://asean.org/about-us/asean-summit/">ASEAN summit</a> as a topic for their lures to infect individuals participating in this summit. Using such topics enables the APT to infect a wide range of targets (the ASEAN association consists of <a href="https://asean.org/about-asean/member-states/">10 member countries</a> in Southeast Asia). This tactic is in line with Mustang Panda's practice of using an overlapping topic of interest to target multiple entities with the same lures.</p><p>In March 2021, the APT targeted government entities in Hong Kong using a malicious archive named "Report.rar". This archive contained a lure named "Report 18-3-2021 101A.exe" for sideloading a malicious DLL-based meterpreter stager. The keyword "101A" refers to <a href="https://www.elegislation.gov.hk/hk/cap221!en">Section 101A of the Criminal Procedure Ordinance</a> which dictates terms of use of force in making arrests in Hong Kong, a hot topic on account of recent civil unrest and protests.</p><p>Japanese government officials have also been targeted recently using lures masquerading as minutes of the Japanese cabinet's meetings in 2021. Lures such as "210615_Cabinet_Meeting_Minutes.exe" and "210831_21st Cabinet Meeting Minutes.rar" have been actively used to infect victims with custom stagers.</p><h2 id="latest-infection-vectors">Latest infection vectors</h2><h3 id="downloaders">Downloaders</h3><p>Beginning in 2022, we observed Mustang Panda distributing malicious executables acting as downloaders, and disguised as fake reports on various Europe-related subjects as initial infection vectors against targets in Europe. These executables were usually distributed wrapped up in an archive file to the targets. Recently, <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2022/03/23/mustang-panda-hodur-old-tricks-new-korplug-variant/">ESET</a> disclosed a similar infection delivering a previously unknown PlugX variant.</p><p>As recently as March 2022, we discovered a downloader pretending to be a report on the current situation along European borders with Belarus. In another instance, we observed an executable named "Благовещенск - Благовещенский Пограничный Отряд.exe" roughly translating to "Blagoveshchensk - Blagoveshchensk Border Guard Detachment.exe", a report on the border detachment to Blagoveshchensk, a town located on the Sino-Russian border.</p><p>The downloader loads all the artifacts in the infection chain. All the artifacts are data files that need to be decoded by the various infection components before being activated on the infected endpoint. There are four components downloaded as part of the infection chain:</p><ul><li>The first component is a decoy PDF masquerading as an official European Union report on the conflict in Ukraine and its effects on NATO countries. This document is not malicious and only serves to project authenticity and distract the victim.</li><li>A benign executable that loads the third component — a malicious DLL-based loader — via the DLL sideloading technique. DLL sideloading involves tricking a benign process into loading a malicious DLL that disguises itself as legitimate.</li><li>The DLL loader responsible for decoding, loading and activating the final malicious implant, is also a DLL. First, it reads a data file downloaded by the downloader binary from a hardcoded location on disk and decodes the data file into a DLL. Then, the loader reflectively loads the final DLL-based implant into the memory of the current process and runs it.</li><li>A RAT called PlugX, Mustang Panda's malware of choice.</li></ul><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/img/b/R29vZ2xl/AVvXsEickLzHuU1JqODxRj70iR6TFxkC5tLRihaLeMaP-GRlSwG9yv3H65jGHVcECD0f8RYwhWsLXpp_5h18Yz2Dz0rUp1eki0BnxszmhP69v1m_xYTSV_wSKQMMHh2fNsDcN71VwkBHhX0ZSyB3rqjp2L5yq6BMiXSovvHBkVJgr6lgjOeQIQB9dxWuNe-FMQ/s2000/image6.png" class="kg-image" alt loading="lazy"><figcaption>Decoy document consisting of a report from the European Commission on the current security status of EU borders with Belarus.</figcaption></figure><p>The benign executable is executed on the endpoint using a command such as:<br><code>cmd.exe /c ping.exe 8.8.8.8 -n 70&amp;&amp;"%temp%\FontEDL.exe"</code></p><p>The executable is simply meant to load the DLL and call one of its exported APIs to activate its malicious functionality.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img By using summit- and conference-themed lures in Asia and Europe, this attacker aims to gain as much long-term access as possible to conduct espionage and information theft.</p><p>Apart from Mustang Panda's tool of choice, PlugX, we've observed a steady increase in the use of intermediate payloads such as a variety of stagers and reverse shells.
