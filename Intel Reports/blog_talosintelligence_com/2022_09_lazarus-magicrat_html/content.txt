    <h1 class="text-center">MagicRAT: Lazarus’ latest gateway into victim networks</h1>

    <div class="text-center m-1">
        <div class="post-author">
            <span>By </span>
                <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/author/asheer-malhotra/">Asheer Malhotra</a>,

    <section class="post-content-wrapper mt-5">
        <div class="post-content">
            <figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2022/12/magic-rat-1.jpg" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="2000" height="987" srcset="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w600/2022/12/magic-rat-1.jpg 600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1000/2022/12/magic-rat-1.jpg 1000w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1600/2022/12/magic-rat-1.jpg 1600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2022/12/magic-rat-1.jpg 2000w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"></figure><ul><li>Cisco Talos has discovered a new remote access trojan (RAT) we're calling "MagicRAT," developed and operated by the <a href="https://www.cisa.gov/uscert/northkorea">Lazarus APT group</a>, which the U.S. government believes is a North Korean state-sponsored actor.</li><li>Lazarus deployed MagicRAT after the successful exploitation of vulnerabilities in VMWare Horizon platforms.</li><li>We've also found links between MagicRAT and another RAT known as "TigerRAT," disclosed and attributed to Lazarus by the Korean Internet &amp; Security Agency (<a href="https://www.boho.or.kr/krcert/publicationView.do?bulletin_writing_sequence=36276">KISA</a>) recently. </li><li>TigerRAT has evolved over the past year to include new functionalities that we illustrate in this blog.</li></ul><h2 id="executive-summary">Executive Summary</h2><p><br>Cisco Talos has discovered a new remote access trojan (RAT), which we are calling "MagicRAT," that we are attributing with moderate to high confidence to the <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/actor/lazarus_group">Lazarus</a> threat actor, a state-sponsored APT <a href="https://www.cisa.gov/uscert/northkorea">attributed to North Korea</a> by the U.S. Cyber Security &amp; Infrastructure Agency (CISA). This new RAT was found on victims that had been initially compromised through the exploitation of publicly exposed VMware Horizon platforms. While being a relatively simple RAT capability-wise, it was built with recourse to the <a href="https://www.qt.io/">Qt Framework</a>, with the sole intent of making human analysis harder, and automated detection through machine learning and heuristics less likely.<br><br>We have also found evidence to suggest that once MagicRAT is deployed on infected systems, it launches additional payloads such as custom-built port scanners. Additionally, we've found that MagicRAT's C2 infrastructure was also used to host newer variants of known Lazarus implants such as <a href="https://www.boho.or.kr/krcert/publicationView.do?bulletin_writing_sequence=36276">TigerRAT</a>.<br><br>The discovery of MagicRAT in the wild is an indication of Lazarus' motivations to rapidly build new, bespoke malware to use along with their previously known malware such as TigerRAT to target organizations worldwide.<br></p><h2 id="actor-profile">Actor profile</h2><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><a href="https://www.blogger.com/u/1/blog/post/edit/1029833275466591797/5970649745953151650#"><img src="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/a/AVvXsEgMuDpnKzbM8Ne3puL8z4GOTu9JqS5qdSV9omgQ5_lf5hkVNxnyQuGaDiouFydOKoTx7Trr8rnv5thO27yquzlVMTrryAD27n0NuNTBUgncXrrcgdAp4U2Ck16SDX0c-mijS0B_HZMno1wuYgA5BAEm7nOBfGWclLF0GoBNzvMYuESfaZP4u-x1dUPK4A=s16000" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="1999" height="1172"></a></figure><h2 id="attribution">Attribution</h2><p><br>Cisco Talos assesses with moderate to high confidence these attacks have been conducted by the North Korean state-sponsored threat actor Lazarus Group. This attribution is based on tactics, techniques and procedures (TTPs), malware implants and infrastructure overlap with known Lazarus campaigns.<br>We have observed overlaps in C2 servers serving MagicRAT and <a href="https://asec.ahnlab.com/ko/34107/">previously disclosed</a> Lazarus campaigns utilizing the Dtrack RAT family. Furthermore, Talos has also discovered C2 servers hosting and serving <a href="https://www.boho.or.kr/krcert/publicationView.do?bulletin_writing_sequence=36276">TigerRAT</a> to existing MagicRAT infections. TigerRAT is a malware family attributed to the Lazarus APT groups by the Korean Internet &amp; Security Agency (<a href="https://www.krcert.or.kr/filedownload.do?attach_file_seq=3277&amp;attach_file_id=EpF3277.pdf">KISA</a>).<br><br>In some infections, we observed the deployment of MagicRAT by the attackers for some time, followed by its removal and the subsequent download and execution of another custom-developed malware called "VSingle," another implant disclosed and attributed to Lazarus by <a href="https://blogs.jpcert.or.jp/en/2021/03/Lazarus_malware3.html">JPCERT</a>.</p><h2 id="technical-analysis">Technical analysis<br></h2><p>MagicRATMagicRAT is programmed in C++ programming language and uses the Qt Framework by statically linking it to the RAT on 32- and 64-bit versions. The Qt Framework is a programming library for developing graphical user interfaces, of which this RAT has none. Talos believes that the objective was to increase the complexity of the code, thus making human analysis harder. On the other hand, since there are very few examples (if any) of malware programmed with Qt Framework, this also makes machine learning and heuristic analysis detection less reliable.<br><br>The 32-bit version was compiled with GCC v3.4 using mingw/cygwin for support on the Microsoft Windows platform, the 64-bit version, however, was compiled with VisualC64, version 7.14.<br><br>The RAT uses the Qt classes throughout its entire code. The configuration is dynamically stored in a <a href="https://doc.qt.io/qt-6/qsettings.html">QSettings class</a> eventually being saved to disk, a typical functionality provided by that class.<br><br>The malware configuration (containing author-defined QSettings) is stored in the file "visual.1991-06.com.microsoft_sd.kit" in the path "\ProgramData\WindowsSoftwareToolkit"- names and paths obviously chosen to trick the victim into believing they were part of the operating system.<br><br>The image below shows an example of a configuration file. During our analysis, we identified three sections in the configuration file:</p><ul><li><strong>[os]</strong> which contains the command and control (C2) URLs.</li><li><strong>[General]</strong> which holds general information.</li><li><strong>[company] </strong>which holds data used in the communication with the C2.</li></ul><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><a href="https://www.blogger.com/u/1/blog/post/edit/1029833275466591797/5970649745953151650#"><img src="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/a/AVvXsEjyZYyEiVDm5mOYJxp9kzZQhXE2VAyRQcS9YTo8UK4VAVJxVePrNk23SwFMUfKpvNlZ8EfBIvzeedc34Z3jZ8cWUNwHPxtjAhVz03cYRxdUFcuXM8e_rsaRjfsta57xg3ka-Jz6SNZ28fE02UeE3puNHqnF-SmkUsWW2C2ZJc7J3ABNq_ywCJPm7Yczaw=s16000" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="881" height="317"></a></figure><p><br>All analyzed samples had three encoded C2 URLs that are used to register infections and then receive commands to execute on the infected endpoint. The URLs are stored in the configuration file with the keys "windows", "linux" and "mac." The values are prefixed with "LR02DPt22R" followed by the URL encoded in base64.<br><br>Upon execution, MagicRAT achieves persistence for itself by executing a hardcoded command that creates scheduled tasks on the victim machine.</p>

<p><br>Upon achieving persistence, the RAT contacts the C2.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><a href="https://www.blogger.com/u/1/blog/post/edit/1029833275466591797/5970649745953151650#"><img src="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/a/AVvXsEjrZ5ZXKFGNIsU4Hr8fD748vA2vnIEvtA8jpzMSyHBe6wMrBqRkWSYqnUkFihX9AZjEeldudyPQ_CIYjvbudBD4iFnjgR7q463Ndxwp4fBRq8-wLV19XkAiB5LUvYmgEDMoYEzcukwJjb1z1jTgxeSsL4nVFQUsz_sqzgU0f2pUrrfifBUaAwib_fn0BQ=s16000" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="841" height="248"></a></figure><p><br><br><br>During the initial stages of execution, MagicRAT will perform just enough system reconnaissance to identify the system and environment in which the attackers are operating. This is done by executing the commands whoami, systeminfo and ipconfig /all. The last command has its results returned via the upload of the file zero_dump.mix to the C2.<br><br>MagicRAT is rather simple — it provides the operator with a remote shell on the victim's system for arbitrary command execution, along with the ability to rename, move and delete files on the endpoint. The operator can determine the timing for the implant to sleep, change the C2 URLs and delete the implant from the infected system.<br><br>We also discovered a new variant of MagicRAT in the wild generated in April 2022. This sample now consisted of the ability to delete itself from the infected endpoint using a BAT file.<br><br></p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><a href="https://www.blogger.com/u/1/blog/post/edit/1029833275466591797/5970649745953151650#"><img src="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/a/AVvXsEilIkKvY6dzT2QSIcD_NhkfGdrtDBZsE0cNSKaEsP1ptgf_zBzkBuZLNzK-R6YSuabNmdHD4pY5rHDzrxmIdVfRTJu1FCizrUQ1J4N0lBibGthMpvGN89gi1cdf20AGyPWuKtbfXbXgA70lBIsMrFYgF-iiuGUKX3FbmVGM5RtZQqbLtqBSfdKFFC76-w" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="320" height="87"></a></figure><p><br><br></p><h2 id="additional-malware">Additional malware</h2><p><br>One of the C2 servers used by the new MagicRAT sample, 64[.]188[.]27[.]73, hosted two more distinct implants masquerading as GIF URLs. Now, MagicRAT can make requests to its C2 and download a GIF file, which is actually an executable.</p><h3 id="lightweight-port-scanner">Lightweight port scanner</h3><p>One of the GIF files discovered on the MagicRAT C2 is called "pct.gif," which is an extremely simple port scanner, whose main code fits into the image below.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><a href="https://www.blogger.com/u/1/blog/post/edit/1029833275466591797/5970649745953151650#"><img src="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/a/AVvXsEhGKtEdVvsSRkKYq3DOF1BvIfpBEyUj9kMimIZ2v_Iykm66QsNysqGKPplBNKX_2u_IOcHC-4k3ZCDYj-1fPTtWDNfEamiVbdpb0SD-PD6RPfFKyRzTrJgMcMq13DI8dhqHPYCTxvHP_cViDdHhEOZeKrpQ093QYA_a3VWvv1yfdanVYPR9Y7DDY2KJQA=s16000" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="515" height="591"></a></figure><p>It takes three arguments: The IP to connect to, followed by the port number and, finally, a value dictating whether the output of the port scan must be written to a log file on disk or the standard output. After a successful connection, the executable will either write the string "Connection success!" to the standard output or to a log file called "Ahnupdate.log" located in the current user's temporary directory.</p><h3 id="tigerrat">TigerRAT</h3><p>The second implant hosted on MagicRAT's C2 is a remote access trojan (RAT) known as <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/details/win.tiger_rat">TigerRAT</a>. TigerRAT is an implant disclosed in 2021 by <a href="https://www.boho.or.kr/krcert/publicationView.do?bulletin_writing_sequence=36276">KISA and KRCERT</a> as part of "Operation ByteTiger'' detailing TigerRAT and its downloader "TigerDownloader."<br><br>This implant consists of several RAT capabilities, ranging from arbitrary command execution to file management. Capabilities of the implant include:</p><ul><li>Gather system information: username, computer name, network interface info, system info including product and version.</li><li>Run arbitrary commands on the endpoint: set/get CWD, run command via cmd.exe</li></ul><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><a href="https://www.blogger.com/u/1/blog/post/edit/1029833275466591797/5970649745953151650#"><img src="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/a/AVvXsEhEw79ezEZCrdL_nDrGVCK65FlcxdQjhoLXuzQpbnYVBYpnqplLFyeHQ24mvE_VIFAjMOOE3RnVEgiaSAoEbuixroQvTs8NxAYH5YiUtV81mNL5NAuCNVGjOLo8i49L4RKRvHKcdwu9CqRnl7i2vOFc4XYBc1CBPVrvf8JZMSCRRjUj8FR-GyI3tnkh0Q=s16000" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="558" height="514"></a></figure><p><br>Implant capability to run arbitrary commands.<br></p><ul><li>Screen capture.</li><li>Socks tunneling.</li><li>Keylogging.</li><li>File Management: drive reconnaissance, enumerate/delete files, create and write to files, read files and upload contents to C2, create processes,</li><li>Self delete/uninstall from system.The latest TigerRAT versions included one new capability with indicators of a second capability set to be introduced soon. One of these capabilities is called "USB dump." The authors have also created skeleton code in preparation for implementing video capture from Web cameras, though it hasn't been implemented yet.</li></ul><h4 id="usb-dump">USB Dump</h4><p><br>The USB Dump capability gives the attackers the ability to:</p><ul><li>Enumerate files for path "LOCAL_APPDATA\GDIFONTC".</li><li>Delete files.</li><li>Find files of specific extensions in a specified drive and folder: .docx, .hwp, .doc, .txt, .pdf, .zip, .zoo, .arc, .lzh, .arj, .gz, .tgz. Add these files to an existing archive - in preparation for exfiltration. This is the main functionality of this new capability.<br></li></ul><p><br>The image below shows the code used to check the file extensions.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><a href="https://www.blogger.com/u/1/blog/post/edit/1029833275466591797/5970649745953151650#"><img src="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/a/AVvXsEjT8QMy0U5qfTLJZmHsgwlblx2V93NYCN8232sW6yRvZVy5a_0L8IsLGwO01wKs_NqXUaW99ll_CLaJbItepnsNHvjSwiPsLNDno-qDFCCpxOz5yff7Fq-OliBt05qEzbwOCDYprbk7I4D765QwZlHsKvW_2RXB0J05lCq4e23gcnOyxXqczKQ5vIgY_g=s16000" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="634" height="589"></a></figure><p><br>Lazarus' implants commonly stitch together functionalities, including occasionally removing and adding different functions, which is evident from the latest TigerRAT samples:</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><a href="https://www.blogger.com/u/1/blog/post/edit/1029833275466591797/5970649745953151650#"><img src="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/a/AVvXsEgqgNru975x4NqOO7AgfpX_Qhd4J3QKch7N7D6LblZ2DxUYTilsGBZYN_NmX4ycRHHhSScNHwzwObevt5sxYAPbLsYUNiGsbMqvmyhD4TmTotdvy-HNKWC7tb70NiL4A1AobmYg5y7oSxQ1GoKPgHEi3zuqeJSrCNgEVJusYBp1dOe7P4EX6orkof3tXg=w549-h640" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="549" height="640"></a></figure><p><br>While Lazarus added a new capability (USB dumping and skeleton code for Webcam capture) they removed the port forwarding capability in the latest version. Older variants of TigerRAT (seen in 2020-2021) consisted of encrypted strings but the latest variant consists of strings in plaintext.<br><br></p><h2 id="coverage-ways-our-customers-can-detect-and-block-this-threat-are-listed-below">Coverage<br>Ways our customers can detect and block this threat are listed below.<br><br></h2><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><a href="https://www.blogger.com/u/1/blog/post/edit/1029833275466591797/5970649745953151650#"><img src="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/a/AVvXsEiQSNE96ttN0zN-qBYUIJHSMI9fFEoEM5jzb3jZDWQQg0-27u3fyQB4AYJGsxPB8qeTdMNfhJxWO8tJ_tkh0yT-yjd3-7oJvsLY92s5sqPYN9V3uPG_pIqNnQog6Zn9S0nN4RBHEteA1r4zS4WP0iC_j-9LKZzP0H-EFl1xtVZTewCLKHuk4UiVtF7-iw=w640-h622" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="640" height="622"></a></figure><p><br><br><a href="https://www.cisco.com/c/en/us/products/security/amp-for-endpoints/index.html">Cisco Secure Endpoint</a> (formerly AMP for Endpoints) is ideally suited to prevent the execution of the malware detailed in this post.
