    <h1 class="text-center">Great Scott, I’m tired</h1>

    <div class="text-center m-1">
        <div class="post-author">
            <span>By </span>
                <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/author/hazel-burton/">Hazel Burton</a>
        </div>

        <br/>
        <time class="post-datetime" datetime="September 25, 2025 14:00">
            Thursday, September 25, 2025 14:00
        </time>

        <div class="m-3">
                        <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/category/threat-source-newsletter/" class="category primary-category">
                            Threat Source newsletter
                        </a>
                            </div>
    </div>

    <section class="post-content-wrapper mt-5">
        <div class="post-content">
            <p>Welcome to this week’s edition of the Threat Source newsletter.&nbsp;</p><p>“Back to the Future” is 40 years old this year, and at the risk of giving away sensitive information to an audience of hackers… so am I.&nbsp;</p><p>I don’t really know what 40 is supposed to feel like. Honestly, I don’t feel all that different from my 20s, with two key exceptions: One, I care a whole lot less about what people think of me. And two, my trainer recently stopped mid-set to ask, “Was that your knee making that sound?”&nbsp;</p><p>I’ve always loved “Back to the Future” (mommy issues aside). For my 30th birthday, I threw a BTTF-themed party. Guests had to dress for either 1955, 1985 or 1885. (2015 was also allowed, but only if you wore two ties.)&nbsp;</p><p>But watching the documentary “Still” recently gave me a whole new appreciation for what Michael J. Fox went through to make it happen.&nbsp;</p><p>Because he was still under contract with “Family Ties,” and because the original Marty had been fired five weeks into filming, Fox had to shoot both projects <em>at the same time.</em> He’d wrap “Back to the Future “at 2:00 a.m., sleep in the back of a car, then be on set for the sitcom a few hours later.&nbsp;</p><p>In “Still,” he talks about mixing up lines between scripts, barely functioning from exhaustion and constantly fearing a call from his agent saying he wasn’t doing a good job. The pressure. The pace. The fear he was messing it up. Fox himself admits the experience nearly broke him. But he kept showing up, because people were counting on him.&nbsp;</p><p>Sound familiar?&nbsp;</p><p>That “I can’t stop, people are relying on me” mindset is something I see a lot in this industry. We care about the mission. We care about our teams. We don’t want to give the adversary any opportunity.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p><p>So we say yes. We log back in. We fix the thing no one else will notice, but we know it matters.&nbsp;</p><p>Fox’s schedule and resultant exhaustion weren’t the only issues behind the scenes of “Back to the Future.” The “What Went Wrong” podcast (a favourite of mine) recently covered the mishaps and difficulties, from the DeLorean doors constantly jamming shut, to having to change the entire ending. The film was originally supposed to climax at a nuclear test site, with Marty manufacturing a time machine out of a fridge.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p><p>That ending was axed as the producers were concerned children would copy the idea and get trapped in fridges. Thankfully, Steven Spielberg (a producer on the film) would use the concept 20 years later in “Indiana Jones and the Kingdom of the Crystal Skull” to huge success. Ahem.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p><p>So much about the making of “Back to the Future” was fraught and uncertain. But what we, the audience, saw was pure delight. And that’s the thing — what looks effortless on the surface is often the result of long hours, unfair compromises, and the kind of behind-the-scenes effort that nobody ever sees.&nbsp;</p><p>I want to echo the thoughts of my colleague Joe from <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/put-together-an-ir-playbook/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>last week’s newsletter</u></a>: B<strong>urnout is brutal, a</strong>nd it takes no prisoners. Trying to be there for everyone and everything all the time is unsustainable. And (trust me on this one), the longer we put off taking care of ourselves, the harder and longer the recovery.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p><p>Creating boundaries is one of the best things we can do for ourselves. So, this week, whether you're coordinating an incident, researching something cool, supporting your team or just trying to be a functioning human, give yourself a moment. Identify your boundaries. Move them closer if you need to.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p><p>In fact, write down just one thing that will help decompress you this week, and do that thing. Whether that’s less screen time, a short walk after dinner or playing a game.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p><p>Just… give yourself permission, okay?&nbsp;As Doc Brown says:&nbsp;</p><p>“The future is whatever you make it. So make it a good one.”</p><h2 id="the-one-big-thing">The one big thing&nbsp;</h2><p>Cisco Talos <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/how-rainyday-turian-and-a-new-plugx-variant-abuse-dll-search-order-hijacking/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>uncovered a new PlugX malware variant</u></a> targeting telecom and manufacturing sectors in Central and South Asia since 2022, using the same sneaky tactics as the RainyDay and Turian backdoors. These threats abuse legitimate software and share unique technical fingerprints, suggesting they're the work of the same or closely linked attackers. The campaign shows a high level of sophistication and ongoing risk for targeted industries.&nbsp;</p><h3 id="why-do-i-care">Why do I care?&nbsp;</h3><p>If your organization is in telecom or manufacturing, especially in Central or South Asia, you’re squarely in the crosshairs of advanced attackers using updated, evasive malware that can compromise your systems, steal data and lurk undetected for years.&nbsp;</p><p>Even if you’re in a different industry, attackers are getting smarter at hiding in plain sight and any organization could be at risk if these tactics spread.&nbsp;</p><h3 id="so-now-what">So now what?&nbsp;</h3><p>Double down on security controls.
