    <h1 class="text-center">Lessons from Ted Lasso for cybersecurity success</h1>

    <div class="text-center m-1">
        <div class="post-author">
            <span>By </span>
                <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/author/william-largent/">William Largent</a>
        </div>

        <br/>
        <time class="post-datetime" datetime="April 24, 2025 14:00">
            Thursday, April 24, 2025 14:00
        </time>

        <div class="m-3">
                        <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/category/threat-source-newsletter/" class="category primary-category">
                            Threat Source newsletter
                        </a>
                            </div>
    </div>

    <section class="post-content-wrapper mt-5">
        <div class="post-content">
            <p>Welcome to this week’s edition of the Threat Source newsletter.&nbsp;</p><p>"Be curious, not judgmental," Ted Lasso says, misattributing Walt Whitman. We forgive Ted because... well, he's Ted Lasso.&nbsp;</p><p>If you’ve not watched the first season of Ted Lasso, there is a defining moment where Ted confronts a nefarious bully. While putting him in his place with kindness and skill, Ted refers to this quote. It’s a defining moment not only for Ted but for the secondary and tertiary characters in the scene. One of the questions that I'm asked most when public speaking is “How do I get into Talos?” For people considering a new career, it’s “How do I get into cybersecurity?” To all those questions, my answer is "Be curious, not judgmental."&nbsp;</p><p>I think there is no greater skill necessary in security than intellectual curiosity. If you have that, you can learn the rest. The hiring process to get in the door at Talos is extremely challenging and the candidates are incredible. That's why when I interview candidates for various roles in Talos I rarely, if ever, fixate on a niche skillset, instead focusing on the prospective employee’s intellectual curiosity. I ask weird questions that don’t seem related to the specific job role, not in an effort to throw them off but simply because I am curious and hope that they are as well. &nbsp;</p><p><em>Do you like to read? Do you ever read books outside of your normal wheelhouse? What are some favorite fiction and non-fiction books? Do you have a favorite craft or hobby? How many different Linux distributions have you installed? What are your 5 favorite board games? Do you play video games, and if so, what are a few favorites from each platform and decade?&nbsp; </em>&nbsp;</p><p>These kinds of questions help me identify what kind of innate curiosity that the prospective candidate possesses and from their answers we will invariably fall down a rabbit hole while my co-workers shake their heads at me in disdain. &nbsp;</p><p>Beyond that, I always listen for my favorite answer: “I don’t know, but...”&nbsp;There’s no better answer to a very difficult question than “I don’t know, but I’d probably try X,” or “I don’t know, but I’d love to learn...”&nbsp;</p><p>Barbecue sauce.</p><h2 id="the-one-big-thing">The one big thing&nbsp;</h2><p>Cisco Talos has released a blog post on the initial access broker (IAB) we call <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/introducing-toymaker-an-initial-access-broker/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>“ToyMaker”</u></a>&nbsp;— a financially-motivated threat actor. They deploy their custom-made backdoor we call “LAGTOY” and extract credentials from the victim enterprise. LAGTOY can be used to create reverse shells and execute commands on infected endpoints.&nbsp;</p><h3 id="why-do-i-care">Why do I care?&nbsp;</h3><p>A compromise by LAGTOY may result in access handover to a secondary threat actor. Specifically, we’ve observed ToyMaker hand over access to Cactus, a double extortion gang who employed their own tactics, techniques and procedures (TTPs) to carry out malicious actions across the victim’s network. <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/introducing-toymaker-an-initial-access-broker/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>Our blog</u></a> details a timeline with turnaround time from ToyMaker to Cactus.&nbsp;</p><h3 id="so-now-what">So now what?</h3><p>Cisco Talos has released information to help ensure protection including techniques and related IOCs. (<a href="https://techcrunch.com/2025/04/16/apple-says-zero-day-bugs-exploited-against-specific-targeted-individuals-using-ios/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>TechCrunch</u></a>)</p><p><strong>Microsoft purges millions of cloud tenants in the wake of Storm-0558.</strong> In an effort to thwart state-sponsored activity stemming from preventable security issues, Microsoft is making significant efforts to purge inactive Azure cloud tenants and take comprehensive inventory of cloud and network assets. <a href="https://www.darkreading.com/cloud-security/microsoft-millions-cloud-tenants-storm-0558" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>(DarkReading</u></a>)&nbsp;</p><p><strong>Researchers warn of critical flaw found in Erlang OTP SSH.</strong> The vulnerability could allow unauthenticated attackers to gain full access to a device. Many of these devices are widely used in IoT and telecom platforms. (<a href="https://www.cybersecuritydive.com/news/researchers-warn-of-critical-flaw-found-in-erlang-otp-ssh/745900/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>cybersecuritydrive</u></a>)&nbsp;</p><p><strong>CISA flags actively exploited vulnerability in SonicWall SMA devices. </strong>The U.S. Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) added a security flaw impacting SonicWall Secure Mobile Access 100 Series gateways to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities (KEV) catalog, based on evidence of active exploitation. (<a href="https://thehackernews.com/2025/04/cisa-flags-actively-exploited.html" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>The Hacker News</u></a>)</p><h2 id="can%E2%80%99t-get-enough-talos">Can’t get enough Talos?&nbsp;</h2><ul><li><a href="https://www.buzzsprout.com/2018149/episodes/16988482" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>Talos Takes: Year in Review Special: Part 3</u></a>&nbsp;</li><li><a
