    <h1 class="text-center">Malvertising campaign leads to PS1Bot, a multi-stage malware framework</h1>

    <div class="text-center m-1">
        <div class="post-author">
            <span>By </span>
                <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/author/edmund-brumaghin/">Edmund Brumaghin</a>, 
                <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/author/jordyn/">Jordyn Dunk</a>
        </div>

        <br/>
        <time class="post-datetime" datetime="August 12, 2025 16:00">
            Tuesday, August 12, 2025 16:00
        </time>

        <div class="m-3">
                        <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/category/threat-spotlight/" class="category primary-category">
                            Threat Spotlight
                        </a>
                                            <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/category/stealer/" class="category primary-category">
                            Stealer
                        </a>
                                            <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/category/securex-3/" class="category primary-category">

                        </a>

    <section class="post-content-wrapper mt-5">
        <div class="post-content">
            <ul><li>Cisco Talos has observed an ongoing malware campaign that seeks to infect victims with a multi-stage malware framework, implemented in PowerShell and C#, which we are referring to as “PS1Bot.” </li><li>PS1Bot features a modular design, with several modules delivered used to perform a variety of malicious activities on infected systems, including information theft, keylogging, reconnaissance and the establishment of persistent system access.</li><li>PS1Bot has been designed with stealth in mind, minimizing persistent artifacts left on infected systems and incorporating in-memory execution techniques to facilitate execution of follow-on modules without requiring them to be written to disk.</li><li>PS1Bot distribution campaigns have been extremely active since early 2025, with new samples being observed frequently throughout the year.</li><li>The information stealer module implementation leverages wordlists embedded into the stealer to enumerate files containing passwords and seed phrases that can be used to access cryptocurrency wallets, which the stealer also attempts to exfiltrate from infected systems.</li></ul><h2 id="campaign-overview">Campaign Overview</h2><p>Cisco Talos has been monitoring an ongoing malware campaign that has been active throughout 2025. The campaign appears to be leveraging malvertising to direct victims to a multi-stage malware framework, implemented in PowerShell and C#, that possesses robust functionality, including the ability to deliver follow-on modules including an information stealer, keylogger, screen capture collector and more. It also establishes persistence to continue operations following system reboots. The design of this malware framework appears to attempt to minimize artifacts left on infected systems by facilitating the delivery and execution of modules in-memory, without requiring them to be written to disk. Due to similarities in the design and implementation with the malware family <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/highlighting-ta866-asylum-ambuscade/"><u>AHK Bot</u></a>, we are referring to this PowerShell-based malware as “PS1Bot.”</p><p>This campaign has been extremely active, with new samples being observed continuously over the past several months. The cluster of malicious activity associated with this campaign also overlaps with prior reporting, including reporting on <a href="https://catalyst.prodaft.com/public/report/skitnet/overview#heading-1000"><u>Skitnet</u></a>. While Talos has not observed delivery of the Skitnet binary in any of the infection chains we analyzed, the PowerShell implementation described in that reporting appears to match the components delivered throughout the infection chain in this case as well. We have also observed significant overlap in the C2 infrastructure used in both cases. Likewise, we have observed code and indicator overlap with previously <a href="https://x.com/Unit42_Intel/status/1882448037030584611"><u>reported</u></a> malvertising campaigns. </p><h3 id="delivery">Delivery</h3><p>The victim is initially delivered a compressed archive. The file names Talos observed in the wild are consistent with what is typically seen during search engine optimization (SEO) poisoning and/or malvertising campaigns, where the file name matches the keyword phrase being targeted in the campaigns:</p><ul><li>chapter 8 medicare benefit policy manual.zip</li><li>Counting Canadian Money Worksheets Pdf.zip.e49</li><li>zebra gx430t manual.zip.081</li><li>kosher food list pdf (1).zip.c9a</li><li>pambu panchangam 2024-25 pdf.zip.a7a</li></ul><p>Prior <a href="https://www.reddit.com/r/cybersecurity/comments/1mf8o87/seo_poisoning_leading_to_malware/"><u>reporting</u></a> on social media further strengthens this assessment, where researchers have observed the malvertising campaigns leading to the compressed archives delivered in this campaign.</p><p>Inside of the compressed archive is a single file called “FULL DOCUMENT.js” that functions as a downloader, retrieving the next stage of the infection. In the cases analyzed, the JS file contained VBScript, which employed a variety of obfuscation methods throughout 2025. Below is an example of one of the more simplistic examples observed recently.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://lh7-rt.googleusercontent.com/docsz/AD_4nXeJp97QUaIgk33XJLnx-pJ4ZG0MttdvAfYASUJSS5xwcOPQ7EqGhdOaRSzi3pJKmu5CpNMNVNtjZ2cj7xrGmymFM8k5bVmx4Lm7aWpclyhSJ2F4qh70MfH95saXUsoWzkO5pYG3MQ?key=S0d5griJyBtZEed2y7A3sw" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="624" height="361"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Figure 1. Deobfuscating the downloader script.</span></figcaption></figure><h3 id="stage-1-retrieval">Stage 1 retrieval</h3><p>When executed, the malware retrieves a JScript scriptlet from an attacker controlled server, the contents of which are then executed.&nbsp;</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://lh7-rt.googleusercontent.com/docsz/AD_4nXc8NmwARp542Kbd5q-Nb971c3jChXyxdu9R784TLXss-2fNaLHei0CftUqizgkH5yRLwqCM9lqFqYZORAK6NYaGJ0DbGU9DAnSaA-sZtVjnZoPReZCanfTCtAgzGe5cLnjtMZ2n?key=S0d5griJyBtZEed2y7A3sw" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="624" height="361"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Figure 2. Example JScript scriptlet contents.</span></figcaption></figure><p>This script is responsible for performing the environmental setup needed for subsequent malware operations to function properly. This includes writing a PowerShell script to <code>C:\ProgramData\</code> (<code>ntu.ps1</code> in this case) and executing the script contents written to the file created in the previous step and redacted for space in the previous screenshot. This PowerShell script obtains the serial number of the C:\ drive and uses it to construct a URL, which it uses to attempt to establish a connection to the command and control (C2) server to retrieve additional malicious content to execute. Any PowerShell content received is then passed to Invoke-Expression (IEX) and executed within the existing PowerShell process. This is repeated in a loop with <code>Sleep()</code> delays added between each iteration.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://lh7-rt.googleusercontent.com/docsz/AD_4nXcw5b_Nox9qD0SeWyk9jo3C5mkB-6uqVBn48taDC1NVWDPhYqWuk2uRlwEHv9LOXn7LuUYhFy4hBqmO-yYsEBnOcZ9LL4b-K1A8bPF2uRqP2-3UYekNXKn001vf0HBObgjdzz0V?key=S0d5griJyBtZEed2y7A3sw" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="624" height="455"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Figure 3. PowerShell module retrieval and C2 polling.</span></figcaption></figure><p>This allows the malware to continue to run, periodically attempting to poll the attacker’s C2 server to retrieve additional commands to execute within the PowerShell process running on the system. We have observed this technique used to deliver a variety of additional modules, each enabling the attacker to conduct additional operations on the system, obtain additional environmental information about systems under their control, and enable the theft of sensitive information such as credentials, session tokens and financial account details (cryptocurrency wallet data).&nbsp;</p><h3 id="powershell-modules">PowerShell modules</h3><p>We have observed the delivery of the following types of PowerShell modules during and after the initial infection process. Each module is responsible for carrying out its respective task, and several rely on delivery of C# classes that are dynamically compiled to generate assembly DLLs and executed to assist with collection of survey information, keylogging, and screenshot capture. </p><ul><li>Antivirus detection</li><li>Screen capture</li><li>Wallet grabber</li><li>Keylogger</li><li>Information collection&nbsp;</li><li>Persistence</li></ul><p>In most of the modules analyzed, logging functionality has been built in to allow the attacker to monitor the installation and runtime status during and post-deployment. In most cases, these status updates are delivered to the C2 server in the form of URL parameters that are included as part of HTTP GET requests to the URL used to establish an initial C2 connection.&nbsp;</p><p>We assess with high confidence that additional modules likely exist and are deployable as desired by the adversary. The modular nature of the implementation of this malware provides flexibility and enables the rapid deployment of updates or new functionality as needed. While analyzing activity associated with PS1Bot throughout 2025, we have observed development activities occurring over time, indicating that this is a rapidly evolving threat.</p><h3 id="antivirus-detection">Antivirus detection</h3><p>This PowerShell module is delivered after initial C2 establishment and is responsible for obtaining and reporting the antivirus programs present on the infected system. This is accomplished by querying Windows Management Instrumentation (WMI) to obtain a list of installed antivirus products. </p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://lh7-rt.googleusercontent.com/docsz/AD_4nXdZ9hLYATChXnxpgTThkI5UWN8Vv8t_8mKUWIJxlZhwn7wuKfucQJ2t7Nv95mpZdfl_0xv5Sn_lWjjB2wr5QXdyfJIO3Q5IEr74Shqm5MEIekNL7IKyg2rYWCkZuJeumIrhOyK_?key=S0d5griJyBtZEed2y7A3sw" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="624" height="513"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Figure 4. Antivirus detection logic.</span></figcaption></figure><p>The returned product list is then transmitted to the attacker via an HTTP GET request containing the results of the operation as URL parameters.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://lh7-rt.googleusercontent.com/docsz/AD_4nXf09-5k8nCTdpK3Lfomx2NbeJxu_eMRP1TsgRkR91aaw3x9lIJwNRa9YSrPYsCRWf3cY2THwMuan5Hnx9sIAer_pEifmDF5T0UPdRakaBoGxCBu75SMQny-MhS06r-bkYAu0LAN?key=S0d5griJyBtZEed2y7A3sw" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="624" height="151"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Figure 5. Status logging implementation.</span></figcaption></figure><p>The following is an example of the URL structure used to transmit the information to the C2 server:</p><p><code>hxxp[:]//[C2_SERVER_IP]/[DRIVE_SERIAL]?k=result%20=%20Windows%20Defender;%20%20status%20=%20success</code></p><p>Once this is completed, execution is passed back to the main PowerShell script and C2 beaconing continues until additional instructions are received. In several cases, we have observed the delivery of several distinct PowerShell scripts during the infection process. To facilitate delivery of new PowerShell scripts, we have observed that the attacker simply manipulates the response content associated with the C2 URL derived initially. Each time the infected system beacons to the C2 server, any delivered PowerShell is dynamically passed to IEX and executed.</p><h3 id="screen-capture">Screen capture</h3><p>Once antivirus detection has been performed, we have observed the delivery of additional PowerShell modules, one of which is used to capture screenshots on infected systems and transmit the resulting images to the C2 server. This is often performed for a variety of reasons, including to identify when systems may be in active use by victims versus unattended or to collect sensitive information that may be displayed on screen but not otherwise recorded for easy exfiltration.&nbsp;</p><p>In this case, the adversary is using PowerShell to dynamically compile and execute a C# assembly DLL at runtime. </p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://lh7-rt.googleusercontent.com/docsz/AD_4nXdzFC30wmjc6Z1M1rZDGKYWSod6cQhVfN0YI7gbNLgsJIgakQAx2J4E0olIonhdVTlhiSK-EMuiiauXLm4S8nansl0VT92surEX-bOl-zGuLDbTe2t_-qhoMTc1T59wPlVXcqfq?key=S0d5griJyBtZEed2y7A3sw" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="624" height="232"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Figure 6. Example use of Add-Type for C# compilation.</span></figcaption></figure><p>The resulting DLL is then used to capture the screenshot and create a Bitmap image (.BMP) inside of the <code>%TEMP%</code> directory. The image is later converted and stored as a JPEG at <code>%APPDATA%\Screenshot.jpg</code>.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://lh7-rt.googleusercontent.com/docsz/AD_4nXccgYcxGsbDUUJuvmbkEwvHfGP_vdNhIZ8Znu_iZ5EGieyB7y4oBdVXHhxGHmRzTDPqRRaaYuS65FfnmTb-7g7C-cc9gJiqkxENnLwgHCt2VVhLVZ_50OuSwXH4S1Wzs6Uet0m80A?key=S0d5griJyBtZEed2y7A3sw" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="624" height="309"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Figure 7. Screenshot generation logic.</span></figcaption></figure><p>The content stored within the image file is then Base64 encoded and the resulting data is then transmitted to C2. Survey collection status logging message.</span></figcaption></figure><p>The following WMI queries are performed as part of this process:</p><p><code>SELECT Domain, PartOfDomain FROM Win32_ComputerSystem</code></p><p><code>SELECT DomainName FROM Win32_NTDomain WHERE ClientSiteName IS NOT NULL</code></p><p>In addition, the <code>%USERDNSDOMAIN%</code> environment variable is also queried to attempt to enumerate the domain membership of the infected system. The collected information is transmitted to the attacker’s C2 server, consistent with what was described for other modules.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://lh7-rt.googleusercontent.com/docsz/AD_4nXe31aO_1UdQNndFnFPP-mcHPHIz79SxnIeBy0yDKUX7nEDY5fKKF6kkEV1v_F8bXJG71wi32ea3QmJsKK4XeFOxtEQg5IKAUj_DdrlizxRYbNFuNxbT80HMHisHvMLf_aHb2-W_?key=S0d5griJyBtZEed2y7A3sw" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="624" height="407"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Figure 20. Example status logging implementation.</span></figcaption></figure><h2 id="persistence">Persistence</h2><p>We have also observed the delivery of a persistence module that can be used as desired to ensure that the main looping mechanism is re-executed following a system restart or user session termination. This allows for the reestablishment of a C2 communications channel and enables the delivery of additional modules as desired by the adversary.</p><p>The module begins by attempting to create a PowerShell script that will be executed each time the system restarts. The module creates a randomly generated directory within the <code>%PROGRAMDATA%</code> directory that will be used to store the components needed for persistence. These include a randomly-named PowerShell script (PS1) as well as a randomly-named shortcut file (ICO). A malicious randomly-named LNK file is also created in the Startup directory that is configured to point to the PowerShell script previously created so that it can be executed each time the system is rebooted.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://lh7-rt.googleusercontent.com/docsz/AD_4nXfvnCB5PD2vBYHw8oMsW9XyzYVl363-oj6jfYwQlmSoHURVrIwMik9A3QRlo3kcCLWlroUJuwcDQqCabecD5D62WKvy9cmvrsr7p2aM0RkXgIKuMopOKUFjEL-2mI6-lpr-gGO4?key=S0d5griJyBtZEed2y7A3sw" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="624" height="229"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Figure 21. Persistence module file creation parameters.</span></figcaption></figure><p>The ICO file is created using base64-encoded content delivered as part of the module itself. The PowerShell script contents are generated by retrieving an obfuscated blob from the C2 server, which in our sample was hosted at the URL path <code>/transform</code>.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://lh7-rt.googleusercontent.com/docsz/AD_4nXdVGCfFB3jgxNYkO6emIWKoNL451zosaYi_5Ctw6DDnlDql3n6Wnn_WfdipD2JKZ8fVNbgJ3Yb4LGSt9l3B-lIFaSN2P8hUaY5Rg41THsG-25jpfYQsQE01DDUYXOY06R_ip876tQ?key=S0d5griJyBtZEed2y7A3sw" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="624" height="152"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Figure 22. Persistence payload retrieval.</span></figcaption></figure><p>A simulated example of this process is shown in Figure 23.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://lh7-rt.googleusercontent.com/docsz/AD_4nXdibXn7BxEvBMpnIq0ejmHFU8E8upjxQQw_Dge5BbBKKvuGBSiZujrUqXQ3imqklC3drovhT9fWseZVFpb94AqacE99K-JTqDIK9SOWjoEZwR3raOaR1PtZt08FtbzxLliah2sH?key=S0d5griJyBtZEed2y7A3sw" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="624" height="277"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Figure 23. Simulated delivery of obfuscated persistence payload.</span></figcaption></figure><p>This content is then written to the PS1 file and the LNK file is generated with the appropriate parameters to enable execution in the future. When deobfuscated, the contents of the PowerShell simply contain the same logic used to establish the C2 polling process previously described early in the infection chain.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://lh7-rt.googleusercontent.com/docsz/AD_4nXek-7RbHjjti3fXVd0-DgSRwtsEmKWIS9dZ8c0ARn5jB5rzPRNCUVZzbfR7f7mK2iULwqoPmPtEd966UTKaeZW_3jKOJ0qjEpOn19hYJasIf3Pe_QyjV8fPw7phU-Pam-4jmlZ8Ug?key=S0d5griJyBtZEed2y7A3sw" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="624" height="452"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Figure 24. Deobfuscated persistence payload.</span></figcaption></figure><p>We assess with high confidence that there are likely additional modules available for deployment as-needed by the adversary and the use of this framework provides a flexible means to enhance and increase the functionality available rapidly as needed.</p><h2 id="links-to-previous-intrusion-activity">Links to previous intrusion activity</h2><p>During our analysis of the code and functionality associated with this infection chain, we observed similarities with components referenced in <a href="https://catalyst.prodaft.com/public/report/skitnet/overview"><u>prior reporting</u></a> related to the use of Skitnet/Bossnet to deliver PowerShell modules to infected systems. We have also observed multiple overlaps in the C2 infrastructure used in this campaign and the one described by the aforementioned reporting. Additionally, we assess with high confidence that the final deobfuscated payload dropped by the persistence module previously described was likely created by the same entity who created the PowerShell script described in the prior reporting. The overall implementation, use of specific variables throughout the code, and matching C2 URL construction strengthen this assessment. Below is a comparison of the code in both instances.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://lh7-rt.googleusercontent.com/docsz/AD_4nXc-vhg6G1J23zjwwlgfc6dUuVir1WvA34h_dcWTEFwsXhFNYRp_OBP4OhPtWnEyTBBYimipGMCxAbGpJyaxFOVgykAaIeQGhqKlAIB89azp3lIyLlkVarKxUwZaisK4g_9OCgFoLA?key=S0d5griJyBtZEed2y7A3sw" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="624" height="221"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Figure 25. Comparison of persistence payload (left) vs. ProDaft reporting (right).</span></figcaption></figure><p>As observable in Figure 25, the only difference between the two samples is the addition of mutex handling and sleep periods.</p><p>While Talos did not identify any direct overlap in activity related to these malware families, we noted similarities in the design architecture and functionality provided by the PS1Bot malware delivered in this case and that present in another malware family Talos previously reported on called <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/highlighting-ta866-asylum-ambuscade/"><u>AHK Bot</u></a>. The derivation of the C2 URL path based on the drive serial number is consistent across both malware families. Likewise, the use of a main polling script and subsequent delivery and execution of purpose-built modules is also similar to the design architecture found with AHK Bot. There are also several similarities in the types of modules available for both malware families. Heavy use of URL parameters when communicating with C2 is another similarity between the two families.</p><h2 id="coverage">Coverage</h2><p>Ways our customers can detect and block this threat are listed below.&nbsp; </p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://lh7-rt.googleusercontent.com/docsz/AD_4nXejc7blYY7ztGnanwLersjYV7ZyF-dZ_N-Jbq55Y3eEdu0C4GMKg0rk8C5dipUdVyopDPnTXwDy5htHYgaDKu-FxQ0c613mHkvONGBOPHiEI6gjZP44kZRY6A-mvwpcAo7zJ3a_?key=S0d5griJyBtZEed2y7A3sw" class="kg-image" alt="Picture, Picture, Picture" loading="lazy" width="624" height="201"></figure><p><a href="https://www.cisco.com/c/en/us/products/security/amp-for-endpoints/index.html"><u>Cisco Secure Endpoint</u></a> (formerly AMP for Endpoints) is ideally suited to prevent the execution of the malware detailed in this post.
