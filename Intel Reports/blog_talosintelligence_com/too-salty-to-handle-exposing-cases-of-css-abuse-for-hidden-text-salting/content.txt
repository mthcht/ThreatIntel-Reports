    <h1 class="text-center">Too salty to handle: Exposing cases of CSS abuse for hidden text salting</h1>

    <div class="text-center m-1">
        <div class="post-author">
            <span>By </span>
                <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/author/omid/">Omid Mirzaei</a>
        </div>

        <br/>
        <time class="post-datetime" datetime="October 7, 2025 06:00">
            Tuesday, October 7, 2025 06:00
        </time>

        <div class="m-3">
                            <a href="/category/threat-spotlight/" class="category primary-category">
                                Threat Spotlight
                            </a>
                                </div>
    </div>

    <section class="post-content-wrapper mt-5">
        <div class="post-content">
            <ul><li>Cisco Talos has been closely monitoring the abuse of cascading style sheets (CSS) properties to include irrelevant content (or salt) in different parts of messages, a technique known as hidden text salting.&nbsp;</li><li>This blog is a follow-up to our previous reports in <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/seasoning-email-threats-with-hidden-text-salting/" rel="noreferrer">January</a> and <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/css-abuse-for-evasion-and-tracking/" rel="noreferrer">March</a> 2025 on CSS abuse in emails and shares highlights from a talk given at <a href="https://blueteamcon.com/directory/unseen-but-not-unheard-exposing-the-cases-of-css-abuse-in-email-threats-and-fortifying-defenses/" rel="noreferrer">Blue Team Con 2025</a>.</li><li>Talos explores why hidden text salting is used, where it typically appears in emails, the types of content and techniques involved, how common content concealment (including hidden text salting) is in both spam and legitimate messages, and the impact that hidden text salting has on email security solutions.</li><li>There is widespread use of hidden text salting in malicious emails to bypass detection. Attackers embed hidden salt in the preheader, header, attachments and body — using characters, paragraphs and comments — by manipulating text, visibility and sizing properties. Talos has observed that hidden content is far more often found in spam and other email threats than in legitimate emails, posing a substantial challenge to both basic and advanced email defense solutions that leverage machine learning.</li></ul><hr><p>In a legitimate context, cascading style sheets (CSS) are widely used to control the appearance and layout of content in emails. CSS allows for the styling of text, images and other elements in ways that enhance the visual appeal, readability and branding of the message.</p><p>However, Cisco Talos has observed a rise in the abuse of CSS by the addition of irrelevant content to different parts of emails – a technique known as hidden text salting – for a variety of purposes. In the following blog, we share key findings with the community. This analysis is the result of over one year (March 1, 2024 – July 31, 2025) of continuous monitoring of how adversaries employ this technique in their attacks.</p><h2 id="why-do-threat-actors-employ-hidden-text-salting">Why do threat actors employ hidden text salting?</h2><p>Talos has observed hidden text salting being used to evade detection directly and indirectly by influencing other components of the detection pipeline, such as email language detection.</p><p>The example in Figure 1 is a scam message that impersonates PayPal. In this case, some hidden text has been added to the email to evade detection.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2025/10/1.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="2000" height="991" srcset="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w600/2025/10/1.png 600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1000/2025/10/1.png 1000w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1600/2025/10/1.png 1600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w2400/2025/10/1.png 2400w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Figure 1. A scam email impersonating the PayPal brand.</span></figcaption></figure><p>When the HTML source of the above email is inspected, one can find the sentence "Great news, we’ve got your order," which is hidden using different CSS properties.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2025/10/2.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="2000" height="797" srcset="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w600/2025/10/2.png 600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1000/2025/10/2.png 1000w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1600/2025/10/2.png 1600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w2400/2025/10/2.png 2400w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Figure 2. The HTML source snippet of the above scam email shows how salt is hidden in the above email.</span></figcaption></figure><p>When the "<em>font-size</em>" property of the above "div" (i.e., the container for hidden text) is increased from 1px to 20px, and the "<em>line-height</em>" is removed, the hidden text becomes visible in the top left corner.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2025/10/3.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="1920" height="822" srcset="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w600/2025/10/3.png 600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1000/2025/10/3.png 1000w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1600/2025/10/3.png 1600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2025/10/3.png 1920w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Figure 3. Revealing the hidden salt in the above scam email by changing the "</span><i><em class="italic" style="white-space: pre-wrap;">font-size</em></i><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">" and "</span><i><em class="italic" style="white-space: pre-wrap;">line-height</em></i><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">" properties.</span></figcaption></figure><p>Hidden text salting has also been used to confuse language detection procedures, thus evading possible spam filters that rely on such procedures. The example in Figure 4 shows a phishing email that impersonates the Harbor Freight brand. The language of this email is visibly English.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2025/10/4.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="2000" height="1109" srcset="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w600/2025/10/4.png 600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1000/2025/10/4.png 1000w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1600/2025/10/4.png 1600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w2400/2025/10/4.png 2400w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Figure 4. A phishing email impersonating the Harbor Freight brand.</span></figcaption></figure><p>When the HTML source of the above email is inspected, several French words are found that are visually hidden. In this case, threat actors have used the "<em>display</em>" property of the "div" element to hide the French words, thus confusing the language detection module of Microsoft. <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/defender-office-365/message-headers-eop-mdo#x-microsoft-antispam-message-header-fields">The LANG field</a> specifies the language in which the message was written, and the "X-Forefront-Antispam-Report" header contains information about the message and how it was processed. This header is added to each message by <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/defender-office-365/eop-about">Exchange Online Protection (EOP)</a>, Microsoft's cloud-based filtering service.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2025/10/5-1.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="700" height="525" srcset="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w600/2025/10/5-1.png 600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2025/10/5-1.png 700w"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Figure 5. The HTML source snippet of the above phishing email, with French characters that are hidden using the "</span><i><em class="italic" style="white-space: pre-wrap;">display"</em></i><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;"> property.</span></figcaption></figure><h2 id="what-are-the-most-frequent-parts-of-an-email-where-hidden-salt-is-added">What are the most frequent parts of an email where hidden salt is added?</h2><p>Cisco Talos has identified four main places where hidden salt is added in emails: the preheader, header, attachments and the email body.</p><p>The first two – and least common – places where salt has been added to email threats are the preheader and header. The preheader is a short text that appears after the subject line when an email is viewed in the inbox preview of most email clients. For example, Figure 6 displays a phishing email impersonating the Blue Cross Blue Shield organization.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2025/10/6.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="2000" height="1045" srcset="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w600/2025/10/6.png 600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1000/2025/10/6.png 1000w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1600/2025/10/6.png 1600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w2400/2025/10/6.png 2400w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Figure 6. Phishing email impersonating the Blue Cross Blue Shield organization.</span></figcaption></figure><p>When the HTML source of the above email is examined, one can identify the phrase "FOUR yummy soup recipes just for you!" in the preheader of this message. Most email templates enable threat actors to add preheader text to their emails, which allows attackers to entice readers with additional information.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2025/10/7.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="2000" height="446" srcset="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w600/2025/10/7.png 600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1000/2025/10/7.png 1000w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1600/2025/10/7.png 1600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w2400/2025/10/7.png 2400w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Figure 7. HTML source snippet of the above phishing email, showing how salt is added to the pre-header text.</span></figcaption></figure><p>In this example, the attacker has set the CSS "<em>opacity</em>" property to zero, making the element fully transparent and invisible. Note that this preheader text is kept hidden by relying on multiple CSS properties, including "<em>color</em>," "<em>height</em>," "<em>max-height</em>," and "<em>max-width</em>." Additionally, the "<em>mso-hide</em>" property is set to <em>all</em> to make the preheader invisible in Outlook email clients.</p><p>A third place where hidden text has been added is in attachments. One of the most popular techniques is to introduce irrelevant characters into HTML attachments to hinder the static analysis of files. Figure 8 shows an example phishing email that was sent to a Cisco customer who employed Cisco Secure Email Threat Defense (ETD).</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2025/10/8.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="2000" height="320" srcset="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w600/2025/10/8.png 600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1000/2025/10/8.png 1000w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1600/2025/10/8.png 1600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w2400/2025/10/8.png 2400w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Figure 8. A spear phishing email with HTML attachment.</span></figcaption></figure><p>A snippet of the HTML attachment from the above email is shown in Figure 9, in which threat actors have inserted multiple irrelevant comments between the Base64-encoded characters to complicate the URL decoding process.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2025/10/9.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="2000" height="420" srcset="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w600/2025/10/9.png 600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1000/2025/10/9.png 1000w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1600/2025/10/9.png 1600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w2400/2025/10/9.png 2400w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Figure 9. HTML source snippet.</span></figcaption></figure><p>The HTML source snippet of the above spear phishing email’s attachment shows how comments are added to JavaScript to complicate the static analysis of the HTML attachment.</p><h2 id="what-are-the-most-common-methods-for-hiding-salt-in-emails">What are the most common methods for hiding salt in emails?</h2><p>Several ways exist to hide salt in emails, and attackers rely on various CSS properties to add irrelevant content to different parts of emails while making it visually invisible to recipients. In this section, we cover the most popular methods we’ve observed in the wild.</p><p>Cisco Talos has identified three major categories of properties that have been abused to hide added salt in emails most frequently: text properties, visibility and display properties, and clipping and sizing properties.</p><p>Text properties in CSS allow threat actors to change the characteristics of text, including font size, color, height and width. One simple technique Talos has observed is setting the "<em>font-size</em>" property to a very small number, if not zero, to make text almost invisible to the naked eye. Another common method is to make the font color match its background color.</p><p>Visibility and display properties help threat actors conceal content by changing the display properties of messages. One popular approach is to set the "<em>opacity</em>" property to zero, making the content transparent and invisible to the recipient. Another frequent method is to set the "<em>display</em>" property to "<em>none</em>," which removes the element (e.g., text or image) from the email without affecting the layout. Alternatively, an element’s visibility is often turned off by setting the "<em>visibility</em>" property to "<em>hidden</em>."</p><p>CSS properties can also be used to change the size of a container element. These elements (e.g., a text box) act as containers for other elements in emails. Additionally, CSS properties can be used to clip elements in emails (e.g., clipping text into a circle or rectangle). Threat actors abuse both of these properties to hide salt in emails. One popular method Talos has observed is setting the "<em>width</em>" property of the container element — the area that contains salt — to zero. Another method is to force the added salt to overflow the container element (e.g., by placing large text into a circle with radius zero) and then leverage CSS properties to control the behavior when content extends beyond the container’s boundaries. For example, in one campaign, the added salt was made invisible by clipping it in a tiny rectangle and setting the "<em>overflow</em>" property to "<em>hidden</em>."</p><h2 id="how-prevalent-is-content-concealment-in-spam-and-ham-messages">How prevalent is content concealment in spam and ham messages?</h2><p>Talos has observed that content concealment using CSS properties occurs much more frequently in spam messages and email threats than in legitimate emails (ham). We then searched for these indicators in emails reclassified by Cisco Secure ETD customers. Therefore, two possible countermeasures are: first, to detect the presence of hidden text (or salt) in emails, and more importantly, to filter out the added salt before passing the message to downstream detection engines.</p><p>In what follows, we provide a few mitigation solutions for each domain.</p><p>Detection: One security mitigation solution is to rely on advanced filtering mechanisms that can more effectively detect hidden text salting and content concealment. This approach is particularly beneficial against image-based threats.</p><p>Filtering: With respect to discarding irrelevant content (or salt) that has been added to emails, at least two options are available. The first option is to perform HTML sanitization at ingestion in order to strip or escape invisible text before it reaches downstream detection engines.
