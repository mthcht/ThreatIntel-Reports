<div class="section post-body">
<span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><p><span data-contrast="auto">At the beginning of the invasion of Ukraine, we released a </span><a href="/security-advisory-a-risk-based-approach-for-improving-your-cybersecurity-posture-due-to-the-invasion-of-ukraine?hsLang=en-us" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span data-contrast="none">security advisory</span></a><span data-contrast="auto"> with recommendations based on different risk tiers. Since then, our </span><a href="https://www.bitdefender.com/blog/labs/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span data-contrast="none">Threat Intelligence (TI)</span></a><span data-contrast="auto"> and </span><a href="https://www.bitdefender.com/business/enterprise-products/managed-detection-response-service.html" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span data-contrast="none">Managed Detection and Response (MDR)</span></a><span data-contrast="auto"> teams have been actively monitoring the situation and identifying active threats. Not surprisingly, the highest risk group contains businesses and organizations located in Ukraine, especially government entities and critical infrastructure.</span></p>
<!--more-->
<p><span data-contrast="auto">One of the groups actively engaged in pro-Russian cyber-attacks is </span><strong><span data-contrast="auto">UAC-0056. </span></strong><span data-contrast="auto">This group has been active since at least March 2021, and its primary objective seems to be cyber espionage with a focus on key state sectors. Other names for this group are Lorec53, UNC2589, EmberBear, LorecBear, BleedingBear, SaintBear, and TA471.</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">This group has been associated with attacks using OutSteel and GraphSteel stealers (malicious software designed to steal data). OutSteel was written in the AutoIt language, while GraphSteel was written in the Go language (often referred to as Golang). While both languages are known for their ease of use, AutoIt is a simpler language often used by system administrators and scripters. The behavior of Go-based GraphSteel is also more sophisticated – while its primary purpose is harvesting credentials, it is also trying to exfiltrate the most common archives and Office formats like </span><code><span data-contrast="auto" style="background-color: #cccccc;">.docx</span></code><span data-contrast="auto"> or </span><code><span data-contrast="auto" style="background-color: #cccccc;">.xlsx</span></code><span data-contrast="auto"> and locate sensitive files like </span><code><span data-contrast="auto" style="background-color: #cccccc;">.ssh</span></code><span data-contrast="auto">, </span><code><span data-contrast="auto" style="background-color: #cccccc;">.crt</span></code><span data-contrast="auto">, </span><code><span data-contrast="auto" style="background-color: #cccccc;">.key</span></code><span data-contrast="auto">, </span><code><span data-contrast="auto" style="background-color: #cccccc;">.ovpn</span></code><span data-contrast="auto">, or </span><code><span data-contrast="auto" style="background-color: #cccccc;">.json</span></code><span data-contrast="auto">. </span></p>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">Due to the inclusion of media files with extensions like </span><code><span data-contrast="auto" style="background-color: #cccccc;">.jpg</span></code><span data-contrast="auto">, </span><code><span data-contrast="auto" style="background-color: #cccccc;">.png</span></code><span data-contrast="auto">, </span><code><span data-contrast="auto" style="background-color: #cccccc;">.gif</span></code><span data-contrast="auto">, </span><span style="background-color: #cccccc;"><code>.webp</code></span><span data-contrast="auto">, </span><code><span data-contrast="auto" style="background-color: #cccccc;">.avi</span></code><span data-contrast="auto">, </span><span style="background-color: #cccccc;"><code>.mkv</code></span><span data-contrast="auto">, </span><code><span data-contrast="auto" style="background-color: #cccccc;">.mpg</span></code><span data-contrast="auto">, </span><span style="background-color: #cccccc;"><code>.mpeg</code></span><span data-contrast="auto">, and </span><span style="background-color: #cccccc;"><code>.3gp</code></span><span data-contrast="auto">, GraphSteel limits its searches to files that are 50 MB or smaller. While there are certain similarities between OutSteel and GraphSteel, there is not a clear connection between them currently. </span><a href="https://cert.gov.ua/article/37704" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank"><span>The original announcement</span></a><span data-contrast="auto"> by the Computer Emergency Response Team of Ukraine (CERT-UA) regarding GraphSteel indicates an average level of certainty for attribution to UAC-0056.</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">For the rest of this report, we will focus on attacks involving the use of GraphSteel malware. GraphSteel is part of the Elephant Framework – a collection of tools also written in the Go language and deployed in a recent wave of <a href="https://www.bitdefender.com/business/infozone/what-is-phishing.html" rel="noopener">phishing attacks</a> on </span><code><span data-contrast="auto" style="background-color: #cccccc;">.gov.ua</span></code><span data-contrast="auto"> targets. Recently, three different attacks have been observed which relied on the Elephant Framework:</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<ul>
<li data-leveltext="-" data-font="Calibri" data-listid="6" aria-setsize="-1" data-aria-posinset="0" data-aria-level="1"><span data-contrast="auto">February 11</span><span data-contrast="auto">th</span><span data-contrast="auto">, 2022 – SentinelOne </span><a href="https://www.sentinelone.com/blog/threat-actor-uac-0056-targeting-ukraine-with-fake-translation-software/" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank"><span data-contrast="none">detected</span></a><span data-contrast="auto"> an attack with fake dictionary software</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></li>
<li data-leveltext="-" data-font="Calibri" data-listid="6" aria-setsize="-1" data-aria-posinset="0" data-aria-level="1"><span data-contrast="auto">March 11</span><span data-contrast="auto">th</span><span data-contrast="auto">, 2022 – CERT-UA </span><a href="https://cert.gov.ua/article/37704" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank"><span data-contrast="none">reported</span></a><span data-contrast="auto"> an attack with fake antivirus software</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></li>
<li data-leveltext="-" data-font="Calibri" data-listid="6" aria-setsize="-1" data-aria-posinset="0" data-aria-level="1"><span data-contrast="auto">March 28</span><span data-contrast="auto">th</span><span data-contrast="auto">, 2022 – CERT-UA </span><a href="https://cert.gov.ua/article/38374" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank"><span data-contrast="none">reported</span></a><span data-contrast="auto"> an attack with an “Unpaid wages” email subject</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></li>
</ul>
<h2><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}"><span data-contrast="none" xml:lang="EN-US" lang="EN-US">Anatomy of an Attack</span></span></h2>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">In all known Elephant Framework attacks, the <a href="https://www.bitdefender.com/business/infozone/what-is-spear-phishing.html" rel="noopener">spear-phishing</a> tactic was used for initial compromise. The group demonstrated a good knowledge of social engineering techniques, with emails originating from spoofed Ukrainian email addresses. Email subject and body would often use trending themes (COVID) or use official-looking text.</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">In one of the emails, the threat actor included recommendations for effective security controls after warning about intensified computer attacks by the Russian Federation, including recommendations to use email and web traffic filtering, avoid the use of 3</span><span data-contrast="auto">rd</span><span data-contrast="auto"> party DNS servers, and provide a briefing to employees about possible phishing attacks. This “helpful” email cleverly embedded a link to a malicious payload (masquerading as a recommended antivirus tool).</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">A few different techniques were used to execute the malicious launcher. In this example, the link to the malicious download is included in the body of the email. In other cases, an attached Excel spreadsheet with embedded macros was used.</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<h3><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}"><span data-contrast="none" xml:lang="EN-US" lang="EN-US">Launcher Component</span></span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}"><span data-contrast="none" xml:lang="EN-US" lang="EN-US"></span></span></h3>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">There are a few different variants of </span><strong><span data-contrast="auto">launchers</span></strong><span data-contrast="auto"> for GraphSteel that we have seen to date. In the case reported by </span><a href="https://www.sentinelone.com/blog/threat-actor-uac-0056-targeting-ukraine-with-fake-translation-software/" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank"><span data-contrast="none">SentinelOne</span></a><span data-contrast="auto">, the downloaded launcher was a Python script converted to an executable (using </span><a href="https://pypi.org/project/pyinstaller/" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank"><span data-contrast="none">pyinstaller</span></a><span data-contrast="auto">). In the other cases, the launcher was written in the Go language like the rest of the Elephant Framework with the launcher’s name varying depending on the attack.</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">Why might threat actors choose the Go language, which is not a mainstream programming language, for this malicious software? Potential reasons include:</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<ul>
<li data-leveltext="-" data-font="Calibri" data-listid="5" aria-setsize="-1" data-aria-posinset="0" data-aria-level="1"><span data-contrast="auto">Some security vendors may struggle to detect malware written in the Go language given its less frequent use</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></li>
<li data-leveltext="-" data-font="Calibri" data-listid="5" aria-setsize="-1" data-aria-posinset="0" data-aria-level="1"><span data-contrast="auto">The payload can be compiled for both Windows and Linux (without code changes)</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></li>
<li data-leveltext="-" data-font="Calibri" data-listid="5" aria-setsize="-1" data-aria-posinset="0" data-aria-level="1"><span data-contrast="auto">It is easy to use and can be expanded by 3</span><span data-contrast="auto">rd</span><span data-contrast="auto"> party modules</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></li>
</ul>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">After analyzing the GraphSteel code, we identified references to other community modules used by the Elephant Framework; for example, for </span><a href="https://github.com/jcmturner/aescts" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank"><span data-contrast="none">AES cipher</span></a><span data-contrast="auto">, </span><a href="https://github.com/denisbrodbeck/machineid" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank"><span data-contrast="none">generating a unique client ID</span></a><span data-contrast="auto">, or </span><a href="https://github.com/redcode-labs/Coldfire" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank"><span data-contrast="none">Coldfire</span></a><span data-contrast="auto"> (a <a href="https://www.bitdefender.com/business/infozone/what-is-malware.html" rel="noopener">malware</a> development framework for Golang).&nbsp;</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">The launcher does not have the malware payload embedded – instead, it acts as a combination of a downloader and dropper. Upon execution, the launcher connects to the command and control (C&amp;C) server, downloads the malware payload encoded as base64 string, saves it to the local disk and then executes it. The address of the C&amp;C server is hardcoded in this executable and, in all recorded cases, the file dropped by this executable is named </span><code><span data-contrast="auto" style="background-color: #cccccc;">Java-sdk.exe</span></code><span data-contrast="auto">.</span></p>
<h3><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}"><span data-contrast="none" xml:lang="EN-US" lang="EN-US">Downloader Component</span></span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}"><span data-contrast="none" xml:lang="EN-US" lang="EN-US"></span></span></h3>
<p><code><span data-contrast="auto" style="background-color: #cccccc;">Java-sdk.exe</span></code><span data-contrast="auto"> acts as a </span><strong><span data-contrast="auto">downloader</span></strong><span data-contrast="auto"> of the Elephant Framework and, as you probably are expecting by now, is written in the Go language. It uses a similar technique as the launcher – first connecting to a C&amp;C server, then streams a string encoded in base64 which contains the malicious payload, saves it as an executable to disk, and executes it. The address of the C&amp;C server is not embedded – it is provided by the launcher as </span><span data-contrast="auto">a <code><span style="background-color: #cccccc;">base64(AES(&lt;C&amp;C&gt;))</span></code></span><span data-contrast="auto"> argument. Two different malware files are downloaded – GraphSteel (</span><code><span data-contrast="auto" style="background-color: #cccccc;">Microsoft-cortana.exe</span></code><span data-contrast="auto">) and GrimPlant (</span><span style="background-color: #cccccc;"><code>Oracle-java.exe</code></span><span data-contrast="auto">) which are automatically executed. </span><strong><span data-contrast="auto">GrimPlant</span></strong><span data-contrast="auto"> is a relatively simple backdoor that allows remote execution of PowerShell commands. </span><strong><span data-contrast="auto">GraphSteel</span></strong><span data-contrast="auto"> is used for data exfiltration of credentials, certificates, passwords, and other sensitive information.</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">This downloader component is also responsible for establishing persistence by creating a registry value </span><code><span data-contrast="auto" style="background-color: #cccccc;">Java-SDK</span></code><span data-contrast="auto"> under the registry key </span><code><span data-contrast="auto" style="background-color: #cccccc;">HKEY_CURRENT_USER\SOFTWARE\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Run\</span></code><span data-contrast="auto">.&nbsp;</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}"><span data-contrast="auto" xml:lang="EN-US" lang="EN-US"></span><span role="presentation" contenteditable="false"><img alt="Diagram
Description automatically generated" src="https://businessresources.bitdefender.com/hubfs/undefined-Apr-22-2022-02-47-07-32-PM.png" loading="lazy"></span><span data-contrast="auto" xml:lang="EN-US" lang="EN-US"></span></span></p>
<p style="font-size: 14px; text-align: center;"><i><span data-contrast="auto">Typical flow of an attack based on the Elephant Framework</span></i></p>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">This covers our findings for the initial phase where the Elephant Framework is deployed to the compromised machine. In the next section, we will look in more detail at the core components of the Elephant Framework, GrimPlant and GraphSteel. Both implants are written in the Go language, </span><a href="https://www.intezer.com/blog/research/elephant-malware-targeting-ukrainian-orgs/" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank"><span>comprehensive research</span></a><span data-contrast="auto"> is available from Intezer.</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<h3 style="font-size: 16px; text-align: left;"><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:2,&quot;335551620&quot;:2,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}"> <span data-contrast="none" xml:lang="EN-US" lang="EN-US">GrimPlant (Backdoor) Component</span></span></h3>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">GrimPlant’s primary purpose is to allow a threat actor to execute PowerShell commands remotely. The address of the C&amp;C server is provided by </span><code><span data-contrast="auto" style="background-color: #cccccc;">Java-sdk.exe</span></code><span data-contrast="auto"> using the command line parameter </span><code><span data-contrast="auto" style="background-color: #cccccc;">-addr</span></code><span data-contrast="auto">. This address is not provided in plain text, instead, it uses the same </span><code><span data-contrast="auto" style="background-color: #cccccc;">base64(AES(&lt;C&amp;C&gt;))</span></code><span data-contrast="auto"> syntax as the downloader.</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">Communication with the C&amp;C server uses port 80 and is based on </span><a href="https://grpc.io/" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank"><span data-contrast="none">gRPC</span></a><span data-contrast="auto"> – an open-source Remote Procedure Call (RPC) framework, originally designed by Google. The communications are encrypted with TLS, with the certificate hardcoded in the binary.&nbsp;</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">After establishing a connection to the C&amp;C server, GrimPlant sends a heartbeat message every 10 seconds. Included in the heartbeat message is information about the infected endpoint (</span><code><span data-contrast="auto" style="background-color: #cccccc;">uploadSystemInfo</span></code> <span data-contrast="auto">function)</span><span data-contrast="auto">:</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<ul>
<li data-leveltext="" data-font="Symbol" data-listid="8" aria-setsize="-1" data-aria-posinset="1" data-aria-level="1"><span data-contrast="auto">Operating System – Hostname, operating system, number of CPUs</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></li>
<li data-leveltext="" data-font="Symbol" data-listid="8" aria-setsize="-1" data-aria-posinset="2" data-aria-level="1"><span data-contrast="auto">IP Address – Runs a query to </span><code><span data-contrast="auto" style="background-color: #cccccc;">api.ipify.org</span></code><span data-contrast="auto"> to retrieve a public IP address</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}"> <br></span></li>
<li><span data-contrast="auto">User Info – Name, username, HomeDir</span></li>
</ul>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">This malware and its heartbeat message run in an infinite loop, waiting to receive commands from the C&amp;C server and execute them using </span><code><span data-contrast="auto" style="background-color: #cccccc;">PowerShell.exe</span></code><span data-contrast="auto">.</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<h3><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}"><span data-contrast="none" xml:lang="EN-US" lang="EN-US">GraphSteel (Stealer) Component</span></span></h3>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">GraphSteel’s primary purpose is to exfiltrate data from infected machines. The address of the C&amp;C server is retrieved using the same method as GrimPlant. All communication is encrypted using the AES cipher on port 443. To communicate with the C&amp;C server, it uses WebSockets and the GraphQL query language.</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">Below are the functions used by this malware:</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<ul>
<li data-leveltext="" data-font="Symbol" data-listid="9" aria-setsize="-1" data-aria-posinset="1" data-aria-level="1"><strong><span data-contrast="auto">getFileHash()</span></strong><span data-contrast="auto"> – </span><span data-contrast="auto">Checks if the file has been uploaded on the server</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></li>
