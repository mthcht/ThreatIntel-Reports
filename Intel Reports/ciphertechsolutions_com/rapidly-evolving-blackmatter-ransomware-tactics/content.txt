<h1 class="has-text-color wp-block-heading" style="color:#0b6287">Rapidly Evolving BlackMatter Ransomware Tactics</h1>



<div class="wp-block-image is-style-default"><figure class="aligncenter size-large is-resized"><img fetchpriority="high" decoding="async" src="https://www.ciphertechsolutions.com/wp-content/uploads/BlackMatter-Ransomware.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-1397" width="721" height="398" srcset="https://www.ciphertechsolutions.com/wp-content/uploads/BlackMatter-Ransomware.jpg 815w, https://www.ciphertechsolutions.com/wp-content/uploads/BlackMatter-Ransomware-300x166.jpg 300w, https://www.ciphertechsolutions.com/wp-content/uploads/BlackMatter-Ransomware-768x424.jpg 768w" sizes="(max-width: 721px) 100vw, 721px" /></figure></div>



<p>September 8, 2021 | By Cipher Tech ACCE Team</p>



<h1 class="has-text-color wp-block-heading" style="color:#0b6287">Summary&nbsp;</h1>



<p style="font-size:17px">Cipher Tech analysts monitoring VirusTotal for&nbsp;BlackMatter&nbsp;ransomware activity discovered new variants&nbsp;of&nbsp;BlackMatter&nbsp;malware self-reporting as versions&nbsp;1.9&nbsp;and&nbsp;2.0. The new&nbsp;BlackMatter&nbsp;malware samples contain additional functionality,&nbsp;changes to the configuration data, and version 2.0 additionally introduces changes to the configuration decryption algorithm. Cipher Tech analysts developed an ACCE module to automate the extraction of&nbsp;BlackMatter&nbsp;malware’s configuration data. Cipher Tech’s analysis reveals&nbsp;new variants&nbsp;of&nbsp;BlackMatter&nbsp;malware and a refinement in BlackMatter&#8217;s tactics, techniques, and procedures.&nbsp;</p>



<h1 class="has-text-color wp-block-heading" style="color:#0b6287">Who is&nbsp;BlackMatter&nbsp;</h1>



<p style="font-size:17px">BlackMatter&nbsp;is a ransomware affiliate program actively attacking victims that was first identified in July 2021&nbsp;by&nbsp;<a rel="noreferrer noopener" href="https://www.recordedfuture.com/blackmatter-ransomware-successor-darkside-revil/" target="_blank">RecordedFuture</a>&nbsp;and a security researcher&nbsp;<a rel="noreferrer noopener" href="https://twitter.com/pancak3lullz/status/1420022168314945549" target="_blank">pancak3</a>.&nbsp;BlackMatter&nbsp;claims&nbsp;to derive from the now inactive REvil and&nbsp;DarkSide&nbsp;ransomware affiliate programs. Supporting BlackMatter’s&nbsp;claim,&nbsp;a&nbsp;joint code correlation effort between&nbsp;<a rel="noreferrer noopener" href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/darkside-ransomware-gang-returns-as-new-blackmatter-operation/" target="_blank">BleepingComputer&nbsp;and&nbsp;Emsisoft</a>&nbsp;suggests&nbsp;that&nbsp;BlackMatter&nbsp;malware&#8217;s’ encryption routines contain&nbsp;strong similarities to&nbsp;DarkSide&nbsp;malware samples.&nbsp;</p>



<p style="font-size:17px">BlackMatter&nbsp;claims to target companies in the US, UK, Canada, or Australia with between 500 – 15,000 hosts and revenues of at least 100K. BlackMatter claims they&nbsp;will not target the following&nbsp;industries and&nbsp;will provide free decryption services to&nbsp;BlackMatter&nbsp;victims in these industries.&nbsp;</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list" style="font-size:17px"><li>Hospitals&nbsp;</li><li>Critical infrastructure facilities&nbsp;</li><li>Oil and gas industry&nbsp;</li><li>Defense industry&nbsp;</li><li>Non-profit companies&nbsp;</li><li>Government sector&nbsp;</li></ul>



<h1 class="has-text-color wp-block-heading" style="color:#0b6287">Background&nbsp;</h1>



<p style="font-size:17px">Cipher Tech analysts&nbsp;leveraged ACCE to discover, decrypt,&nbsp;and parse&nbsp;configuration data from&nbsp;BlackMatter&nbsp;ransomware samples&nbsp;with reported version numbers&nbsp;of 1.9&nbsp;and 2.0.&nbsp;Cipher Tech has not identified&nbsp;detailed&nbsp;public reporting&nbsp;for samples&nbsp;with a version number above 1.2. This suggests either a fast development cycle or a&nbsp;refinement&nbsp;in TTPs with respect to versioning.&nbsp;</p>



<p style="font-size:17px">Cipher Tech’s ACCE software enables the automated extraction of configuration data from&nbsp;supported malware samples. ACCE indicated that parsing the 1.9&nbsp;and 2.0&nbsp;samples configuration&nbsp;data&nbsp;differed from the expected format&nbsp;of ACCE’s&nbsp;existing&nbsp;BlackMatter&nbsp;configuration parser. Based on the change in configuration, Cipher Analysts&nbsp;examined&nbsp;both&nbsp;samples to identify differences between BlackMatter&#8217;s 1.2 ransomware sample, BlackMatter&#8217;s&nbsp;1.9&nbsp;ransomware&nbsp;sample, and&nbsp;BlackMatter’s&nbsp;2.0 ransomware sample.&nbsp;</p>



<p style="font-size:17px">Analysis of the&nbsp;BlackMatter&nbsp;1.9&nbsp;sample and its configuration data&nbsp;revealed that&nbsp;BlackMatter&nbsp;added additional capabilities to BlackMatter 1.9. BlackMatter 1.9 contains the following new functionality:&nbsp;</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list" style="font-size:17px"><li>Print ransom notes to a locally installed computer&nbsp;</li><li>Check the hostname of a compromised system prior to installation&nbsp;</li><li>Additional verification checks to ensure&nbsp;BlackMatter&nbsp;does not encrypt their own ransom note&nbsp;</li><li>Provide&nbsp;additional details and evidence of stolen information within the ransom note&nbsp;</li></ul>



<p class="has-text-align-left" style="font-size:17px">Further analysis of the&nbsp;BlackMatter&nbsp;2.0 sample, reported by&nbsp;<a rel="noreferrer noopener" href="https://twitter.com/sisoma2/status/1431175543341338626?s=21" target="_blank">@sisoma2 on Twitter</a>,&nbsp;and its configuration data revealed that BlackMatter uses all the changes indicated for 1.9 above, and changed the decryption algorithm&nbsp;for the configuration data and some encrypted strings. BlackMatter 2.0 uses the following decryption methodology:&nbsp;</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list" style="font-size:17px"><li>A 64-bit seed/key for configuration and string decryption&nbsp;</li><li>A modified MMIX-LCG&nbsp;pseudo-random number generator (PRNG) algorithm for generating the key used to decrypt each 8-byte block of the encrypted configuration data / string.&nbsp;</li><li>A byte-swap algorithm for&nbsp;adjusting the generated MMIX-LCG state value into an XOR key for decrypting each 8-byte block.&nbsp;</li></ul>



<h1 class="has-text-color wp-block-heading" style="color:#0b6287">Samples Compared&nbsp;</h1>



<p style="font-size:17px">This post&nbsp;will demonstrate these changes&nbsp;in&nbsp;BlackMatter&nbsp;Versions&nbsp;1.9&nbsp;and 2.0&nbsp;in comparison to a&nbsp;BlackMatter&nbsp;Version 1.2 sample.&nbsp;For the purposes of&nbsp;the analysis presented below:&nbsp;</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list" style="font-size:17px"><li>Version 1.2 reflects the&nbsp;BlackMatter&nbsp;Ransomware with MD5&nbsp;598c53bfef81e489375f09792e487f1a, compiled&nbsp;2021-07-23T20:51:18+00:00&nbsp;</li><li>Version 1.9 reflects the&nbsp;BlackMatter&nbsp;Ransomware with MD5&nbsp;f1c260c31b9d3f9ff54a142d508ec602, compiled&nbsp;2021-08-12T22:22:01+00:00&nbsp;</li><li>Version 2.0 reflects the&nbsp;BlackMatter&nbsp;Ransomware with MD5&nbsp;38035325b785329e3f618b2a0b90eb75, compiled&nbsp;2021-08-16T07:13:07+00:00&nbsp;</li></ul>

<p class="has-text-align-center"></p>



<p style="font-size:17px">As displayed in the screenshot below, the comparison is achieved by&nbsp;hashing and&nbsp;comparing the hostname&nbsp;to the list of invalid hostname hashes, using the ROR-13-ADD&nbsp;hash algorithm (lowercase string, include null terminator).&nbsp;</p>



<div class="wp-block-image is-style-default"><figure class="aligncenter size-large is-resized"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" src="https://www.ciphertechsolutions.com/wp-content/uploads/image-18-961x1024.png" alt="" class="wp-image-1352" width="559" height="596" srcset="https://www.ciphertechsolutions.com/wp-content/uploads/image-18-961x1024.png 961w, https://www.ciphertechsolutions.com/wp-content/uploads/image-18-282x300.png 282w, https://www.ciphertechsolutions.com/wp-content/uploads/image-18-768x818.png 768w, https://www.ciphertechsolutions.com/wp-content/uploads/image-18.png 1184w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 559px) 100vw, 559px" /></figure></div>



<p class="has-text-align-center"><em>Figure&nbsp;13: Version 1.9 Invalid Host Name Check</em>&nbsp;</p>



<p style="font-size:17px">The Version 1.9 analyzed sample was not configured with&nbsp;any invalid hostname hashes, however, it can be surmised that&nbsp;the malware authors are attempting to avoid running&nbsp;in Sandboxes&nbsp;or other Virtual Analysis Environments.&nbsp;</p>



<h5 class="has-text-color wp-block-heading" style="color:#0b6287">Difference 3:&nbsp;Configuration Parameter&nbsp;–&nbsp;Ransom Note Hash&nbsp;</h5>



<div class="wp-block-image is-style-default"><figure class="aligncenter size-large is-resized"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" src="https://www.ciphertechsolutions.com/wp-content/uploads/image-34.png" alt="" class="wp-image-1387" width="662" height="418" srcset="https://www.ciphertechsolutions.com/wp-content/uploads/image-34.png 626w, https://www.ciphertechsolutions.com/wp-content/uploads/image-34-300x189.png 300w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 662px) 100vw, 662px" /></figure></div>



<p class="has-text-align-center"><em>Figure&nbsp;14: Version 1.2/1.9 Configuration Structure</em>&nbsp;</p>



<p style="font-size:17px">The final configuration difference is the inclusion of the ransom note&nbsp;hash,&nbsp;using the ROR-13-ADD hash algorithm (include null terminator, use seed of -1).&nbsp;</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large is-resized is-style-default"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" src="https://www.ciphertechsolutions.com/wp-content/uploads/image-36-1024x234.png" alt="" class="wp-image-1391" width="1234" height="281" srcset="https://www.ciphertechsolutions.com/wp-content/uploads/image-36-1024x234.png 1024w, https://www.ciphertechsolutions.com/wp-content/uploads/image-36-300x68.png 300w, https://www.ciphertechsolutions.com/wp-content/uploads/image-36-768x175.png 768w, https://www.ciphertechsolutions.com/wp-content/uploads/image-36.png 1314w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 1234px) 100vw, 1234px" /></figure>



<p class="has-text-align-center"><em>Figure&nbsp;15: Version 1.2/1.9 Verify Not Ransom Note Function</em>&nbsp;</p>



<p style="font-size:17px">In Version 1.2 on the left, we can observe that&nbsp;the&nbsp;BlackMatter&nbsp;ransomware will&nbsp;verify the&nbsp;input file is the same size as the ransom note, and that the filename hash&nbsp;(ROR-13-ADD hash algorithm (lowercase string, include null terminator, use seed of -1))&nbsp;is the same as the previously calculated value.&nbsp;On the right, Version 1.9 contains the same checks, but additionally hashes the file data to compare against the configured hash value.&nbsp;</p>



<p style="font-size:17px">Ransom notes have&nbsp;been observed in varying sizes, with some containing extra information in comparison to others, so the purpose of this change is unknown.&nbsp;</p>



<h5 class="has-text-color wp-block-heading" style="color:#0b6287">Difference 4:&nbsp;Ransom Note&nbsp;</h5>



<p style="font-size:17px">The ransom note&nbsp;obtained from Version 1.9 contains significantly more&nbsp;content&nbsp;than the one obtained from Version 1.2. The additional content includes&nbsp;details of the&nbsp;type&nbsp;of information stolen&nbsp;from the victim and&nbsp;<em>print.sc</em>&nbsp;links&nbsp;which presumably provide evidence that the information has been stolen.&nbsp;<em>print.sc</em>&nbsp;is a&nbsp;screenshot sharing service associated with the&nbsp;<a rel="noreferrer noopener" href="https://app.prntscr.com/en/index.html" target="_blank">LightShot</a>&nbsp;screenshot application.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<div class="wp-block-image is-style-default"><figure class="aligncenter size-large is-resized"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" src="https://www.ciphertechsolutions.com/wp-content/uploads/image-23-1024x807.png" alt="" class="wp-image-1357" width="756" height="595" srcset="https://www.ciphertechsolutions.com/wp-content/uploads/image-23-1024x807.png 1024w, https://www.ciphertechsolutions.com/wp-content/uploads/image-23-300x236.png 300w, https://www.ciphertechsolutions.com/wp-content/uploads/image-23-768x605.png 768w, https://www.ciphertechsolutions.com/wp-content/uploads/image-23.png 1286w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 756px) 100vw, 756px" /></figure></div>



<p class="has-text-align-center"><em>Figure&nbsp;16: Version 1.9 Ransom Note</em>&nbsp;</p>

<h1 class="has-text-color wp-block-heading" style="color:#0b6287">Conclusion&nbsp;</h1>



<p style="font-size:17px">The&nbsp;BlackMatter&nbsp;ransomware group is actively compromising victims and&nbsp;evolving their ransomware tooling and processes; network defenders need to remain just as vigilant.&nbsp;Cipher Tech’s ACCE solution&nbsp;leverages more than a decade of defense expertise to&nbsp;provide&nbsp;a service that can support your incident response and malware&nbsp;analysis&nbsp;teams in&nbsp;automating the process of&nbsp;identifying,&nbsp;unpacking,&nbsp;deobfuscating, decrypting, and parsing&nbsp;supported&nbsp;adversarial tooling.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<p style="font-size:17px">Cipher Tech will continue to monitor the ransomware threat landscape and do our part to inform defenders on the threats to their networks and their customers’ data.&nbsp;</p>
