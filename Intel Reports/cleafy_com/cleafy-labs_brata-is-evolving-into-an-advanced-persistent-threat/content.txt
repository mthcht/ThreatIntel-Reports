During the last months, a new <strong>BRATA.A</strong> variant has been spotted in EU territory posing as specific bank applications, including some internal changes, such as:</p><ul role="list"><li>A new phishing technique that is in charge of mimicking a login page of the targeted bank;</li><li>Brand new classes in charge to acquire GPS, overlay, SMS and device management permissions;</li><li>Sideloading a piece of code (second stage) downloaded from its C2 to perform Event Logging.</li></ul><p>In this article, we give an overview of these Â new features in order to figure out their purposes and forecast new evolutions.</p><figure style="max-width:2578pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/62a9a0ed47d29345ace50e65_1.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 1 â BRATA activities during 2021/2022"/></div><figcaption>Figure 1 â BRATA activities during 2021/2022</figcaption></figure><p> [<a href="https://www.cleafy.com/labs">1</a>] <a href="https://www.cleafy.com/labs">https://www.cleafy.com/labs</a></p><p> [<a href="https://www.cleafy.com/cleafy-labs/how-brata-is-monitoring-your-bank-account">2</a>] <a href="https://www.cleafy.com/cleafy-labs/how-brata-is-monitoring-your-bank-account">https://www.cleafy.com/cleafy-labs/how-brata-is-monitoring-your-bank-account</a></p><p> [<a href="https://www.cleafy.com/cleafy-labs/mobile-banking-fraud-brata-strikes-again">3</a>] <a href="https://www.cleafy.com/cleafy-labs/mobile-banking-fraud-brata-strikes-again">https://www.cleafy.com/cleafy-labs/mobile-banking-fraud-brata-strikes-again</a></p></div></section><section id="2" class="bigmarginbottom"><h3 class="titlesectionclab">BRATA updates</h3><div class="blog w-richtext"><p>As we already mentioned in the previous paragraph, TAs are modifying their code in order to tailor their malware on specific banking institutions. This code refractory is actually doing small changes compared to old versions of BRATA, as there are a bunch of classes that have been added for very specific purposes. </p><p>Nevertheless, before proceeding with a deep dive into BRATAâs features, in Figure 2 we have highlighted all new functions that we observed in the last month. Most of them are very specific and their purpose is crystal clear.</p><figure style="max-width:794pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/62a9a192b111a952c671530d_2.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 2 â Differences between BRATA variants"/></div><figcaption>Figure 2 â Differences between BRATA variants</figcaption></figure><p>Speaking about examples, we could observe a login class that disguises a classic login page in order to harvest credentials from unaware users, as well as classes like <em>startactdevmang</em>, <em>startactgpper</em>, <em>startactoverlay</em> and <em>startsmspermnew</em> have been introduced to request additional permissions for later fraud phases (e.g., device administration, gps, overlay, SMS, etc. ).</p></div></section><section id="3" class="bigmarginbottom"><h3 class="titlesectionclab">Credential Harvesting Attack</h3><div class="blog w-richtext"><p>Investigation on this sample has led our researchers to discover that BRATA has been equipped with a phishing page that recreates a login page of a famous Italian bank. In this way, TAs are trying to steal sensitive information from their victims to perform some sort of social engineering in a later stage of the fraud. As shown in Figure 3, the victim is lured to type <em>Numero Cliente</em> and <em>PIN</em>. This information is the foundation of the authentication process commonly used by banks.</p><figure style="max-width:1124pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/62a9a25313fdaf9854fec58c_3.png" loading="lazy" alt=""/></div><figcaption>Figure 3 - BRATA phishing page</figcaption></figure><p>Itâs worth mentioning that, at the time of writing, this information seems to be under development. This hypothesis is supported by the fact that there is no data exchange between the victim device and the TA infrastructure. Moreover, log information and local databases did not show evidence that these information are stored somewhere on the device. Another technique is related to the usage of screen recording functionality that could avoid storing this information, however, adopting this strategy requires additional alerting mechanisms in order to properly see all codes typed. </p><p>Furthermore, the current version of BRATA has introduced two new permissions inside the AndroidManifest file, the RECEIVE_SMS and SEND_SMS. The combination of the phishing page with the possibility to receive and read the victimâs sms could be used to perform a complete Account Takeover (ATO) attack.</p><figure style="max-width:1966pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/62a9a8f9e671c86caa3cfc31_4.png" loading="lazy" alt=""/></div><figcaption>Figure 4 â Function used to create the phishing page</figcaption></figure></div></section><section id="4" class="bigmarginbottom"><h3 class="titlesectionclab">External Payload - Event logging</h3><div class="blog w-richtext"><p>As we already mentioned above, after being correctly installed on the victimâs phone, this BRATA version is going to download a .zip file from its C2. This file contains a jar file named <em>unrar.jar</em>.</p><figure style="max-width:1960pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/62a9a9515513ec5256853a78_5.png" loading="lazy" alt=""/></div><figcaption>Figure 5 â BRATA external payload</figcaption></figure><p>From the information retrieved, this <em>plugin</em> seems to be in charge of monitoring events that are generated from applications. More specifically, each time there is a change in a text view, it stores within a local database a pair of <em>Event Text</em> and a <em>Date</em> when the event occurred.</p><figure style="max-width:1956pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/62a9b2511d9a7c807b7b4ba5_6.png" loading="lazy" alt=""/></div><figcaption>Figure 6 â BRATA keylogger function</figcaption></figure><p>At the time of writing, this feature seems to be under development too. However, our hypothesis is that TAs are trying to extend the functionality of the malware to get data from other applications, abusing the Accessibility Service.</p></div></section><section id="5" class="bigmarginbottom"><h3 class="titlesectionclab">SMS Stealer</h3><div class="blog w-richtext"><p>During our analysis of the last BRATA campaign, we found a suspicious app connected to the same BRATA C2 infrastructure.</p><figure style="max-width:1948pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img develop new types of malware or they are just making some simple experiments to create new types of attacks, like contacts harvesting or SMS sniffers in order to stay undetected.</p><figure style="max-width:932pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/62a9b33d03db8b3f98437847_9.png" BRATA and SMS stealer"/></div><figcaption>Figure 10 â Code similarities between BRATA and SMS stealer</figcaption></figure><p>The malicious app seems to target three different countries: Great Britain, Italy and Spain. During the installation phases, in fact, it requires you to choose the language of the app.</p><figure style="max-width:1538pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/62a9b39f5231334e413bf28b_11.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 11 â SMS stealer targeted countries"/></div><figcaption>Figure 11 â SMS stealer targeted countries</figcaption></figure><p>Once installed, the pattern of the attack is similar to other SMS stealers.
