By doing so, Threat Actors (TAs) can drastically reduce the possibility of being flagged &quot;as suspicious&quot;, since the device&#x27;s fingerprinting is already known to the bank.</p><p>In this report, we analyze the attack chain and the modus operandi used by Threat Actors, from the sending of the malicious SMS to the fraudulent transaction carried out through an app installed in the infected device. </p><p>Moreover, we highlight the main indicators to explain the attack chain used by these TAs:</p><ul role="list"><li>The malware campaign targets mainly one of the biggest Italian retail banks as well as other minor banks. The campaign targeted the customers of one of the biggest Italian retail banks.</p><figure style="max-width:1282pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/61a8caa8aeaf31a13652b171_f2.png" loading="lazy" alt=""/></div><figcaption>Figure 2 - Some BRATA samples and related occurrences</figcaption></figure><p>â</p><p>After the first wave, lasted from June to mid-September, the attack stopped for about a month. Â In mid-October, our TIR team discovered that new samples called â<strong>Sicurezza Avanzata</strong>â were again in action and were targeting mainly the customers of three Italian banks. This time the malware was almost undetectable by antivirus solutions (as shown in Figure 3).</p><figure style="max-width:1252pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/61a8cb2d36468604f3534a10_F3.png" loading="lazy" alt=""/></div><figcaption>Figure 3 - Difference between two BRATA samples detected by antivirus solutions</figcaption></figure></div></section><section id="3" class="bigmarginbottom"><h3 class="titlesectionclab">How the BRATA malware works</h3><div class="blog w-richtext"><p>In June 2021, for the first time we detected on Cleafyâs dashboards a new variant of BRATA malware. After a couple of weeks, a customer reported to us some incidents related to the same campaign.</p><figure style="max-width:1490pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/61a8cb70ac69d7dae5d4fdcc_F4.png" loading="lazy" alt=""/></div><figcaption>Figure 4 - Example of BRATA malware intercepted and blacklisted in Cleafy console</figcaption></figure><p>Thanks to an in-depth technical analysis of the Indicators of Compromise intercepted, we were able to reconstruct the detailed chain of events and the methodologies used by these Threat Actors to conduct bank frauds.</p><p>The attack chain usually starts with a fake SMS containing a link to a website. The SMS seems to come from the bank (the so-called spoofing scam), and it tries to convince the victim to download an anti-spam app, with the promise to be contacted soon by a bank operator.</p><figure style="max-width:1280pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/61a8cbb553b6512cee75795f_F5.png" loading="lazy" alt=""/></div><figcaption>Figure 5 - Example of one of the SMS received from the victim</figcaption></figure><p>In some cases, the link redirects the victim to a phishing page that looks like the bankâs, and it is used to steal credentials and other relevant information (e.g. fiscal code and security questions).</p><figure style="max-width:1158pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/61a8cbf2783b58694de04946_F6.png" loading="lazy" alt=""/></div><figcaption>Figure 6 - Phishing kit used by TAs to deliver BRATA and for credential harvesting</figcaption></figure><figure style="max-width:1166pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/61a8cc077a211b9c45e2ce5e_F7.png" loading="lazy" alt=""/></div><figcaption>Figure 7 - Example of information stolen by BRATA group</figcaption></figure><p>After the victim visits the website (only visible via mobile[1]) and downloads the malicious app, a fraud operator calls the victim and uses social engineering techniques to persuade the user to install the malicious app.</p><figure class="w-richtext-align-center w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/61a8cc511c7b7c620e9aa2cf_F8.png" loading="lazy" alt=""/></div><figcaption>Figure 8 - Example of website used to spread BRATA malware in Italy</figcaption></figure><p>During the installation phases of the malware (Figure 9), multiple permissions are required to allow the attackers to perform fraudulent activities.</p><p>Once the malicious app is installed, the fraud operators can take control of the victim infected devices thanks to the abuse of the Accessibility services, the SMS permission, and the recording/casting module of the malware.</p><figure style="max-width:1350pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/61a8ccec373d62334fda97f5_F9.png" loading="lazy" alt=""/></div><figcaption>Figure 9 - Installation phase of BRATA malware on Android device</figcaption></figure><p>Through the malware installed on the victim device, Threat Actors can receive on their server the 2FA code sent by the bank and perform fraudulent transactions. Therefore, as we observed also in other scenarios, with the abuse of Accessibility Service and the screen recording, TAs can perform actions on the infected device with the help of social engineering used to persuade the victim.</p><figure style="max-width:1384pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/61a9ec4d0488efb33d8b0b59_Screenshot%202021-12-03%20at%2011.01.52.png" loading="lazy" alt=""/></div><figcaption>Figure 10 - Example of fraud transactions performed by TAs inside the infected device</figcaption></figure><p>As shown in Figure 11, we also intercepted multiple attempts of pin/otp validations stolen by TAs through the malicious app (or phishing website). This specific pattern was observed also in other past campaigns of mobile and workstation malware.</p><figure style="max-width:1018pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/61a8ce1ff1dcb45fd8c57f6e_F11.png" loading="lazy" alt=""/></div><figcaption>Figure 11 - Attempt to use stolen credentials intercepted in Cleafy Console</figcaption></figure><p>The mule accounts used by the BRATA malware campaign mainly come from Italy, as well as from Lithuania and the Netherlands, as shown in Figure 12. From this information, we assume that the TAs behind these campaigns could come from European countries unlike the previous BRATA malware campaign observed in Brazil in 2019.</p><figure style="max-width:828pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/61a8ce4140190558cbef9c91_F12.png" loading="lazy" alt=""/></div><figcaption>Figure 12 - Distribution of mule used by BRATA campaign</figcaption></figure><p>[1] TAs used a legitimate open source project (https://github.com/serbanghita/Mobile-Detect) to detect if the website is opened with a mobile phone or a PC.</p></div></section><section id="4" class="bigmarginbottom"><h3 class="titlesectionclab">BRATA main functionalities and capabilities</h3><div class="blog w-richtext"><p>By analyzing the code of the malicious apps, it was possible to trace back the threat to the BRATA malware, a Brazilian malware discovered in 2019. However, these new samples present multiple differences compared to the previous one. </p><p>Several Portuguese/Brazilian logs embedded in the malicious app are shown to the victim in Italian. Our assumption is that, perhaps, the group responsible for maintaining the BRATA codebase, probably located in the LATAM area, is reselling this malware to other local groups. As a result, this threat is gradually expanding in several European countries.</p><figure style="max-width:1250pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/61a8ce6a1eec4866ad4011fe_F13.png" loading="lazy" alt=""/></div><figcaption>Figure 13 - Some BRATA logs (on the left) and a screen with Italian text (on the right)</figcaption></figure><p>Like other Android bankers previously appeared online (e.g., Teabot[<a href="https://www.cleafy.com/cleafy-labs/teabot">2</a>], Alien, Oscorp[<a href="https://www.cleafy.com/cleafy-labs/ubel-oscorp-evolution">3</a>], etc.), this version of BRATA has RAT capabilities. The main difference resides in the implementation used to develop the malware: TAs used the b4a framework[<a href="https://www.b4x.com/b4a.html">4</a>], already used by another Brazilian banker in 2019, called BasBanker. One of the reasons behind this choice is the possibility to import modules already designed by other developers. This characteristic may allow the TAs to speed up the implementation of new features or the malware itself.</p><figure style="max-width:1538pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/61a8cebfcfeb8b139226276d_F14.png" loading="lazy" alt=""/></div><figcaption>Figure 14 - List of commands used by BRATA malware</figcaption></figure><p>The main functionalities of this new version of BRATA are not very different from other âfamousâ banking trojan:</p><ul role="list"><li>Intercept SMS messages and forward them to a C2 server. This feature is used to get 2FA sent by the bank via SMS during the login phase or to confirm money transactions.</li><li>Screen recording and casting capabilities that allow the malware to capture any sensitive information displayed on the screen. This includes audio, passwords, payment information, photo, and messages (as shown in Figure 15). Through the Accessibility Service, the malware clicks the âstart nowâ button (of the popup) automatically, so the victim is not able to deny the recording/casting of the owned device.</li><li>Remove itself from the compromised device to reduce detection.</li><li>Uninstall specific applications (e.g., antivirus).</li><li>Hide its own icon app to be less traceable by not advanced users.</li><li>Disable Google Play Protect to avoid being flagged by Google as suspicious app.</li><li>Modify the device settings to get more privileges.</li><li>Unlock the device if it is locked with a secret pin or pattern.</li><li>Show phishing page.</li><li>Abuse the accessibility service to read everything that is shown on the screen of the infected device or to simulate click on the screen. This information is then sent to the C2 server of the attackers.</li></ul><p>[2] <a href="https://www.cleafy.com/cleafy-labs/teabot">https://www.cleafy.com/cleafy-labs/teabot</a><br/>[3]<a href="https://www.cleafy.com/cleafy-labs/ubel-oscorp-evolution"> https://www.cleafy.com/cleafy-labs/ubel-oscorp-evolution</a><br/>[4] <a href="https://www.b4x.com/b4a.html">https://www.b4x.com/b4a.html</a></p></div></section><section id="5" class="bigmarginbottom"><h3 class="titlesectionclab">Conclusion</h3><div class="blog w-richtext"><p>The Android Banking Trojan <strong>BRATA</strong> is already classified and blacklisted in our Threat Intelligence data with the following tags: </p><ul role="list"><li><strong>ASK_BANKER_ANDROID_BRATA_V1</strong></li><li><strong>ASK_BANKER_ANDROID_BRATA_V2</strong></li></ul><p>â</p><h5>Appendix 1:Â IOCs</h5><p>â</p><p>First campaign (June-mid September)</p><div class="w-embed"><style type="text/css">
