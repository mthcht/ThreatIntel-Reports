MaaS stands for Malware-as-a-Service, and it is a model used in the cybercrime world to offer their malware for rent or sale to other TAs who lack the technical expertise to develop their malware.</p><p><strong>This model is prevalent in Android banking trojans</strong>, where malware authors use MaaS platforms to distribute their malware to a broader audience. The MaaS model allows criminals to monetize their malware more efficiently by providing a ready-made infrastructure to their customers, who can then use the malware to attack their targets. </p><p>It is common for MaaS providers to impose restrictions on the geographies where their customers can conduct attacks using rented or purchased malware. The Nexus authors, for example, have a &quot;code of conduct&quot; rule<strong> prohibiting using their malware in Russia and CIS countries</strong>.</p><figure style="max-width:1290pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/6419bae8c115c067e783576b_f2.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 2 - Nexus code of conduct for customers "/></div><figcaption>Figure 2 - Nexus code of conduct for customers</figcaption></figure><h3>Previous activities and linking with SOVA</h3><p>Despite the official launch of the Nexus MaaS program on 27th January 2023, our internal telemetries identified previous related activities behind this botnet, as shown in the following Figure:</p><figure style="max-width:1348pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/6419bb33648f9951e151fe86_f3.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 3 - Nexus activities (Cleafy telemetries) "/></div><figcaption>Figure 3 - Nexus activities (Cleafy telemetries)</figcaption></figure><p>On August 2022, during the analysis of those samples, <strong>technical indicators suggested some code similarity between Nexus samples and SOVA</strong>, an Android banking trojan emerged in mid-2021. At that time, it was considered a new variant of SOVA, as described in our <a href="https://www.cleafy.com/cleafy-labs/sova-malware-is-back-and-is-evolving-rapidly">previous blog article</a>.</p><p>Despite the new MaaS program launched under the name Nexus, the authors may have reused some parts of SOVA internals, to write new features (and rewrite some of the existing ones). </p><p>Recently, the SOVA author, who operates under the alias â<strong>sovenok</strong>â, started sharing some insights on Nexus and its relationship with SOVA, calling out an affiliate who previously rented SOVA for stealing the entire source code of the project.</p><figure style="max-width:1362pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/6419bb7e5ec43becb3a5e88e_f4.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 4 - sovenok accusing other TAs of stealing SOVA source code"/></div><figcaption>Figure 4 - sovenok accusing other TAs of stealing SOVA source code</figcaption></figure><p><strong>This event could explain why parts of the SOVA source code have been passing through multiple banking trojans.</strong> In fact, <strong>sovenok</strong> identified another Android botnet that operates under the name of <strong>POISON</strong>, which he considers to be highly linked to Nexus:</p><figure style="max-width:1376pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/6419bbd4ecd9b4660923f181_f5.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 5 - âsovenokâ linking Nexus and POISON banking trojans"/></div><figcaption>Figure 5 - âsovenokâ linking Nexus andPOISON banking trojans</figcaption></figure><p>Focusing on technical indicators, the following Figure shows the overlap found between the commands on SOVA and Nexus. As a result, it appears that<strong> most of the SOVA commands</strong> (marked in green)<strong> were also reused on Nexus</strong>:</p><figure style="max-width:1346pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/6419bc580f1c1473d1e3d173_f6.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 6 - Commands overlap between Nexus and SOVA."/></div><figcaption>Figure 6 - Commands overlap between Nexus and SOVA</figcaption></figure><p>Another similarity between Nexus and SOVA is how geographic location is checked. Analyzing the bootstrap of the infection, it was possible to discover that Nexus implements a function called <em>preloadCheck()</em> to identify if the victim is actually in a country âallowedâ to be attacked. If the check succeeds, it starts the infection chain. Otherwise, the application simply terminates the activity.</p><figure style="max-width:1350pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/6419bce3f7c3fb40b2f75e4a_f7.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 7 - Checking the geographic location of the device"/></div><figcaption>Figure 7 - Checking the geographic location of the device</figcaption></figure><p>Currently, countries that are ignored are: <strong>AZ</strong> (Azerbaijan), <strong>AM</strong> (Armenia), <strong>BY</strong> (Belarus), <strong>KZ </strong>(Kazakhstan), <strong>KG</strong> (Kyrgyzstan), <strong>MD</strong> (Moldova), <strong>RU</strong> (Russian Federation), <strong>TJ</strong> (Tajikistan), <strong>UZ</strong> (Uzbekistan), UA (Ukraine), <strong>ID</strong> (Indonesia).</p><figure style="max-width:1356pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/6419bd1bc204ab94ae3ea6bb_f8.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 8 - Excluded countries"/></div><figcaption>Figure 8 - Excluded countries</figcaption></figure><p>Moreover, another routine is deployed to discern if the victim is eligible to become part of the botnet. In this case, the attackers try to verify if the official Sberbank (Russia&#x27;s biggest bank) application is installed.</p><figure style="max-width:1332pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/6419bd3f648f99771554a063_f9.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 9 - Check the presence of the Sberbank mobile application"/></div><figcaption>Figure 9 - Check the presence of the Sberbank mobile application</figcaption></figure><p>Lastly, there are many APIs similarities used by both SOVA and Nexus to communicate with the C2 server, as confirmed by <strong>sovenok</strong>.</p><figure style="max-width:1354pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/6419bd6e2cb46e1e078626ad_f10.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 10 - sovenok accuses other TAs of stealing SOVA APIs"/></div><figcaption>Figure 10 - sovenok accuses other TAs of stealing SOVA APIs</figcaption></figure></div></section><section id="3" class="bigmarginbottom"><h3 class="titlesectionclab">Technical Analysis: Main features and Commands</h3><div class="blog w-richtext"><p>Nexus contains all the main features to perform <strong>Account Takeover attacks</strong> (ATO) against banking apps from all over the world and cryptocurrency services. In particular, it can:</p><ul role="list"><li>Perform O<strong>verlay attacks and keylogging </strong>activities to steal users&#x27; credentials.</li></ul><figure style="max-width:1230pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/6419bddd3c69c55b1bb37f7a_f11.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 11 - Nexus overlay attack "/></div><figcaption>Figure 11 - Nexus overlay attack</figcaption></figure><ul role="list"><li><strong>Steal SMS messages</strong> to obtain the two-factor authentication codes.</li><li>Through the abuse of the Accessibility Services, Nexus can steal some information from <strong>crypto wallets </strong>(such as seeds and balance), <strong>the 2FA codes of Google Authenticator app</strong>, and the cookies from specific websites.</li></ul><p>Comparing the list of commands of two Nexus samples (one from August 2022 and one from March 2023), <strong>it is possible to note the addition of some new commands</strong>, such as the ability to remove received SMS and the feature to activate or stop the 2FA stealer module.</p><p>The following evidence, in addition to some claims by the Nexus author concerning the possibility of adding a VNC module, underlines the desire to further improve the malware with new features.</p><figure style="max-width:1148pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/6419bea99939dd57b6a4b1b1_f12.png" loading="lazy" alt="figure 12 - New Nexus commands in recent samples"/></div><figcaption>Figure 12 - New Nexus commands in recent samples</figcaption></figure></div></section><section id="4" class="bigmarginbottom"><h3 class="titlesectionclab">Update Capacity</h3><div class="blog w-richtext"><p><strong>Nexus is also equipped with a mechanism for autonomous updating</strong>. A dedicated function asynchronously checks against its C2 server for updates when the malware is running. More specifically, it performs a check towards a dedicated endpoint asking for the last version; the query look like the following line:</p><p><em>http(s)://C2_domain/lastversion?access=XXXXX&amp;key=XXXXXXXX</em></p><p>If the value sent back from the C2 does not correspond to the one installed on the device, the malware starts the update process. Â Otherwise, it ignores the value and continues with all its routine activities. The update begins concatenating the URL related to the C2 and a key that will be used as an authorization token.</p><figure style="max-width:1328pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/6419bf02281b06440da28f1e_f13.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 13 - Update APK routine"/></div><figcaption>Figure 13 - Update APK routine</figcaption></figure><p>At the time of writing, the version available is related to the build <em>7.20</em>.</p><figure style="max-width:1344pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/6419bf2960f3006a82301ba5_f14.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 14 - Latest build version available"/></div><figcaption>Figure 14 - Latest build version available</figcaption></figure><p>Analysts could use the âkeyâ value to keep track of different custom versions of Nexus, it was possible to identify a slightly modified version of the malware for each key. This suggests that more actors are renting customized samples that aim to hit specific targets and countries.</p><figure style="max-width:1332pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/6419bf53c8f17f49fb5146e6_f15.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 15 - Latest build version available "/></div><figcaption>Figure 15 - Latest build version available</figcaption></figure></div></section><section id="5" class="bigmarginbottom"><h3 class="titlesectionclab">Ransomware module?</h3><div class="blog w-richtext"><p>According to the information retrieved from various samples, <strong>Nexus is equipped with encryption capabilities</strong>. However, this module seems to be under development due to the presence of debugging strings and the lack of usage references (especially within the C2 command list).</p><p>However, we canât exclude that this function is a âtypoâ associated with the cut-and-paste activities that seem to involve many parts of the code. </p><figure style="max-width:1356pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/6419bfab4c516d2ba8b6903d_f16.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 16 - AES encryption routine (not in use)"/></div><figcaption>Figure 16 - AES encryption routine (not in use)</figcaption></figure><p>It is reasonable to ask why this encryption paradigm has been implemented. An explanation could be found in an attempt of making it harder for the user to realize what happened. A similar approach, even more destructive, has been used by <a href="https://www.cleafy.com/cleafy-labs/brata-is-evolving-into-an-advanced-persistent-threat">Brata</a>, where TAs adopted the strategy of factory resetting the device right after an unauthorized wire transfer attempt to reduce the evidence. Â  Â </p><p>According to our experience, itâs also hard to think about a ransomware modus operandi on mobile devices since most information stored is synced with cloud services and easily recoverable. In fact, as confirmed by <strong>sanovek</strong> it started to design a ransomware capability in SOVA, but it was pointless for a banking trojan on mobile devices.</p><figure style="max-width:1364pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/6419bfec37ccc34ee43f44b3_f17.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 17 - SOVA author explains his initial goal of a ransomware module"/></div><figcaption>Figure 17 - SOVA author explains his initial goal of a ransomware module</figcaption></figure></div></section><section id="6" class="bigmarginbottom"><h3 class="titlesectionclab">Noisy Logs</h3><div class="blog w-richtext"><p>Another interesting characteristic of Nexus that strengthens the hypothesis of its development stage is the number of logging messages spread throughout the code. Those messages are intended to track all actions performed, and some of them are paired with a debugging string that contains the message âplz report this accidentâ.</p><figure style="max-width:1344pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/6419c03fae811959800bfaa5_f18.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 18 - Logging strings   "/></div><figcaption>Figure 18 - Logging strings</figcaption></figure><p>Logging messages are not limited to local devices. In fact, it was possible to discover that most of the messages are also intended to be sent over the C2 communication channel. The message should follow this syntax: Â <em>[ botId : Log Message]</em>. However, at the time of writing, due to the lack of references to the variable <strong>botID</strong> related to the logging class, this capability still seems to be in the development phase.</p></div></section><section id="7" class="bigmarginbottom"><h3 class="titlesectionclab">C2 web panel</h3><div class="blog w-richtext"><p>Once Nexus is installed on a victim&#x27;s device, it connects to its C2 server. This server is used by TAs to remotely control the malware, issue commands, and receive stolen data.</p><p>Nexus provides a C2 web panel, which is an essential tool for cyber criminals who are using malware or a botnet to carry out attacks. It provides a centralized interface for managing the malware and the data it collects, making it easier for attackers to carry out their malicious activities.</p><p>Typically Nexus C2 web panels expose the login page on the Internet through port 80 or 5000 as follows:</p><figure style="max-width:1322pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/6419c09e5ec43b0358a885b6_f19.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 19 - Management console: Home page"/></div><figcaption>Figure 19 - Management console: Home page</figcaption></figure><p>The string âJojo no Kimyou na Bouken: Eyes of Heavenâ references an action video game based on the âJo Jo&#x27;s Bizarre Adventureâ manga series by Hirohiko Araki, a Japanese manga artist.</p><p>Once logged in, the panel offers the following features:</p><ul role="list"><li><strong>Dashboard</strong>: displays the status of the botnet, including the number of infected devices, data collected, and any recent activity.</li><li><strong>Bots</strong>: a detailed list of the infected devices, locations, and other metrics.</li><li><strong>Data</strong> <strong>Collection</strong>: tools for collecting and analyzing data from infected devices. This includes login credentials, cookies, credit card details, and other sensitive information.</li><li><strong>Injects</strong>: a comprehensive list of 450 banking application login pages for grabbing valid credentials.</li><li><strong>Builder</strong>: interface for creating customized versions of Nexus, allowing TAs to customize various aspects, such as the command-and-control server (C&amp;C) address, the icon and name of the app, and more.</li></ul><figure style="max-width:1342pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/6419c0eb783c0b3516309b5a_f20.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 20 - Dashboard with detailed botnet information"/></div><figcaption>Figure 20 - Dashboard with detailed botnet information</figcaption></figure><figure style="max-width:1330pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/6419c1071ff7db437900cea8_f21.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 21 - Details of exfiltrated data (known as âlogsâ)"/></div><figcaption>Figure 21 - Details of exfiltrated data (known as âlogsâ)</figcaption></figure><p>As the alleged author claimed on multiple hacking forums, the complete list of injections has 450 different targets, which are searchable through the panel. They also offer the possibility to create custom injections.</p><figure style="max-width:1334pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/6419c135acb29c08a4cb7a5e_f22.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 22 - Injection list "/></div><figcaption>Figure 22 - Injection list</figcaption></figure><p>Pivoting C2 fingerprints through Internet search engines, such as Shodan, could provide excellent information, and in this case, it confirms that the growing trend began in the very first of 2023 when Nexus was officially promoted on multiple hacking forums:</p><figure style="max-width:1342pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/6419c1551ff7db49ac013104_f23.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 23 - C2 fingerprinting over time (source: Shodan Trends)"/></div><figcaption>Figure 23 - C2 fingerprinting over time (source: Shodan Trends) </figcaption></figure></div></section><section id="8" class="bigmarginbottom"><h3 class="titlesectionclab">Conclusion</h3><div class="blog w-richtext"><p>Nexus is an emerging malware that allegedly has taken more than a few âideasâ from SOVA (a threat that hit the international landscape in the last year). According to the similarities observed in the code, and the insights retrieved from underground forums, it is possible to confirm that Nexus represents a new malware, guided by an entirely new group, which was capable of retrieving parts of SOVA source code after they were stolen by an Android botnet operator called <strong>POISON</strong>. </p><p>By analyzing the latest retrieved samples, we can say that Nexus is still in an early stage of development, still including snippets of code that seem to belong to its ancestor.</p><p>As always, the main question here is: Does it represent a threat to Android users?</p><p>At the time of writing,<strong> the absence of a VNC module limits its action range and its capabilities</strong>; however, according to the infection rate retrieved from multiple C2 panels, <strong>Nexus is a real threat that is capable of infecting hundreds of devices around the world</strong>.
