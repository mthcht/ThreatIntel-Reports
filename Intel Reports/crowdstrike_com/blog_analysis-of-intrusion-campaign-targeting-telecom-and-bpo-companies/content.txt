       <span><p><i><b>CrowdStrike Services reviews a recent, extremely persistent intrusion campaign targeting telecommunications and business process outsourcing (BPO) companies and outlines how organizations can defend and secure their environments.</b></i></p>
<ul><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">CrowdStrike Services has performed multiple investigations into an intrusion campaign targeting telecommunications and business process outsourcing (BPO) companies.</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;

"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Initial access is varied: Social engineering using phone calls and text messages to impersonate IT personnel, and either directing victims to a credential harvesting site or directing victims to run commercial remote monitoring and management (RMM) tools.</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">These campaigns are extremely persistent and brazen. Once the adversary is contained or operations are disrupted, they immediately move to target other organizations within the telecom and BPO sectors.</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Organizations should focus on </span><a href="/en-us/cybersecurity-101/identity-protection/"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">identity-based security</span></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> through authentication restrictions and secure multifactor authentication (MFA) configurations to most effectively disrupt this campaign. </span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">CrowdStrike Intelligence has attributed this campaign with low confidence to the SCATTERED SPIDER eCrime adversary.</span></li></ul>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Since June 2022, CrowdStrike Services, CrowdStrike Falcon OverWatch™ and CrowdStrike Intelligence teams have observed an increase in the targeting of Telco and BPO industries. These investigations appear to be tied to a financially-motivated campaign with links to an adversary CrowdStrike tracks as SCATTERED SPIDER. This blog will discuss the ongoing campaign in greater detail, highlighting the various techniques used by the adversary to gain and maintain access, and evade detection and response, as well as what organizations should be aware of to best defend and respond to this campaign.</span></p>
<h2><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Background</span></h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">In this attack campaign, the adversary demonstrates persistence in trying to gain access to victim environments and performs constant, and typically daily, activity within the target environment once access is gained. It is imperative for organizations to swiftly implement containment and mitigation actions if this adversary is in the environment. In multiple investigations, CrowdStrike observed the adversary become even more active, setting up additional persistence mechanisms, i.e. VPN access and/or multiple RMM tools, if mitigation measures are slowly implemented. And in multiple instances, the adversary reverted some of the mitigation measures by re-enabling accounts previously disabled by the victim organization.</span></p>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Also of note, as CrowdStrike assisted one organization through the investigation and to a successful containment phase, the adversary moved onto other organizations in the same vertical. CrowdStrike was subsequently engaged to support the new victim organizations battling against the same campaign, as evidenced by overlapping indicators of compromise (IOCs) and techniques.</span></p>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">In all observed intrusions, the adversary attempted to leverage access to mobile carrier networks from a Telco or BPO environment, and in two investigations, SIM swapping was performed by the adversary.</span></p>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Below is a summary timeline outlining a sampling of intrusions CrowdStrike Services responded to along with corresponding findings.</span></p>

<h2><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Initial Access and Privilege Escalation</span></h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">In most of the investigations CrowdStrike performed, initial access was achieved through social engineering, where the adversary leveraged phone calls, SMS and/or Telegram to impersonate IT staff. The adversary instructed victim users to either navigate to a credential-harvesting website containing the company logo and enter their credentials, or download a RMM tool that would allow the adversary to remotely connect and control their system. If MFA was enabled, the adversary would either engage the victim directly by convincing them to share their one-time password (OTP), or indirectly by leveraging MFA push-notification fatigue. This is when an adversary continuously prompts MFA to the victim user until they accept the MFA push challenge.</span></p>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">In another investigation, the adversary leveraged compromised credentials from a victim user and authenticated to the organization’s Azure tenant. Using this access, the adversary instantiated Azure VMs to conduct credential theft activity and lateral movement to on-premises systems.</span></p>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">In a third tactic observed in another investigation, the adversary leveraged <a class="c-link c-link--underline" href="https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2021-35464" target="_blank" data-stringify-link="https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2021-35464" data-sk="tooltip_parent" data-remove-tab-index="true" rel="noopener noreferrer">CVE-2021-35464</a> to exploit a ForgeRock OpenAM application server, which front-ends web applications and remote access solutions in many organizations (a patch for this CVE was released in October 2021). In this example, the adversary showcased their knowledge of AWS. Leveraging AWS Instance Roles to assume or elevate privileges from the Apache Tomcat user, the adversary would request and assume permissions of an instance role using a compromised AWS token. As shown in Figure 2, the adversary used elevated privileges to execute the open-source </span><a href="https://github.com/carlospolop/PEASS-ng/tree/master/linPEAS"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">LINpeas privilege escalation utility</span></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">.</span></p>

<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;

The devices would be assigned to compromised users for whom they had captured credentials. This technique, performed by taking advantage of user self-enrollment policies with the MFA provider, allowed the adversary to maintain a deeper and less obvious level of persistence instead of simply installing a remote access trojan to maintain access.</span></p>

During active hands-on-keyboard activity at most the intrusions CrowdStrike Services responded to, the adversary would often deploy multiple RMM tools and would quickly deploy another one if the organization blocked the previously used utilities.</span></p>

<h2><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Reconnaissance and Lateral Movement</span></h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">The adversary operates across Windows, Linux, Google Workspace, AzureAD, M365 and AWS environments. They have also accessed SharePoint and OneDrive environments for reconnaissance information, specifically searching for VPN information, MFA enrollment information, “how to” guides, help desk instructions and new hire guides.</span></p>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">In one investigation, the adversary accessed Azure Active Directory and performed bulk downloads of group members and users. By doing so, they were able to identify privileged users, along with the email addresses and AD attributes of all users within the victim tenant. Additional techniques employed by the adversary during this investigation included </span><a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1003/006/"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">domain replication</span></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">, lateral movement </span><a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1047"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">via Windows Management Instrumentation (WMI)</span></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> using </span><a href="https://attack.mitre.org/software/S0357/"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Impacket</span></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">, </span><a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1572/"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">SSH tunneling</span></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> and various </span><a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1219/"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">remote access tools</span></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">In some investigations, the adversary downloaded tools using victim organization systems from sites such as file&lt;.&gt;io, GitHub, and paste&lt;.&gt;ee. The site transfer&lt;.&gt;sh was used by the adversary to perform data exfiltration of reconnaissance information.</span></p>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">In another investigation, an </span><a href="https://github.com/NetSPI/aws_consoler"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">open source tool called </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">aws_consoler</span></code></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> was used by the adversary to create temporary federated credentials for non-existent users issued by identity and access management (IAM) users. Federated Credentials help obfuscate which AWS credential is compromised and enables the adversary to pivot from the AWS CLI to console sessions without the need for MFA.</span></p>
<h2><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Mitigations and Containment Measures</span></h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">In all investigations performed by CrowdStrike incident responders, the faster the organization implemented swift and bold security measures, the faster the adversary activity ceased. These containment and mitigation measures focused on secure identity and MFA controls and configurations, as highlighted below.</span></p>
<h3><b>CrowdStrike Falcon Identity Threat Protection</b></h3>
<ul><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">CrowdStrike Services leveraged </span><a href="/en-us/products/identity-protection/falcon-identity-threat-protection/"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Falcon Identity Threat Protection</span></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> (ITP) in all related investigations as one of the primary detection and mitigation vehicles.</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Enable Falcon ITP rules to enforce restrictions on where privileged accounts can authenticate to and from (e.g., specific system to system only, blocking all RDP access, etc.) </span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Enforce MFA challenges for privileged account authentication across all access methods (e.g., PowerShell, RDP, etc.) </span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Monitor for ITP alerts regarding anomalous use of accounts, stale account usage, custom detection rules, DCsync and other domain replication activity.</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Identify compromised and at-risk accounts and credentials via custom rules and queries.</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Maintain good Active Directory hygiene monitoring and review any newly created accounts, modified groups or re-enabled accounts.</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Leverage the Protected Users Security Group in Active Directory to guard against NTLM used for privileged accounts.</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Real-time alerting for known compromised credential detection.</span></li></ul>

<h3><b>Multifactor Authentication</b></h3>
<ul><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Implement MFA everywhere possible, especially for accounts that have access to third-party environments.</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Disable MFA simple push notifications in place of number-matching MFA where possible, or use One Time Passcodes with manual entry. </span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Avoid unsupervised MFA self-enrollment or reset, and disallow any self-enrollment from external IP space.</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Allow only one trusted MFA device per user.</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Implement a global password reset and KRBTGT account reset twice per domain if compromise is suspected.</span></li></ul>
<h3><b>AWS Token Pivoting</b></h3>
<ul><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Ensure IMDSv2 is enabled on all EC2 instances to the extent possible (many products unfortunately still do not support v2).</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Enable GuardDuty in all active regions (GuardDuty has detections for abuse of EC2 instance credentials outside of an EC2 instance).</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Deprecate static IAM user access keys in favor of IAM roles where possible.</span></li></ul>
<h3><b>Azure</b></h3>
<ul><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Enforce Azure Conditional Access Policies (CAP):</span><ul><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Block legacy authentication</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Restrict logon by geographic region</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Enforce multifactor authentication for all users</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Enforce compliant devices</span></li></ul>
</li></ul>
<h3><b>Network Access Controls</b></h3>
<ul><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">VPN host checking or other Network Access Control technology can limit the adversary’s ability to log in remotely from non-organizational hosts.</span></li></ul>
<h3><b>General Vigilance</b></h3>
<ul><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Ensure user accounts, especially those with access to sensitive company information and/or access to mobile carrier networks, are assigned Principle of Least Privilege policies within all identity management applications e.g., Active Directory, Group Policy Objects, Identity Access Management, etc. </span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Due diligence should be performed by internal security teams to ensure company insiders remain at a minimal risk of purposely supporting the adversary. </span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Be cognizant of endpoint security tool bypass attempts. In many of the investigations CrowdStrike performed, the adversary attempted to bypass AV or EDR security tools on the endpoints.</span></li></ul>
