       <span><p><span style="	font-weight: 400;

">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;

">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> to affiliates through a</span><a href="/en-us/cybersecurity-101/ransomware/ransomware-as-a-service-raas/"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> ransomware-as-a-service (RaaS)</span></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> program, allowing other actors to use the ransomware while paying CARBON SPIDER a portion of the received ransom.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">The </span><a href="/en-us/blog/carbon-spider-embraces-big-game-hunting-part-1/"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">first part</span></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> of this two-part blog series explored CARBON SPIDER’s initial BGH campaigns in depth. This blog discusses the </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> ransomware incident at U.S. oil pipeline system Colonial Pipeline in May 2021 and how CARBON SPIDER responded to fallout from this event. Despite the termination of the </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> program, the adversary continued malware distribution campaigns and subsequently introduced the </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">BlackMatter</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> RaaS. Due to numerous technical overlaps with </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">, </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">BlackMatter</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> is attributed to CARBON SPIDER.</span></p>
<h2><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Colonial Pipeline Incident</span></h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">On May 8, 2021, Colonial Pipeline disclosed that it had been the victim of a ransomware incident the day before;<sup>1</sup> however, it would be several days until the FBI indicated that Colonial Pipeline fell victim to </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> ransomware.<sup>2</sup> On May 9, 2021, a ransom payment of approximately $4.4 million USD (75 BTC) was made to a probable </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> affiliate. The U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ) later announced the seizure of the affiliate’s portion of this payment.<sup>3</sup></span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">On May 10, 2021, CARBON SPIDER posted a response to media attention to the Colonial Pipeline incident on the </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> dedicated leak site (DLS) stating they are “apolitical,” do not participate in “geopolitics,” and that their “goal is to make money, and not creating problems for society.” The post further mentioned a vetting process for all victims, providing further evidence that the Colonial Pipeline incident was conducted by an affiliate rather than the core CARBON SPIDER group. These statements were likely made in an attempt to correct certain public speculation that the attack was politically motivated.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">A statement on May 13, 2021 — purportedly from CARBON SPIDER — claimed the adversary group lost access to the </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> DLS, payment servers and content delivery network (CDN) servers. The statement also claimed CARBON SPIDER servers had been blocked “at the request of law enforcement agencies.” Since then, CrowdStrike Intelligence has not observed any new valid </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> samples, indicating this date marked the end of the </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> RaaS. Separately on May 13, 2021, several forum administrators banned posts relating to </span><a href="/en-us/cybersecurity-101/ransomware/"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">ransomware</span></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">, likely to avoid media attention.</span></p>
<h2><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Subsequent CARBON SPIDER Operations</span></h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Despite </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">’s termination, CARBON SPIDER did not cease their operations or entirely abandon prior tooling. On May 25, 2021, </span><a href="/en-us/blog/how-crowdstrike-stopped-an-sql-injection-campaign/"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">CrowdStrike Falcon® Complete and Falcon OverWatch detected a SQL injection incident that delivered a PowerShell (PS) stager tracked by CrowdStrike Intelligence as </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Demux</span></i></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">From May 31 through June 29, 2021, CARBON SPIDER used malicious Microsoft Excel and Word documents as well as </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Leo VBS</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> to distribute an updated version of </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">JSS Loader</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">. This version of </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">JSS Loader </span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">— written in C&#43;&#43;, compared to its .NET progenitor — introduced a new packer.</span></p>
<p> </p>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">On July 9 and 12, 2021, CARBON SPIDER used malicious Microsoft Word documents to distribute the </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Harpy</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> backdoor. These documents used Windows 11 Alpha-themed content identical to lure content used in a </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">JSS Loader</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> campaign (Figure 1).</span></p>
<p> </p>
<div class="wp-caption" id="attachment_48570"><img class="wp-image-48570 size-full" src="/content/dam/crowdstrike/www/en-us/wp/2021/11/Picture1.png" alt="" width="2275" height="1031"/>Figure 1. JSS Loader/Harpy document content</div>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">In this campaign, after </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Harpy</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> successfully contacted its command-and-control (C2) server, a JavaScript system enumeration module was delivered and executed by </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Harpy</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">. This enumeration module strongly resembles a PS system enumeration module written for the </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Domenus</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> suite, providing evidence that both tools share a common developer.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">In June 2021, a probable CARBON SPIDER actor deployed the open-source </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Hidden Tear</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> ransomware during a ransomware operation. This incident was attributed to CARBON SPIDER based on prior use of the </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Demux</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> stager and the </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Sekur</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> Remote Access Tool (RAT) to establish persistent access, in addition to </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Cobalt Strike</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">. This incident marks the first known CARBON SPIDER ransomware campaign following the Colonial Pipeline incident; CARBON SPIDER likely chose to use </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Hidden Tear</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> to avoid attracting additional attention.</span></p>
<h2><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">BlackMatter</span></h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">On 21 July 2021, a Russian-language forum member named </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">BlackMatter</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> sought to purchase access to a variety of corporate networks. The actor specifically expressed interest in the U.S., Canada, Australia and the UK, and expressed disinterest in targeting medical or government institutions. Subsequent CrowdStrike Intelligence analysis confirmed that </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">BlackMatter</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> is provided to affiliates via a RaaS program, similar to </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">.</span></p>
<p> </p>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Windows PE and Linux ELF versions of </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">BlackMatter </span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">were subsequently obtained and analyzed. Extensive coding similarities indicate </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">BlackMatter</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> is highly likely the successor to </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">. Windows version overlaps include:</span></p>
<p> </p>
<ul><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Building an import address table at runtime using dynamic function resolution</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Using aPLib to decompress the embedded ransomware configuration</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Overlaps in configuration formats and multiple configuration items</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Using the same file encryption system, including using Salsa20 to encrypt files through a randomly generated state matrix and protecting the state matrix with an embedded RSA-1024 key</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Using two HTTP C2 requests before and after file encryption containing system information and encryption statistics</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Using Windows Management Instrumentation (WMI) for shadow copy deletion</span></li></ul>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Linux version overlaps include:</span></p>
<ul><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">A Red Hat Linux build environment</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Being written in C&#43;&#43; and compiled using GCC with statically linked libcurl, Boost and CryptoPP libraries</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Using the CryptoPP </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">RandomPool</span></code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> random number generator to produce symmetric keys</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Using an embedded RSA-4096 public key to protect generated symmetric keys</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Using an embedded configuration that specifies an RSA-4096 public key, an allowlist of file extensions to encrypt, a thread count to use during encryption, a debug log file path and C2 domains</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">All</span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> BlackMatter</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> extension allowlist values exist within </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">’s configuration</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Performing a C2 request via cURL containing embedded system information</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Placing a hard-coded URL containing a unique URL string within the ransom note to the victim payment portal</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">The ability to stop ESXi virtual machines using </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">esxcli</span></code></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
">The ability to enumerate ESXi volumes for encryption using <code>esxcli</code></li></ul>
<p><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">BlackMatter </span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">ransom notes direct victims to communicate via a portal hosted on Tor. If victims do not pay ransom demands, stolen files are typically posted on a DLS that is also hosted on Tor. CrowdStrike Intelligence has identified </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">BlackMatter</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> victims spanning numerous sectors across North and South America, Asia and Europe.</span></p>
<h2><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Conclusion and Outlook</span></h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">CrowdStrike Intelligence assesses CARBON SPIDER is highly likely behind the development of </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">BlackMatter</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> and operating the </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">BlackMatter</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> RaaS. This assessment carries high confidence based on the extensive amount of technical overlaps between </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> and </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">BlackMatter</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">. CARBON SPIDER’s resilience and launch of </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">BlackMatter</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> shortly following the termination of </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> demonstrates how difficult it is to disrupt cybercrime adversaries and their operations. The potential profits from ransomware are evidently worth risking potential law enforcement actions. Without fundamental changes in the economics of cybercrime, CARBON SPIDER and other actors will likely continue to provide RaaS programs to affiliates.</span></p>
