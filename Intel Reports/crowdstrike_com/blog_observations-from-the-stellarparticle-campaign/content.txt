       <span><ul><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">StellarParticle is a campaign tracked by CrowdStrike as related to the SUNSPOT implant from the SolarWinds intrusion in December 2020 and associated with COZY BEAR (aka APT29, &#34;The Dukes&#34;).</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">The StellarParticle campaign has continued against multiple organizations, with COZY BEAR using novel tools and techniques to complete their objectives, as identified by CrowdStrike incident responders and the CrowdStrike Intelligence team.</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Browser cookie theft and Microsoft Service Principal manipulation are two of the novel techniques and tools leveraged in the StellarParticle campaign and are discussed in this blog.</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Two sophisticated malware families were placed on victim systems in mid-2019: a Linux variant of </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">GoldMax </span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">and a new implant dubbed </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">TrailBlazer.</span></i></li></ul>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Supply chain compromises are an increasing threat that impacts a range of sectors, with <a href="https://www.crowdstrike.com/cybersecurity-101/threat-actor/">threat actors</a> leveraging access to support several motivations including financial gain (such as with the Kaseya ransomware attack) and </span><a href="/en-us/cybersecurity-101/threat-intelligence/cyber-espionage/"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">espionage</span></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">. Throughout 2020, an operation attributed to the Foreign Intelligence Service of the Russian Federation (SVR) by the U.S. government was conducted to gain access to the update mechanism of the SolarWinds IT management software and use it to broaden their intelligence collection capabilities. This activity is tracked by CrowdStrike as the StellarParticle campaign and is associated with the </span><a href="https://adversary.crowdstrike.com/en-US/adversary/cozy-bear.html"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">COZY BEAR</span></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> adversary group.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">This blog discusses the novel tactics and techniques leveraged in StellarParticle investigations conducted by CrowdStrike. These techniques include:</span></p>
<ul><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Credential hopping for obscuring lateral movement</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Office 365 (O365) Service Principal and Application hijacking, impersonation and manipulation</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Stealing browser cookies for bypassing multifactor authentication</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Use of the </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">TrailBlazer</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> implant and the Linux variant of </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">GoldMax</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> malware</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Credential theft using Get-ADReplAccount</span></li></ul>
<h2><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Credential Hopping</span></h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">The majority of StellarParticle-related investigations conducted by CrowdStrike have started with the identification of adversary actions within a victim&#39;s O365 environment. This has been advantageous to CrowdStrike incident responders in that, through investigating victim O365 environments, they could gain an accurate accounting of time, account and source IP address of adversary victimization of the O365 tenant. In multiple engagements, this led CrowdStrike incident responders to identify that the malicious authentications into victim O365 tenants had originated from within the victim’s own network.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Armed with this information, CrowdStrike investigators were able to identify from which systems in these internal networks the threat actor was making authentications to O365. These authentications would typically occur from servers in the environment, leading to natural investigative questions: Why would a user authenticate into O365 from a domain controller or other infrastructure server? What credentials were used as part of the session from which the O365 authentication occurred?</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">This led our responders to identify the occurrence of “credential hopping,&#34; where the threat actor leveraged different credentials for each step while moving laterally through the victim&#39;s network. While this particular technique is not necessarily unique to the StellarParticle campaign, it indicates a more advanced threat actor and may go unnoticed by a victim.</span></p>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Below is an example of how a threat actor performs credential hopping:</span></p>
<ul><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Gain access to the victim&#39;s network by logging into a public-facing system via Secure Shell (SSH) using a local account &lt;user sftp&gt; acquired during previous credential theft activities.</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Use port forwarding capabilities built into SSH on the public-facing system to establish a Remote Desktop Protocol (RDP) session to an internal server (Server 1) using a domain service account.</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">From Server 1, establish </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">another</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> RDP session to a different internal server (Server 2) using a domain administrator&#39;s account.</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Log in to O365 as a user with privileged access to cloud resources.</span></li></ul>
<div class="wp-caption" id="attachment_54032"><img class="size-full wp-image-54032" src="/content/dam/crowdstrike/www/en-us/wp/2022/01/Picture1-10.png" alt="" width="6539" height="1037"/>Figure 1. Example of “credential hopping” technique</div>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">This technique could be hard to identify in environments where defenders have little visibility into identity usage. In the example shown in Figure 1, the threat actor leveraged a service interactively, which should generate detections for defenders to investigate. However, the threat actor could have easily used a second domain administrator account or any other combination of accounts that would not be easily detected. A solution such as </span><a href="/en-us/products/identity-protection/"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">CrowdStrike Falcon® Identity Threat Detection</span></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> would help identify these anomalous logons — and especially infrequent destinations for accounts. (Read how CrowdStrike incident responders leverage the module in investigations in this blog: </span><a href="/en-us/blog/how-crowdstrike-supercharges-incident-response-with-identity-threat-detection/"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Credentials, Authentications and Hygiene: Supercharging Incident Response with Falcon Identity Threat Detection</span></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">.)</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">But how had the threat actor succeeded in authenticating into victim O365 tenants, when <a href="https://www.crowdstrike.com/cybersecurity-101/multifactor-authentication-mfa/">multifactor authentication</a> (MFA) had been enabled for every O365 user account at each victim organization investigated by CrowdStrike?</span></p>
<h2><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Cookie Theft to Bypass MFA</span></h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Even though the victims required MFA to access cloud resources from all locations, including on premises, the threat actor managed to bypass MFA through the theft of Chrome browser cookies. The threat actor accomplished this by using administrative accounts to connect via SMB to targeted users, and then copy their Chrome profile directories as well as data protection API (DPAPI) data. In Windows, Chrome cookies and saved passwords are encrypted using DPAPI. The user-specific encryption keys for DPAPI are stored under </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">C:\Users\&lt;user&gt;\AppData\Roaming\Microsoft\Protect\</span></code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">. To leverage these encryption keys, the threat actor must first decrypt them, either by using the user account’s Windows password, or, in Active Directory environments, by using a DPAPI domain backup key that is stored on domain controllers.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Once the threat actor had a Chrome cookies file from a user that had already passed an MFA challenge recently (for example, a timeout was 24 hours), they decrypted the cookies file using the user’s DPAPI key. The cookies were then added to a new session using a “Cookie Editor” Chrome extension that the threat actor installed on victim systems and removed after using.</span></p>
<h2><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Shellbags, Falcon Telemetry and RDP Bitmap Cache</span></h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">From a forensic standpoint, the use of the Cookie Editor Chrome extension would have been challenging to identify, due to the threat actor’s penchant for strict operational security. This activity was identified via a </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">NewScriptWritten</span></code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> event within Falcon when a JavaScript file was written to disk by a threat actor-initiated Chrome process. This event captured the unique extension ID associated with the extension, thereby allowing CrowdStrike incident responders to validate via the Chrome Store that the JavaScript file was associated with the “Cookie Editor” plugin. This extension permitted bypassing MFA requirements, as the cookies, replayed through the Cookie Editor extension, allowed the threat actor to hijack the already MFA-approved session of a targeted user.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Shellbags were also instrumental in identifying the cookie theft activity. This artifact very clearly showed the threat actor accessing targeted users’ machines in sequence and browsing to the Chrome and DPAPI directories one after another. Parsing Shellbags for an administrative account leveraged by the threat actor resulted in entries similar to the below.</span></p>
<div class="wp-caption" id="attachment_54033"><img class="wp-image-54033" src="/content/dam/crowdstrike/www/en-us/wp/2022/01/Screen-Shot-2022-01-18-at-11.05.12-AM.png" alt="" width="600" height="97"/>Figure 2. Shellbag artifacts showing targeting of Chrome directories</div>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">CrowdStrike identified forensic evidence that showed the entire attack path: browsing to a target user’s Chrome and DPAPI directories via administrative share, installing the Cookie Editor extension, and using Chrome to impersonate the targeted user in the victim’s cloud tenants. The decryption of the cookies is believed to have taken place offline after exfiltrating the data via the clipboard in the threat actor’s RDP session.</span></p>
<div class="wp-caption" id="attachment_54034"><img class="size-full wp-image-54034" src="/content/dam/crowdstrike/www/en-us/wp/2022/01/Screen-Shot-2022-01-18-at-11.06.44-AM.png" alt="" width="1632" height="842"/>Figure 3. Representation of lateral movement to cookie theft to O365 authentication</div>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">CrowdStrike identified a similar TTP where the threat actor connected via RDP to a user’s workstation with the workstation owner’s account (e.g., connecting via RDP to user1-pc using the account user1). In cases where the user had only locked their screen and not signed out, the threat actor was able to take over the user’s Windows session, as the RDP session would connect to the existing session of the same user. By examining RDP Bitmap Cache files, CrowdStrike was able to demonstrate that the threat actor had opened Chrome and exported all of the user’s saved passwords as plaintext in a CSV file during these sessions.</span></p>
<div class="wp-caption" id="attachment_54035"><img class="wp-image-54035" src="/content/dam/crowdstrike/www/en-us/wp/2022/01/Picture1-11.png" alt="" width="450" height="294"/>Figure 4. RDP Bitmap Cache reconstruction showing exportation of Chrome passwords</div>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">In addition, the threat actor visited sensitive websites that the user had access to, which in one instance allowed them to browse and download a victim’s customer list. After this, the threat actor navigated to the user’s Chrome history page and deleted the specific history items related to threat actor activity, leaving the rest of the user’s Chrome history intact.</span></p>
<h2><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">O365 Delegated Administrator Abuse</span></h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">CrowdStrike also identified a connection between StellarParticle-related campaigns and the abuse of Microsoft Cloud Solution Partners’ O365 tenants. This threat actor abused access to accounts in the Cloud Solution Partner’s environment with legitimate delegated administrative privileges to then gain access to several customers’ O365 environments.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">By analyzing Azure AD sign-ins, CrowdStrike was able to use known indicators of compromise (IOCs) to identify several threat actor logins to customer environments. These cross-tenant sign-ins were identified by looking for values in the </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">resourceTenantId</span></code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> attribute that did </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">not</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> match the Cloud Solution Partner’s own Azure tenant ID.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">CrowdStrike also identified a limitation within Microsoft’s Delegated Administration capabilities for Microsoft Cloud Solution Partners. While a normal O365 administrator can be provided dozens of specific administrative roles to limit the privileges granted, this same degree of customization cannot be applied to Microsoft Cloud Solution Partners that use the delegated administrator functionality in O365.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">For Microsoft Cloud Solution Partners, there are only two substantial administrative options today when managing a customer’s environment, </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Admin agent</span></code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> or <code>Helpdesk agent</code>.<sup>2</sup></span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">The </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Helpdesk agent</span></code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> role provides very limited access that is equivalent to a password admin role, whereas the </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Admin agent</span></code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> role provides broad access more equivalent to global administrator. This limitation is scheduled to be resolved in 2022 via Microsoft’s scheduled feature, Granular Delegated Admin Privileges (GDAP).<sup>3</sup></span></p>
<h2><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">User Access Logging (UAL)</span></h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">The Windows User Access Logging (UAL) database is an extremely powerful artifact that has played an instrumental role in the investigation of StellarParticle-linked cases. In particular, UAL has helped our responders identify earlier malicious account usage that ultimately led to the identification of the aforementioned TrailBlazer implant and Linux version of the GoldMax variant.</span></p>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">The UAL database is available by default on Server editions of Windows starting with Server 2012. This database stores historical information on user access to various services (or in Windows parlance, Roles) on the server for up to three years (three years minus one day) by default. UAL contains information on the type of service accessed, the user that accessed the service and the source IP address from which the access occurred. One of the most useful roles recorded by UAL is the File Server role, which includes SMB access, though other role types can also be very helpful. An overview of UAL, what information it contains and how it can be leveraged in forensic investigations can be found </span><a href="/en-us/blog/user-access-logging-ual-overview/"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">here</span></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">In multiple StellarParticle-related cases, because the threat actor used the same set of accounts during their operations in the environment, CrowdStrike was able to identify previous malicious activity going back multiple years, based solely on UAL data. Even though it’s only available on Server 2012 and up, UAL can still be used to trace evidence of threat actor activity on legacy systems as long as the activity on the legacy system involves some (deliberate or unintentional) access to a 2012&#43; system. For example, in addition to tracking SMB activity, UAL databases on Domain Controllers track Active Directory access.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">This allowed CrowdStrike to demonstrate that a given user account was also authenticating to Active Directory from a given source IP address two years prior. Because the user account was known to have recently been abused by the threat actor, and the source IP of the system in question was not one that account would typically be active on, the investigation led to the source system and ultimately resulted in the timeline of malicious activity being pushed back by years, with additional compromised systems even being discovered still running unique malware from that time period.</span></p>
<h2><i>TrailBlazer <span style="	font-style: normal;
">and</span> GoldMax</i></h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Throughout StellarParticle-related investigations, CrowdStrike has identified two sophisticated malware families that were placed on victim systems in the mid-2019 timeframe: a Linux variant of </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">GoldMax</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> and a completely new family CrowdStrike refers to as </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">TrailBlazer</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">.</span></p>
<h3><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">TrailBlazer</span></i></h3>
<ul><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Attempted to blend in with a file name that matched the system name it resided on</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Configured for WMI persistence (generally uncommon in 2019)</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Used likely compromised infrastructure for C2</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Masquerades its command-and-control (C2) traffic as legitimate Google Notifications HTTP requests</span></li></ul>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"><i>TrailBlazer</i> is a sophisticated malware family that provides modular functionality and a very low prevalence. The malware shares high-level functionality with other malware families. In particular, the use of random identifier strings for C2 operations and result codes, and attempts to hide C2 communications in seemingly legitimate web traffic, were previously observed tactics, techniques and procedures (TTPs) in <i>GoldMax</i> and SUNBURST. <i>TrailBlazer</i> persists on a compromised host using WMI event subscriptions<sup>4</sup> — a technique also used by </span><a href="https://attack.mitre.org/software/S0053/"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">SeaDuke</span></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> — although this persistence mechanism is not exclusive to COZY BEAR.<sup>5</sup></span></p>

">In the obfuscated example above, </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;

<h3><i>GoldMax <span style="	font-style: normal;
">(Linux variant)</span></i></h3>
<ul><li><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Attempted to blend in with a file name that matched the system name it resided on</span></li><li><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Configured for persistence via a crontab entry with a </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">&#64;reboot</span></code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> line</span></li><li><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Used likely compromised infrastructure for C2</span></li></ul>
<p><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">GoldMax</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> was first observed during post-exploitation activity in the campaign leveraging the SolarWinds supply chain attacks. Previously identified samples of </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">GoldMax</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> were built for the Windows platform, with the earliest identified timestamp indicating a compilation in May 2020, but a recent CrowdStrike investigation discovered a </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">GoldMax</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> variant built for the Linux platform that the threat actor deployed in mid-2019. </span><b>This variant extends the backdoor’s known history and shows that the threat actor has used the malware in post-exploitation activity targeting other platforms than Windows.</b> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">The 2019 Linux variant of the </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">GoldMax</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> backdoor is almost identical in functionality and implementation to the previously identified May 2020 Windows variant. The very few additions to the backdoor between 2019 and 2020 likely reflect its maturity and longstanding evasion of detections. It is likely </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">GoldMax</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> has been used as a long-term persistence backdoor during StellarParticle-related compromises, which would be consistent with the few changes made to the malware to modify existing functions or support additional functionality.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Persistence was established via a crontab entry for a non-root user. With the binary named to masquerade as a legitimate file on the system and placed in a hidden directory, a crontab entry was created with a </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">&#64;reboot</span></code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> line so the </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">GoldMax</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> binary would execute again upon system reboot. Additionally, the threat actor used the </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">nohup</span></code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> command to ignore any hangup signals, and the process will continue to run even if the terminal session was terminated.</span></p>
<div class="wp-caption" id="attachment_54036"><img class="wp-image-54036" src="/content/dam/crowdstrike/www/en-us/wp/2022/01/Screen-Shot-2022-01-18-at-11.22.46-AM-1024x78.png" alt="" width="600" height="46"/>Figure 5. Crontab entry for <i>GoldMax</i> persistence</div>
<h2><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Enumeration Tools/Unique Directory Structure</span></h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Throughout our StellarParticle investigations, CrowdStrike identified what appeared to be a VBScript-based Active Directory enumeration toolkit. While the script’s contents have not been recovered to date, CrowdStrike has observed identical artifacts across multiple StellarParticle engagements that suggest the same or similar tool was used.</span></p>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">In each instance the tool was used, Shellbags data indicated that directories with random names of a consistent length were navigated to by the same user that ran the tool. After two levels of randomly named directories, Shellbags proved the existence of subdirectories named after the FQDNs for the victims’ various domains. In addition, the randomly named directories are typically created in a previously existing directory that’s one level off of the root of the C drive. The randomly named directories have a consistent length where the first directory is six characters and the next directory is three characters. To date, the names of the directories have always been formed from lowercase alphanumeric characters. For example, Shellbags indicated that directories matching the naming patterns below were browsed to (where “XX” is a previously existing directory on the system):</span></p>
<ul><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">C:\XX\{6}</span></code></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">C:\XX\{6}\{3}</span></code></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">C:\XX\{6}\{3}\domain.FQDN</span></code></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">C:\XX\{6}\{3}\domain-2.FQDN</span></code></li></ul>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">In each case, immediately prior to the creation of the directories referenced above, there was evidence of execution of a VBScript file by the same user that browsed to the directories. This evidence typically came from a UserAssist entry for wscript.exe, as well as RecentApps entries for wscript.exe (that would also include the VBScript filename). In addition, the Jump List for wscript.exe contained evidence of the VBScript files. The name of the VBScript files varied across engagements and was generally designed to look fairly innocuous and blend in. Two examples are </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">env.vbs</span></code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> and </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">WinNet.vbs</span></code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">. Due to the subdirectories that are named after the FQDNs for victim domains, CrowdStrike assesses with moderate confidence that the scripts represent an AD enumeration tool used by the adversary.</span></p>
<h2><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Internal Wiki Access</span></h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Across multiple StellarParticle investigations, CrowdStrike identified unique reconnaissance activities performed by the threat actor: access of victims&#39; internal knowledge repositories.<sup>6</sup> Wikis are commonly used across industries to facilitate knowledge sharing and as a source of reference for a variety of topics. While operating in the victim&#39;s internal network, the threat actor accessed sensitive information specific to the products and services that the victim organization provided. This information included items such as product/service architecture and design documents, vulnerabilities and step-by-step instructions to perform various tasks. Additionally, the threat actor viewed pages related to internal business operations such as development schedules and points of contact. In some instances these points of contact were subsequently targeted for further data collection.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">The threat actor&#39;s wiki access could be considered an extension of &#34;Credential Hopping&#34; described earlier. The threat actor established RDP sessions to internal servers using privileged accounts and then accessed the wiki using a </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">different</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> set of credentials. CrowdStrike observed the threat actor accessing the wiki as users who would be considered &#34;non-privileged&#34; from an Active Directory perspective but had access to sensitive data specific to the victim&#39;s products or services.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">At this time, the malicious access of internal wikis is an information gathering technique that CrowdStrike has only observed in StellarParticle investigations. CrowdStrike was able to identify the wiki access primarily through forensic analysis of the internal systems used by the threat actor. Given the threat actor&#39;s penchant for clearing browser data, organizations should not rely upon the availability of these artifacts for future investigations. CrowdStrike recommends the following best practices for internal information repositories:</span></p>
<ul><li><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Enable detailed access logging</span></li><li><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Ensure logs are centralized and stored for at least 180 days</span></li><li><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Create detections for anomalous activity such as access from an unusual location like a server subnet</span></li><li><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Enable MFA on the repository site, or provide access via Single Sign On (SSO) behind MFA</span></li></ul>

<h2>O365 Company Service Principal Manipulation</h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">The threat actor also deployed several layers of persistence utilizing both pre-existing and threat actor-created Service Principals with the ultimate goal of gaining global access to email.</span></p>
<h3><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Attacker-created Service Principal</span></h3>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">First, the threat actor used a compromised O365 administrator account to create a new Service Principal with a generic name. This Service Principal was granted </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">company administrator</span></code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> privileges. From there, the threat actor added a credential to this Service Principal so that they could access the Service Principal directly, without use of an O365 user account.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">These actions were recorded in Unified Audit Logs with the following three operation names:</span></p>
<ul><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Add service principal</span></code></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Add member to role</span></code></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Add service principal credentials.</span></code></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Update Service Principal</span></code></li></ul>
<h3><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Company-Created Service Principal Hijacking</span></h3>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Next, the threat actor utilized the threat actor-created Service Principal to take control of a second Service Principal. This was done by adding credentials to this second Service Principal, which was legitimately created by the company. This now compromised company-created Service Principal had </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">mail.read</span></code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> graph permissions consented on behalf of all users within the tenant.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">This action was recorded by just one operation type in Unified Audit Logs. This operation type is named <code>Add service principal credentials</code>.</span></p>
<h3><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Mail.Read Service Principal Abuse</span></h3>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Finally, the threat actor utilized the compromised Service Principal with the assigned </span><code style="	font-size: 14.0px;
">mail.read</code> permissions to then read emails of several different users in the company’s environment. CrowdStrike was able to use the Unified Audit Logs’ (UAL) <code style="	font-size: 14.0px;
">MailItemsAccessed</code> operation events to see the exact emails the threat actor viewed, as the majority of the users in the tenant were assigned O365 E5 licenses. When performing analysis on the UAL, CrowdStrike used the <code style="	font-size: 14.0px;
">ClientAppId</code> value within the <code style="	font-size: 14.0px;
">MailItemsAccessed</code> operation and cross-correlated with the Application ID of the compromised service principal to see what activities were performed by the threat actor.</p>
<h2><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">O365 Application Impersonation</span></h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Another consistent TTP identified during StellarParticle investigations has been the abuse of the </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">ApplicationImpersonation</span></code><sup>9</sup><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> role. When this role was assigned to a particular user that was controlled by the threat actor, it allowed the threat actor to impersonate any user within the O365 environment. These impersonated events are </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">not</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> logged verbosely by the Unified Audit Logs and can be difficult to detect.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">While the assignment of these </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">ApplicationImpersonation</span></code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> roles were not logged in the Unified Audit Logs, CrowdStrike was able to identify this persistence mechanism via the management role configuration settings, which can be exported with the Exchange PowerShell command:</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
"><code>Get-ManagementRoleAssignment -Role ApplicationImpersonation</code>.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">CrowdStrike then analyzed the exported configuration settings and identified several users (not service accounts) that the threat actor likely gave direct </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">ApplicationImpersonation</span></code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> roles during the known periods of compromise.</span></p>
<h2><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Remote Tasklist</span></h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">The threat actor attempted to remotely list running processes on systems using <code>tasklist.exe</code>. As tasklist uses WMI “under the hood,” this activity was captured by Falcon as </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">SuspiciousWmiQuery</span></code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> events that included the query and the source system. Additionally, the failed (not successful) process listing resulted in a DCOM error that was logged in the System.evtx event log under Event ID 10028. A sample of the information included with this event is below:</span></p>
<div class="wp-caption" id="attachment_54040"><img class="wp-image-54040" src="/content/dam/crowdstrike/www/en-us/wp/2022/01/Screen-Shot-2022-01-18-at-11.40.45-AM-1024x113.png" alt="" width="600" height="66"/>Figure 9. Event ID 10028 showing failed execution of remote tasklist</div>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">This remote process listing was consistently used by the threat actor targeting the same or similar lists of remote systems, and the owners of the targeted systems also happened to be the individuals with cloud access that the threat actor was interested in. While unproven, it’s possible the threat actor was running </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">tasklist</span></code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> remotely on these systems specifically to see which of the target systems was running Google Chrome. This is because a current or recent Chrome session to the victim’s cloud tenants would be potentially beneficial in the hijacking of sessions that the threat actor performed in order to access the victim’s cloud resources.</span></p>
<h2><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">FTP Scanning/Identity Knowledge</span></h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">In one instance, after being evicted from a victim environment, the threat actor began probing external services as a means to regain access, initially focusing on (S)FTP servers that were internet-accessible. Logs on the servers indicated that the threat actor attempted to log in with multiple valid accounts and in several cases was successful. There was little to no activity during the (S)FTP sessions. This likely was an exercise in attempting to identify misconfigured (S)FTP accounts that also had shell access, similar to what’s described in the Credential Hopping section earlier. Some of the accounts used were not in the victim’s Active Directory, as these were accounts for customers of the victim and stored in a separate LDAP database. However, the threat actor had knowledge of these accounts and used them on the correct systems, which further confirmed that the threat actor had advanced knowledge of the victim’s environment.</span></p>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">After confirming the FTP accounts did not provide shell access into the environment, the threat actor began attempting to connect into the environment via VPN. The threat actor attempted to log in to the VPN using several user accounts but was prevented from connecting, either due to not having the correct password, or due to having the correct password but not getting past the recently implemented MFA requirement. Eventually, the threat actor attempted an account that they had the correct password for but that had not been set up with MFA. This resulted in a prompt being displayed to the threat actor that included an MFA setup link. The threat actor subsequently set up MFA for the account and successfully connected to the victim’s network via VPN.</span></p>
<h2><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">TA Masquerading of System Names</span></h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">During the attempted and successful VPN authentications described above, the threat actor ensured the hostname of their system matched the naming convention of hostnames in the victim’s environment. This again showed a strong knowledge of the victim’s internal environment on the part of the threat actor. Not only did the masqueraded hostnames follow the correct naming convention from a broad perspective, they were also valid in terms of what would be expected for the user account the threat actor leveraged (i.e., in terms of the site name and asset type indicated in the hostname). This masqueraded hostname technique has been observed at multiple StellarParticle-related investigations.</span></p>

<h2><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Credential Refresh</span></h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">On some investigations, the dwell time of the threat actor spanned years. Given this extended period, it is logical to assume that some credentials obtained by the threat actor would be rotated during normal business operations. To combat this, the threat actor periodically &#34;refreshed&#34; their credential set by performing credential theft activities in an already compromised environment. At one victim, CrowdStrike identified multiple instances of domain credential theft months apart, each time with a different credential theft technique.</span></p>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">One of the credential theft techniques identified by CrowdStrike was the use of a PowerShell script to execute Mimikatz in-memory. While in-memory Mimikatz is not particularly unique, the script executed by the threat actor was heavily obfuscated and encrypted the output using AES256. CrowdStrike was able to reconstruct the PowerShell script from the PowerShell Operational event log as the script&#39;s execution was logged automatically due to the use of specific keywords. CrowdStrike recommends that organizations upgrade PowerShell on their systems, as this functionality is only available with PowerShell version 5 and above.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">In addition to refreshing the threat actor&#39;s credentials, the threat actor would also refresh their understanding of the victim&#39;s AD environment. Around the time when the threat actor executed </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Get-ADReplAccount</span></code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">, the threat actor also executed a renamed version of AdFind to output domain reconnaissance information. In this instance, AdFind was renamed to masquerade as a legitimate Windows binary. The usage of renamed AdFind is consistent with other industry reporting on this campaign.</span></p>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">In addition to using scripted commands, operators were repeatedly observed manually executing several standard PowerShell cmdlets to enumerate network information from AD, including </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Get-ADUser</span></code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> and </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Get-ADGroupMember</span></code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> to query specific members in the directory. This information provided the adversary with a list of accounts possessing particular privileges — in this case, the ability to make VPN connections — that would be subject to later credential stealing attempts and leveraged to access the victim at a later time.</span></p>
<h2><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Password Policies/Hygiene</span></h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">In some cases, the threat actor was able to quickly return to the environment and essentially pick up where they left off, even though the organization had performed an enterprise-wide password reset, including a reset of all service accounts and the double-reset of the krbtgt account. CrowdStrike determined that in these cases, administrative users had “reset” their own password to the same password they previously used, essentially nullifying the impact of the enterprise-wide reset. This was possible even though the customer’s Active Directory was configured to require new passwords to be different from the previous five passwords for a given account. Unfortunately, this check only applies when a user is changing their password via the “password change” method — but if a “password reset” is performed (changing the password without knowing the previous password), this check is bypassed for an administrative user or a Windows account that has the Reset Password permission on a user’s account object.<sup>12</sup> Since the </span><code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Get-ADReplAccount</span></code><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> cmdlet described above included the NTHashHistory values (i.e., previous password hashes) for user accounts, CrowdStrike was able to verify that some administrative accounts indeed had the exact same password hash showing up multiple times in the password history, as well as in the current NTHash value.</span></p>
<h2><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Close Out</span></h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">The StellarParticle campaign, associated with the COZY BEAR adversary group, demonstrates this threat actor’s extensive knowledge of Windows and Linux operating systems, Microsoft Azure, O365, and Active Directory, and their patience and covert skill set to stay undetected for months — and in some cases, years.</span></p>
