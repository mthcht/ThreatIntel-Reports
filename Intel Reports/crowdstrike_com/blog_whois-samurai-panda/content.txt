        <h1>Who is Samurai Panda</h1>
        
        <div class="publish_info">
            <p>April 12, 2013</p>
            <span>|</span>
            <a href="/en-us/blog/author.adamm/" title="Posts by AdamM" rel="author">
                AdamM</a>
            <span>|</span>
                
            <a href="/en-us/blog/category.counter-adversary-operations/" title="Threat Hunting &amp; Intel">Threat Hunting &amp; Intel</a>
        </div>
        <div class="post_image">
            <img width="1060" height="698" src="/content/dam/crowdstrike/www/en-us/wp/2016/03/Blue.jpg" class="attachment-post-thumbnail size-post-thumbnail wp-post-image" alt="" decoding="async" fetchpriority="high" srcset="/content/dam/crowdstrike/www/en-us/wp/2016/03/Blue.jpg 530w,https://assets.crowdstrike.com/is/image/crowdstrikeinc/Blue-300x198?wid=300&amp;hei=198&amp;fmt=png-alpha&amp;qlt=95,0&amp;resMode=sharp2&amp;op_usm=3.0,0.3,2,0 300w" sizes="(max-width: 1060px) 100vw, 1060px"/>

<div class="text text--blog-content aem-GridColumn aem-GridColumn--default--12">



<div class="cmp-text" id="text-000a8f6a24" data-cmp-name="cmp-text-" data-cmp-is="text" data-target-location="false">
    
       <span><p><span style="	line-height: 1.5em;
">This week we’re back to our old friends with a Chinese nexus. To recount the last few weeks of our adversary blog posts, we first introduced</span> <a style="	line-height: 1.5em;
" href="/en-us/blog/whois-anchor-panda/index/">Anchor Panda</a><span style="	line-height: 1.5em;
">, an adversary we attribute to China and associate with the PLAN.</span></p>
<p><span style="	line-height: 1.5em;
">We then moved on to another Chinese adversary we dubbed</span> <a style="	line-height: 1.5em;
" href="/en-us/blog/whois-numbered-panda/index/">Numbered Panda</a><span style="	line-height: 1.5em;
"> to highlight the issue of community naming for adversaries and the lack of a common lexicon for characterizing these attackers.</span></p>
<p><span style="	line-height: 1.5em;
">Next, in an effort to demonstrate it wasn&#39;t relegated to China, we exposed</span> <a style="	line-height: 1.5em;
" href="/en-us/blog/whois-clever-kitten/">Clever Kitten</a><span style="	line-height: 1.5em;
">, an actor we track out of Iran who leverages some very distinct TTPs when viewed next to a more visible adversary. <b>This week we will discuss another Chinese nexus adversary we call Samurai Panda.</b></span></p>
<p>Samurai Panda is interesting in that their <b>target selection tends to focus on Asia Pacific victims in Japan, the Republic of Korea, and other democratic Asian victims</b>. Beginning in 2009, we’ve observed this actor conduct more than 40 unique campaigns that we’ve identified in the malware configurations&#39; campaign codes. These codes are often leveraged in the malware used by coordinated targeted attackers to differentiate victims that were successfully compromised from different target sets.</p>
<p>When conducting programmatic espionage activity, it can presumably become quite confusing if the attacker targets a heavy industry company, an avionics program, and seven other unique targets as to which infected host you will collect what information from. While investigating these targeted attacks, the campaign key can be leveraged in much the same way by the investigator to sort out how various malware samples are related, and perhaps what sectors the various builds were meant for.</p>
<h2>Samurai Panda&#39;s Installation Process</h2>
<p>The implant delivered by Samurai Panda uses a typical installation process whereby they:</p>
<ol><li><b>leverage a spearphish with an exploit to get control of the execution flow of the targeted application</b>. This file “drops” an XOR-encoded payload that unpacks itself and a configuration file.</li><li>Next, the implant, which can perform in several different modes, typically will install itself as a service and then begin beaconing out to an adversary-controlled host.</li><li>If that command-and-control host is online, the malicious service will download and instantiate a backdoor that provides remote access to the attacker, who will see the infected host’s identification information as well as the campaign code.</li></ol>
<p>Using CrowdRE with IDA Pro, you can import the CrowdStrike Intelligence Team&#39;s annotations for this malware and the associated backdoor to leverage the reverse engineering that we have already conducted.</p>
<h2>How to Detect Samurai Panda</h2>
<p>Here are some Snort rules that will enable you to detect Samurai Panda activity on your enterprise. These rules detect the malware “beaconing” to the command-and-control server, the initial malware check-in, and an attempt to download a backdoor module.</p>
