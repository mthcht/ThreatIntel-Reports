                            <span class="posted-on">Posted on <time class="entry-date published" datetime="2020-10-16T08:57:53+00:00">October 16, 2020</time><time class="updated" datetime="2023-10-02T15:46:05+00:00">October 2, 2023</time></span><span class="sep">•</span>14 min read                        </div>
                        <p>Recently, we have observed ongoing attacks on residential gateways. These attacks had a common trait: they all originated fromofuxico[.]com.br with the help of malvertising. Once a victim visits this site, they are led through a loop of referrers and redirectors to a malicious JavaScript file. Its end goal is to change the DNS settings on the residential router by initiating a CSRF attack. The victim usually does not detect any malicious activity without <a href="/sentry/" data-wpel-link="internal">proper device protection</a> and the fact that the attack is executed in the background via hidden iframes and malicious redirectors. In this article, I will present a case study of home router DNS hijacking in Brazil.</p>
<h2>Cyber Crime in Brazil</h2>
<p>Malvertising attacks are very common amongst compromised Brazilian sites that have been under pressure and constant attacks for years. Many previous articles have elaborated (Novidade <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/en_us/research/18/l/new-exploit-kit-novidade-found-targeting-home-and-soho-routers.html" rel="”nofollow” external noopener noreferrer" data-wpel-link="external" target="_blank">Exploit Kit hitting Brazil</a> or the surge in <a href="https://blog.avast.com/avast-blocks-dns-hijacking-in-brazil" rel="”nofollow” external noopener noreferrer" data-wpel-link="external" target="_blank">DNS hijacking</a>) on the fact that threat actors in Brazil are very profit-oriented, and extremely successful: many Brazilian websites seem to lack basic security features and exploiting them is very profitable for actors.</p>
<h2>CSRF Attacks</h2>
<p>Cross-site request forgery (CSRF) is a type of attack that forces the victim to unknowingly carry out actions in a web application where they are authenticated (or where the attacker is aware of the default password to a specific system). These attacks are becoming popular because they allow attackers to execute an action in an internal system or network by tricking the victim from the outside. Popular CSRF attacks include money transfers, e-mail address changes, changing a victim’s password or DNS settings, etc.</p>
<h2>DNS Hijacking</h2>
<p>Hijacking DNS settings is a risky attack, it forces websites’ addresses to be resolved incorrectly by a 3rd party DNS resolver. It is a similar approach to cache poisoning, but the victim is diverted to an attacker-controlled environment instead of the original website. It has dangerous implications for the victim: for instance, opening your banking institution’s website would redirect you to a fake banking website, and banking login credentials would be at risk of theft. We have visualized the recent campaign below. In normal circumstances, end users reach Internet Banking services via a legitimate DNS resolver.</p>
<figure id="attachment_133186" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-133186" style="width: 830px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-133186 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/Screenshot-2020-10-16-at-10.50.15.png" alt="" width="830" height="872" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-133186" class="wp-caption-text">An everyday scenario, where a legitimate DNS resolver is used to reach Internet Banking services</figcaption></figure>
<p>When a malicious CSRF request executed by the unknowing end-user, their home router&#8217;s DNS settings are changed. After this happens, all further domains that the victim&#8217;s laptop requests will be resolved by the malicious DNS resolver, translating the requested domains to an IP that is controlled by the threat actor.</p>
<figure id="attachment_133185" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-133185" style="width: 1278px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-133185 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/Screenshot-2020-10-16-at-10.49.52.png" alt="" width="1278" height="958" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-133185" class="wp-caption-text">Victim unknowingly executes the CSRF request that changes the home router&#8217;s DNS settings</figcaption></figure>
<p>At this stage, the victim home router&#8217;s DNS settings are changed and the user is redirected to a fake banking site whenever the domain is requested. Threat actors will usually get the banking credentials and transfer money from the affected accounts, sending it to off-shore accounts or converting the money to cryptocurrency.</p>
<figure id="attachment_133184" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-133184" style="width: 1278px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-133184 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/Screenshot-2020-10-16-at-10.48.28.png" alt="" width="1278" height="1094" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-133184" class="wp-caption-text">Victim visits fake banking site via the hijacked DNS resolver, exposing Internet Banking credentials</figcaption></figure>
<h2> </h2>
<h2>Case Study: DNS hijacking Attacks Targeting Routers in Brazil</h2>

<p>  Visiting the site ofuxico[.]com[.]br initiates several requests to 3rd party ad-networks. Oftentimes, ad networks are the sources of malvertising attacks, as malicious ads are injected into the benign ad rotation. It is up to the 3rd party ad provider to screen and remove malicious ad content, and there are ways to defend against these attacks, such as using ad-blocking plugins.</p>
<figure id="attachment_132921" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-132921" style="width: 1144px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-132921 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dnscsrf1.png" alt="SSL Client Hello requests to Advertising networks" width="1144" height="230" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-132921" class="wp-caption-text">SSL Client Hello requests to Advertising networks</figcaption></figure>
<p>Once the malicious ad is loaded, the victim is looped through a series of requests. These requests usually happen in the background and are invisible to the victim. There are two common ways of doing this:</p>
<ul>
<li>&#8211; Opening a new, hidden window</li>
<li>&#8211; Clever use of zero-pixel iframes</li>
</ul>
<p>  The first request is sent to a resource called <i>ads.php</i>, which is a malicious redirector. After the content of the PHP file is successfully processed on the web server, and the output is displayed in the browser, a second-stage script is called, which is a JavaScript file. These two resources are the core of the attack, executing a set of <a href="https://cujo.com/honeypot-journals-part-ii-attacks-on-residential-endpoints/" data-wpel-link="internal">malicious actions against the residential gateways</a>. There are two requests to googleapis.com to get the <i>jquery.min.</i><i>js</i>JavaScript file: these do not serve a purpose in the chain of the attack and are being called from <i>change.js</i>. Finally, there are<strong> 2 requests to 192.168.0.1, </strong><strong>that</strong><strong> are responsible for changing the victim router&#8217;s DNS settings</strong>. As I&#8217;ve noted in the introduction, hijacking DNS settings has major implications. Finally, we also see a call to ip-api.com, which is a sort of a pre-check for this type of attack: only routers and modems that are in Brazil were targeted by this attack.</p>
<figure id="attachment_132922" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-132922" style="width: 1236px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-132922 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dnscsrf2.png" alt="A timeline of requests initiated when visiting the infected site" width="1236" height="224" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-132922" class="wp-caption-text">A timeline of requests initiated when visiting the infected site</figcaption></figure>
<p>Let’s break down each request in a bit more detail.  </p>
<h2>Ads.php Malicious Script Analysis</h2>
<p>The very first malicious GET request is to run ads.php. We captured the traffic flow to understand the server&#8217;s responses. In this case, the server responded with several base64 encoded blobs, which are also executed immediately due to the embedding tag and the specified Content-Type.</p>
<figure id="attachment_132923" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-132923" style="width: 1200px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-132923 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dnscsrf3.png" alt="Network capture of ads.php being requested" width="1200" height="810" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-132923" class="wp-caption-text">Network capture of ads.php being requested</figcaption></figure>
<p>After decoding the base64 blobs, we find ourselves with several smaller HTML code blocks that have a single purpose: all of them try to change the DNS settings for the victim’s network.</p>
<figure id="attachment_132924" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-132924" style="width: 1200px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-132924 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dnscsrf4.png" alt="Hardcoded base64 encoded string YWRtaW4= translates to admin" width="1200" height="520" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-132924" class="wp-caption-text">Hardcoded base64 encoded string YWRtaW4= translates to admin</figcaption></figure>
<p>In the next step, a request is sent to ip-api.com/json. The response JSON is parsed and a logic function decides what action to take based on the <i>regionName</i><i> </i>field. Two fields, <i>vpi</i> and <i>vci</i> are set to a certain value, which is based on municipality names. The developer of the scripts tried to achieve location-based differentiation: for example, if the victim is located in Sao Paulo, the two fields (vpi and vci) would be set to 8 and 35 respectively.</p>
<figure id="attachment_132925" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-132925" style="width: 1200px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-132925 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dnscsrf5.png" alt="Municipality based differentiation for DNS hijacking in Brazil" width="1200" height="904" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-132925" class="wp-caption-text">Municipality based differentiation (Brazil)</figcaption></figure>
<p>At the time of the writing, these fields are hidden and do not serve a purpose. We suspect that these specific values might gain some meaning later, as the developer enhances their script. <i>&lt;input type=&#8221;hidden&#8221; name=&#8221;</i><i>vpi</i><i>&#8220;&gt;</i> <i>&lt;input type=&#8221;hidden&#8221; name=&#8221;</i><i>vci</i><i>&#8220;&gt;</i> Another decoded blob targets ASUS RT-N13U routers. The crafted POST request uses the <i>start_apply.htm</i> resource to change the router’s DNS settings via the <i>wan_dns1_x</i> parameter. The default credentials are also included in the script, so the request gets through.</p>
<figure id="attachment_132926" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-132926" style="width: 1200px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-132926 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dnscsrf6.png" alt="Crafted POST request for the Asus RT-N13U router" width="1200" height="836" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-132926" class="wp-caption-text">Crafted POST request for the Asus RT-N13U router</figcaption></figure>
<p>Another script targets TP-Link routers on 192.168.0.1:80. Again, the crafted POST request changes DNS settings via the <i>WanDynamicIpCfgRpm.htm</i> resource by using the <i>dns server</i><i> </i>parameter.</p>
<figure id="attachment_132927" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-132927" style="width: 1200px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-132927 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dnscsrf7.png" alt="Crafted POST request for TP-Link routers" width="1200" height="694" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-132927" class="wp-caption-text">Crafted POST request for TP-Link routers</figcaption></figure>
<p>After each snippet is decoded and executed, another script gets invoked, called change.js. There is also a small image included towards the end. The developer is using the service amung.us, which provides real-time web statistics and information on their victims.</p>
<figure id="attachment_132951" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-132951" style="width: 1086px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-132951 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dns08.png" alt="The second-stage JavaScript embedded inside ads.php" width="1086" height="414" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-132951" class="wp-caption-text">The second-stage JavaScript embedded inside ads.php</figcaption></figure>
<p>  Since we&#8217;re done analyzing ads.php, let&#8217;s continue by analysing a script it invoces – the change.js JavaScript file.  </p>
<h2>Change.js Malicious Script Analysis</h2>
<p>First, the malicious JavaScript defines a loadScript function, which then calls the resource <a href="https://ajax.googleapis.com/ajax/libs/jquery/1.6.1/jquery.min.js" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer noopener external" data-wpel-link="external">https://ajax.googleapis.com/ajax/libs/jquery/1.6.1/jquery.min.js</a>. This may be an attempt to stay under the radar by making the malicious requests blend in with normal network traffic.</p>
<figure id="attachment_132928" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-132928" style="width: 1200px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-132928 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dnscsrf9.png" alt="Captured network traffic of change.js" width="1200" height="844" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-132928" class="wp-caption-text">Captured network traffic of change.js</figcaption></figure>

<ul>

<p>It seems that these two were added deliberately for testing and might not serve any other purpose. Next up, we have a variable that defines http:// as a base64 encoded string. The two other functions defined here will be used to invoke HTTP requests. It seems that the developer wanted separate functions for calling stylesheets(<i>loadjscssfile</i>) and zero pixel iframes (<i>loadjscssfile1</i>). <ul>

<ul>
<li>Set a timeout for 6,000,000,000 milliseconds (69 days) to delay further action, then navigate to <a href="https://www.google.com.br/" rel="nofollow noreferrer noopener external" data-wpel-link="external" target="_blank">www.google.com.br</a></li>
<li>Set a notification message in Brazilian for the current page (English translation):</li>
</ul>
<blockquote><p><i>We believe that you will find one of the links listed below useful: </i><i>You may not be able to view the requested page for one of the following reasons: </i><i>An outdated bookmark </i><i>link </i><i>A search engine that has an outdated reference to our site </i><i>A misspelled URL</i></p></blockquote>
<figure id="attachment_132933" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-132933" style="width: 1188px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-132933 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dnscsrf14.png" alt="Brazilian notification message in case the victim is not located in Brazil" width="1188" height="448" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-132933" class="wp-caption-text">Brazilian notification message in case the victim is not located in Brazil</figcaption></figure>
<p>If the victim’s IP is from Brazil, the script invokes the previously defined function <i>loadjscssfile</i><i> </i>and tries to change the remote router&#8217;s DNS settings by sending hundreds of requests. The variable <i>pht</i><i> </i>equals to <i>http://</i>. These requests contain the login credentials before the variables, which store the hex-encoded version of the target IP addresses (192.168.0.1). The IP address is then followed by the actual resource, in this case /dnscfg.cgi, which is responsible for changing the residential router’s DNS settings. This resource would change from router to router, depending on the vendor and the actual model, but the actors have managed to collect plenty of examples from actual routers. All in all, <strong>change.</strong><strong>js can invoke 1,414 distinct requests</strong> with different combinations of login credentials, IP addresses and URI resources. This shows that the developer tried to cast a wide net and reach as many routers/modems as possible.</p>

<h2>Analyzing the Malicious Infrastructure</h2>
<p>Let’s look behind the curtain to try and understand the attacker’s infrastructure. We know that the malicious redirector and JavaScript file is served from <i>1xb5bkr[.]</i><i>googleads</i><i>[.]store</i>. Enumerating DNS records for this domain reveals a couple of things:</p>
<ul>
<li>This subdomain has an A record of 191.232.252[.]215, which is in Brazil and served through Microsoft’s Cloud hosting. The A record is connected to <i>googleads</i><i>[.]store</i> too.</li>
</ul>
<figure id="attachment_132935" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-132935" style="width: 1146px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-132935 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dnscsrf16.png" alt="IP information for 191[.]232[.]252[.]215" width="1146" height="368" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-132935" class="wp-caption-text">IP information for 191[.]232[.]252[.]215</figcaption></figure>
<ul>
<li>Initiating a reverse lookup and correlating the result with VirusTotal queries shows that this IP address has many other domains attached to it. It looks like the attackers are generating a new subdomain every day in order to change the address of their infrastructure, but all subdomains still use the same IPv4 server address.</li>
</ul>
<figure id="attachment_132936" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-132936" style="width: 1006px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-132936 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dnscsrf17.png" alt="Correlated malicious domains via “A” DNS records" width="1006" height="878" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-132936" class="wp-caption-text">Correlated domains via “A” DNS records</figcaption></figure>
<ul>
<li>Crawling one of these domains reveals that web directory listing is enabled on the server: we can spot four directories inside the <i>/</i><i>mbl</i><i>/</i> directory. All four directories have theads.php redirector and the change.js malicious JavaScript inside. It seems that the purpose of these directories was to test different redirectors for different scenarios, but all four contain the same set of files at the moment.</li>
</ul>
<figure id="attachment_132937" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-132937" style="width: 770px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-132937 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dnscsrf18.png" alt="Maldvertisers left web directories browsable" width="770" height="534" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-132937" class="wp-caption-text">Browsing web directories left enabled</figcaption></figure>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2>DNS Trickery: Fake Brazilian Banking Websites Stealing Client Credentials</h2>
<p>Commonly these DNS changer attacks manifest in phishing or credential harvesting. One revelation is that the malicious DNS servers send a malicious IP address back when certain Brazilian Bank websites are queried:</p>
<p><figure id="attachment_132938" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-132938" style="width: 604px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-132938 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dnscsrf19.png" alt="BB Bank hijacking" width="604" height="408" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-132938" class="wp-caption-text">BB Bank</figcaption></figure> <figure id="attachment_132939" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-132939" style="width: 624px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-132939 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dnscsrf20.png" alt="Itau Bank fake" width="624" height="406" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-132939" class="wp-caption-text">Itau Bank</figcaption></figure> <figure id="attachment_132940" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-132940" style="width: 638px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-132940 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dnscsrf21.png" alt="Fake Banco Bradesco" width="638" height="502" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-132940" class="wp-caption-text">Banco Bradesco</figcaption></figure> <figure id="attachment_132941" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-132941" style="width: 700px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-132941 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dnscsrf22.png" alt="Fake Santander Bank site" width="700" height="406" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-132941" class="wp-caption-text">Santander Bank</figcaption></figure></p>
<p>  These attackers might be trying to redirect the victim to a fake Banking website, and eventually steal their banking credentials. As of writing this article, the IP addresses serve a fake Banco do Brasil front-end under <i>www.bb.com.br/dktp/</i><i>logon.php</i>, which looks like a registration for new visitors to sign up for the fake service.</p>
<figure id="attachment_132942" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-132942" style="width: 1016px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-132942 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dnscsrf23.png" alt="Fake Banco Do Brasil banking website (Note the warning on the TLS certificate)" width="1016" height="812" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-132942" class="wp-caption-text">Fake Banco Do Brasil banking website (Note the warning on the TLS certificate)</figcaption></figure>
<p>Analysing the TLS certificate reveals that it is a self-signed certificate and registered with the e-mail address <a href="/cdn-cgi/l/email-protection" class="__cf_email__" data-cfemail="9af7fbf4f3eef5daf7f3fdeffff6f3eef5b4f9f5f7">[email&#160;protected]</a>, which is a fake name. The domain is listed for sale and is not currently owned by anyone.</p>
<figure id="attachment_132943" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-132943" style="width: 924px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-132943 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dnscsrf24.png" alt="A self-signed certificate of the fake banking website" width="924" height="652" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-132943" class="wp-caption-text">A self-signed certificate of the fake banking website</figcaption></figure>
<p>Another login panel was found in pfelogin.php, asking for a username and a password as well. This is the main login page for the Internet Banking service.</p>
<figure id="attachment_132944" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-132944" style="width: 802px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-132944 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dnscsrf25.png" alt="Login panel on the fake Banco do Brasil website" width="802" height="874" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-132944" class="wp-caption-text">Login panel on the fake Banco do Brasil website</figcaption></figure>
<p>Below you can see how the original Banco do Brasil website looks like when the DNS settings are not altered, and the request to the original domain goes to the proper IP address 170.66.11.10. Also, the original website does not have a <i>/</i><i>dktp</i><i>/</i> folder, unlike the fake website. The login page for the Internet Bank is also at a different path: <i>https://www2.bancobrasil.com.br/aapf/login.jsp</i></p>
<figure id="attachment_132945" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-132945" style="width: 1136px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-132945 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dnscsrf26.png" alt="The original Banco do Brasil website" width="1136" height="744" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-132945" class="wp-caption-text">The original Banco do Brasil website</figcaption></figure>
<p>On the fake website, once the victim has passed his/her credentials, the login page redirects the visitor to update.php, which is then followed by a form action to update2.php.</p>
<figure id="attachment_132946" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-132946" style="width: 1102px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-132946 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dnscsrf27.png" alt="The fake dktp login form redirects victims to update2.php" width="1102" height="440" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-132946" class="wp-caption-text">The fake dktp login form redirects victims to update2.php</figcaption></figure>
<p>An automatic refresh action is executed by a meta tag, which then calls aguarde2.php.</p>
<figure id="attachment_132947" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-132947" style="width: 970px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-132947 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dnscsrf28.png" alt="The next link in the redirect chain is aguarde2.php" width="970" height="536" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-132947" class="wp-caption-text">The next link in the redirect chain is aguarde2.php</figcaption></figure>
<p>Then the user is finally redirected to atualizando.php with a Timeout function, and is presented with the login page again.</p>
<figure id="attachment_132948" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-132948" style="width: 1070px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-132948 size-full" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dnscsrf29.png" alt="After aguarde2.php, the destination is atualizando.php" width="1070" height="648" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-132948" class="wp-caption-text">After aguarde2.php, the destination is atualizando.php</figcaption></figure>
<p>At this point the damage is done, and the threat actors have received the victim’s credential for the Online Banking service. The attackers will usually empty the accounts in a manner of hours, and victims will have a hard time chasing down their money, after it is funnelled over several accounts or turned into some type of cryptocurrency.  </p>
<h2>Basic Recommendations for Protection Against Attacks</h2>
<p>There are many common-sense rules for <a href="/sentry/" data-wpel-link="internal">security online</a>, but since these attacks on Brazilian routers spread through advertisements and trackers on compromised websites, our tips focus on ad and tracker blocking options.</p>
<h3>For users:</h3>
<ul>
<li>Change the default login passwords on residential routers to protect you against DNS setting hijacking</li>
<li>Use browser addons and strict browser settings against malvertising:
<ul>
<li>uBlock Origin</li>
<li>Privacy Badger</li>
<li>HTTPS Everywhere</li>
<li>Use the Strict mode for Trackers in Firefox</li>
<li>Use the Google Safe Browsing feature</li>
<li>Use an anti-virus on your computer and router</li>
</ul>
<h3>For banks:</h3>
<ul>
<li>Implement a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HTTP_Strict_Transport_Security" data-wpel-link="external" target="_blank" rel="external noopener noreferrer">HSTS</a> policy, so users are protected againstMitM and cookie hijacking (upon a certificate error, users are not let through)</li>
</ul>
<p> </p>
