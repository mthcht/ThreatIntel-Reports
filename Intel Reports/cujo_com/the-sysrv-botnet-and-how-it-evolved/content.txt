    <h1 class="entry-title">The Sysrv Botnet and How It Evolved</h1><img src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAAAAACH5BAEKAAEALAAAAAABAAEAAAICTAEAOw==" width="2424" height="630" data-src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/The-Sysrv-Botnet-and-How-It-Evolved_01.jpg" data-sizes="auto" srcset="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/The-Sysrv-Botnet-and-How-It-Evolved_01.jpg 2424w, https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/The-Sysrv-Botnet-and-How-It-Evolved_01-300x78.jpg 300w, https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/The-Sysrv-Botnet-and-How-It-Evolved_01-1024x266.jpg 1024w, https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/The-Sysrv-Botnet-and-How-It-Evolved_01-768x200.jpg 768w, https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/The-Sysrv-Botnet-and-How-It-Evolved_01-1536x399.jpg 1536w, https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/The-Sysrv-Botnet-and-How-It-Evolved_01-2048x532.jpg 2048w, https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/The-Sysrv-Botnet-and-How-It-Evolved_01-400x104.jpg 400w" loading="lazy" class="lazyload post--thumb cover-object" alt=""></header>
            <div class="col-12 entry-content--actions order-1">
    <a class="back-link-btn link lazyload" href="https://cujo.com/blog/" data-bg="https://cujo.com/wp-content/themes/cujo/assets/images/left_back.svg" data-wpel-link="internal">All posts</a>

                        <div class="entry-meta">
                            <span class="posted-on">Posted on <time class="entry-date published" datetime="2021-09-22T11:46:58+00:00">September 22, 2021</time><time class="updated" datetime="2023-09-30T07:04:57+00:00">September 30, 2023</time></span><span class="sep">•</span>15 min read                        </div>
                        <p>People used to say that Linux was free from malware. Well, not only was it not true for the past 25 years, but we now live in an age where Linux is as promising a target for threat actors as some Windows endpoints due to its widespread usage as an operating system (OS) across many organizations. And, even more importantly, it serves as the OS for <a href="/15-most-popular-iot-products-and-devices-in-2021/" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-wpel-link="internal">popular Internet-of-Things (IoT) devices</a>. This article deals with the evolution of the Sysrv malware that infects Linux devices.</p>
<p>Nowadays, the Linux IoT threat landscape is highly homogeneous and consists mainly of <a href="https://cujo.com/mirai-gafgyt-with-new-ddos-modules-discovered/" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-wpel-link="internal">Mirai and Gafgyt forks</a>. Nevertheless, new breeds do surface and spread through poorly secured and vulnerable IoT devices.</p>
<p>This blog post takes a deep dive into the Sysrv botnet, which first made headlines at the end of December 2020 and has continued to evolve since then. As of the time of writing, Sysrv is still active and has gone through several iterations, meaning its developers continued to enhance its capabilities. Our analysis shows how it evolved, gained new features, and changed its behavior up until today.</p>
<h2>Overview of the Sysrv Botnet</h2>
<p>The Sysrv botnet was first mentioned in a blog post by <a href="https://www.intezer.com/blog/research/new-golang-worm-drops-xmrig-miner-on-servers/" target="_blank" rel="noopener external noreferrer" data-wpel-link="external">Intezer</a> at the end of December 2020. It stood out due to its use of Golang (Go) – a relatively new programming language that a growing number of malware developers have picked up since early 2020.</p>
<blockquote><p>Learn more about <a href="/reverse-engineering-go-binaries-with-ghidra/" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-wpel-link="internal">reverse engineering Golang binaries with Ghidra</a>.</p></blockquote>
<h3>Using Golang</h3>
<p>There are several reasons why a malware developer might choose Golang:</p>
<ul>
<li>Most importantly, Go has cross-compilation capabilities that make it a popular choice. It allows malware developers to <strong>maintain only a single codebase</strong> and then quickly build executables for various architectures.</li>
<li>Golang binaries are considered <strong>harder to reverse-engineer</strong> due to their file size (many functions to go through). Also, few decompilers and disassemblers support Golang.</li>
<li>Go is popular among developers thanks to its <strong>low barrier to entry, performance benefits, and flexibility</strong>.</li>
<li><strong>Leaked botnet source codes are available</strong> in Golang, such as the C&amp;C (command and control) module for Mirai.</li>
</ul>
<h3>What is Sysrv</h3>
<p>At its core, <strong>Sysrv is a worm and a cryptocurrency miner</strong>. The two modules were in separate files in its early versions, but its developers have since combined the two. The worm module simply initiates port scans against random IPs to find vulnerable Tomcat, WebLogic, and MySQL services and tries to infiltrate the servers with a hardcoded password dictionary attack. As Sysrv continued to evolve, it gradually introduced <strong>more exploits</strong> into its arsenal to enhance its worm capabilities. The malware propagation starts with a simple loader script file, which pulls down those modules upon successful execution. The botnet is distributed for both Linux and Windows environments, but in this blog post, we will detail the inner workings of the Linux variant.</p>

<h2>Sysrv’s Technical Details and Analysis</h2>
<h3>The Loader Scripts</h3>
<p>Two Sysrv loader scripts are circling for Linux and Windows: <strong>ldr.sh</strong> and <strong>ldr.ps1</strong>, respectively. Both achieve the same thing, even though they are written in different languages: the former in Bash and the latter in Powershell.</p>
<p><figure id="attachment_140260" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-140260" style="width: 1186px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-140260" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/word-image-7-1.png" alt="Sysrv botnet loader script windows ldr.ps1" width="1186" height="756" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-140260" class="wp-caption-text">The ldr.ps1 loader script</figcaption></figure> <figure id="attachment_140261" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-140261" style="width: 1342px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-140261" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/word-image-8-1.png" alt="Sysrv botnet loader script linux ldr.sh" width="1342" height="636" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-140261" class="wp-caption-text">The ldr.sh loader script</figcaption></figure></p>
<p>The <strong>first version of the Linux loader script</strong> had a few interesting functions like:</p>
<ul>
<li>Distribution server and ID were hardcoded</li>
<li>64-bit check (getconf LONG_BIT)</li>
<li>Kill existing sysrv process (pkill -9)</li>
<li>Enumerate processlist to see if sysrv process is running (ps -fe)</li>
<li>Get function to download binary (curl, get, php -r)</li>
</ul>
<p>As the development progressed, these features underwent significant revisions, and new, improved functions can be seen in the latest loader scripts. These <strong>new, enhanced features</strong> include:</p>
<ul>
<li>curl with a timeout parameter and five retries</li>
<li>Exit if a 32-bit system is detected</li>
<li>Kill running mining process (network01, kthreaddi)</li>
<li data-stringify-indent="0" data-stringify-border="0">Edit crontab file to achieve persistence</li>
<li>User-Agent changed to _ldr$bit and _cron</li>
<li data-stringify-indent="0" data-stringify-border="0">If crontab command is not available install cron via apt, crontabs via yum and start the appropriate services</li>
<li data-stringify-indent="0" data-stringify-border="0">Filtering for high CPU consuming processes and kill them if not sysrv or network01 (mining process)</li>
<li>The loader script includes the Miner dropper XMR32/XMR64 and an XMR configuration file for the mining pool and crypto address</li>
<li>Bash history cleanup</li>
<li>Another mining pool in a config.json file</li>
<li>Changing DNS resolvers to 1.1.1.1</li>
<li>Hardcoded SSH key for persistence</li>
<li>/tmp/ folder cleanup</li>
<li>Kill processes listening on TCP ports 52018 and 52019</li>
<li>Removing and renaming the iptables binary</li>
<li>Aliyun AV software uninstaller</li>
<li>Docker xmr image removal</li>
<li>Checking for the presence of nvidia-smi tool, listing GPUs for cryptomining</li>
</ul>
<p>Newer versions also include a <strong>reference to the IoT botnet operator group #keksec</strong>. Ever since the first appearance of the Sysrv botnet, new scripts have been released every month. March and April were the most active months for Sysrv development, as new scripts were released almost every two days. The newest samples we examined appeared in the middle of September.</p>

<h4>Grouping the binaries</h4>
<p>To aid our analysis, we first tried to branch out the investigation into smaller parts. A fundamental part was to try and group the observed samples by specific characteristics to see:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>How different</strong> the Sysrv samples are,</li>
<li><strong>How many</strong> variants there are.</li>
</ul>
<p>As every Sysrv binary we discovered was written in Golang, we grouped the malicious binaries based on their package structures. <strong>A package in Golang</strong> is a collection of source files in the same directory that are compiled together. All constants, variables, functions, and types defined in one of the source files are visible to every other source file within the same package. Based on this, we grouped Sysrv samples into eight groups. There can be minor differences between samples in the same group, but their main functionalities and structure are the same. To examine the package structures, we used <a href="https://github.com/goretk/redress" target="_blank" rel="noopener external noreferrer" data-wpel-link="external">Redress</a>, a tool for analyzing stripped Go binaries compiled with the Go compiler.</p>

<h4>Obfuscation</h4>
<p>Most malicious samples were packed with <a href="https://cujo.com/upx-anti-unpacking-techniques-in-iot-malware/" data-wpel-link="internal">vanilla </a>UPX, and during our analysis, we have not discovered any other packers being used by Sysrv. The malware authors didn’t use any kind of obfuscation for the earliest samples. Reading the code of the binaries was relatively easy thanks to the tools that help reverse engineer stripped Golang binaries. We’ve already mentioned using Redress, and we also used Ghidra for static analysis and several Ghidra scripts that our research team wrote to aid Golang analysis. Our write-up about how to reverse engineer Go binaries can be found <a href="/reverse-engineering-go-binaries-with-ghidra/" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-wpel-link="internal">here</a>. <strong>The first obfuscated sample appeared in April 2021, the only one that belongs to Group 8. </strong> This sample also had obfuscated Go packages and some function names.</p>
<figure id="attachment_140263" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-140263" style="width: 1908px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-140263" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/text-description-automatically-generated.png" alt="Obfuscated packages of the Sysrv botnet recognized by Redress" width="1908" height="374" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-140263" class="wp-caption-text">Obfuscated packages recognized by Redress</figcaption></figure>
<p>The obfuscation tool used is <strong>gobfuscate</strong>, a known utility for Go binaries:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://github.com/unixpickle/gobfuscate" target="_blank" rel="noopener external noreferrer" data-wpel-link="external">https://github.com/unixpickle/gobfuscate</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Currently, gobfuscate can manipulate package names, global variable and function names, type names, method names, and strings. Binaries released after that sample belong to Group 7. In these, some function names were obfuscated, but not the package names. Obfuscated names are used for the functions that are responsible for executing the various exploits.</p>

<h4>Mutex</h4>
<p>Mutex is a synchronization primitive. In malware, mutexes are mostly used to ensure that the malware is not deployed to the target system already. This ensures that only a single malware instance is running at a time, as multiple instances might hinder each other, leaving the initially deployed malicious process to wreak havoc all by itself. In Sysrv’s case, <strong>a check is implemented to see whether a hardcoded TCP port is open on the infected system</strong>. If it is open, the malware will not re-infect the system. The appropriate functions are within the <em>nu</em> (network utility) package in all samples. A function called <strong>IsPortOpen </strong>is responsible for checking whether a specific port is open, and this is called from one of the following four functions, based on the version of the binary:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>MutexPort</strong></li>
<li><strong>CreateMutex</strong></li>
<li><strong>NewMutex</strong></li>
<li><strong>SingleInstance</strong></li>
</ul>
<figure id="attachment_140266" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-140266" style="width: 1352px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-140266" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/word-image-9-1.png" alt="Port: 0xcb2d = 52013" width="1352" height="384" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-140266" class="wp-caption-text">Port: 0xcb2d = 52013</figcaption></figure>
<p>We have observed the following TCP port numbers being leveraged as Mutex ports:</p>
<ul>
<li>52013 (0xcb2d)</li>
<li>52014 (0xcb2e)</li>
<li>52015 (0xcb2f)</li>
<li>52016 (0xcb30)</li>
<li>52017 (0xcb31)</li>
<li>52018 (0xcb32)</li>
<li>52020 (0xcb34)</li>
<li>52021 (0xcb35)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Exploits in Sysrv</h2>
<p>Sysrv included a small set of exploits in its initial campaigns. Over time, as it was developed and transformed, Sysrv continually incorporated new exploits to spread more effectively. Sysrv is primarily targeting Linux and Windows servers, not IoT devices. Interestingly, we not only saw exploits being added to the code, but also some specific exploits undergoing several development stages. Sysrv’s developers updated some functions in multiple samples until they either reached a satisfying result or simply got rid of them. Some exploits were used only in one or two samples, while others proved useful and stuck around. The Ignition Remote Code Execution (RCE) exploit has the newest CVE number; it was published in January 2021 and was already used by Sysrv at the beginning of March.</p>

<h2>Sysrv’s Mining Operation</h2>
<p>The main goal of the Sysrv botnet is to mine the Monero cryptocurrency. Monero is getting more popular among malicious actors. There are several reasons why Monero is a popular choice for attackers. Most importantly, its transactions are more difficult to trace and it can be mined using the CPUs of ordinary computers (whereas other cryptocurrencies require special hardware, such as application-specific integrated circuits – ASICs). Even though Sysrv uses the open-source <a href="https://github.com/xmrig/xmrig" data-wpel-link="external" target="_blank" rel="external noopener noreferrer">XMRig</a> project to mine Monero, each group for Sysrv samples slightly differs in its crypto mining configuration. To see these differences, we extracted all miner modules and grouped them by their configuration.</p>
<h3>Case 1 – Embedded miner, command execution</h3>
<p>For the first sample, the miner was embedded in the binary using the <a href="https://github.com/go-bindata/go-bindata" data-wpel-link="external" target="_blank" rel="external noopener noreferrer">go-bindata</a> package. According to its documentation, “this package converts any file into manageable Go source code. Useful for embedding binary data into a go program. The file data is optionally gzip compressed before being converted to a raw byte slice.” The miner is embedded as a gzip file. <img decoding="async" class="wp-image-140267" src="https://cujo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/text-description-automatically-generated-3.png" alt="Sysrv cryptominer embedded" /> Once the embedded miner is extracted, it will be saved to the /tmp folder as network01. It will be executed with the following command:</p>
<pre>/tmp/network01 -B --donate-level 1 -o pool.minexmr.com:5555 -u 49dnvYkWkZNPrDj3KF8fR1BHLBfiVArU6Hu61N9gtrZWgbRptntwht5JUrXX1ZeofwPwC6fXNxPZfGjNEChXttwWE3WGURa.linux</pre>
