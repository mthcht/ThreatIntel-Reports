      <h1><span id="hs_cos_wrapper_name" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="text">Cybereason vs. Lorenz Ransomware</span></h1>
      <div class="cr-mln__post-author-share">
      <div id="hubspot-author_data" class="hubspot-editable cr-mln__post-meta" data-hubspot-form-id="author_data" data-hubspot-name="Blog Author">
        
          <span class="descriptor">Written By</span>

      </div>
    </div>
     
    </div>

        <div class="column is-7-fullhd is-7-desktop is-10-tablet is-10-mobile is-offset-1-fullhd is-offset-1-desktop is-offset-1-tablet is-offset-1-mobile">
          <span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><p>Lorenz is a ransomware strain observed first in February of 2021, and is believed to be a rebranding of the “.sZ40” ransomware that was discovered in October 2020. Lorenz targets organizations worldwide with customized attacks demanding hundreds of thousands of dollars, and <a href="https://en.secnews.gr/377356/langs-xtipithike-me-ransomware-alla-den-plirwse-ta-litra/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>even millions</span></a> in ransom fee.&nbsp;</p>
<!--more-->
<p>The group is targeting victims mostly in English-speaking countries, and according to their website, the group has published stolen data from more than 20 victims, although the estimated number of successful attacks is believed to be higher.</p>
<div class="hs-embed-wrapper" data-service="fast.wistia" data-script-embed="true" style="position: relative; overflow: hidden; width: 100%; height: auto; padding: 0px; max-width: 791px; max-height: 445px; min-width: 256px; display: block; margin: auto;"><div class="hs-embed-content-wrapper"><script src="https://fast.wistia.com/embed/medias/bh60zye5sj.jsonp" async></script><script src="https://fast.wistia.com/assets/external/E-v1.js" async></script><div class="wistia_responsive_padding" style="padding:56.25% 0 0 0;position:relative;"><div class="wistia_responsive_wrapper" style="height:100%;left:0;position:absolute;top:0;width:100%;"><span class="wistia_embed wistia_async_bh60zye5sj popover=true popoverAnimateThumbnail=true videoFoam=true" style="display:inline-block;height:100%;position:relative;width:100%">&nbsp;</span></div></div><script src="https://fast.wistia.net/assets/external/E-v1.js" async></script></div></div>
<p style="font-size: 16px; text-align: center;"><em>Cybereason detects and prevents Lorenz Ransomware</em></p>
<p><a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/meet-lorenz-a-new-ransomware-gang-targeting-the-enterprise/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>According reports</span></a>, Lorenz appears to be the same as ThunderCrypt ransomware observed all the way back in May 2017. However, it’s not clear if Lorenz was created by the same group or if the group purchased the source code of ThunderCrypt and created its own variant.</p>
<p>Shortly after Lorenz was discovered, the group faced a temporary problem after researchers published a free <a href="https://www.zdnet.com/article/lorenz-ransomware-attack-victims-can-now-retrieve-their-files-for-free-with-this-decryption-tool/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>decryptor</span></a> (<a href="https://www.nomoreransom.org/en/decryption-tools.html#Lorenz" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>download here</span></a>). The decryptor was released by the project <a href="https://www.nomoreransom.org/en/index.html" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>No More Ransom</span></a>, a joint project by law enforcement agencies including Europol's European Cybercrime Center.</p>
<p>It’s worth noting that that decrypter is very limited and only supports .docx, .pptx, .xlsx and .zip. In addition, in the test that we ran for both old and newer samples - the decrypter did not work and kept alerting that it doesn’t support the files (we tried encrypted docx files: &lt;name&gt;.docx.Lorenz.sz40 type):</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image1-Feb-01-2022-08-24-29-16-PM.png?width=1514&amp;name=image1-Feb-01-2022-08-24-29-16-PM.png" alt="image1-Feb-01-2022-08-24-29-16-PM" width="1514" loading="lazy" style="width: 1514px; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block;" srcset="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image1-Feb-01-2022-08-24-29-16-PM.png?width=757&amp;name=image1-Feb-01-2022-08-24-29-16-PM.png 757w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image1-Feb-01-2022-08-24-29-16-PM.png?width=1514&amp;name=image1-Feb-01-2022-08-24-29-16-PM.png 1514w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image1-Feb-01-2022-08-24-29-16-PM.png?width=2271&amp;name=image1-Feb-01-2022-08-24-29-16-PM.png 2271w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image1-Feb-01-2022-08-24-29-16-PM.png?width=3028&amp;name=image1-Feb-01-2022-08-24-29-16-PM.png 3028w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image1-Feb-01-2022-08-24-29-16-PM.png?width=3785&amp;name=image1-Feb-01-2022-08-24-29-16-PM.png 3785w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image1-Feb-01-2022-08-24-29-16-PM.png?width=4542&amp;name=image1-Feb-01-2022-08-24-29-16-PM.png 4542w" sizes="(max-width: 1514px) 100vw, 1514px"><span style="font-size: 16px;"><em><span style="color: #859da6;">Lorenz Leaks website</span></em></span></p>
<h2 style="font-size: 30px;"><span style="color: #000000;">Key Details</span></h2>
<ul>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Ever Evolving Ransomware</strong>: The Lorenz group keeps changing the ransomware capabilities and behavior frequently, making it customized to their victims.</li>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>High Severity</strong>: The <a href="/blog/authors/cybereason-nocturnus" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Cybereason Nocturnus Team</span></a> assesses the threat level as HIGH given the destructive potential of the attacks.</li>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Human Operated Attack: </strong>Prior to the deployment of the ransomware, the attackers attempt to infiltrate and move laterally throughout the organization, carrying out a fully-developed <a href="/_hcms/analytics/search/conversion?redirect=aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuY3liZXJlYXNvbi5jb20vYmxvZy9hbi1vcGVyYXRpb24tY2VudHJpYy1hcHByb2FjaC10by1yYW5zb21vcHMtcHJldmVudGlvbg%3D%3D&amp;ct=SEARCH&amp;pid=3354902&amp;cid=58384320625&amp;t=cmFuc29tb3Bz&amp;d=www.cybereason.com&amp;c=2&amp;c=6&amp;rp=1&amp;ab=false&amp;opcid=&amp;rs=UNKNOWN&amp;hs-expires=1675276449&amp;hs-version=1&amp;hs-signature=APUk-v6hRcsS8hLIu2WnSKH6cJTTTko9iQ" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><em><span>RansomOps </span></em></a>attack.</li>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Interesting Way of Leaking Data:</strong> The Lorenz group has a few steps in their leaking data process. From selling it to other threat actors, releasing password-protected RAR archives containing the victim's data, and also selling DBs and access to internal networks.</li>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Detected and Prevented</strong>: <a href="/platform" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>The Cybereason XDR Platform</span></a> fully detects and prevents the Lorenz ransomware.&nbsp;</li>
</ul>
<h2 style="font-size: 30px;"><span style="color: #000000;">Breaking Down the Attack</span></h2>
<h3 style="font-size: 24px;"><span style="color: #434343;">Not A Typical Spear-Phishing Attack</span></h3>
<p>The Lorenz operators put a lot of effort into their attacks. They study their target’s employees, suppliers and partners. This way, the Lorenz group can even go from one, already compromised victim, to another. The knowledge they have collected is used to customize the attack specifically for the target.&nbsp;</p>
<p>In a <a href="https://www.zdnet.com/article/this-company-was-hit-with-ransomware-but-didnt-have-to-pay-up-heres-how-they-did-it/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>reported incident</span></a>, the attackers used one compromised victim to “jump” to another. The group gained access to the network via a phishing email, but not just any phishing email. The group, after doing their research on the target, sent the email from a legitimate email account of a real employee at a supplier that they'd already been compromised. This way the email appears to be legitimate and increases the chances of falling to the scam.</p>
<p>Then the attackers trick employees into installing an application that provides the attackers with full access to the network, including the employees’ email, even after they reset their passwords. <a href="https://news.sophos.com/en-us/2021/10/28/the-top-10-ways-ransomware-operators-ramp-up-the-pressure-to-pay/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>In some cases</span></a>, the attackers even used the compromised email accounts to email the IT, legal, and cyber insurance teams working with the targeted organization to threaten further attacks if they didn’t pay.</p>
<h3 style="font-size: 24px;"><span style="color: #434343;">Downloading the Ransomware</span></h3>
<p>After gaining an initial foothold in the network, the attackers start to perform reconnaissance commands, move laterally within the network, and collect sensitive data including credentials, file, databases and emails.</p>
<p>The main goal for the attackers when moving laterally is to compromise a domain controller and obtain domain administrator credentials. This allows them to perform additional activities, and later on selling access to the compromised network.</p>
<p>Since the Lorenz group customize the attack for the target, we have observed different binaries of Lorenz that have different behavior. This can also point to the fact that the Lorenz group continues to update the ransomware, even if that means to create changes frequently.</p>
<h3 style="font-size: 24px;"><span style="color: #434343;">Ransomware Capabilities</span></h3>
<p>Since the ransomware binary files are customized for nearly every attack,, there are different behaviors and capabilities observed in different samples, some of them were seen used combined. Some of the capabilities were observed only in older versions, and some made a comeback in the newer and then disappeared again.&nbsp;</p>
<p>To put things into order, following are the main behavior and capabilities observed among the ransomware binaries:</p>
<h4 style="font-size: 20px;"><span style="color: #666666;">Deleting the Shadow Copies</span></h4>
<p>Some of the Lorenz binaries observed used the well known vssadmin command to delete the virtual shadow copies of the system. <a href="https://docs.microsoft.com/en-us/windows-server/administration/windows-commands/vssadmin" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Vssadmin.exe</span></a> is a command-line tool that manages Volume Shadow Copy Service (VSS), which captures and copies stable images for backup on running systems.</p>
<p>Ransomware commonly uses vssadmin.exe to delete shadow copies and other backups of files before encrypting the files themselves. This is another way to ensure that the victim will be forced to pay to decrypt the valuable files when they can neither be decrypted or retrieved from VSS.</p>
<p>Lorenz creates a scheduled task whose name starts with “sz40” and then sets it to run Vssadmin with the following command line. After running, the scheduled task is deleted.</p>
<p><em>cmd.exe /c schtasks /Create /F /RU System /SC ONLOGON /TN </em><strong><em>sz403</em></strong><em> /TR "vssadmin Delete Shadows /For=C:" &amp;SCHTASKS /run /TN </em><strong><em>sz403</em></strong><em>&amp;SCHTASKS /Delete /TN </em><strong><em>sz403</em></strong><em> /F</em></p>

<h4 style="font-size: 20px;"><span style="color: #666666;">Creating a New Boot Entry</span></h4>
<p>One unique behavior observed in some of the Lorenz binary is the creation of another boot entry for possibly misleading purposes. A boot entry is a set of options that defines a load configuration for an operating system or bootable program. It is possible to have multiple boot entries for an operating system, each with a different set of boot parameters.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Lorenz uses the command utility bcdedit to copy the existing boot entry and modify it, which is the most common way to create a new boot entry. But it does one strange thing. The /timeout operator is set to 100,000 seconds, which is about 27 (!) hours.</p>
<p>By doing so, the system waits 27 hours before the boot manager selects the default entry if the user doesn’t choose manually. Since Lorenz changes the description of the boot entries to “Lorenz Encrypt System”, the user can be misled that the operating system is compromised entirely. In addition, if it is a system that operates without user interaction or that the system is not in the network and it’s impossible to connect, the system will not load the OS for 27 hours.</p>
<p><em>cmd.exe /c </em><a href="https://docs.microsoft.com/en-us/windows-hardware/manufacture/desktop/bcdedit-command-line-options?view=windows-11"><em><span>bcdedit</span></em></a><em> /copy {current} /d "Lorenz Encrypt System" &amp; bcdedit /set {current} description "Lorenz Encrypt System" &amp; bcdedit /timeout 100000 &amp;&amp; ipconfig</em></p>

<h3 style="font-size: 24px;"><span style="color: #434343;">An Interesting Way of Leaking Stolen Data</span></h3>
<p>Lorenz has created a relatively unique extortion technique. After stealing files, emails, credentials and databases from victims, the group threatens to publish them in their data leaks website. When Lorenz publishes data, they do things a bit differently compared to other ransomware gangs.</p>
<p>First, Lorenz makes the data available for sale to other threat actors, hackers or possible competitors. After a while, they start releasing password-protected RAR archives containing the victim's data. If no ransom is paid, and the data is not purchased, Lorenz releases the password for the RAR archives containing the data leak so that they are publicly available to anyone who downloads the files.</p>
<p>Beside giving access to the stolen data, Lorenz, in order to maximize profit, sell access to the internal network they have compromised. <a href="https://www.zdnet.com/article/ransomware-operators-buy-network-access-from-the-underground-to-speed-up-infection/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>This trend is starting to gain popularity</span></a> among other ransomware gangs as well, due to the understanding that for some threat actors, access to the networks could be more valuable than the data itself:</p>
