      <h1><span id="hs_cos_wrapper_name" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="text">Cybereason vs. Prometheus Ransomware</span></h1>
      <div class="cr-mln__post-author-share">
      <div id="hubspot-author_data" class="hubspot-editable cr-mln__post-meta" data-hubspot-form-id="author_data" data-hubspot-name="Blog Author">
        
          <span class="descriptor">Written By</span>
          <p><span class="author">Cybereason Nocturnus</span></p>
         
      </div>
    </div>
     
    </div>
    
    <!-- Sticky Author and Social Box -->
    
    <!-- END Sticky Author and Social Box -->
      <div class="container-is-blog columns is-multiline page-center cr-mln__blog-post--body">
        
        <div class="column is-7-fullhd is-7-desktop is-10-tablet is-10-mobile is-offset-1-fullhd is-offset-1-desktop is-offset-1-tablet is-offset-1-mobile">
          <span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><p>Prometheus is a relatively new variant of the <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/details/win.hakbit"><span>Thanos</span></a> ransomware that is operated independently by the Prometheus group, and was first observed in February of 2021. In just a short period of time, Prometheus caused a lot of damage, and breached over 40 companies.</p>
<!--more-->
<h3 style="font-size: 30px;"><span style="color: #000000;">Key Findings</span></h3>
<ul style="font-size: 18px;">
<li aria-level="1"><strong>High Severity</strong>: The Cybereason Nocturnus Team assesses the threat level as HIGH given the destructive potential of the attacks.</li>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Human Operated Attack: </strong>Prior to the deployment of the ransomware, the attackers attempt to infiltrate and move laterally throughout the organization, carrying out a fully-developed attack operation.</li>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Shared Builder: </strong>The Prometheus group, as well as other threat actors, used the Thanos builder to build and customize their ransomware.</li>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Group of REvil?:</strong> Prometheus ransomware branding themselves as part of the REvil group, probably in an attempt to piggyback on the fame of one of the most infamous - and successful - ransomware groups.</li>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Detected and Prevented</strong>: <a href="/platform" rel="noopener"><span>The Cybereason Defense Platform</span></a> fully detects and prevents the Prometheus ransomware:</li>
</ul>
<div class="hs-embed-wrapper" data-service="fast.wistia" data-script-embed="true" style="position: relative; overflow: hidden; width: 100%; height: auto; padding: 0px; max-width: 776px; max-height: 436px; min-width: 256px; display: block; margin: auto;"><div class="hs-embed-content-wrapper"><script src="https://fast.wistia.com/embed/medias/l8llszmdyr.jsonp" async>&nbsp;</script><script src="https://fast.wistia.com/assets/external/E-v1.js" async></script><div class="wistia_responsive_padding" style="padding:56.25% 0 0 0;position:relative;"><div class="wistia_responsive_wrapper" style="height:100%;left:0;position:absolute;top:0;width:100%;"><span class="wistia_embed wistia_async_l8llszmdyr popover=true popoverAnimateThumbnail=true videoFoam=true" style="display:inline-block;height:100%;position:relative;width:100%">&nbsp;</span></div></div><script src="https://fast.wistia.net/assets/external/E-v1.js" async></script></div></div>
<p style="text-align: center; font-size: 16px;"><em>Cybereason Detects and Blocks Prometheus Ransomware</em></p>
<p>Like other prominent ransomware groups, such as the <a href="/blog/cybereason-vs-darkside-ransomware" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>DarkSide</span></a> group, Prometheus follows the RaaS business model and operates as a professional enterprise where it refers to its victims as “customers,” and communicates with them using a customer service ticketing system.</p>
<p>In addition, Prometheus follows the <a href="/blog/rise-of-double-extortion-shines-spotlight-on-ransomware-prevention" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>double extortion trend</span></a> and hosts a leak site, where it has a “hall of shame” for victims and posts stolen data for sale. The names of the victims are posted on the website even before the victims decide whether to pay or not, either under the status “waiting for the company decision” or “company paid, data is not for sale.”</p>
<p>When it comes to the affected industries and regions, the group seems to attack almost indiscriminately. According to their website, the group claims to have breached over 40 organizations f<span style="color: #3c4043;">rom different industries/sectors</span>. Among their victims observed were companies in the following industries: consulting, oil and gas, financial, media, governments, advertising, manufacturing, retail, food, hotels, manufacturing, insurance, transportation, and medical services. The regions affected are South America, US, UK, Middle-East, UAE, Asia and Europe.</p>
<p>It’s also interesting to note that some victims appear to be on the list more than once, but attacked in different time periods. Since those victims had paid, it’s unclear at this point if it’s by mistake or that the group has attacked the same victim more than once before or even after paying.&nbsp;</p>
<p>A recent Cybereason report titled <a href="/ebook-ransomware-the-true-cost-to-business" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><em><span>Ransomware: The True Cost to Business</span></em></a>, found that 80% of organizations that paid a ransom were hit by a second attack, and almost half of those were hit by the same threat group.</p>
<h3 style="font-size: 30px;"><span style="color: #000000;">Group of REvil?</span></h3>
<p>Up until June 14th, the operators of <a href="/hs-search-results?term=revil&amp;type=BLOG_POST&amp;type=LISTING_PAGE" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Prometheus claimed to be part of the notorious REvil ransomware group</span></a>, and even mentioned them in their logo. On June 15th, the group decided to delete the name of REvil from their logo, and remove any potential relation to the group.&nbsp;</p>
<p>It is worth noting that there hasn’t been strong or conclusive evidence of a real connection or collaboration between the two groups and the <a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/prometheus-ransomware/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>assumption</span></a> is that the Prometheus group was most likely just using the name and reputation of REvil to increase the likelihood of ransom payments.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Although it is unclear why the group has decided to remove the name of REvil from their logo, it’s interesting to look at the timing. The REvil group was just attributed to <a href="/blog/cybereason-vs-revil-ransomware-the-kaseya-chronicles" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>another major attack infecting potentially thousands of companies</span></a> by way of an exploit against Kaseya VSA remote management service which propagated ransomware through the IT service provider’s Managed Services Provider network, and a recent <a href="https://www.npr.org/2021/06/03/1002819883/revil-a-notorious-ransomware-gang-was-behind-jbs-cyberattack-the-fbi-says" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>attack against the global food company JBS</span></a> which drew attention to them from the US authorities.&nbsp;</p>
<p>In May, it was the <a href="/_hcms/analytics/search/conversion?redirect=aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuY3liZXJlYXNvbi5jb20vYmxvZy9jeWJlcmVhc29uLXZzLWRhcmtzaWRlLXJhbnNvbXdhcmU%3D&amp;ct=SEARCH&amp;pid=3354902&amp;cid=44194973264&amp;t=ZGFya3NpZGU%3D&amp;d=www.cybereason.com&amp;c=2&amp;c=6&amp;rp=1&amp;ab=false&amp;opcid=&amp;rs=UNKNOWN&amp;hs-expires=1657129691&amp;hs-version=1&amp;hs-signature=APUk-v4w7sXWik9kxyVU_-9L7cJ5dXS_tw" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>DarkSide group that made big headlines after attacking the Colonial Pipeline network</span></a>, which caused the US authorities to take actions that eventually led to the DarkSide group shutting down their operations (allegedly). Ransomware operators will usually try to evade such&nbsp; unwanted publicity because of their <a href="/blog/inside-the-darkside-ransomware-attack-on-colonial-pipeline" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>fear of retaliation from law enforcement agencies</span></a>.</p>

<h3 style="font-size: 30px;"><span style="color: #000000;">Prometheus Ransomware Analysis</span></h3>
<p>The binary generated by the builder is an obfuscated .NET executable that consists of a main function that is responsible to decode base64 strings in memory and pass them to the other functions.</p>
<p>Among the functionality observed by the malware is the ability to enumerate processes and manipulate with them, changing registry keys, setting persistence, downloading additional files, collecting information about the machine and more:</p>

<h3 style="font-size: 18px;">&nbsp;</h3>
<h3 style="font-size: 30px; line-height: 1.75;"><span style="color: #434343;">Spreading Across The Network</span></h3>
<p>Once successfully executed, Prometheus will try to spread in the network using different methods. First, it will “prepare the ground” by performing some reconnaissance commands that include running “Net view” and “arp -a”, followed by a ping sweep to check the connections and potential machines to infect.</p>
<p>Then it continues with changing local firewall rules, downloading <a href="https://docs.microsoft.com/en-us/sysinternals/downloads/psexec"><span>Psexec</span></a>/<a href="https://www.poweradmin.com/paexec/"><span>Paexec</span></a> and in some cases <a href="https://github.com/M00nRise/ProcessHider"><span>ProcessHider</span></a> as well, and enabling SMB1 protocol - most likely to exploit a vulnerability for spreading using SMB, much like as <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EternalBlue"><span>EternalBlue:</span></a></p>
<p style="padding-left: 40px;"><em>netsh advfirewall firewall set rule group=\"Network Discovery\" new enable=Yes</em></p>
<p style="padding-left: 40px;"><em>netsh advfirewall firewall set rule group="File and Printer Sharing" new enable=Yes</em></p>
<p style="padding-left: 40px;"><em>powershell.exe &amp; Enable-WindowsOptionalFeature -Online -FeatureName SMB1Protocol</em></p>
<p>After that, the malware <span style="font-size: 10px; color: #3c4043;">begins </span>the spreading process. Initially it will try an easy way using the “Net use” command to try to copy itself into shared folders. Then it will run PsExec/PaExec remotely to execute the binary. On other occasions it will try to exploit a SMB vulnerability to spread:</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><span style="color: #000000;"><img src="https://lh6.googleusercontent.com/zhwo9GNQp4vn0VpK6nWjqEt78stCp9kPHuY9qSfspq6612gB_Ian0hlaowEimWlt7FuBBApgEADID_VvsB_Eq54Wv_Fi45q7cjvkY3OH0HuUMWhWLbzmsfipGs6ya9zheqAF9QmH" width="1049" loading="lazy" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block; width: 1049px;"></span></p>
<p style="text-align: center; font-size: 16px;"><em><span style="color: #859da6;">A VirusTotal graph showing the connection between Prometheus binaries and Psexec, Paexec and ProcessHider</span></em></p>
<h3 style="font-size: 30px; line-height: 1.75;"><span style="color: #434343;">Encrypting The Files</span></h3>
<p>After ensuring successful execution of the malware and deleting backup files, Prometheus begins it’s encryption routine. First, it will search for files matching extensions that were passed in build time. Those extensions vary from Microsoft Office files, images, scripts, archives, music, videos, and different database files:</p>
<p><img src="https://lh5.googleusercontent.com/jPbXQVkHdarAu7hFued7-bmEUyYo2TU3av7p5TxcAjnY6rivRuCVYPxoMdZYEHaA5l62v7652deSJtZmD9H1Sdj-TSEsthmv8uXkM6fzJGGh0cLCyLMccXNMNZsqiUzw6MOOxJyu" width="1048" loading="lazy" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block; width: 1048px;"></p>
<p style="text-align: center; font-size: 16px;"><em><span style="color: #859da6;">Searching for DB files</span></em></p>
<p>The builder also supports a “fast mode” of encryption where only a portion of each file is encrypted. When this mode is enabled during build time, the ransomware encrypts a preconfigured amount of data from each file and overwrites the file with the encrypted content. This technique saves Prometheus time and shortens the entire encryption time, which can take just seconds up to a few minutes, depending on the number of files on the targeted machine.</p>
<p>Prometheus appends a custom extension that is unique for every executable and in some variants even contains the name of the victim:</p>
<p><img src="https://lh4.googleusercontent.com/DAQ0E1o_5ozImG55nnT6kGM6ea-lUYua8V1b_4v9ncHHi7wxvQ5NFNREeZt8dhDaNzWSvYCeaydAUvuHAinXrcjrBBSvqIOHqqiCUymii1GwUJ4JTPQt8pPxc6kbQkjQML-z7nIV" width="847" loading="lazy" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block; width: 847px;"></p>
<p style="text-align: center; font-size: 16px;"><em><span style="color: #859da6;">Custom extension appended to the encrypted files</span></em></p>
<p>Finally, Prometheus drops a ransom note in .hta and text format, and presents the .hta file to the end user:</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://lh5.googleusercontent.com/wWV2jMM77kDyfc2SUMPCLvf1Mvl6Km9TklIegnV8VobFUqmuwG5JCNO8rhlH6vc_GxOiPrDbShB9o093VI8ZqTj6Khfhx-JM15kH3UAmPKl6YOUo-S3TmaiRKZSwXUV9dmMNyC0c" width="1029" loading="lazy" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block; width: 1029px;"></p>
<p style="text-align: center; font-size: 16px;"><em><span style="color: #859da6;">.hta file ransom note</span></em></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://lh6.googleusercontent.com/GJ7FN6fMnuFmQTEQxRos5fNwT1QMpCFkq1dc1lyCxb6qGlhp70dFRrnaa9VM_yjld-WLtSLbFIq5qL1f-BFWXa1lcHn_pxMITTDvQo2vgljc9wbox3HVk2sU6I3sr-Oui3VEdL1Z" width="832" loading="lazy" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block; width: 832px;"></p>
<p style="text-align: center; font-size: 16px;"><em><span style="color: #859da6;">.txt file ransom note</span></em></p>
<h2 style="font-size: 30px;"><span style="color: #000000;">Cybereason Detection and Prevention</span></h2>
<p>Ransomware attacks are on the rise. A recently released report by Cybereason, titled <a href="/ebook-ransomware-the-true-cost-to-business" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><em><span>Ransomware: The True Cost to Business</span></em></a>, detailed how malicious actors are fine-tuning their ransomware campaign tactics and how both the frequency and severity of successful ransomware attacks have tremendous impact on victim organizations and their ability to conduct business.</p>
<p>The <a href="/platform" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Cybereason Defense Platform</span></a> is able to prevent the execution of the Prometheus Ransomware using multi-layer protection that detects and blocks ransomware with threat intelligence, machine learning, and next-gen antivirus (NGAV) capabilities. Additionally, when the Anti-Ransomware feature is enabled, behavioral detection techniques in the platform are able to detect and prevent any attempt to encrypt files and automatically generates a <a href="/platform#malop" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Malop</span></a><span style="font-size: 12px;"><sup>TM</sup></span> for it with the complete attack narrative:</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://lh6.googleusercontent.com/sreC7eYUSiYt2ZHyX09Md-ZVtj9jnII60lrrgnjLbk4_baqi2KBVPe_dmR_bBFZV6ymovFoAydrXHYkQXLQZUm_6BWR86UAYbGO-XNgyaXk2YBHzP3Iz_DMYzh_-LaPQuRsf8e02" width="1052" loading="lazy" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block; width: 1052px;"></p>
