      <h1><span id="hs_cos_wrapper_name" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="text">DeadRinger: Exposing Chinese Threat Actors Targeting Major Telcos</span></h1>
      <div class="cr-mln__post-author-share">
      <div id="hubspot-author_data" class="hubspot-editable cr-mln__post-meta" data-hubspot-form-id="author_data" data-hubspot-name="Blog Author">
        
          <span class="descriptor">Written By</span>
          <p><span class="author">Cybereason Nocturnus</span></p>
         
      </div>
    </div>
     
    </div>
    
    <!-- Sticky Author and Social Box -->
    
    <!-- END Sticky Author and Social Box -->
      <div class="container-is-blog columns is-multiline page-center cr-mln__blog-post--body">
        
        <div class="column is-7-fullhd is-7-desktop is-10-tablet is-10-mobile is-offset-1-fullhd is-offset-1-desktop is-offset-1-tablet is-offset-1-mobile">
          <span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><a id="executive-summary" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<p>Following the discovery of Hafnium attacks targeting Microsoft Exchange vulnerabilities, the Cybereason <a href="/blog/authors/cybereason-nocturnus" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Nocturnus </span></a>and <a href="/services/incident-response" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Incident Response</span></a> teams proactively hunted for various threat actors trying to leverage similar techniques in-the-wild. In the beginning of 2021, the Cybereason Nocturnus Team investigated clusters of intrusions detected targeting the telecommunications industry across Southeast Asia. During the investigation, three clusters of activity were identified and showed significant connections to known threat actors, all suspected to be operating on behalf of Chinese state interests.</p>
<p><span>The report comes on the heels of the <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2021/07/19/us/politics/microsoft-hacking-china-biden.html" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Biden administration’s public rebuke of China’s Ministry of State Security for the recent HAFNIUM attacks</a> that exploited vulnerabilities in unpatched Microsoft Exchange Servers and put thousands of organizations worldwide at risk. Exploitation of these same vulnerabilities were central to the success of the attacks detailed in this research.</span></p>
<!--more-->
<p>Based on our analysis, we assess that the goal of the attackers behind these intrusions was to gain and maintain continuous access to telecommunication providers and to facilitate cyber espionage by collecting sensitive information, compromising high-profile business assets such as the billing servers that contain <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Call_detail_record#:~:text=A%20call%20detail%20record%20(CDR,through%20that%20facility%20or%20device." rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Call Detail Record</span></a> (CDR) data, as well as key network components such as the Domain Controllers, Web Servers and Microsoft Exchange servers.</p>

<p style="text-align: center; font-size: 16px;"><em>DeadRinger Overview Video</em></p>
<ul>
<li style="list-style-type: none;">
<ul style="font-size: 18px;">
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Cluster A</strong>: Assessed to be operated by <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/groups/G0093/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Soft Cell</span></a>, an activity group in operation since 2012, previously attacking Telcos in multiple regions including Southeast Asia, which was first <a href="/blog/operation-soft-cell-a-worldwide-campaign-against-telecommunications-providers" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>discovered by Cybereason in 2019</span></a>. We assess with a high level of confidence that the Soft Cell activity group is operating in the interest of China. The activity around this cluster started in 2018 and continued through Q1 2021.</li>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Cluster B: </strong>Assessed to be operated by the <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/groups/G0019/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Naikon APT</span></a> threat actor, a highly active cyber espionage group in operation since 2010 which mainly targets ASEAN countries. The Naikon APT group was <a href="https://cdn2.hubspot.net/hubfs/454298/Project_CAMERASHY_ThreatConnect_Copyright_2015.pdf" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>previously attributed</span></a> to the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Chinese People’s Liberation Army’s</span></a> (PLA) Chengdu Military Region Second Technical Reconnaissance Bureau (<a href="https://cdn2.hubspot.net/hubfs/454298/Project_CAMERASHY_ThreatConnect_Copyright_2015.pdf" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Military Unit Cover Designator 78020</span></a>). The activity around this cluster was first observed in Q4 2020 and continued through Q1 2021.</li>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Cluster C: </strong>A “mini-cluster” characterized by a unique OWA backdoor that was deployed across multiple Microsoft Exchange and IIS servers. Analysis of the backdoor shows significant code similarities with a previously documented backdoor observed being used in the operation dubbed <a href="http://www.erai.com/CustomUploads/ca/wp/2015_12_wp_operation_iron_tiger.pdf" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Iron Tiger</span></a>, which was attributed to a Chinese threat actor tracked by various researchers as <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/groups/G0027/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Group-3390</span></a> (APT27 / Emissary Panda). The activity around this cluster was observed between 2017 and Q1 2021.<br><br></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p><img src="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/deadringer-social-02%20(1).png?width=5000&amp;name=deadringer-social-02%20(1).png" alt="deadringer clusters" width="5000" loading="lazy" style="width: 5000px;" srcset="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/deadringer-social-02%20(1).png?width=2500&amp;name=deadringer-social-02%20(1).png 2500w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/deadringer-social-02%20(1).png?width=5000&amp;name=deadringer-social-02%20(1).png 5000w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/deadringer-social-02%20(1).png?width=7500&amp;name=deadringer-social-02%20(1).png 7500w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/deadringer-social-02%20(1).png?width=10000&amp;name=deadringer-social-02%20(1).png 10000w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/deadringer-social-02%20(1).png?width=12500&amp;name=deadringer-social-02%20(1).png 12500w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/deadringer-social-02%20(1).png?width=15000&amp;name=deadringer-social-02%20(1).png 15000w" sizes="(max-width: 5000px) 100vw, 5000px"></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><span style="font-size: 16px;"><em>The correlation between the three clusters</em></span></p>
<p>It is noteworthy to mention that the Cybereason Nocturnus Team also observed an interesting overlap among the three clusters: In some instances, all three clusters of activity were observed in the same target environment, around the same timeframe, and even on the same endpoints. At this point, there is not enough information to determine with certainty the nature of this overlap -- namely, whether these clusters represent the work of three different threat actors working independently, or whether these clusters represent the work of three different teams operating on behalf of a single threat actor. Regardless, we do offer several plausible hypotheses that might account for this observation.&nbsp;</p>
<p>We hope that the information provided in this report will assist in shedding light on further related intrusions, and as time goes by more information will be made available with regard to the connection between the clusters, the suspected threat actors, and the relationship between them.&nbsp;</p>
<a id="key-findings" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h2 style="text-align: left; font-size: 36px; font-weight: bold;">Deadringer: Key Findings</h2>
<ul>
<ul style="font-size: 18px;">
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Adaptive, Persistent and Evasive: </strong>The highly adaptive attackers worked diligently to obscure their activity and maintain persistence on the infected systems, dynamically responding to mitigation attempts after having evaded security efforts since at least 2017, an indication that the targets are of great value to the attackers.</li>
</ul>
</ul>
<ul style="font-size: 18px;">
<li style="list-style-type: none;">
<ul>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Microsoft Exchange Vulnerabilities Exploited: </strong>Similar to the HAFNIUM attacks, the threat actors exploited recently disclosed vulnerabilities in Microsoft Exchange Servers to gain access to the targeted networks. They then proceeded to compromise critical network assets such as Domain Controllers (DC) and billing systems which contain highly sensitive information like Call Detail Record (CDR) data, allowing them access to the sensitive communications of anyone using the affected telecoms’ services.&nbsp;</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<ul style="font-size: 18px;">
<li aria-level="1"><strong>High Value Espionage Targets: </strong>Based on previous findings from the <a href="/blog/operation-soft-cell-a-worldwide-campaign-against-telecommunications-providers" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Operation Soft Cell Report</span></a> Cybereason published in 2019, as well as other published analysis of operations conducted by these threat actors, it is assessed that the telecoms were compromised in order to facilitate espionage against select targets. These targets are likely to include corporations, political figures, government officials, law enforcement agencies, political activists and dissident factions of interest to the Chinese government.</li>
</ul>
</ul>
<ul>
<li style="list-style-type: none;">
<ul style="font-size: 18px;">
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Operating in the Interest of China: </strong>Three distinct clusters of attacks have varying degrees of connection to APT groups Soft Cell, Naikon and Group-3390 -- all known to operate in the interest of the Chinese government. Overlaps in attacker TTPs across the clusters are evidence of a likely connection between the threat actors, supporting the assessment that each group was tasked with parallel objectives in monitoring the communications of specific high value targets under the direction of a centralized coordinating body aligned with Chinese state interests.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>

<a id="cluster-a" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h3 style="text-align: left; font-size: 36px; font-weight: bold;">Cluster A: Suspected Soft Cell Activity (2018-2021+)</h3>
<p>Following up on Cybereason’s <a href="/blog/operation-soft-cell-a-worldwide-campaign-against-telecommunications-providers" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>discovery of the Soft Cell</span></a> activity group in 2019, the Nocturnus Team continued to track the group’s activity and related breaches, which led us to find evidence that the group continued its operation - targeting Telcos in various regions, especially in Southeast Asian countries, all the way to mid-2021.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Similar to our 2019 report, the attackers practiced the “Low and Slow“ approach, allowing them to maintain access and conduct their activity clandestinely without alerting the end users nor the security teams. Based on our investigation, this cluster consisted of four main phases, with earliest signs of intrusion going back to 2018.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Each phase of the operation demonstrates the attackers’ adaptiveness in how they responded to various mitigation efforts, changing infrastructure, toolsets, and techniques while attempting to become more stealthy. Though the attackers did change some of their tools and techniques since their exposure, their core modus operandi and tools still seem to be inline with our previous findings.&nbsp;</p>
<p>From the telemetry and forensic evidence available to us, it appears that the attackers gained initial access to the network by exploiting several vulnerabilities in Microsoft Exchange servers, including the <a href="https://msrc-blog.microsoft.com/2021/03/05/microsoft-exchange-server-vulnerabilities-mitigations-march-2021/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>recent set of vulnerabilities</span></a> published by Microsoft in March 2021. It is noteworthy to mention that it appears the attackers had exploited the recent Microsoft Exchange vulnerabilities long before they became publicly known:</p>

<h3 style="font-size: 36px; font-weight: normal;">Phase 1: Key Detected Activity&nbsp;</h3>
<p>Each phase starts with the exploitation of several Microsoft Exchange server vulnerabilities which grant the attackers an initial foothold on the targeted network, ultimately allowing them to compromise additional assets. Following the exploitation, the attackers installed the <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/software/S0020/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>China Chopper WebShell</span></a> on the compromised server and used it to perform a variety of tasks at each phase. In the first phase, the attackers mainly focused on reconnaissance activity, mapping out the network and identifying critical assets. In addition, they deployed other tools that allowed them to harvest credentials, move laterally in the network, and exfiltrate data:</p>

<p>In addition, the attackers used different scripts for reconnaissance. <p>The credentials were sent back to the attackers and were used for lateral movement and privilege escalation.</p>
<a id="phase-1-lateral-movement" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h3 style="font-size: 30px; font-weight: normal;"><span style="color: #434343;">Lateral Movement</span></h3>
<p>The attackers used different methods and tools to move laterally to different endpoints on the network, such as Cobalt Strike implants, <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1047/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>WMI</span></a> and Net Use.&nbsp;</p>

<h3 style="font-size: 30px; font-weight: normal;"><span style="color: #666666;">WMI and Net Use</span></h3>
<p>The attackers used the command “net use” to configure connections to shared resources on the network, and to copy their tools to different systems. After the tools were copied, the attackers were able to execute them remotely using WMI and by creating scheduled tasks remotely to run them:</p>

<h3 style="font-size: 30px; font-weight: normal;"><span style="color: #434343;">Data Exfiltration</span></h3>
<p>In an attempt to hide the contents of the stolen data, the threat actors <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1560/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>compressed and password-protected the data</span></a> using the <a href="https://www.win-rar.com/start.html?&amp;L=0" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>WinRAR</span></a> tool. The RAR files were then placed in the C:\users\SUPPORT_388945a0\Documents folder. This folder belongs to a <a href="https://www.serverbrain.org/active-directory-infrastructure-2003/builtin-domain-user-accounts.html" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>built-in user account (SUPPORT_388945a0)</span></a> that is used for help and support service, which is disabled by default but was purposefully enabled by the attacker. The data was then exfiltrated using the China Chopper WebShell.&nbsp;</p>
<p>It is also interesting to mention that the nefarious use of this specific account (SUPPORT_388945a0) was previously seen with the <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/groups/G0022/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Chinese APT3</span></a> and the <a href="https://gist.github.com/MSAdministrator/7a61025263e279a740835da4b205e6d0" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Iranian Leafminer</span></a> threat actors:</p>

<a id="phase-1-pcshare-backdoor" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h3 style="font-size: 30px; font-weight: normal;"><span style="color: #434343;">Maintaining Foothold: PcShare Backdoor</span></h3>
<p>Aside from the China Chopper WebShell, the attackers relied heavily on a known backdoor named <a href="https://github.com/LiveMirror/pcshare" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>PcShare</span></a>, whose <a href="https://github.com/LiveMirror/pcshare" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>code is publicly available</span></a> and was reported being mostly used by <a href="https://blogs.blackberry.com/en/2019/09/pcshare-backdoor-attacks-targeting-windows-users-with-fakenarrator-malware" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Chinese threat actors attacking Southeast Asian countries</span></a>. PcShare has the following capabilities:</p>
<ul style="font-size: 18px;">
<li aria-level="1">Controlling the file system</li>
<li aria-level="1">Manipulation of system services</li>
<li aria-level="1">Uploading and downloading files&nbsp;</li>
<li aria-level="1">Process manipulation</li>
<li aria-level="1">Manipulating the Windows Registry</li>
<li aria-level="1">Executing arbitrary commands using Windows CMD Shell</li>
<li aria-level="1">Rebooting/shutting down the system</li>
<li aria-level="1">Display message boxes to the user<br><br></li>
</ul>
<p>PcShare was executed via a Loader DLL (NvSmartMax.dll) and a Payload (NvSmartMax.dat) attempting to masquerade as a legitimate module by NVIDIA named “NvSmartMax.dll”: “NVIDIA Smart Maximise Helper Host” application (part of NVIDIA GPU graphics driver).</p>
<p>In most cases, the attackers used the legitimate nvSmarEx.exe to <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1574/002/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>side-load</span></a> the loader DLL (“NvSmartMax.dll”). The loader then decrypts the PcShare core payload (“NvSmartMax.dat”) placed on the same directory. Interesting to note, the payload .dat file that was used during this attack has the exact same hash mentioned in a <a href="https://blogs.blackberry.com/en/2019/09/pcshare-backdoor-attacks-targeting-windows-users-with-fakenarrator-malware" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>report</span></a> by BlackBerry from 2019, detailing the same execution technique used to stealthily load PcShare into memory.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Cybereason observed that in addition to what was reported by BlackBerry, the NvSmartMax.dll was also executed directly via rundll32.exe in certain instances:</p>

<h3 style="font-size: 36px; font-weight: normal;">Phase 2: Changes in TTPs</h3>
<p>In addition to the activities performed in the first phase, such as reconnaissance activity or the use of PcShare and WMI - the attackers also used tools that were not used by them in the previous phase.</p>
<p>The attackers used a tool called <a href="https://www.joeware.net/freetools/tools/lg/index.htm" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Local Group</span></a>, which is useful for adding and enumerating users in a domain, and a different Mimikatz, this time using a DLL search order hijacking technique.</p>

<a id="cluster-b-naikon-apt" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h3 style="text-align: left; font-size: 36px; font-weight: bold;">Cluster B: Suspected Naikon APT Activity</h3>
<p>During our investigation, as more evidence was collected, Cybereason identified another cluster of activity targeting Telcos in ASEAN countries. This cluster exhibits rather unique TTPs compared to the ones detailed in Cluster A; namely the use of different tools and C2 server infrastructure. In addition, this cluster’s activity was first observed in Q4 of 2020, while Cluster A goes back to 2018.&nbsp;</p>
<p>The main tool used in this cluster is the <a href="https://www.bitdefender.com/files/News/CaseStudies/study/396/Bitdefender-PR-Whitepaper-NAIKON-creat5397-en-EN.pdf" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>newly discovered Nebulae backdoor</span></a>, which according to BitDefender is attributed to the <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/groups/G0019/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Naikon APT</span></a> group. In addition, the attackers deployed a previously undocumented keylogger dubbed “<em>EnrollLoger</em>” on selected high-profile assets, most likely to obtain sensitive information and to harvest credentials of high-privilege user accounts.&nbsp;</p>
<p>As previously mentioned, while Cluster B has its unique characteristics that separate it from Cluster A, there were some overlaps observed in terms of the victimology, time frame, the endpoints and some generic tools that were also observed in cluster A.&nbsp;</p>
<a id="maintaining-foothold-nebulae-backdoor" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h3 style="font-size: 30px; font-weight: normal;">Maintaining Foothold: The Nebulae Backdoor</h3>
<p>One of the unique tools spotted in the course of the attack is the rare <a href="https://www.bitdefender.com/files/News/CaseStudies/study/396/Bitdefender-PR-Whitepaper-NAIKON-creat5397-en-EN.pdf" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Nebulae backdoor</span></a>, which was first reported in April 2021 and attributed to the <span>Naikon</span> group. <p>In addition, the keylogger also steals data stored on Windows’ Clipboard. <a id="cluster-c" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h3 style="text-align: left; font-size: 36px; font-weight: bold;">Cluster C: OWA Backdoor Activity (Mini-Cluster)</h3>
<p>During the investigation, we revealed a third cluster, which is in fact a mini-cluster characterized mainly by the deployment of multiple instances of a custom <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outlook_on_the_web" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>OWA</span></a> (Outlook Web Access) backdoor. The backdoor was used to harvest credentials of users logging into Microsoft OWA services, granting the attackers the ability to access the environment stealthily.&nbsp;</p>
<p>According to the forensic evidence available to us, the earliest indications of use of this backdoor begin in 2017. The deployment of the backdoor continued all the way to 2021, bearing the hallmark of a true advanced persistent threat (APT). From 2017-2020 we have observed only a few instances of the backdoor. However, in March 2021, the attackers installed the backdoor on over 20 machines in a short period of time. This interesting uptick could be explained by the fact that the attackers lost access due to mitigation efforts and needed to re-establish it. Another possible explanation could be related to Microsoft releasing patches for the newly discovered Microsoft Exchange Server vulnerabilities, which caused a sharp rise in attacks against Microsoft Exchange Servers that were unpatched.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Code analysis of this backdoor showed considerable similarities with previously documented backdoors dubbed “Dllshellexc2007” and “Dllshellexc2010”, which were discovered by TrendMicro in their <a href="https://paper.seebug.org/papers/APT/APT_CyberCriminal_Campagin/2015/2015.09.17.Operation_Iron_Tiger/wp-operation-iron-tiger.pdf" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Operation Iron Tiger report</span></a> and attributed to Group-3390 (also tracked by some vendors as APT27, Emissary Panda). According to the Iron Tiger report, the backdoor is compatible and can integrate with the China Chopper WebShell.&nbsp;</p>
<p>The activity of this backdoor however, could not be tied directly to the other clusters, which is why we decided to keep it as a separate cluster. That being said, there were some instances where we have observed this backdoor deployed on the same victim as clusters A and B, around the same time frames, and in some cases even on the same endpoints.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Given these overlaps and the previously documented compatibility of this OWA backdoor with the China Chopper WebShell, it is possible that Cluster C is somehow related to the activity described in Cluster A, yet a direct connection between the two was not observed in our investigation.&nbsp;</p>
<a id="custom-owa-backdoor" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h3 style="font-size: 30px; font-weight: normal;">Custom OWA Backdoor - Core Functionality</h3>
<p>The custom .NET backdoor deployed is named “Microsoft.Exchange.Clients.Event.dll” and can be installed on either Microsoft Exchange or Internet Information Services (IIS) servers. The main purpose of this backdoor is to harvest credentials of any user that authenticates to OWA services. In addition, the backdoor also contains further functionality similar to a WebShell, allowing the attackers to run arbitrary commands, exfiltrate data and deploy additional tools.&nbsp;</p>
<p>The .NET binary itself is obfuscated with <a href="https://www.eziriz.com/dotnet_reactor.htm" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>.NET Reactor</span></a>, which is a code protection and software licensing system. This kind of obfuscation software is often used by malware authors in an effort to hinder analysis. The backdoor intercepts requests that contain “owa/auth.owa'' in the URI (a default login URI for OWA), and steals the login credentials:</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/dam/images/images-web/blog-images/deadringer-blog-34.png?width=497&amp;name=deadringer-blog-34.png" alt="deadringer-blog-34" width="497" loading="lazy" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block; width: 497px;" srcset="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/dam/images/images-web/blog-images/deadringer-blog-34.png?width=249&amp;name=deadringer-blog-34.png 249w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/dam/images/images-web/blog-images/deadringer-blog-34.png?width=497&amp;name=deadringer-blog-34.png 497w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/dam/images/images-web/blog-images/deadringer-blog-34.png?width=746&amp;name=deadringer-blog-34.png 746w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/dam/images/images-web/blog-images/deadringer-blog-34.png?width=994&amp;name=deadringer-blog-34.png 994w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/dam/images/images-web/blog-images/deadringer-blog-34.png?width=1243&amp;name=deadringer-blog-34.png 1243w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/dam/images/images-web/blog-images/deadringer-blog-34.png?width=1491&amp;name=deadringer-blog-34.png 1491w" sizes="(max-width: 497px) 100vw, 497px"></p>
<p style="text-align: center; font-size: 16px;"><em><span style="color: #99acc2;">The backdoor checks the URI of the http request</span></em></p>
<p>The backdoor logs the following information from the HTTP requests:</p>
<ul style="font-size: 18px;">
<li aria-level="1">Connection date and time</li>
<li aria-level="1">Remote IP address</li>
<li aria-level="1">Username and password used to login</li>
<li aria-level="1">User agent&nbsp;<br><br></li>
</ul>
<p>In order to protect the stolen data, the backdoor XORes each byte of the collected information with the value “183”, and saves the result in base64 encoding to a file named “~ex.dat” in the %temp% directory. If the attacker connects to the server with a specifically crafted session id, the attacker's http request is parsed in order to execute various commands, such as:&nbsp;</p>
<ul style="font-size: 18px;">
<li aria-level="1">Downloading additional files&nbsp;</li>
<li aria-level="1">Uploading files (for data exfiltration)</li>
<li aria-level="1">Deleting files</li>
<li aria-level="1">Executing arbitrary commands via CMD Shell<br><br></li>
</ul>

<h3 style="font-size: 36px; font-weight: normal;">Connections to Winnti’s Tools and Infrastructure</h3>
<h3 style="font-size: 30px; font-weight: normal;"><span style="color: #434343;">Connections Between Naikon APT Nebulae Backdoor and Winnti’s ShadowPad Infrastructure</span></h3>

It is also worth mentioning that Winnti is known to attack telecommunications companies. <h3 style="font-size: 30px; font-weight: normal;"><span style="color: #434343;">Use of PcShare Backdoor in Previous Winnti-Related Attacks</span></h3>
<p>Another possible connection to Winnti’s Shadowpad backdoor is via the usage of a customized PcShare Backdoor that was found on multiple endpoints in Cluster A described in this report. In October 2020, Dr. Web <a href="https://st.drweb.com/static/new-www/news/2020/october/Study_of_the_ShadowPad_APT_backdoor_and_its_relation_to_PlugX_en.pdf" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>released a report</span></a> detailing targeted attacks in Kazakhstan and Kyrgyzstan involving Winnti’s Shadowpad backdoor. That same report also mentions a backdoor dubbed <em>“BackDoor.Farfli.125'' </em>that was deployed alongside the Shadowpad payloads. Our analysis of the <em>“BackDoor.Farfli.125'' </em>backdoor concluded that it is a variant of the open-source PcShare backdoor.&nbsp;</p>
<p>From a tradecraft perspective, it is interesting to note that the attackers in the aforementioned Dr. Web report also chose to use a loader that masquerades as a legitimate NVIDIA product, as shown in Cluster A of our report.&nbsp;</p>
<a id="connection-between-apt41-and-soft-cell" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>

ChipShot is a .NET binary that drops a modified China Chopper WebShell, which is found in the resource section of the file.&nbsp;</p>

<h3 style="font-size: 30px; font-weight: normal;">Possible Connection Between Tropic Trooper and Soft Cell</h3>
<p>As previously mentioned in our report, one of the PcShare payloads analyzed had the same hash that was <a href="https://blogs.blackberry.com/en/2019/09/pcshare-backdoor-attacks-targeting-windows-users-with-fakenarrator-malware" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>mentioned in a report</span></a> by BlackBerry from 2019. In both instances, the attackers used the exact same DLL search order hijacking technique with a fake NVIDIA product.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Aside from the forensic evidence, the geographical locations of the attacks and the timeline also point to a strong tie between the BlackBerry report and this report. BlackBerry hypothesized the threat actor behind the attack was <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/groups/G0081/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Tropic Trooper</span></a>, however, as mentioned in their report, they could not establish that attribution with high-certainty.&nbsp;</p>
<p>At this point, we can conclude that both intrusions were carried out by the same threat actor that had access to the same code and used an identical tradecraft. Whether Tropic Trooper and Soft Cell are the same actor remains unclear at the moment, since we could not verify the BlackBerry attribution.&nbsp;</p>

<h3 style="font-size: 36px; font-weight: normal;">Attribution</h3>
<a id="attributing-cluster-a-b-c" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h3 style="font-size: 30px; font-weight: normal;">Attributing Clusters A, B and C</h3>
<p>After analyzing all of the data we accumulated through our platform, incident response efforts, malware analysis, and threat intelligence, we were able to define three distinct clusters of malicious activity. Each cluster is characterized with its own set of TTPs and infrastructure which appear to have operated independently, according to our analysis:&nbsp;</p>
<ul>
<li aria-level="1"><span style="font-size: 18px;"><strong>Cluster A: Attributed to the Soft Cell Activity Group&nbsp;</strong></span></li>
</ul>
<p style="padding-left: 40px;"><span style="font-size: 18px;">Based on the evidence provided in this report as well as internal and publicly available information, Cybereason assesses with high level of confidence that the intrusions detailed in this cluster are consistent with previous activities carried out by the Soft Cell activity group. Soft Cell has yet been attributed to a specific threat actor, however, it is assessed that the group operates on behalf of Chinese state interests. As shown in our report, there are some interesting links between the Soft Cell activity group and the APT41/Winnti threat actor, nevertheless, at the time of writing this report, there is not enough evidence to tie the two with sufficient certainty.</span>&nbsp;</p>
<ul style="font-size: 18px;">
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Cluster B: Suspected to be the Naikon APT Group</strong></li>
</ul>
<p style="padding-left: 40px; font-size: 18px;">Based on the information provided in this report as well as information that is publicly available regarding the Naikon APT threat actor activity, Cybereason assesses with moderate confidence that the intrusions detailed in this cluster were carried out by the Naikon APT group.&nbsp;</p>
<ul style="font-size: 18px;">
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Cluster C: Potentially Related to Group-3390 (also tracked as Emissary Panda, APT27)</strong></li>
</ul>
<p style="padding-left: 40px; font-size: 18px;">Based on the information provided in this report, Cybereason assesses with low-to-moderate confidence that the intrusions detailed in this cluster were carried out by a threat actor who had access to the code of the “Dllshellexc2007” and “Dllshellexc2010” backdoors detailed in operation “Iron Tiger” and attributed by TrendMicro to Group-3390.&nbsp;</p>
<p>That being said, we cannot ignore certain interesting overlaps when it comes to the aforementioned clusters. In one particular instance, Cybereason observed all three clusters on the same environment, and in some cases even operating on the same endpoints around similar time frames. Whether this is merely a coincidence, or the clusters are somehow inter-connected, is not entirely clear at this point in time.</p>
<p>Among the three clusters, we lean towards the possibility that Cluster A and C might be connected based on the fact that they have been operating in the same environment for over three years (since 2017/2018), while Cluster B only emerged in 2020 Q4. In addition, the OWA backdoor described in Cluster C was <a href="http://www.erai.com/CustomUploads/ca/wp/2015_12_wp_operation_iron_tiger.pdf" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>previously proven to interact with the China Chopper WebShell</span></a> that was used extensively in Cluster A.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Based on our understanding and past experience with Chinese threat actors, there are several hypotheses that might explain those overlaps:</p>
<ul style="font-size: 18px;">
<li aria-level="1"><strong>One hypothesis</strong> is that the clusters represent the work of<strong> two or more teams</strong> with different sets of expertise (e.g initial access team, foothold, telco-technology specialized team, etc.) all working together and reporting to the same Chinese threat actor.&nbsp;</li>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>A second hypothesis</strong> is that there are <strong>two or more Chinese threat actors </strong>with different agendas / tasks that are aware of each other’s work and potentially even working in tandem.&nbsp;</li>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Another plausible hypothesis </strong>is that the clusters are not interconnected and that the threat actors are working independently with no collaboration, or even piggybacking on the access achieved by one of the actors involved.&nbsp;</li>
</ul>
<p>One thing that remains consistent and evident in all three clusters is that they all point to threat actors that are believed to be operating on behalf of Chinese state interests. It is also not surprising that the Telcos targeted in these intrusions are located in <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASEAN"><span>ASEAN countries</span></a>, some of which have long term publicly known disputes with the PRC (People’s Republic of China).&nbsp;</p>
<a id="note-cti-attribution" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h3 style="font-size: 30px; font-weight: normal;">A Note on CTI Attribution</h3>
<p>In the world of threat intelligence, attribution is often not an exact science and should be continuously re-assessed overtime as new information emerges that can shed more light on the identity of the threat actors. Therefore, we encourage our readers to use the information provided in this report and draw their own conclusions. In our attribution, inspired by the famous <a href="https://www.activeresponse.org/wp-content/uploads/2013/07/diamond.pdf" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Diamond Model</span></a>, we have taken into account the following aspects of the intrusions: <span style="font-weight: normal;">Victimology</span> (location, industry), <span style="font-weight: normal;">Capabilities</span> (mainly tools, techniques and procedures) and <span style="font-weight: normal;">Infrastructure.</span>&nbsp;</p>
<p>When analyzing intrusions that happened over years, it is often difficult to separate one kill-chain from another even when there is just a single threat actor. With the possibility of more than one threat actor operating in the same environment, this task can be even more daunting, and oftentimes it can be tempting to treat everything as part of one larger attack originating from the same threat actor, which could lead to misattribution. We encourage other analysts to work with a well-defined attribution model that can make the fickle task of attribution less prone to mistakes and biases.&nbsp;</p>
<a id="conclusion" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h3 style="font-size: 36px; font-weight: normal;">Conclusion</h3>
<p>In this blog we uncovered three clusters of intrusions targeting Telcos in ASEAN countries that were active for several years, with one cluster going back as far as 2017. We assess that the goal behind the intrusions was to facilitate cyber espionage efforts by gaining access to cellular providers for the purpose of exfiltrating sensitive data about the targeted companies and their customers.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Each cluster appears to have its own unique characteristics, distinguishing it from the other clusters detailed in this report. In our report, we also mention the interesting overlaps observed among those clusters - namely the targeting of the same victims, operating around similar time frames, and in some cases the existence of all three clusters on the same endpoints.&nbsp;</p>
<p>According to our analysis, Cluster A was executed by the Soft Cell activity group, a group that is known to have attacked Telcos in the past in multiple regions and believed to be operating on behalf of Chinese state interests. The intrusions in this cluster span over three years, going back to 2018. The attackers behind it have shown great resourcefulness and adaptiveness in light of mitigation efforts, finding their way back in repeatedly, which may demonstrate how important it was for them to obtain the data from the targeted Telcos.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Cluster B was discovered in late 2020 and exhibited a different set of tools and techniques, including the rare Nebulae backdoor and the previously undocumented EnrollLogger keylogger. We suspect that the activity in this cluster was carried out by the Naikon APT group, a very active cyber espionage group previously attributed to the Chinese People’s Liberation Army’s (PLA).&nbsp;</p>
<p>Cluster C is the oldest among the clusters, with first signs of intrusions going back to 2017. This cluster exhibited a rare OWA backdoor that shows considerable code and functionality similarities to previously documented backdoors that were used by the Group-3390 (APT27), an infamous cyber espionage APT group operating on behalf of Chinese state interests.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Whether these clusters are in fact inter-connected or operated independently from each other is not entirely clear at the time of writing this report. We offered several hypotheses that can account for these overlaps, hoping that as time goes by more information will be made available to us and to other researchers that will help to shed light on this conundrum.&nbsp;</p>
