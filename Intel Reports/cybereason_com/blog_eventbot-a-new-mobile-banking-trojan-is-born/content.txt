          <span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><p><strong>Research by:</strong> Daniel Frank, Lior Rochberger, Yaron Rimmer and Assaf Dahan</p>
<!--more-->
<h3>Key Findings&nbsp;</h3>
<ul>
<li>The Cybereason Nocturnus team is investigating EventBot, a new type of Android mobile malware that emerged around March 2020. EventBot is a mobile banking trojan and infostealer that abuses Android’s accessibility features to steal user data from financial applications, read user SMS messages, and steal SMS messages to allow the malware to bypass two-factor authentication.</li>
<li>EventBot targets users of over 200 different financial applications, including banking, money transfer services, and crypto-currency wallets. Those targeted include applications like<a href="https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.paypal.merchant.client&amp;hl=en_US" rel="noopener" target="_blank"> Paypal Business</a>, <a href="https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.revolut.revolut&amp;hl=en_US" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Revolut</a>, <a href="https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.barclays.android.barclaysmobilebanking&amp;hl=en_US" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Barclays</a>, <a href="https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.unicredit&amp;hl=en_US" rel="noopener" target="_blank">UniCredit</a>, <a href="https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.ie.capitalone.uk&amp;hl=en_US" rel="noopener" target="_blank">CapitalOne UK</a>, <a href="https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=uk.co.hsbc.hsbcukmobilebanking&amp;hl=en_US" rel="noopener" target="_blank">HSBC UK</a>, <a href="https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=uk.co.santander.santanderUK&amp;hl=en_US" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Santander UK</a>, <a href="https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.transferwise.android&amp;hl=en_US" rel="noopener" target="_blank">TransferWise</a>, <a href="https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.coinbase.android&amp;hl=en_US" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Coinbase</a>, <a href="https://twitter.com/paysafecard" rel="noopener" target="_blank">paysafecard</a>, and many more.&nbsp;</li>
<li>&nbsp;It specifically targets financial banking applications across the United States and Europe, including Italy, the UK, Spain, Switzerland, France, and Germany. The full list of banking applications targeted is included in the appendix.&nbsp;</li>
<li>EventBot is particularly interesting because it is in such early stages. This brand new malware has real potential to become the next big mobile malware, as it is under constant iterative improvements, abuses a critical operating system feature, and targets financial applications.&nbsp;</li>
<li>This research gives a rare look into the process improvements malware authors make when optimizing before launch. By going on the offensive and hunting the attackers, our team was able to unearth the early stages of what may be a very dangerous mobile malware.&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<a id="security-recommendations" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h3>Security Recommendations</h3>
<ul>
<li>Keep your mobile device up-to-date with the latest software updates from legitimate sources.</li>
<li>Keep Google Play Protect on.</li>
<li>Do not download mobile apps from unofficial or unauthorized sources. Most legitimate Android apps are available on the Google Play Store.&nbsp;</li>
<li>Always apply critical thinking and consider whether you should give a certain app the permissions it requests.&nbsp;</li>
<li>When in doubt, check the APK signature and hash in sources like <a href="https://www.virustotal.com/gui/home/search" rel="noopener" target="_blank">VirusTotal</a> before installing it on your device.&nbsp;</li>
<li>Use mobile threat detection solutions for enhanced security.</li>
</ul>
<a id="intro" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h3>Introduction</h3>
<p>For the past few weeks, the Cybereason Nocturnus team has been investigating a new type of Android malware dubbed <a href="https://ti.qianxin.com/blog/articles/new-bank-trojan-eventbot-affects-234-financial-applications/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">EventBot</a>, which was first identified in March 2020. This malware appears to be newly developed with code that differs significantly from previously known Android malware. EventBot is under active development and is evolving rapidly; new versions are released every few days with improvements and new capabilities.&nbsp;</p>
<p>EventBot abuses Android’s accessibility feature to access valuable user information, system information, and data stored in other applications. In particular, EventBot can intercept SMS messages and bypass two-factor authentication mechanisms.</p>
<p>The Cybereason Nocturnus team has concluded that EventBot is designed to target over 200 different banking and finance applications, the majority of which are European bank and crypto-currency exchange applications.</p>
<p>By accessing and stealing this data, Eventbot has the potential to access key business data, including financial data. <a href="https://www.rsaconference.com/industry-topics/blog/the-battle-to-address-mobile-threats-in-the-endpoint-security-space" rel="noopener" target="_blank">60% of devices</a> containing or accessing enterprise data are mobile, and mobile devices tend to include a significant amount of personal and business data, assuming the organization has a bring-your-own-device policy in place. Mobile malware is a significant risk for organizations and consumers alike, and must be considered when protecting personal and business data.&nbsp;</p>

<p><strong>Malware Capabilities</strong></p>
<p>The Cybereason Nocturnus team has been following EventBot since the beginning of March 2020. The team has encountered different versions of the malware over time as it has rapidly evolved. At the time of writing this research, four versions of the EventBot malware were observed: Version 0.0.0.1, 0.0.0.2, and 0.3.0.1 and 0.4.0.1. Each version expands the bot’s functionality and works to obfuscate the malware against analysis. In this research, we review common features of the malware and examine the improvements the threat actor made in each version.</p>
<a id="common-features" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h3>Common Features</h3>
<p><strong>Permissions</strong></p>
<p>When installed, EventBot requests the following <a href="https://developer.android.com/reference/android/Manifest.permission" rel="noopener" target="_blank">permissions</a> on the device:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>SYSTEM_ALERT_WINDOW</strong> - allow the app to create windows that are shown on top of other apps.</li>
<li><strong>READ_EXTERNAL_STORAGE</strong> - read from external storage.</li>
<li><strong>REQUEST_INSTALL_PACKAGES</strong> - make a request to install packages.</li>
<li><strong>INTERNET</strong> - open network sockets.</li>
<li><strong>REQUEST_IGNORE_BATTERY_OPTIMIZATIONS</strong> - whitelist the app to allow it to ignore battery optimizations.</li>
<li><strong>WAKE_LOCK</strong> - prevent the processor from sleeping and dimming the screen.</li>
<li><strong>ACCESS_NETWORK_STATE</strong> - allow the app to access information about networks.</li>
<li><strong>REQUEST_COMPANION_RUN_IN_BACKGROUND</strong> - let the app run in the background.</li>
<li><strong>REQUEST_COMPANION_USE_DATA_IN_BACKGROUND</strong> - let the app use data in the background.</li>
<li><strong>RECEIVE_BOOT_COMPLETED</strong> - allow the application to launch itself after system boot. EventBot uses this permission in order to achieve persistence and run in the background as a service.</li>
<li><strong>RECEIVE_SMS </strong>- allow the application to receive text messages.</li>
<li><strong>READ_SMS</strong> - allow the application to read text messages.</li>
</ul>

<a id="abuse-of-accessibility-services" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h4>Abuse of Accessibility Services</h4>
<p>EventBot <a href="https://labs.f-secure.com/blog/how-are-we-doing-with-androids-overlay-attacks-in-2020/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">abuses the accessibility services</a> of Android devices for the majority of its activity. Accessibility features are typically used to help users with disabilities by giving the device the ability to write into input fields, auto-generate permissions, perform gestures for the user, etc. However, when used maliciously, accessibility features can be used to exploit legitimate services for malicious purposes, like with EventBot. EventBot uses multiple methods to exploit accessibility events for webinjects and other information stealing purposes.&nbsp;</p>

<h4>Data Gathering</h4>
<ul>
<ul>
<ul>
<li><strong>Getting a list of all installed applications</strong>: Once EventBot is installed on the target machine, it lists all the applications on the target machine and sends them to the C2.&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

Also, the botnet IDs increment over time as they are submitted. Given this, and the naming convention of the submissions (<i>&lt;hash&gt;.virus</i>), the submitter hash most likely belongs to an AV vendor or sandboxing environment that automatically submits samples to online malware databases. It may be that these submissions are made from the author’s machine, or that they submit it to a detection service that in turn submits to online malware databases.</p>
<a id="eventbot-threat-actors" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h3>EventBot Threat Actors</h3>
<p>As a part of this investigation, the Cybereason Nocturnus team has attempted to identify the threat actors behind the development of EventBot. The evidence above suggests that EventBot is still in the development stage, and as such, is not likely to have been used for large attack campaigns thus far.&nbsp;</p>
<p>The Cybereason Nocturnus team is monitoring multiple underground platforms in an attempt to identify chatter relating to EventBot. New malware is often introduced to underground communities by being promoted and sold or offered as a giveaway. However, at the time of writing, we were unable to identify relevant conversations about the EventBot malware. This strengthens our suspicion that this malware is still undergoing development and has not been officially marketed or released yet.&nbsp;</p>

<a id="impact" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h3 style="font-weight: bold;">Impact</h3>
<p>EventBot is a mobile malware banking trojan that steals financial information, is able to hijack transactions. Once this malware has successfully installed, it will collect personal data, passwords, keystrokes, banking information, and more. This information can give the attacker access to personal and business bank accounts, personal and business data, and more.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Letting an attacker get access to this kind of data can have severe consequences. <a href="https://www.rsaconference.com/industry-topics/blog/the-battle-to-address-mobile-threats-in-the-endpoint-security-space" rel="noopener" target="_blank">60% of devices</a> containing or accessing enterprise data are mobile. Giving an attacker access to a mobile device can have severe business consequences, especially if the end user is using their mobile device to discuss sensitive business topics or access enterprise financial information. This can result in brand degradation, loss of individual reputation, or loss of consumer trust.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Much like we have seen in recent months, anyone can be impacted by a mobile device attack. These attacks are only becoming more common, with <a href="https://www.zimperium.com/mobile-malware" rel="noopener" target="_blank">one third of all malware now targeting mobile endpoints</a>. Care and concern both for using a mobile device and for securing a mobile device is critical, especially for those organizations that allow bring-your-own-devices.&nbsp;</p>

<a id="conclusion" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h3>Conclusion</h3>
<p>In this research, the Nocturnus team has dissected a rapidly evolving Android malware in the making. This malware abuses the Android accessibility feature to steal user information and is able to update its code and release new features every few days. With each new version, the malware adds new features like dynamic library loading, encryption, and adjustments to different locales and manufacturers. EventBot appears to be a completely new malware in the early stages of development, giving us an interesting view into how attackers create and test their malware.</p>
<p>Cybereason classifies EventBot as a mobile banking trojan and infostealer based on the stealing features discussed in this research. It leverages webinjects and SMS reading capabilities to bypass two-factor authentication, and is clearly targeting financial applications.</p>
<p>Although the threat actor responsible for the development of EventBot is still unknown and the malware does not appear to be involved in major attacks, it is interesting to follow the early stages of mobile malware development. The Cybereason Nocturnus team will continue to monitor EventBot’s development.&nbsp;</p>
<p>In recent years, online activity has gradually been shifting from personal computers to mobile devices. Naturally, this resulted in the introduction of malware for mobile platforms, especially Android devices, including <a href="https://www.threatfabric.com/blogs/cerberus-a-new-banking-trojan-from-the-underworld.html" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Cerberus</a>, <a href="https://www.cisomag.com/mysterious-malware-infects-over-45000-android-phones/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Xhelper</a> and the <a href="https://cyware.com/news/exploring-the-nature-and-capabilities-of-anubis-android-banking-trojan-6ea7dec4" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Anubis Banking Trojan</a>. As many people use their mobile devices for online shopping and even to manage their bank accounts, the mobile arena became increasingly profitable for cyber criminals.</p>
<p>This is why we recently released <a href="/platform/mobile" rel=" noopener">Cybereason Mobile,</a> a new offering that strengthens the Cybereason Defense Platform by bringing prevention, detection, and response capabilities to mobile devices. With Cybereason Mobile, our customers can protect against modern threats across traditional and mobile endpoints, all within a single console.&nbsp;</p>
