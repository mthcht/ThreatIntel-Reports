      <h1><span id="hs_cos_wrapper_name" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="text">Hunting Raccoon: The New Masked Bandit on the Block</span></h1>
      <div class="cr-mln__post-author-share">
      <div id="hubspot-author_data" class="hubspot-editable cr-mln__post-meta" data-hubspot-form-id="author_data" data-hubspot-name="Blog Author">

          <span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><p><strong>Research by: Assaf Dahan &amp; Lior Rochberger</strong></p>
<!--more-->
<h2>Introduction</h2>
<p>Since April 2019, the Cybereason Nocturnus team has investigated multiple infections of the Raccoon stealer in the wild across organizations and individuals. In this research, we focus on two key aspects:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>A Glimpse Into the Underground:</strong> A survey of Raccoon’s origin, team members, business model, and marketing efforts, as well as Raccoon’s reception by the underground cybercrime community and the existing feuds between Raccoon’s team and their direct competitors.</li>
<li><strong>A Technical Breakdown:</strong> A comprehensive technical overview of Raccoon’s current capabilities and delivery methods, with a look into their future plans for the malware.</li>
</ul>
<div><img src="https://www.cybereason.com/hubfs/Raccoon%20Blog.png" alt="" style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"></div>
<p style="text-align: center;"><em>The top 10 malware mentions over seven months in 2019 from </em><a href="https://www.recordedfuture.com/measuring-malware-popularity/">Recorded Future. </a></p>
<p>The Raccoon stealer is one of the <a href="https://www.recordedfuture.com/measuring-malware-popularity/">2019 top 10 most-mentioned malware in the underground economy</a> and is widely known to have infected hundreds of thousands of devices around the world, despite it not being overly sophisticated or innovative. This strain of malware first emerged as recently as 2019, and has already established a strong following among cybercriminals. Its popularity, even with a limited feature set, signals the continuation of a growing trend of the commoditization of malware as they follow a MaaS (Malware-as-a-Service) model and evolve their efforts.</p>
<p><em>Build an iterative defense that addresses threats like these. <a href="/unleashing-the-true-potential-mitre-attck?hsCtaTracking=d8b5fccc-df9b-4621-86be-9d72ad635e7f%7C31de2ef9-bf97-4290-a751-4c3ec822e56c" rel=" noopener">Read our white paper on how to use MITRE ATT&amp;CK to create a closed-loop security process.&nbsp;</a></em></p>
<h2>Key Points</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>The Raccoon Infostealer</strong>: The Cybereason Nocturnus team has been investigating multiple incidents involving the Raccoon infostealer since April 2019, and is now able to give a thorough analysis of the technical aspects of the malware alongside a look into the likely Russian team behind it.</li>
<li><strong>Steals a Wide Range of Data</strong>: Raccoon lacks sophistication, but leverages several potential delivery methods and is able to steal a large swath of important data, including credit card information, cryptocurrency wallets, browser data, and email credentials.</li>
<li><strong>Is Quickly Gaining Traction</strong>: Despite being released earlier this year, the Raccoon stealer is exploding in popularity in the underground community to become one of the top 10 most-referenced malware on the market in 2019, infecting hundreds of thousands of endpoints globally across organizations and individuals in North America, Europe, and Asia.</li>
<li><strong>Enables Any Individual to Easily Commit Cybercrime</strong>: Raccoon follows a malware-as-a-service model, allowing individuals a quick-and-easy way to make money stealing sensitive data without a huge personal investment or technical know-how.</li>
<li><strong>Has a Strong Following Underground:</strong> The team behind Raccoon is lauded in the underground community for their level of service, support, and user experience, but has faced several bouts of public feuds and internal disputes.</li>
</ul>
<h2>TABLE OF CONTENTS</h2>
<a id="what" style="font-family: DIN-Condensed, sans-serif; font-size: 40px; letter-spacing: 0.05em; text-transform: uppercase;" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<ul>
<li><a href="#what" rel=" noopener">What is the Raccoon Infostealer?</a></li>
<li><a href="#overview" rel=" noopener">Threat Actor Overview</a></li>

<li><a href="#overview" rel=" noopener">Raccoon Stealer Overview</a></li>
<li><a href="#how" rel=" noopener">How is Raccoon Affecting Businesses and Individuals?</a></li>
<li><a href="#conclusion" rel=" noopener">Conclusion</a></li>
<li><a href="#appendix" rel=" noopener">Appendix</a><span style="color: #000000; font-family: DIN-Condensed, sans-serif; font-size: 40px; letter-spacing: 0.05em; text-transform: uppercase;"></span><br><span style="color: #000000; font-family: DIN-Condensed, sans-serif; font-size: 40px; letter-spacing: 0.05em; text-transform: uppercase;">What is the Raccoon Infostealer?</span></li>
</ul>
<p>Raccoon, also known as “<a href="https://twitter.com/avman1995/status/1119451757451665410">Mohazo</a>” or “<a href="https://twitter.com/malwaremustd1e/status/1174333091319447552">Racealer</a>”, is at its core a simple information stealer often seen delivered by the <a href="https://www.cybereason.com/blog/watch-where-you-browse-the-fallout-exploit-kit-stays-active">Fallout</a> and <a href="https://blog.malwarebytes.com/threat-analysis/2019/05/exploit-kits-spring-2019-review/">RIG</a> Exploit Kits. It is used to steal data like credit card information, cryptocurrency wallets, browser-related data, and mail clients. Although it is not an advanced malware, it is estimated to have infected hundreds of thousands of devices around the world and is in the <a href="https://www.recordedfuture.com/measuring-malware-popularity/">top 10 mentioned in the underground communities</a> for 2019.</p>
<p>Raccoon is written in C++ and works on both 32-bit and 64-bit operating systems. Though it was originally classified as a password stealer by many AV companies, we and others in the community see it leverage broader capabilities and categorize it as an information stealer.</p>
<p>Raccoon searches system files for a range of confidential data which it saves and sends to its operator. It is able to collect the following data:</p>
<ul>
<li>Credit Card Data</li>
<li>Cryptocurrency Wallets</li>
<li>Passwords</li>
<li>Emails</li>
<li>Data from All Popular Browsers Including Credit Card Info, URLs, Usernames, Passwords</li>
<li>Cookies</li>
<li>System Information</li>
</ul>
<a id="overview" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h2>Threat Actor Overview</h2>
<p>The Raccoon stealer is developed by a team that appears to originate in Russia and be Russian-speaking. The team initially promoted the stealer in exclusively Russian-speaking hacking forums, but now actively promotes it in English-speeaking communities as well. It is aggressively marketed in the cybercrime underground and has been since April 2019.</p>
<div><img src="https://www.cybereason.com/hubfs/Raccoon%20Blog-12.png" alt="" style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"></div>
<p style="text-align: center;"><em>Raccoon stealer marketed in a Russian underground forum. </em></p>
<p>Raccoon is sold as a MaaS with features like an easy-to-use automated backend panel, bulletproof hosting, and 24/7 customer support in both Russian and English. <h2>Raccoon Stealer Overview</h2>
<h3>How is Raccoon Delivered?</h3>
<p>Raccoon is delivered in multiple ways, though we see Raccoon delivered most often through exploit kits, phishing attacks, and through bundled malware.</p>
<h4>Delivery by Exploit Kit</h4>
<p><a href="https://www.cybereason.com/blog/exploit-kits-shade-into-new-territory">Exploit kits</a> automatically exploit vulnerabilities on a victim’s machine while they are browsing the web. In browsing the web, the user visits a malicious page that redirects to a landing page containing exploit code, often executed without the users consent or interaction.</p>
<p>In order to deliver Raccoon, the attackers leverage the <a href="https://www.cybereason.com/blog/watch-where-you-browse-the-fallout-exploit-kit-stays-active">Fallout exploit kit </a>to spawn a PowerShell instance from Internet Explorer and subsequently download the main payload of the infostealer.</p>
<div><img src="https://www.cybereason.com/hubfs/Raccoon%20Blog-16.png" alt="" style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"></div>
<p style="text-align: center;"><em>The Fallout exploit kit delivers Raccoon, as seen in the Cybereason platform. </em></p>
<h4>Delivery by Phishing</h4>
<p>Phishing is a social engineering attack where a user is tricked into executing malicious content. Most commonly, the user receives an email with an attached Office document. This document contains embedded malicious macro code that, when opened, executes.</p>
<p>In order to deliver Raccoon, the attackers use an email with an attached Word document. Upon opening the Word document and enabling macros, the macro code creates a connection to a malicious domain and downloads the main payload of the infostealer.</p>
<div><img src="https://www.cybereason.com/hubfs/Raccoon%20Blog-23.png" alt="" style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"></div>
<p style="text-align: center;"><em>The malicious Word document downloading the Raccoon stealer payload, as seen in the Cybereason platform. </em></p>
<h4>Delivery by Bundled Malware</h4>
<p>Bundled malware is malware that is bundled with legitimate software downloaded from “shady” websites. The bundled malware is often hidden from the user during installation or makes use of social engineering techniques to enable installation.</p>
<p>In order to deliver Raccoon, the attackers use legitimate software bundled with the main payload of the infostealer to infect unsuspecting users. Raccoon installs itself behind-the-scenes, hidden from the user.</p>

<p>As mentioned above, the Raccoon team appears to be of Russian origin. A typo found in the internal path also suggests they are not native English speakers.</p>

<p style="text-align: center;"><em>The Raccoon team issuing a recommendation for a third-party crypter.</em></p>
<p>The main payload of Raccoon is not packed and does not include built-in <a href="https://www.apriorit.com/dev-blog/367-anti-reverse-engineering-protection-techniques-to-use-before-releasing-software">anti-debug</a> or <a href="https://www.cyberbit.com/blog/endpoint-security/anti-vm-and-anti-sandbox-explained/">anti-VM protections</a>. It is sold <em>as-is</em> without any protection from analysts or detection. However, the Raccoon team does recommend a third-party crypter call GreenCrypt to evade antivirus products and protect against detection and analysis.</p>
<h4><strong>Raccoon’s Communication with its C2 Server </strong></h4>
<p>Once the loader executes on the target machine, it unpacks itself in memory and connects to its C2 server. Raccoon sends a POST request with Base64-encoded parameters<em> bot_id</em> and <em>config_id</em>.</p>
<div><img src="https://www.cybereason.com/hubfs/image-136.png" style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"></div>
<p style="text-align: center;"><em>Raccoon sending a POST request with two parameters. </em></p>
<p>Upon successful connection and verification of Raccoon’s Bot ID, it downloads a compressed zip file with multiple different DLLs. These DLLs are not necessarily malicious on their own, but Raccoon depends on them to successfully collect and steal data on the target machine.</p>
<h4><strong>Gathering Local Settings on the Target Machine</strong></h4>
<div><img src="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Raccoon%20Blog-24.png?width=557&amp;name=Raccoon%20Blog-24.png" alt="" style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; width: 557px;" width="557" srcset="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Raccoon%20Blog-24.png?width=279&amp;name=Raccoon%20Blog-24.png 279w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Raccoon%20Blog-24.png?width=557&amp;name=Raccoon%20Blog-24.png 557w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Raccoon%20Blog-24.png?width=836&amp;name=Raccoon%20Blog-24.png 836w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Raccoon%20Blog-24.png?width=1114&amp;name=Raccoon%20Blog-24.png 1114w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Raccoon%20Blog-24.png?width=1393&amp;name=Raccoon%20Blog-24.png 1393w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Raccoon%20Blog-24.png?width=1671&amp;name=Raccoon%20Blog-24.png 1671w" sizes="(max-width: 557px) 100vw, 557px"></div>
<p style="text-align: center;"><em>The Raccoon stealer code checks the target machine’s local settings.</em></p>
<p>However, the Raccoon stealer does check the target machine’s local settings and compare it against a list of languages, including Russian, Ukrainian, Belarussian, Kazakh, Kyrgyz, Armenian, Tajik, and Uzbek. If the target machine’s local settings match one of these languages, the malware immediately aborts. This is common practice by malware originating from <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Independent_States">CIS countries</a>.</p>
<h4><strong>Collecting Sensitive Data</strong></h4>
<p>After initial infection, the Raccoon stealer uses several methods to collect sensitive information. It stores any sensitive information it finds in the <em>Temp</em> folder.</p>
<h4>Capturing Screenshots from Infected Machine</h4>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<div><img src="https://www.cybereason.com/hubfs/Raccoon%20Blog-9.png" alt="" style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"></div>
<p style="text-align: center;"><em>The Raccoon stealer code takes a screen capture of the target machine.</em></p>
<p>Raccoon takes a screen capture of the target machine using GetDesktopWindow and CreateCompatibleBitmap, and stores it as <em><strong>screen.jpeg</strong></em> in the <em>Temp</em> folder.</p>
<h4>Stealing System Information</h4>
<p>Raccoon collects system information from the infected machine, including username, IP address, language settings, OS version, information on installed apps, and CPU and memory information. The information is stored in a text file found in:</p>
<p><em>C:\Users\[user]\AppData\Local\Temp\<strong>machineinfo.txt</strong></em></p>
<div><img src="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Raccoon%20Blog-3.png?width=560&amp;name=Raccoon%20Blog-3.png" alt="" width="560" style="width: 560px; display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" srcset="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Raccoon%20Blog-3.png?width=280&amp;name=Raccoon%20Blog-3.png 280w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Raccoon%20Blog-3.png?width=560&amp;name=Raccoon%20Blog-3.png 560w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Raccoon%20Blog-3.png?width=840&amp;name=Raccoon%20Blog-3.png 840w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Raccoon%20Blog-3.png?width=1120&amp;name=Raccoon%20Blog-3.png 1120w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Raccoon%20Blog-3.png?width=1400&amp;name=Raccoon%20Blog-3.png 1400w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Raccoon%20Blog-3.png?width=1680&amp;name=Raccoon%20Blog-3.png 1680w" sizes="(max-width: 560px) 100vw, 560px"></div>
<p style="text-align: center;"><em>Information collected by the Raccoon Stealer.</em></p>
<h4><strong>Stealing Browser Information</strong></h4>
<p>A lot of saved browser data is stored within SQLite database files on a local machine. For example, when a user saves their username and password in the browser, the browser stores the data in a <em>Login Data</em> SQLite database file. The browser also stores cookie information in a <em>cookies</em> file, and other autofill data, like credit card data, in a <em>Web Data </em>file.</p>
<p>Raccoon steals this information from over thirty different browser types. It searches the registry <em>software</em> hive for installed browsers and steals credentials, cookies, and autofill data from targeted browsers.</p>

It gives individuals a quick-and-easy way to make money stealing sensitive data without investing a lot of funds or having a deep technical background.</p>

<a id="conclusion" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>Though the Raccoon stealer may not be the most innovative infostealer on the market, it is still gaining significant traction in the underground community. Based on testimonials from the underground community, The Raccoon team provides reliable customer service to give cybercriminals a quick-and-easy way to commit cybercrime without a huge personal investment.</p>
<p>This has not come without strife. The team has faced several public disputes in underground forums, and has received some criticism from competitors. Despite this, Raccoon has quickly become one of the top ten mentioned malware in the underground community, despite being launched in early 2019. Overall, sentiment around Raccoon is positive, with some calling it the best replacement available for the now defunct Azorult infostealer.</p>
<p>Raccoon’s popularity combined with its limited feature set yet high adoption speaks to a growing trend of the commoditization of malware, as malware authors shoot to create platforms for crime instead of committing the crimes directly. As malware authors choose to develop MaaS, they must partake in many of the same activities as a legitimate SaaS business: marketing efforts, relying on positive reviews, responsive customer support, and regularly improving features in their product. We only expect this trend to continue into 2020 and push the evolution of MaaS forward.</p>
<p><em><strong>Endpoint protection is key to defending against techniques like these. <a href="/building-an-all-encompassing-epp-platform-in-todays-market" rel=" noopener">Learn more during our webinar on Endpoint Protection Platforms.&nbsp;</a></strong></em></p>
