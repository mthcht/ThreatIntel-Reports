      <h1><span id="hs_cos_wrapper_name" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="text">New Cyber Espionage Campaigns Targeting Palestinians - Part 1: The Spark Campaign</span></h1>
      <div class="cr-mln__post-author-share">
      <div id="hubspot-author_data" class="hubspot-editable cr-mln__post-meta" data-hubspot-form-id="author_data" data-hubspot-name="Blog Author">

          <span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><p style="text-align: justify;"><strong>Research by:</strong> Cybereason Nocturnus Team</p>
<!--more--><a id="background" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h3 style="text-align: justify;">Background</h3>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Over the last several months, the Cybereason Nocturnus team has been tracking recent espionage campaigns targeting the Middle East. These campaigns are specifically directed at entities and individuals in the Palestinian territories. This investigation shows multiple similarities to previous attacks attributed to a group called <u><a href="https://attack.mitre.org/groups/G0021/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">MoleRATs</a></u> (aka <u><a href="https://securelist.com/gaza-cybergang-group1-operation-sneakypastes/90068/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">The Gaza Cybergang</a></u>), an Arabic-speaking, politically motivated group that has operated in the Middle East since 2012.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">In our analysis, we distinguish between two separate campaigns happening simultaneously. These campaigns differ in tools, server infrastructure, and nuances in decoy content and intended targets.</p>
<ol style="text-align: justify;">
<li><strong>The Spark Campaign</strong>: This campaign uses social engineering to infect victims, mainly from the Palestinian territories, with the <strong>Spark backdoor</strong>. This backdoor <u><a href="https://ti.360.net/blog/articles/suspected-molerats-new-attack-in-the-middle-east-en/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">first emerged in January 2019</a></u> and has been continuously active since then. The campaign’s lure content revolves around recent geopolitical events, espeically the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, the assassination of Qasem Soleimani, and the ongoing conflict between Hamas and Fatah Palestinian movements.</li>
<li><strong>The Pierogi Campaign</strong>: This campaign uses social engineering attacks to infect victims with a <strong>new, undocumented backdoor dubbed Pierogi</strong>. This backdoor first emerged in December 2019, and was discovered by Cybereason. In this campaign, the attackers use different TTPs and decoy documents reminiscent of previous campaigns by MoleRATs involving the <u><a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/2017/06/palestine-delphi.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Micropsia</a></u> and <u><a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/unit42-targeted-attacks-middle-east-using-kasperagent-micropsia/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Kaperagent</a></u> malware.</li>
</ol>
<p style="text-align: justify;">In part one of this research, we analyze the Spark campaign. This campaign is named after a rare backdoor used by the MoleRATs Group, dubbed Spark by Cybereason and previously reported by <u><a href="http://webcache.googleusercontent.com/search?q=cache:https://ti.360.net/blog/articles/suspected-molerats-new-attack-in-the-middle-east-en/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">360’s blog</a></u>.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">For a detailed report on the Pierogi campaign, please see <a href="/blog/new-cyber-espionage-campaigns-targeting-palestinians-part-2-the-discovery-of-the-new-mysterious-pierogi-backdoor" rel="noopener" target="_blank">part 2</a> of this research.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The creators of the Spark backdoor use several techniques to evade detection and stay under the radar. They pack the malware with a powerful commercial tool called <u><a href="https://enigmaprotector.com/en/home.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Enigma Packer</a></u> and implement language checks to ensure the victims are Arabic speaking. This minimizes the risk of detection and infection of unwanted victims.</p>
<a id="key-points" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h3 style="text-align: justify;">Key Points</h3>
<ul style="text-align: justify;">
<li><strong>Cyber Espionage in the Middle East: </strong>The Cybereason Nocturnus team has discovered several recent, targeted attacks in the Middle East. These attacks deliver the Spark and Pierogi backdoors for politically-driven cyber espionage operations.</li>
<li><strong>Targeting Palestinians: </strong>The campaigns seems to target Palestinian individuals and entities, likely related to the Palestinian government.</li>
<li><strong>Politically-motivated APT:</strong> Cybereason suspects that the objective of the threat actor is to obtain sensitive information from the victims and leverage it for political purposes.</li>
<li><strong>Lured Into Deploying a Backdoor: </strong>The attackers use specially crafted lure content to trick targets into opening malicious files that infect the victim’s machine with a backdoor. The lure content in the malicious files relates to political affairs in the Middle East, with specific references to the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, tension between Hamas and Fatah, and other political entities in the region.</li>
<li><strong style="color: #596a6f; word-spacing: -0.1em;">Perpetrated by an Arabic-Speaking APT Group: </strong><span style="color: #596a6f; word-spacing: -0.1em;">The modus-operandi of the attackers in conjunction with the social engineering tactics and decoy content seem aligned with previous attacks carried out by the Arabic-speaking APT group MoleRATs (aka Gaza Cybergang). This group has been operating in the Middle East since 2012.</span></li>
</ul>
<p><span style="color: #596a6f; word-spacing: -0.1em; font-size: 20px;">For a synopsis of this research, check out the <a href="/threat-alert-molerats-pierogis" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Molerats &amp; Pierogis Threat Alert</a>.</span></p>

<a id="suspected-threat-actor" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h3 style="text-align: justify;">Suspected Threat Actor Description</h3>
<p style="text-align: justify;">These attacks show significant similarities to previously documented attacks attributed to the Arabic-speaking threat actor, commonly referred to as the <u><a href="https://attack.mitre.org/groups/G0021/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">MoleRATs group</a></u> (aka, <u><a href="https://securelist.com/gaza-cybergang-group1-operation-sneakypastes/90068/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">The Gaza Cybergang</a></u>, <u>Moonlight</u>, <u><a href="https://www.clearskysec.com/dustysky/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">DustySky</a></u>, Gaza Hacker Team). This group, which has been attributed by various security teams, is believed to be comprised of three subgroups:</p>
<ol style="text-align: justify;">
<li><strong><u><a href="https://securelist.com/gaza-cybergang-group1-operation-sneakypastes/90068/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Gaza Cybergang Group 1</a></u>, also dubbed MoleRATs</strong>: MoleRATs has been active since at least 2012. This Arabic-speaking group uses spear phishing attacks to infect target machines in the Middle East and North Africa with various Remote Access Trojans (RATs). As MoleRATs most prominently targets Palestinian territories, its spear phishing attacks often use attached malicious documents on topical Palestinian Authority-related issues to lure their victims. The group uses a mix of tools and malware, some developed by the group and others that are more generic tools.</li>
<li><strong>Gaza Cybergang Group 2</strong>, also dubbed <u><a href="https://www.kaspersky.com/blog/desert-falcon-arabic-apt/7678/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Desert Falcons</a></u>, <u><a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/actor/aridviper" target="_blank" rel="noopener">APT-C-23</a></u>, <u><a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/actor/aridviper" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Arid Viper</a></u>. This second group is an Arabic-speaking group that mainly targets the Middle East and North Africa, with a few targets in European and Asian countries as well. The group is known for their advanced attacks that leverage custom-built Windows malware (<u><a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/unit42-targeted-attacks-middle-east-using-kasperagent-micropsia/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Kasperagent</a></u>, <u><a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/2017/06/palestine-delphi.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Micropsia</a></u>) as well as Android malware (<u><a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/unit42-targeted-attacks-middle-east-using-kasperagent-micropsia/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Vamp</a></u>, <u><a href="https://blog.trendmicro.com/trendlabs-security-intelligence/new-gnatspy-mobile-malware-family-discovered/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">GnatSpy</a></u>).</li>
<li><strong>Gaza Cybergang Group 3: </strong>This group is believed to be behind <u><a href="https://securelist.com/operation-parliament-who-is-doing-what/85237/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Operation Parliament</a></u>. It is considered to be the most advanced group of the three, and is focused on high-profile targets in the Middle East, North America, Europe and Asia. The group is reported to have previously attacked government institutions, parliaments, senates, diplomatic functions, and even Olympic and other sports bodies.</li>
</ol>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><strong>A Note on Attribution</strong></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">It is important to remember there are many threat actors operating in the Middle East, and often there are overlaps in TTPs, tools, motivation, and victimology. There have been cases in the past where a threat actor attempted to mimic another to thwart attribution efforts, and as such, attribution should rarely be taken as is, but instead with a grain of salt and critical thinking.</p>
<a id="infection-vector" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h3 style="text-align: justify;">Infection Vector - Social Engineering using Targeted Content</h3>
<p style="font-size: 18px; font-weight: normal; text-align: justify;"><strong>Themes of the Content Used to Lure Targets</strong></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">In this attack, the targets are lured to open a document or a link attached to an email. There have been cases in the past where victims also downloaded malicious content from <u><a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/unit42-targeted-attacks-middle-east-using-kasperagent-micropsia/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">fake news websites</a></u>. The names of the files and their content play a major part in luring victims to open them, as they usually relate to current topics pertaining to <u><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamas" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Hamas</a></u>, the <u><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_National_Authority" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Palestinian National Authority</a></u>, or other recent events in the Middle East. The lure documents analyzed by Cybereason in this attack concentrate on the following themes:</p>
<ul style="text-align: justify;">
<li><strong>The Conflict between Hamas and Fatah</strong>: The <u><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatah%E2%80%93Hamas_conflict" target="_blank" rel="noopener">historical rivalry</a></u> between the Hamas and Fatah has resulted in many open battles between the two entities. Since 2006, Hamas has controlled the Gaza strip and Fatah has controlled the West Bank.</li>
<li><strong>Matters pertaining to the Israeli-Palestinian Conflict: </strong>Some of the documents in this campaign reference different aspects of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, and the efforts for ceasefire and peace processes between the Israelis and the Palestinians, including the latest peace plan made by President <u><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trump_peace_plan" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Donald Trump and Senior Advisor to the President of the United States Jared Kushner</a></u>.</li>
<li><strong>Vigilance Following Soleimani’s Assassination: </strong>One of the lure documents mentions sources in Lebanon that report a state of alert and vigilance amongst Iranian, Syrian, and Lebasense militias following <u><a href="https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/world-middle-east-50979463" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Soleimani’s assassination</a></u>.</li>
<li><strong>Tensions Between Hamas and the Egyptian Government: </strong>Egypt plays a major role as a mediator in the Israeli-Palestinian confict and has brokered several ceasefire deals and other negotiations in the past. Changes to Egypt’s internal political climate are known to have affected Egyptian government relations with Hamas over the years. It was recently reported that <u><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ismail_Haniyeh" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Ismail Haniyeh</a></u>, the head of Hamas’ political Bureau, had a <u><a href="https://aawsat.com/english/home/article/2111556/haniyeh-settles-qatar-has-no-plans-return-gaza" target="_blank" rel="noopener">falling-out with the Egyptian government</a></u> over his visit to Tehran to participate in General Qasem Soleimani’s funeral, following <u><a href="https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/world-middle-east-50979463" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Soleimani’s assassination</a></u>.</li>
</ul>

<a id="spark-backdoor-analysis" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h3 style="text-align: justify;">Spark Backdoor Analysis&nbsp;</h3>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The Spark payload is a custom backdoor likely developed by the MoleRATs group. In addition to known generic malware (such as: <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/software/S0385/">njRAT</a>, <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/software/S0012/">Poison Ivy</a>, <a href="https://www.sans.org/reading-room/whitepapers/malicious/xtremerat-unicode-breaks-35897">XtremeRAT</a>), the MoleRATs group has been known to develop its own custom tools such as <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/software/S0062/">DustySky</a>, the <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/details/win.molerat_loader">MoleRAT Loader</a> and <a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/unit42-the-tophat-campaign-attacks-within-the-middle-east-region-using-popular-third-party-services/">Scote</a>. We believe this backdoor is relatively new and seems to have appeared starting in the beginning of 2019.&nbsp;</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The name Spark is derived from the PDB path left in a few of the backdoor binaries:&nbsp;</p>
<ul style="text-align: justify;">
<li>W:\Visual Studio 2017\Spark4.2\Release\Spark4.2.pdb</li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The Spark backdoor allows the attackers to:</p>
<ul style="text-align: justify;">
<li>Collect information about the infected machine.</li>
<li>Encrypt the collected data and send it to the attackers over the HTTP protocol.</li>
<li>Download additional payloads.</li>
<li>Log keystrokes.</li>
<li>Record audio using the computer’s microphone.</li>
<li>Execute commands on the infected machine.</li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The creators of the Spark backdoor use a few techniques that are intended to keep the backdoor under-the-radar, including:&nbsp;</p>
<ul style="text-align: justify;">
<li>Packing the payloads with the Enigma packer.</li>
<li>Checking for antivirus and other security products using WMI.</li>
<li>Validating Arabic keyboard and language settings on the infected machine.</li>
</ul>

<h3 style="text-align: justify;">Checking for the Arabic Language</h3>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Another evasive mechanism used by the backdoor is how it checks whether an Arabic keyboard and Arabic language settings are used on the infected machine. <li>It can thwart detection by automated analysis engines and sandbox solutions.</li>

<a id="conclusion" data-hs-anchor="true"></a>
<h3 style="text-align: justify;">Conclusion</h3>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The Spark campaign detailed in this blog demonstrates how the tense geopolitical climate in the Middle East is used by threat actors to lure victims and infect them with the Spark backdoor for cyber espionage purposes.&nbsp;</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The names of the files and decoy content seem to be carefully crafted, often referencing controversial and topical political issues. Cybereason estimates that the files are specifically meant to lure and appeal to victims from the Middle East, especially towards individuals and entities in the Palestinian territories likely related to the Palestinian government or the Fatah movement.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The techniques, tools, and procedures used in this campaign bear great resemblance to previous attacks attributed to the MoleRATs Group (aka Gaza Cybergang Group), an Arabic-speaking, politically motivated group that has operated in the Middle East since 2012.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Our research demonstrates the efforts used by attackers to reduce the risk of detection of the Spark backdoor by various security products. The backdoor checks for the existence of antivirus and firewall products before it initiates its malicious activity. Importantly, the backdoor simply will not reveal its malicious nature unless Arabic language keyboard and settings are found on the infected machine. This shows how the attackers use this backdoor in a surgical way to exclusively attack specific targets.&nbsp;</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">In addition, analysis of these backdoor delivery methods also highlights a trend by many threat actors where they use legitimate storage platforms to deliver the initial stages of the attack. By storing malicious content on trusted platforms like DropBox, attackers reduce the risk of detection by certain security solutions that are gaining popularity, like email filters.&nbsp;</p>
