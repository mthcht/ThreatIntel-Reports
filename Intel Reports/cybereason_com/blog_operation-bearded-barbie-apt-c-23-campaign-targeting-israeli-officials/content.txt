      <h1><span id="hs_cos_wrapper_name" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="text">Operation Bearded Barbie: APT-C-23 Campaign Targeting Israeli Officials</span></h1>
      <div class="cr-mln__post-author-share">
      <div id="hubspot-author_data" class="hubspot-editable cr-mln__post-meta" data-hubspot-form-id="author_data" data-hubspot-name="Blog Author">
        
          <span class="descriptor">Written By</span>
          <p><span class="author">Cybereason Nocturnus</span></p>
         
      </div>
    </div>
     
    </div>
    
    <!-- Sticky Author and Social Box -->
    
    <!-- END Sticky Author and Social Box -->
      <div class="container-is-blog columns is-multiline page-center cr-mln__blog-post--body">
        
        <div class="column is-7-fullhd is-7-desktop is-10-tablet is-10-mobile is-offset-1-fullhd is-offset-1-desktop is-offset-1-tablet is-offset-1-mobile">
          <span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><p>Over the last several years, the <a href="/blog/authors/cybereason-nocturnus" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Cybereason Nocturnus Team</span></a> has been tracking different APT groups operating in the Middle East region, including two main sub-groups of the Hamas cyberwarfare division: <a href="/hubfs/dam/collateral/reports/Molerats-in-the-Cloud-New-Malware-Arsenal-Abuses-Cloud-Platforms-in-Middle-East-Espionage-Campaign.pdf" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Molerats</span></a> and <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/actor/aridviper" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>APT-C-23</span></a>. Both groups are Arabic-speaking and politically-motivated that operate on behalf of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamas#:~:text=Hamas%20(UK%3A%20%2Fh%C3%A6,%2C%20militant%2C%20and%20nationalist%20organization." rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Hamas</span></a>, the Palestinian Islamic-fundamentalist movement and a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_designated_terrorist_groups"><span>terrorist organization</span></a> that has controlled the Gaza strip since 2006.</p>
<!--more-->
<p>While most of the <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/2022/02/arid-viper-targets-palestine.html" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>previously reported</span></a> APT-C-23 <a href="https://news.sophos.com/en-us/2021/11/23/android-apt-spyware-targeting-middle-east-victims-improves-its-capabilities/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>campaigns</span></a> seemed to <a href="/blog/new-cyber-espionage-campaigns-targeting-palestinians-part-2-the-discovery-of-the-new-mysterious-pierogi-backdoor" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>target Arabic-speaking individuals</span></a> in the Middle East, Cybereason recently discovered a new elaborate campaign targeting Israeli individuals, among them, a group of high-profile targets working for sensitive defense, law enforcement, and emergency services organizations.</p>
<p>The campaign operators use sophisticated social engineering techniques, ultimately aimed to deliver previously undocumented backdoors for Windows and Android devices. The goal behind the attack was to extract sensitive information from the victims devices for espionage purposes.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Our investigation reveals that APT-C-23 has effectively upgraded its malware arsenal with new tools, dubbed <em>Barb(ie) Downloader</em> and <em>BarbWire Backdoor,</em> which are equipped with enhanced stealth and a focus on operational security. The new campaign that targets Israeli individuals seems to have a dedicated infrastructure that is almost completely separated from the known APT-C-23 infrastructure which is assessed to be more focused on Arabic-speaking targets.&nbsp;</p>
<h2 style="font-size: 30px;">Key Findings</h2>
<ul>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>New Espionage Campaign Targeting Israelis: </strong>Cybereason discovered a new and elaborate campaign that targets Israeli individuals and officials. The campaign is characterized as an espionage campaign aiming to steal sensitive information from PCs and mobile devices belonging to a chosen target group of Israeli individuals working for law enforcement, military and emergency services.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Attribution to APT-C-23: </strong>Based on our investigation and previous knowledge of the group, Cybereason assesses with moderate-high confidence that the group behind the new campaign is APT-C-23, an Arabic-speaking, politically motivated group believed to be operating on behalf of Hamas.&nbsp;</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Social Engineering as Primary Infection Vector:</strong> The attackers used fake Facebook profiles to trick specific individuals into downloading trojanized direct message applications for Android and PC, which granted them access to the victims’ devices.&nbsp;</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Upgraded Malware Arsenal: </strong>The new campaign consists of two previously undocumented malware, dubbed <em>Barb(ie) Downloader</em> and <em>BarbWire Backdoor</em>, both of which use an enhanced stealth mechanism to remain undetected. In addition, Cybereason observed an upgraded version of an Android implant dubbed <em>VolatileVenom</em>.&nbsp;</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>APT-C-23 Stepping Up Their Game: </strong>Until recently, the group has been using known tools which served them for years, and were known for their relatively unsophisticated tools and techniques. The analysis of this recent campaign shows that the group has revamped their toolset and playbook.&nbsp;</li>
</ul>
<h2 style="font-size: 30px;">Luring the Victims: A Wolf in a Beauty’s Clothing&nbsp;</h2>
<p>To get to their targets, APT-C-23 has set up a network of fake Facebook profiles that are highly maintained and constantly interacting with many Israeli citizens. The social engineering tactic used in this campaign relies mostly on classic <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catfishing" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>catfishing</span></a>, using fake identities of attractive young women to engage with mostly male individuals to gain their trust.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>
<p>These fake accounts have operated for months, and seem relatively authentic to the unsuspecting user. The operators seem to have invested considerable effort in “tending” these profiles, expanding their social network by joining popular Israeli groups, writing posts in Hebrew, and adding friends of the potential victims as friends:</p>

<h2 style="font-size: 30px;">From Chat to Infection</h2>
<p>After gaining the victim’s trust, the operator of the fake account suggests migrating the conversation from Facebook over to WhatsApp. By doing so, the operator quickly obtains the target's mobile number. In many cases, the content of the chat revolves around sexual themes, and the operators often suggest to the victims that they should use a “safer” and more “discrete” means of communication, suggesting a designated app for Android.&nbsp;</p>
<p>In addition, they also entice the victims to open a .rar file containing a video that supposedly contains explicit sexual content. However, when the users open the video they are infected with malware.&nbsp;</p>

<h2 style="font-size: 30px;">Stage One: Barb(ie) Downloader</h2>
<p>Barb(ie) is a downloader component used by APT-C-23 to install the BarbWire backdoor. As mentioned above, in the infection phase the downloader is delivered alongside a video in a .rar file. The video is meant to distract the victim from the infection process that is happening in the background.&nbsp;</p>
<p>The downloader sample analyzed in this section is named<em> “Windows Notifications.exe”</em>. When first executed, Barb(ie) decrypts strings using a custom base64 algorithm that is also used in the BarbWire backdoor. Those decrypted strings are different Virtual Machine vendor names, WMI queries, command and control (C2), file and folders names which are used in different phases of the execution.</p>
<p>One way the malware uses those strings is in performing multiple checks, such as anti-vm and anti-analysis checks, in order to determine that “the coast is clear.” If the check fails, a custom pop-up message is displayed to the user and the malware terminates itself:</p>

<h2 style="font-size: 30px;">BarbWire Backdoor</h2>
<h3 style="font-size: 24px; font-weight: normal;"><span style="color: #434343;">Background and Capabilities</span></h3>
<p>The backdoor component of APT-C-23’s operation is a very capable piece of malware, and it is obvious that a lot of effort was put into hiding its capabilities using a custom base64 algorithm. Its main goal is to fully compromise the victim machine, gaining access to their most sensitive data. The backdoor’s main capabilities include:</p>
<ul>
<li aria-level="1">Persistence</li>
<li aria-level="1">OS Reconnaissance&nbsp;</li>
<li aria-level="1">Data encryption</li>
<li aria-level="1">Keylogging</li>
<li aria-level="1">Screen capturing</li>
<li aria-level="1">Audio recording</li>
<li aria-level="1">Download additional malware</li>
<li aria-level="1">Local/external drives and directory enumeration</li>
<li aria-level="1">Steal specific file types and exfiltrate data</li>
</ul>

<h3 style="font-weight: normal; font-size: 24px;"><span style="color: #434343;">Data Collection and Exfiltration</span></h3>
<p>The BarbWire backdoor can steal a wide range of file types, depending on the instructions it receives from its operators. It specifically looks for certain file extensions such as PDF files, Office documents, archives, videos, and images.</p>
<p>In addition to the local drives found on the host, it also looks for external media such as a CD-Rom drive. <h3 style="font-weight: normal; font-size: 24px;"><span style="color: #666666;">Capabilities&nbsp;</span><span style="color: #666666;"></span></h3>

<p>The main espionage capabilities are the following:&nbsp;</p>
<ul>
<li aria-level="1">Steal SMS messages&nbsp;</li>
<li aria-level="1">Read contact list information</li>
<li aria-level="1">Use the device camera to take photos</li>
<li aria-level="1">Steal files with the following extensions: pdf, doc, docs, ppt, pptx, xls, xlsx, txt, text</li>
<li aria-level="1">Steal images with the following extensions: jpg, jpeg, png</li>
<li aria-level="1">Record audio</li>
<li aria-level="1">Use Phishing to steal credentials to popular apps such as Facebook and Twitter</li>
<li aria-level="1">Discard system notifications</li>
<li aria-level="1">Get installed applications</li>
<li aria-level="1">Restart Wi-Fi</li>
<li aria-level="1">Record calls / WhatsApp calls</li>
<li aria-level="1">Extract call logs</li>
<li aria-level="1">Download files to the infected device&nbsp;</li>
<li aria-level="1">Take screenshots</li>
<li aria-level="1">Read notifications of the following apps: WhatsApp, Facebook, Telegram, Instagram, Skype, IMO, Viber</li>
<li aria-level="1">Discards any notifications raised by the system</li>

<h3 style="font-size: 24px; font-weight: normal;"><span style="color: #666666;">C2 Communication&nbsp;</span></h3>
<p>VolatileVenom uses HTTPS and Firebase Cloud Messaging (FCM) for C2 communication. The application appears to have two methods to retrieve the C2 domain:</p>
<p>First the malware decrypts a hard coded encrypted domain which is encrypted and encoded with AES and Base64. The encrypted domain is retrieved from a .so (shared object)&nbsp;file. The app loads the .so file (named “<em>liboxygen.so</em>” in the analyzed sample) , and executes a function (named “<em>do932()” </em>in the analyzed sample) that returns the encrypted domain:</p>

In case the attackers wish to update the C2 domain, they may send an SMS message containing a new C2 domain to the infected device. The malware intercepts every SMS message, and if a message arrives from the attackers, the malware will extract the new C2 domain to be used:</p>

<h2 style="font-size: 30px;">Conclusion</h2>
<p>In this report, the Cybereason Nocturnus Team investigated an active espionage campaign that victimizes Israeli citizens, among them high profile targets, for espionage purposes. The campaign featured a classic social engineering tactic known as catfishing, where the group used sexual content in order to lure their victims, mostly Israeli men, into downloading malware.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Cybereason assesses with moderate-high confidence that APT-C-23, a politically-motivated APT group that operates on behalf of Hamas, is behind the campaign detailed in this report. While the APT-C-23 operations against Arab-speaking targets (mostly Palestinians) are still taking place, this newly identified campaign specifically targets Israelis and shows unique characteristics that distinguish it from other campaigns. The attackers use a completely new infrastructure that is distinct from the known infrastructure used to target Palestinians and other Arabic-speakers. In addition, all three malware in use were also specifically designed to be used against Israeli targets, and were not observed being used against other targets.&nbsp;</p>
<p>The Cybereason investigation found that some victims were infected with both PC and Android malware dubbed <em>Barb(ie) Downloader</em>, <em>BarbWire Backdoor</em>, and <em>VolatileVenom</em>. This “tight grip” on their targets attests to how important and sensitive this campaign was for the threat actors.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Lastly, this campaign shows a considerable step-up in APT-C-23 capabilities, with upgraded stealth, more sophisticated malware, and perfection of their social engineering techniques which involve offensive HUMINT capabilities using a very active and well-groomed network of fake Facebook accounts that have been proven quite effective for the group.</p>
<p>Cybereason contacted Facebook and reported the fake accounts.</p>
