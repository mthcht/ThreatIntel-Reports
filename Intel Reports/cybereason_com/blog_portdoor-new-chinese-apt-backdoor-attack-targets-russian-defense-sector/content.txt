      <h1><span id="hs_cos_wrapper_name" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="text">PortDoor: New Chinese APT Backdoor Attack Targets Russian Defense Sector</span></h1>
      <div class="cr-mln__post-author-share">
      <div id="hubspot-author_data" class="hubspot-editable cr-mln__post-meta" data-hubspot-form-id="author_data" data-hubspot-name="Blog Author">
        
          <span class="descriptor">Written By</span>
          <p><span class="author">Cybereason Nocturnus</span></p>
         
      </div>
    </div>
     
    </div>
    
    <!-- Sticky Author and Social Box -->
    
    <!-- END Sticky Author and Social Box -->
      <div class="container-is-blog columns is-multiline page-center cr-mln__blog-post--body">
        
        <div class="column is-7-fullhd is-7-desktop is-10-tablet is-10-mobile is-offset-1-fullhd is-offset-1-desktop is-offset-1-tablet is-offset-1-mobile">
          <span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><p>The <a href="/blog/authors/cybereason-nocturnus" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Cybereason Nocturnus Team</span></a> has been tracking recent developments in the RoyalRoad<a href="https://nao-sec.org/2020/01/an-overhead-view-of-the-royal-road.html"><span> weaponizer</span></a>, also known as the <em>8.t Dropper/RTF exploit builder</em>. Over the years, this tool has become a part of the arsenal of several Chinese-related threat actors such as <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/actor/tick" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Tick</span></a>, <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/actor/tonto_team" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Tonto Team</span></a> and <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/actor/ta428" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>TA428</span></a>, all of which&nbsp; employ RoyalRoad regularly for spear-phishing in targeted attacks against high-value targets.&nbsp;</p>
<!--more-->
<p>While analyzing newly discovered RoyalRoad samples observed in-the-wild, the Nocturnus Team detected one that not only exhibits anomalous characteristics, but also delivers <em>PortDoor</em> <em>malware,</em> a previously undocumented backdoor assessed to have been developed by a threat actor likely operating on behalf of Chinese state-sponsored interests.</p>
<p>According to the phishing lure content examined, the target of the attack was a general director working at the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubin_Design_Bureau" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Rubin Design Bureau</span></a>, a Russian-based defense contractor that designs nuclear submarines for the Russian Federation’s Navy.&nbsp;</p>
<h3 style="text-align: left; font-size: 36px; font-weight: normal;">Key Findings</h3>
<ul>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>RoyalRoad Variants are Under Development:</strong> The variant of the RoyalRoad weaponizer examined altered its encoded payload from the known “8.t” file to a new filename: “e.o”. More new variants are likely to be under development as well.</li>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Previously Undocumented Backdoor:</strong> The newly discovered RoyalRoad RTF variant examined also drops a previously undocumented and stealthy backdoor dubbed <em>PortDoor</em> which is designed with obfuscation and persistence in mind.&nbsp;</li>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Highly Targeted Attack:</strong> The threat actor is specifically targeting the Rubin Design Bureau, a part of the Russian defense sector designing submarines for the Russian Federation’s Navy.</li>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Extensive Malware Capabilities: </strong>Portdoor has multiple functionalities, including the ability to do reconnaissance, target profiling, delivery of additional payloads, privilege escalation, process manipulation static detection antivirus evasion, one-byte XOR encryption, AES-encrypted data exfiltration and more.</li>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>APT Group Operating on Behalf of Chinese State Interests:</strong> The accumulated evidence such as the infection vector, social engineering style, use of RoyalRoad against similar targets, and other similarities between the newly discovered backdoor sample and other known Chinese APT malware all bear the hallmarks of a threat actor operating on behalf of Chinese state-sponsored interests.</li>
</ul>
<h3 style="text-align: left; font-size: 36px; font-weight: normal;">Analysis of the Spear-Phishing Attack:&nbsp;Intro to RoyalRoad</h3>
<p><a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/details/win.8t_dropper"><span>RoyalRoad</span></a> is a tool that generates weaponized RTF documents that exploit the following vulnerabilities in Microsoft’s <a href="https://support.microsoft.com/en-us/office/equation-editor-6eac7d71-3c74-437b-80d3-c7dea24fdf3f" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Equation Editor</span></a>: <a href="https://cve.mitre.org/cgi-bin/cvename.cgi?name=CVE-2017-11882" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>CVE-2017-11882</span></a>, <a href="https://cve.mitre.org/cgi-bin/cvename.cgi?name=CVE-2018-0798" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>CVE-2018-0798</span></a> and <a href="https://cve.mitre.org/cgi-bin/cvename.cgi?name=CVE-2018-0802" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>CVE-2018-0802</span></a>. RoyalRoad is used primarily by threat actors considered to be operating on behalf of Chinese state interests (e.g <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/actor/tick" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Tick</span></a>, <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/actor/tonto_team" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Tonto Team</span></a>, <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/actor/ta428" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>TA428</span></a>, <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/actor/hellsing" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Goblin Panda</span></a>, <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/actor/rancor" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Rancor</span></a>).&nbsp;</p>
<p>RoyalRoad has rather consistent characteristics and most of the weaponized RTF documents usually drop an encoded file named “8.t”, which - once decoded - can deliver a variety of payloads for different threat actors.&nbsp;</p>
<p>In this report, we discuss a deviation from the “classic” RoyalRoad characteristics. The dropped object name was changed from the very consistent “8.t” naming convention to the new “e.o” file name.</p>
<h3 style="font-size: 36px; font-weight: normal;">Spear-Phishing Email Delivers RoyalRoad RTF</h3>
<p>The initial infection vector is a spear-phishing email addressed to the “respectful general director Igor Vladimirovich” at the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubin_Design_Bureau" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Rubin Design Bureau</span></a>, a submarine design center from the “<a href="https://www.gidropribor.ru/en/"><span>Gidropribor</span></a>” concern in St. Petersburg, a national research center that designs underwater weapons like submarines:</p>
<p><img src="https://lh6.googleusercontent.com/ezFbLLvGk_Og1uO9pstRJ-vA3NE2l8KKfbNZ6cnpI4XahdQHvGT3oZweWKLnPfKp10a7KRhq_jBwUXjUXXagHUs2Whl5bkzTleypib4jDH_eTIiyTWHouXVwi9WJu5eyGIg2mhHd" width="624" height="501" loading="lazy" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block;"></p>

<p>The email attachment is a malicious RTF document weaponized with a RoyalRoad payload, with content describing a general view of an <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autonomous_underwater_vehicle" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>autonomous underwater vehicle</span></a>:</p>
<p><img src="https://lh6.googleusercontent.com/PSF2h7kLwVhZed85EH-tDSvldXgaE4ALKlAlbVgIzS0gYb6w97x-QqzSWZ922kIhtKISC7WS8p0hCC0h50hwyOgBYhE_2ybH2f6DMEeXKlS-J4mn5Og9y-8xOjJy86b-xetegY55" width="624" height="335" loading="lazy" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block;"></p>
<p style="text-align: center; font-size: 16px;"><em>Content of the weaponized RTF document</em></p>
<p>The creation time of the RTF is timestomped to 2007, presumably to thwart investigation or detection efforts. <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1070/006/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Timestomping</a> is a known technique used by <a href="/blog/back-to-the-future-inside-the-kimsuky-kgh-spyware-suite" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>threat actors</span></a> to try and remain under the radar:</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://lh6.googleusercontent.com/UU4il_aBVSs7lVgbdZn6jBGihUmo74dKpZrEoCCBhcrDNJa7TQ1iFOG2hgMeGgtb8R7r5xtyDLmh5o8ghX-X2_k0OryUUyY2YWt06O_QMsms3Iz-XnWYFS9oJBor_NdkgF4DexoW" width="258" height="144" loading="lazy" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block;"></p>

This technique is used by various actors to bypass detection of automatic execution persistence, since Word must be relaunched in order to trigger the add-in file, making the persistence mechanism less “noisy”.&nbsp;</p>

<h3 style="font-size: 36px; font-weight: normal;">PortDoor Backdoor Analysis</h3>
<p>The dropped payload, named “winlog.wll”, is a previously undocumented backdoor. Its main capabilities include:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<div>Gathering reconnaissance and profiling of the victim’s machine</div>
</li>
<li>
<div>Receiving commands and downloading additional payloads from the C2 server</div>
</li>
<li>
<div>Communicating with the C2 server using raw socket as well as HTTP over port 443 with proxy authentication support</div>
</li>
<li>
<div>Privilege escalation and process manipulation</div>
</li>
<li>
<div>Dynamic API resolving for static detection evasion&nbsp;</div>
</li>
<li>
<div>One byte XOR encryption of sensitive data and configuration strings&nbsp;</div>
</li>
<li>
<div>The collected information is AES-encrypted before it is sent to the C2 server</div>
</li>
</ul>
<br>

<h3 style="text-align: left; font-weight: normal; font-size: 36px;">Attribution</h3>
<p>At the time of this analysis, there was not enough information available to attribute the newly discovered backdoor to a known threat actor with reasonable certainty. However, there are a couple of known Chinese APT groups that share quite a few similarities with the threat actor behind the new malware samples analyzed in this blog.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Based on previous work done by <a href="https://nao-sec.org/2020/01/an-overhead-view-of-the-royal-road.html"><span>nao_sec</span></a>, the Nocturnus Team was able to determine that the RTF file discussed in this blog was weaponized with RoyalRoad v7, which bears the indicative “b0747746” header encoding and was previously observed being used by the Tonto Team, TA428 and Rancor threat actors, as can be seen below:&nbsp;</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://lh4.googleusercontent.com/F-wBBwHZZXGLdVo3cAegTFUqlymJ33csfJB3qCr-YnqA36XTlCRcbcjIrpLtbCpgWCZ6Sh8lHwo8hyAYVRHYHTBh9F8VmB_vS7aIjvc7X4vshwW3p8O1yOXTB8PqSAYr8rIsATVg" width="624" height="272" loading="lazy" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block;"></p>
<p style="text-align: center; font-size: 16px;"><em>RoyalRoad attribution matrix. Credit: nao_sec</em></p>
<p>Both the Tonto Team and TA428 threat actors have been observed attacking Russian organizations in the past, and more specifically attacking research and defense related targets. For example, it was previously reported that Tonto Team is known to have <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/2020/03/bisonal-10-years-of-play.html" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>attacked Russian organizations</span></a> in the past using the <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/2020/03/bisonal-10-years-of-play.html" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Bisonal</span></a> malware.</p>
<p>When comparing the spear-phishing email and malicious documents in these attacks with <a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/unit42-bisonal-malware-used-attacks-russia-south-korea/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>previously examined phishing emails and lure documents</span></a> used by the Tonto Team to attack Russian organizations, there are certain similarities in the linguistic and visual style used by the attackers in the phishing emails and documents.&nbsp;</p>
<p>The newly discovered backdoor does not seem to share significant code similarities with previously known malware used by the abovementioned groups, other than anecdotal similarities that are quite common to backdoors, leading us to the conclusion that it is not a variant of a known malware, but is in fact novel malware that was developed recently.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Lastly, we are also aware that there could be other groups, known or yet unknown, that could be behind the attack and the development of the PortDoor backdoor. We hope that as time goes by, and with more evidence gathered, the attribution could be more concrete.&nbsp;</p>
<h3 style="text-align: left; font-size: 36px; font-weight: normal;">Conclusion</h3>
<p>RoyalRoad has been one of the most used RTF weaponizers in the Chinese threat actors sphere in recent years. It is mostly observed in the initial compromise phase of targeted attacks where spear-phishing is used to lure victims into opening malicious documents which in turn exploit Microsoft Equation Editor vulnerabilities to drop different malware.&nbsp;</p>
<p>In this report, we discussed the latest changes that were made to the RoyalRoad weaponizer that deviate from some of its well-documented and predictable indicators. It is perhaps an indication that the threat actors who are operating it are attempting to avoid “low hanging fruit” detections.</p>
<p>In addition, we reported the discovery of the novel PortDoor backdoor, a previously undocumented and stealthy tool designed to grant the attackers access to their targets’ machines, collect information, and deploy additional payloads.&nbsp;</p>
<p>At the time of writing this report, it is still unclear which threat actor is behind the new backdoor, however we have identified two potential suspects that fit the profile. Currently there is not enough information available to prove the stated hypothesis with a high level of certainty.</p>
