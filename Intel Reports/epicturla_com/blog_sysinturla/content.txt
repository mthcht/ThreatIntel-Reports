</div></div><div class="sqs-block horizontalrule-block sqs-block-horizontalrule" data-block-type="47" id="block-yui_3_17_2_1_1590686077237_9004"><div class="sqs-block-content"><hr /></div></div><div class="sqs-block html-block sqs-block-html" data-block-type="2" id="block-yui_3_17_2_1_1590686077237_9065"><div class="sqs-block-content">

<div class="sqs-html-content">
  <p class="" style="white-space:pre-wrap;">Today’s threat actor of choice is one of my favorites, Turla (namesake of this blog). This prolific threat actor relies on a variety of toolkits (including Skipper, IcedCoffee, KopiLuwak among others). In the past two weeks alone, two distinct clusters of their activities piqued the interest of multiple research groups (see: <a href="https://www.leonardocompany.com/documents/20142/10868623/Malware+Technical+Insight+_Turla+%E2%80%9CPenquin_x64%E2%80%9D.pdf/ec1e649f-2b41-d608-72dc-0f774571a692?t=1589442318369">Leonardo’s ‘Penquin_x64’</a> and <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/ESET_Turla_ComRAT.pdf">ESET’s COMrat v4</a> reports), but their bag of tricks is hardly exhausted. This short ‘tipper’ will discuss Kazuar and a universal love for Mark Russinovich’s SysInternal Tools.</p><h3 style="white-space:pre-wrap;">A Dangerous Bird with Odd Plumage</h3>

<div class="sqs-html-content">
  <p class="" style="white-space:pre-wrap;">In 2017, Brandon Levene, Robert Falcone, and Tyler Halfpop of Palo Alto networks <a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/unit42-kazuar-multiplatform-espionage-backdoor-api-access/">discovered</a> a new toolkit they called ‘Kazuar’. The name is a transliteration of the Russian term for the Cassowary, a flightless yet somehow dangerous bird.  The malware is a .NET-based trojan obfuscated with ConfuserEx. Oddly enough, the malware includes artifacts suggesting multi-platform variants for MacOS and *nix, though neither were found in-the-wild as far as I know. The Palo Alto report is excellent and can be relied upon for a breakdown of the malware’s behavior. Sporadic samples of Kazuar were reported in 2017-2018.</p>

  <p class="" style="white-space:pre-wrap;">As of 2019, it appears that Turla operators decided to apply different ‘window dressing’ to their Kazuar deployments. –one with security appeal at that. <div class="sqs-html-content">
  <p class="" style="white-space:pre-wrap;">Newer Kazuar samples are (poorly) branded to look like a <a href="https://docs.microsoft.com/en-us/sysinternals/downloads/debugview">SysInternal tool called ‘DebugView’</a>. While the original enables users to monitor debug output from local and remote systems, Turla’s new Kazuar samples enable a far more nefarious remote monitoring. Apart from cosmetic changes, the new Kazuar samples no longer rely on ConfuserEx. The new .NET obfuscator is a <em>pain in the ass</em> and defied my google dorking method of identification. However, it appears to continue to rely on the DLL injection mechanism into explorer.exe described in the Palo Alto blog.</p><p class="" style="white-space:pre-wrap;"><br></p>
