					<style>.blog-text > h3 {color: #693840;font-family:"NeueMachina-Regular", Sans-serif;}.blog-text > h2 {color: #E82F49;font-family:"NeueMachina-Regular", Sans-serif;}.blog-text > img{width: 100%}ul {list-style: none;}.blog-text ul li::before {content: "\2022";color: #E82F49;font-weight: bold;display: inline-block;width: 1em; margin-left: -1em;}figure > div {width: 100%;}td {padding: 5px}figure figcaption {text-align: center;}</style>
<div class="blog-text">
<p><a href="https://blackhat.com/us-23/training/schedule/#atm-breaking-for-security-validation-30772" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><img decoding="async" src="https://www.metabaseq.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/ATM-training-banner.png" alt="" width="100%" /></a><br />
<a href="https://blackhat.com/us-23/training/schedule/#payment-systems-the-art-of-analyzing-mag-stripe-tokenization-nfc-and-emv-technologies-30156" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><img decoding="async" src="https://www.metabaseq.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/Payment-banner.png" alt="" width="100%" /></a></p>
<p><strong><span style="color: #e82f49;">By Jesus Dominguez from Metabase Q&#8217;s Ocelot Team</span></strong></p>
<p>Ploutus, one of the most sophisticated ATM malware families worldwide, is back with a new variant focused on Latin America. <a href="https://www.securityweek.com/new-malware-found-infecting-atms-mexico" data-w-id="7dfb6605-68e3-af8b-3f21-75c0085c595f" data-wf-id="[&quot;7dfb6605-68e3-af8b-3f21-75c0085c595f&quot;]" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input">Discovered for the first time in 2013</a>, Ploutus enables criminals to empty ATMs by taking advantage of ATM XFS middleware vulnerabilities via an externally connected device. Since its first discovery, Ploutus has evolved to target various XFS middleware types, focusing on banks across Mexico and Latin America. Previously, researchers have discovered the following variants and associated target middleware:</p>
<figure class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image" data-w-id="8b89630b-ee90-810e-0b74-bb15ce298d3f" data-wf-id="[&quot;8b89630b-ee90-810e-0b74-bb15ce298d3f&quot;]" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input">
<div data-w-id="8b89630b-ee90-810e-0b74-bb15ce298d40" data-wf-id="[&quot;8b89630b-ee90-810e-0b74-bb15ce298d40&quot;]" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input"><img decoding="async" class="aligncenter" src="https://www.metabaseq.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/01/Ploutus-Fig-01-EN-old-brand.png" alt="" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input" data-wf-id="[&quot;8b89630b-ee90-810e-0b74-bb15ce298d41&quot;]" data-w-id="8b89630b-ee90-810e-0b74-bb15ce298d41" /></div>
</figure>
<p data-w-id="c6fe8bdd-ac34-20f7-c92a-adbfc2ab1211" data-wf-id="[&quot;c6fe8bdd-ac34-20f7-c92a-adbfc2ab1211&quot;]" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input">Ocelot, the Offensive Security research team of Metabase Q, identified a new variant of Ploutus in Latin America. This variant, dubbed Ploutus-I, controls ATMs from the Brazilian vendor Itautec. Itautec has been connected to other major ATM players over the years. In 2013, the Japanese manufacturer, OKI, partnered with Itautec to enter the Brazilian market; subsequently, NCR acquired OKI&#8217;s <a href="https://www.ncr.com/news/newsroom/news-releases/company/ncr-and-oki-announce-definitive-acquisition-agreement-for-key-assets-in-brazil" data-w-id="088d9358-f4ab-15eb-2119-8ac6ef024e3e" data-wf-id="[&quot;088d9358-f4ab-15eb-2119-8ac6ef024e3e&quot;]" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input">IT services and selected software in Brazil</a> in 2019.</p>
<p data-w-id="5a4d2a4c-66c5-d03b-5d6a-1bc61679fe78" data-wf-id="[&quot;5a4d2a4c-66c5-d03b-5d6a-1bc61679fe78&quot;]" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input">Throughout this blog, we will describe the details of this new variant. We will cover the infection methodology, AV bypass technique, obfuscation layers, malware interaction with the crooks, and the XFS control to dispense the money on demand.</p>
<h3 data-w-id="fbe315c4-5678-3040-7733-6716d493f227" data-wf-id="[&quot;fbe315c4-5678-3040-7733-6716d493f227&quot;]" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input">Ploutus-I heist operation overview</h3>
<figure class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image" data-w-id="e3cac201-d008-a65a-6622-ccfdbe272ebc" data-wf-id="[&quot;e3cac201-d008-a65a-6622-ccfdbe272ebc&quot;]" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input">
<div data-w-id="e3cac201-d008-a65a-6622-ccfdbe272ebd" data-wf-id="[&quot;e3cac201-d008-a65a-6622-ccfdbe272ebd&quot;]" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input"><img decoding="async" src="https://www.metabaseq.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/01/Ploutus-Fig-02-EN-old-brand.png" alt="" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input" data-wf-id="[&quot;e3cac201-d008-a65a-6622-ccfdbe272ebe&quot;]" data-w-id="e3cac201-d008-a65a-6622-ccfdbe272ebe" /></div>

<h3 data-w-id="7b073161-229c-f36d-ee30-4ccc16feb12e" data-wf-id="[&quot;7b073161-229c-f36d-ee30-4ccc16feb12e&quot;]" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input">Deobfuscating Ploutus-I</h3>
<p data-w-id="5dfc5c3e-6c97-9f0f-0f72-ac1a9d6b0cf0" data-wf-id="[&quot;5dfc5c3e-6c97-9f0f-0f72-ac1a9d6b0cf0&quot;]" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input">Every new variant of Ploutus is harder to deobfuscate, and this last version is not the exception. This section is highly technical but essential to share for researchers to improve awareness and ATM security in the future. If you are not interested in the technical details, please skip to the next section.</p>
<p data-w-id="f191f62c-a090-a2a2-ee75-63942c57484c" data-wf-id="[&quot;f191f62c-a090-a2a2-ee75-63942c57484c&quot;]" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input">Ploutus-I has always been written in .NET Framework as a method of further obfuscation to avoid signature-based detection and to make the reverse engineering task very challenging.</p>
<p data-w-id="c1cc0253-c547-a121-f2f4-34a507c93b7f" data-wf-id="[&quot;c1cc0253-c547-a121-f2f4-34a507c93b7f&quot;]" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input">Before getting into the deobfuscation details, it is imperative to understand how the execution of .NET managed code(C#, F#, etc.) occurs in memory. For a more detailed explanation, we recommend reading <a href="http://www.phrack.org/papers/dotnet_instrumentation.html" data-w-id="04fa34d3-3435-c368-53ab-ca94bc54633a" data-wf-id="[&quot;04fa34d3-3435-c368-53ab-ca94bc54633a&quot;]" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input">Phrack</a>. For this blog, a minimalistic flow is shown in Figure 4.</p>

At runtime, the malicious code is deobfuscated by the malware and then passed to the Just-In-Time (JIT) Compiler to create the native code that ends up been executed by the CPU. How can we recover the source code and understand the inner workings of the malware? Keep reading.</p>

<h3 data-w-id="e292a028-fcb2-6403-5a8c-57ec8f265af4" data-wf-id="[&quot;e292a028-fcb2-6403-5a8c-57ec8f265af4&quot;]" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input">Deobfuscation strategy</h3>
<p data-w-id="9e44d076-90fe-fbe5-f71d-c6ec72170c5f" data-wf-id="[&quot;9e44d076-90fe-fbe5-f71d-c6ec72170c5f&quot;]" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input">Before digging into the deobfuscation strategy, it is crucial to understand how Reactor obfuscator hides the malicious code in memory. We can explain this obfuscation by looking at Figure 8, where:</p>
<blockquote data-w-id="01d1f7b9-541a-14ae-6f65-bbedad874ce1" data-wf-id="[&quot;01d1f7b9-541a-14ae-6f65-bbedad874ce1&quot;]" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input"><p>When a specific MSIL Code Function (let&#8217;s say the Ploutus-I Keyboard one) is called, JIT is going to call getJIT to get the address of the compileMethod to compile it into Native Code</p>
<p><span style="font-size: 16px;">However, Ploutus-I already installed a hook in memory redirecting that address to its own malicious one. It then deobfuscates the function and finally calls the original compileMethod to proceed with normal execution.</span></p></blockquote>
<p data-w-id="4ec49865-0b27-61bd-ebfa-06d3c7955a53" data-wf-id="[&quot;4ec49865-0b27-61bd-ebfa-06d3c7955a53&quot;]" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input">It is worth mentioning that this process is performed at the memory and only for the function been called at that moment, explaining why there is no visibility of functions in DnSpy.</p>

</figure>

<p data-w-id="53e9f199-7b36-1e84-7ba4-a8027fd6499b" data-wf-id="[&quot;53e9f199-7b36-1e84-7ba4-a8027fd6499b&quot;]" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input">For this, we need to switch to a more advanced tool, the Windbg debugger, with its SOS.dll extension to deal with .NET Managed code.</p>
<p data-w-id="1929f10e-a93a-f26c-c677-38e793a9789b" data-wf-id="[&quot;1929f10e-a93a-f26c-c677-38e793a9789b&quot;]" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input">You can see in Figure 9 that we set a breakpoint at function getJit() (exported by clrjit.dll) because it returns a VTable (array of pointers) where the first value is the address of the compileMethod!</p>

<h3 data-w-id="f9cf334a-5eb7-4675-380f-59ca1afd22b9" data-wf-id="[&quot;f9cf334a-5eb7-4675-380f-59ca1afd22b9&quot;]" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input">Understanding Ploutus-I Inner workings</h3>
<p data-w-id="2dc35f40-80c3-e282-006a-357b7c78e0da" data-wf-id="[&quot;2dc35f40-80c3-e282-006a-357b7c78e0da&quot;]" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input">With the MSIL Code in our hands, we can understand what is going on with this new variant. The primary function we focused on is Launcher.KeyBoard::RealStart() since it triggers all the actions executed by the malware. It implements a keylogger (already seen before) to intercept all keys and numbers entered by the mule via an external keyboard. It is essential to mention that this variant was successfully executed in the Windows 7 and Windows 10 versions.</p>
<p data-w-id="fde26ee7-ca5b-0dce-3617-257b2cd41daf" data-wf-id="[&quot;fde26ee7-ca5b-0dce-3617-257b2cd41daf&quot;]" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input">Ploutus-I encrypts all its strings. When needing one of them, the malware will call the instruction <strong data-w-id="aec3cc13-3e37-c36c-c973-92cf4dcfdeab" data-wf-id="[&quot;aec3cc13-3e37-c36c-c973-92cf4dcfdeab&quot;]" data-automation-id="dyn-item-content-input">ldc.14.s </strong>passing an offset as an argument that will be the pointer into a Unicode byte array decrypted from the resources section at runtime pointing to the plaintext value. For example, in Figure 16, the instruction &#8220;ldc.14.s 0x9f0&#8221;, goes to the offset 0x9f0 and returns the string &#8220;F8F1F1&#8221;. You can see all the strings extracted in the Appendix A section at the end.</p>
