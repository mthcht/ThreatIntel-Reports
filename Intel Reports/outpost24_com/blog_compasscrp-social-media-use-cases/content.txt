					<h1 class="entry-title">CompassDRP Social Media integration: Six real-world use cases</h1>					<div class="post-info">
						<span class="post-category">
							<a href="https://outpost24.com/blog/category/research-and-threat-intel/">Research &amp; Threat Intel</a>							</span>
							<span class="post-date">Last updated: 15 Jul 2025</span>
						</div>
					</div>
											<div class="article-author">
							<span class="written-by">Written By</span>
																							<div class="author-container">
									<span class="author-name"><a href="https://outpost24.com/blog/authors/krakenlabs/">KrakenLabs</a></span>
									<span class="author-title">Threat Intelligence Team, Outpost24</span>
								</div>
													</div>
										
<p>Social media can work both for and against an organization, so it’s worth treating these sites as extensions of your attack surface. CompassDRP’s Social Media integration continuously monitors both corporate and employee profiles across platforms such as Twitter, LinkedIn, and Facebook. It automatically flags unauthorized or impersonating accounts that mimic executive identities or misuse company branding, helping to thwart phishing and fraud campaigns before they gain traction. Security teams are also alerted the moment breaches or leaks first appear on social channels.</p>



<p>Additionally, it detects accidental or malicious sharing of confidential information by employees in public posts, enabling prompt remediation. By catching these social-media–based threats early, organizations can prevent attackers from exploiting publicly available intelligence for targeted attacks or reputational damage. In this post, we’ll walk through how these use cases work in detail with a real-world example.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-how-our-real-world-example-works"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="how-our-real-world-example-works"></span>How our real-world example works<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<p>To walk through a realistic example of how you could apply the power of CompassDRP to your business, we’ve chosen <em>Mercadona</em>, a Spanish retail store. We’ll also use their President <em>Juan Roig</em>, and some key brands&nbsp;(<em>Hacendado</em> and <em>Deliplus</em>). To be clear, we’ve chose <em>Mercadona</em> purely for illustrative purposes and will only show screenshots of publicly available social posts. So the ‘seed terms’ we’ll monitor for in this example are:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Mercadona</li>



<li>Juan Roig</li>



<li>Hacendado</li>



<li>Deliplus</li>
</ul>



<p><strong>Use cases we’ll walk through:</strong>&nbsp;</p>



<ol start="1" class="wp-block-list">
<li>Identifying content related to social media events&nbsp;</li>



<li>Searching for defamatory content</li>



<li>Early notifications for boycotts or labor conflicts</li>



<li>Discovering general controversies or critics with key words&nbsp;</li>



<li>Detecting fraudulent or uncontrolled accounts&nbsp;</li>



<li>Notifications around potential cybersecurity incidents</li>
</ol>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-1-identifying-content-related-to-social-media-events"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="1-identifying-content-related-to-social-media-events"></span>1. Identifying content related to social media events<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<p>Whenever there is a <strong>major event</strong> that is going to index a lot of content, we can <strong>create a tag</strong> in advance for <strong>easily identifying</strong> those <strong>entries related to that event</strong>. Fo example, since there has been much talking related to <em>Mercadona</em> and its president after the <a href="https://www.reuters.com/business/environment/spains-deadly-dana-weather-phenomenon-its-links-climate-change-2024-10-30/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">DANA floods</a> that happened in Valencia in October 2024, we can create an automation rule for adding a tag to all those entries that include the term DANA in the summary. </p>

<p>Depending on the company’s sector, we can create different searches specific to the kind of <strong>defamatory content </strong>they would be interested in.&nbsp;In our example, <em>Mercadona</em> is a supermarket chain, we might want to look for users criticizing the company’s products or the quality of their providers quality. Accusations that could signal potential health risks would be especially important to stay on top of.&nbsp;Let’s say: <em>cautious, hazardous, risk, concerns, concern, &amp; contamination</em>.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="5-detecting-fraudulent-or-uncontrolled-accounts"></span>5. Detecting fraudulent or uncontrolled accounts&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<p>The CompassDRP platform also allows you to find fraudulent <strong>accounts in different social media platforms</strong> like Mastodon, Facebook, or X. Thes might <strong>include the name of the company</strong> either in the username or in the description included for the account.&nbsp;So for this example, we’ll search: <em>MastodonByUsername, FacebookPlaces, FacebookPages, TwitterProfileData365</em>&nbsp;</p>

<p>In CompassDRP, we can create a search for key words related to <strong>public reporting of cybersecurity incidents</strong> to detect when such events are covered through social media accounts.&nbsp;In this example, we might use:<em> hack, leak, ddos, defacement, hacktivist, hacker</em>&nbsp;</p>
