					<h1 class="entry-title">Threat Context monthly, April 2025: EncryptHub &amp; Media Land leak</h1>					<div class="post-info">
						<span class="post-category">
							<a href="https://outpost24.com/blog/category/research-and-threat-intel/">Research &amp; Threat Intel</a>							</span>
							<span class="post-date">Last updated: 29 Apr 2025</span>
						</div>
					</div>
											<div class="article-author">
							<span class="written-by">Written By</span>
																							<div class="author-container">
									<span class="author-name"><a href="https://outpost24.com/blog/authors/krakenlabs/">KrakenLabs</a></span>
									<span class="author-title">Threat Intelligence Team, Outpost24</span>
								</div>
													</div>
										
<p>Welcome to the Threat Context Monthly blog series where we provide a comprehensive roundup of the most relevant cybersecurity news and threat information from KrakenLabs, Outpost24’s cyber threat intelligence team. Here’s what you need to know from April about EncryptHub, EncryptRAT, and the Media Land leak.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-threat-actor-of-the-month-encrypthub"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="threat-actor-of-the-month-encrypthub"></span>Threat actor of the month: <em>EncryptHub</em><span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<p><strong>&#8220;<em>EncryptHub</em>&#8220;</strong> is a <strong>financially motivated</strong> threat actor first observed in June 2024. The adversary&#8217;s main objective is monetary gain through deploying <strong>ransomware</strong>,<strong> selling exploits</strong>, and <strong>stealing sensitive data</strong> from high-value corporate networks globally.</p>



<p>Outpost24 <a href="https://outpost24.com/blog/unveiling-encrypthub-multi-stage-malware/"><strong>KrakenLabs researchers </strong>observed <em>EncryptHub</em></a><em> </em><strong>distributing trojanized versions of widely used applications</strong>, as well as leveraging the third-party PPI service <strong>LabInstalls</strong> to automate the deployment of malicious executables. These campaigns have led to the <strong>deployment of Powershell-based infostealer</strong> malware, <strong>Rhadamanthys</strong>, and <strong>StealC</strong>. The adversary also used <strong>XMRig</strong> to carry out cryptojacking campaigns.</p>



<p><strong>Operational security mistakes </strong>have allowed <strong>Outpost24 KrakenLabs researchers to map his tactics</strong>, uncovering his multi-stage attack chains and methodologies. The threat actor frequently utilizes <strong>Telegram</strong> channels for managing compromised endpoints and <strong>exfiltrating</strong> stolen data. The adversary was also observed <strong>exploiting vulnerabilities in Fortinet and Palo Alto</strong> Networks systems, leveraging scripts specifically designed to compromise administrative credentials, reset passwords, and maintain persistent access.</p>



<p>Researchers observed that <em>EncryptHub </em>also<strong> relies heavily on </strong><a href="https://outpost24.com/blog/unmasking-encrypthub-chatgpt-partner-crime/"><strong>ChatGPT to assist in their malicious operations</strong></a>. The AI chat bot assisted the adversary in the <strong>creation and configuration</strong> of Telegram <strong>bots</strong>, C2 servers, phishing sites, mail servers, and Onion services, learning how to <strong>create macOS apps</strong>, how to properly use a <strong>REST API</strong>, and how to call PowerShell scripts from Go. Furthermore, <strong>ChatGPT helped to write malware,</strong> such as clippers and cookie stealers, understand and <strong>improve code from other sources</strong>, and how to better integrate it with his current malware.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img decoding="async" width="792" height="710" src="//outpost24.com/wp-content/plugins/a3-lazy-load/assets/images/lazy_placeholder.gif" data-lazy-type="image" data-src="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-4.png" alt="EncryptHub asking ChatGPT for help" class="lazy lazy-hidden wp-image-8149" srcset="" data-srcset="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-4.png 792w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-4-300x269.png 300w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-4-768x688.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 792px) 100vw, 792px" /><noscript><img decoding="async" width="792" height="710" src="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-4.png" alt="EncryptHub asking ChatGPT for help" class="wp-image-8149" srcset="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-4.png 792w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-4-300x269.png 300w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-4-768x688.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 792px) 100vw, 792px" /></noscript><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Figure 1. EncryptHub asking ChatGPT for help. Source: Outpost24</figcaption></figure>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-encryptrat-proprietary-c2-panel"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="encryptrat-%e2%80%93-proprietary-c2-panel"></span>EncryptRAT – Proprietary C2 panel<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p><em>EncryptHub </em>has been developing and improving <strong>EncryptRAT,</strong> a <strong>command-and-control </strong>(C2) <strong>panel</strong>. This tool allows the user to manage <strong>active infections</strong>, send remote commands, manage additional modules, monitor and download <strong>logs</strong> from infected devices and <strong>configure</strong> various <strong>malware samples</strong> and <strong>exfiltration</strong> channels.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-exploits-sales-activities"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="exploits-sales-activities"></span>Exploits sales activities<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p>On March 2025, <em>EncryptHub</em> offered for <strong>sale various exploits</strong> on the underground forum <strong>XSS</strong> including <strong>CVE-2025-26633</strong> –RCE in Microsoft Management Console,<strong> CVE-2025-24061</strong> –SmartScreen &amp; MoTW bypass exploit, and <strong>CVE-2025-24071 </strong>–0-click NTLMv2 hash leak exploit. Surprisingly, the adversary <strong>previously reported the</strong> last two <strong>vulnerabilities</strong> to <strong>Microsoft’s </strong>MSRC under the alias <em>SkorikARI</em>.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-spotlight-threat-bulletproof-hosting-provider-media-land-llc-client-s-data-leaked"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="spotlight-threat-bulletproof-hosting-provider-media-land-llc-clients-data-leaked"></span>Spotlight threat: Bulletproof hosting provider Media Land LLC. client’s data leaked<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<p><strong>Media Land LLC.</strong> is a <strong>bulletproof hosting (BPH) provider</strong> that allows clients to host any type of content or use it for any type of activity regardless of its legitimacy. This BPH is advertised in underground forums by “<em>yalishanda</em>”, moniker <a href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/2019/07/meet-the-worlds-biggest-bulletproof-hoster/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow">identified with individual Alexander Volosovik</a>.</p>



<p>On March 28, 2025, <strong>internal data from Media Land was leaked in a Telegram</strong> channel created on February 23. <a href="https://x.com/PRODAFT/status/1909342469617053720" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow">Researchers point to the significance of the leak</a>, as it contains<strong> records of who purchased servers, who paid for them, and possibly personally identifiable information (PII)</strong>. It is possible that the data <a href="https://x.com/PRODAFT/status/1909321427179667789" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow">was leaked</a> by the same actor that exposed <em>Black Basta</em>&#8216;s internal chat logs.</p>



<p>One of the researchers who informed about the leak <a href="https://x.com/3xp0rtblog/status/1909367871169610215" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow">made an observation</a> regarding the possibility that the threat actor who obtained the database could have been creating fraudulent Telegram channels to deceive users.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-malicious-operations-hosted-on-media-land-llc"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="malicious-operations-hosted-on-media-land-llc"></span>Malicious operations hosted on Media Land LLC.<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p>Servers hosted by Media Land infrastructure included the <strong>Dridex </strong>and <strong>Zeus </strong>banking trojans, the defunct <strong>card shop</strong> valid[.]cc, ransomware groups such as <strong>Egregor</strong>, <strong>Maze, Black Basta</strong>, or <a href="https://x.com/PRODAFT/status/1910331939111776435" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow"><strong>Hunters International</strong></a>, <strong>Magecart </strong>attacks infrastructure, the <strong>RIG </strong>Exploit Kit, etc.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-krakenlabs-observed-highlights"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="krakenlabs-observed-highlights"></span>KrakenLabs observed highlights<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-emerging-threats"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="emerging-threats"></span>Emerging threats<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p><em>CVE Program funding controversy:</em> On April 16, the <strong>US government reinstated funding for the Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures database</strong>. The announcement came after hours of uncertainty after MITRE, the non-profit organization that runs the database, said its contract to operate the CVE Program had not been renewed. To ensure continued long-term international participation and stability of the CVE Program, a new <strong>non-profit, the CVE Foundation, has also been founded</strong>. <a href="https://bsky.app/profile/tib3rius.bsky.social/post/3lmulrbygoe2g" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow">Learn more</a> / <a href="https://www.usaspending.gov/award/CONT_AWD_70RCSJ24FR0000018_7001_70RSAT20D00000001_7001" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow">more</a> / <a href="https://www.thecvefoundation.org/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">and more →</a></p>



<p><em>Slopsquatting – New supply chain attack method?</em> ‘<strong>Slopsquatting</strong>’ (aka <strong>package hallucination</strong>) occurs when the code generated by a Large Language Model recommends or references a fictitious package. Researchers warn <strong>threat actors can exploit this in a new type of supply chain attack</strong> by publishing malicious packages with hallucinated names. <a href="https://www.securityweek.com/ai-hallucinations-create-a-new-software-supply-chain-threat/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Learn more</a> / <a href="https://arxiv.org/pdf/2406.10279" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow">and more →</a></p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-oracle-cloud-data-breach"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="oracle-cloud-data-breach"></span>Oracle Cloud data breach<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p><em>Oracle Cloud confirms data breach:</em> A threat actor named <strong>&#8220;<em>rose87168&#8243;</em></strong>claimed in underground forums to have breached <strong>Oracle</strong> <strong>Cloud</strong>. Initially, the vendor denied the breach but has ended up privately acknowledging some customers that attackers had stolen old client credentials clients after breaching a &#8220;legacy environment&#8221; last used in 2017. Security researchers that had access to the leak suggest, however, that the breach could also include more recent data. <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/oracle-privately-confirms-cloud-breach-to-customers/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Learn more</a> / <a href="https://www.cloudsek.com/blog/part-2-validating-the-breach-oracle-cloud-denied-cloudseks-follow-up-analysis#Analysis" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">more</a> / <a href="https://www.reuters.com/technology/cybersecurity/oracle-tells-clients-second-recent-hack-log-in-data-stolen-bloomberg-news-2025-04-02/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">and more →</a></p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-stealc-malware"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="stealc-malware"></span>StealC malware<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p><em>Updated StealC version released</em>: Updated <strong>2.0 version</strong> for infostealer <strong>StealC </strong>has been released. This new version included major changes like fully server-side decryption on Chromium-based servers, automatic brute-forcing of crypto plugins, or a builder integrated directly into the admin panel, among other relevant updates. <a href="https://x.com/g0njxa/status/1907402479202934864" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow">Learn more →</a></p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-ransomware"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="ransomware"></span>Ransomware<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p><em>Hunters International prioritizes exfiltration:</em> Threat group &#8220;<strong><em>Hunters International&#8221;</em></strong>continued their <strong>evolution</strong> from a double extortion Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS) to a dual model <strong>prioritizing exfiltration and extortion without encrypting</strong> the victims. In that regard, according to Group-IB, the adversary released a new data exfiltration service named <strong>“<em>World Leaks</em>”</strong>.&nbsp; <a href="https://www.group-ib.com/blog/hunters-international-ransomware-group/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Learn more →</a></p>
