					<h1 class="entry-title">Threat Context Monthly: Green Nailao &amp; UNC3886 – Briefing for March 2025  </h1>					<div class="post-info">
						<span class="post-category">
							<a href="https://outpost24.com/blog/category/research-and-threat-intel/">Research &amp; Threat Intel</a>							</span>
							<span class="post-date">Last updated: 27 Mar 2025</span>
						</div>
					</div>
											<div class="article-author">
							<span class="written-by">Written By</span>
																							<div class="author-container">
									<span class="author-name"><a href="https://outpost24.com/blog/authors/krakenlabs/">KrakenLabs</a></span>
									<span class="author-title">Threat Intelligence Team, Outpost24</span>
								</div>
													</div>
										
<p>Welcome to the <a href="https://outpost24.com/blog/category/research-and-threat-intel/">Threat Context Monthly blog series</a> where we provide a comprehensive roundup of the most relevant cybersecurity news and threat information from KrakenLabs, Outpost24’s cyber <a href="https://outpost24.com/products/cyber-threat-intelligence/">threat intelligence</a> team. Here’s what you need to know from March about espionage activities by threat actors Green Nailao and UNC3886.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-spotlight-threat-green-nailao-growing-links-between-ransomware-operations-and-state-related-activity-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="spotlight-threat-green-nailao-%e2%80%93-growing-links-between-ransomware-operations-and-state-related-activity"></span>Spotlight threat: <em>Green Nailao</em> – Growing links between ransomware operations and state-related activity&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<p>“<em>Green Nailao</em>” is a threat actor active at least since <strong>April 2024</strong> targeting <strong>European organizations</strong>,<strong> </strong>including entities in the healthcare sector. They are <strong>believed to be a Chinese group</strong> due to their use of attack patterns and implants such as ShadowPad and PlugX, which are typically associated with threat actors of this origin.&nbsp;</p>



<p><a href="https://www.orangecyberdefense.com/global/blog/cert-news/meet-nailaolocker-a-ransomware-distributed-in-europe-by-shadowpad-and-plugx-backdoors" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow">Orange researchers observed</a> them ultimately distributing a relatively unsophisticated and <strong>poorly designed ransomware</strong> strain dubbed<strong> NailaoLocker</strong>, which could lead to the<strong> hypothesis of a</strong> <strong>financial motive</strong>. However, in the observed attacks, <em>Green Nailao</em> has been seen accessing files and creating ZIP archives, suggesting that data exfiltration is a key objective. This raises the likelihood that the ransomware component is merely a <strong>smokescreen to divert attention</strong> from their actual <strong>espionage activities</strong>. The lack of sophistication in NailaoLocker further supports this theory, as the encryption mechanisms are weaker compared to well-established ransomware families, making it a poor tool for financial extortion but an effective distraction for network defenders. </p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-how-green-nailao-operate-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="how-green-nailao-operate"></span>How Green Nailao operate&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p>For gaining <strong>initial access</strong>, <em>Green Nailao</em> managed to exploit a <strong>0-day vulnerability</strong> that had not been related to any other threat group prior to them. More specifically, they had been exploiting <a href="https://www.scrible.com/view/source/R2IO1C0L20LQG2MG3443K8O48P4CM20E:1424161239/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><strong>CVE-2024-24919</strong></a>, a <strong>critical vulnerability affecting Check Point Security Gateways</strong> that have Remote Access VPN or Mobile Access features enabled. This flaw allowed the group to enumerate and extract password hashes for all local accounts, providing an initial foothold in targeted networks. Once inside, they carried out network reconnaissance, lateral movement via RDP, and privilege escalation using legitimate binaries side-loaded with malicious DLLs to deploy their payloads.&nbsp;</p>



<p><em>Green Nailao </em>is the latest addition to a list of likely <strong>state-sponsored activity tied to ransomware distribution</strong>. Their approach follows an emerging pattern where espionage-motivated actors integrate ransomware into their operations, either as a diversionary tactic or as an additional means of disruption. This trend underscores the need for organizations to not only focus on ransomware mitigation strategies but also to consider the <strong>broader implications of data exfiltration</strong> and <strong>cyber espionage</strong> when responding to such threats.&nbsp;</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-spotlight-threat-juniper-routers-exploitation-chinese-espionage-group-unc3886-deploys-custom-malware-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="spotlight-threat-juniper-routers-exploitation-%e2%80%93-chinese-espionage-group-unc3886-deploys-custom-malware"></span>Spotlight threat: Juniper routers exploitation – Chinese espionage group <em>UNC3886</em> deploys custom malware&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<p>In mid-2024, the China-linked cyber espionage group &#8220;<em>UNC3886&#8243; </em>initiated a <a href="https://cloud.google.com/blog/topics/threat-intelligence/china-nexus-espionage-targets-juniper-routers" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">sophisticated campaign</a> targeting <strong>end-of-life (EoL) Juniper Networks</strong> <strong>MX Series routers</strong> running Junos OS. They exploited <a href="https://supportportal.juniper.net/s/article/2025-03-Out-of-Cycle-Security-Bulletin-Junos-OS-A-local-attacker-with-shell-access-can-execute-arbitrary-code-CVE-2025-21590?language=en_US" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><strong>CVE-2025-21590</strong></a>, a newly tracked vulnerability that allows <strong>process injection to bypass Junos OS security mechanisms</strong> such as Veriexec, which is designed to prevent unauthorized code execution.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<p>Leveraging their <strong>deep understanding of network device internals</strong>, the group deployed <strong>custom backdoors</strong> based on the <strong>open-source TinyShell framework</strong>. <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/chinese-cyberspies-backdoor-juniper-routers-for-stealthy-access/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow">These backdoors</a> were meticulously designed to maintain <strong>long-term, covert access to the compromised routers</strong>, including capabilities to disable logging mechanisms on the compromised devices, thereby enhancing their <strong>stealth and persistence</strong>, underscoring <em>UNC3886</em>&#8216;s strategic focus on <strong>persistent espionage</strong>. </p>



<p>The campaign highlights a concerning trend wherein espionage-motivated <strong>adversaries compromise routing devices</strong>, granting them sustained, high-level access to critical network infrastructure. Such access not only facilitates <strong>intelligence gathering</strong> but also poses <strong>potential risks for disruptive actions</strong>.&nbsp;</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-how-do-unc3886-operate-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="how-do-unc3886-operate"></span>How do UNC3886 operate?&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p><em>UNC3886</em>&#8216;s operations primarily target the <strong>defense, technology, and telecommunications sectors</strong> in the U.S. and Asia, aiming to infiltrate high-value organizations for intelligence collection and long-term access. Their ability to maintain persistence within compromised networks suggests a broader strategy of embedding themselves within critical infrastructure, potentially positioning them for further exploitation or strategic cyber operations.&nbsp;</p>



<p>This is not <em>UNC3886</em>’s first use of zero-day vulnerabilities to compromise infrastructure. Previously, they exploited <a href="https://cloud.google.com/blog/topics/threat-intelligence/vmware-esxi-zero-day-bypass/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><strong>CVE-2023-20867</strong></a>, which enabled unauthorized remote execution on VMware ESXi hosts, allowing them to gain deep access into virtualized environments. Additionally, they leveraged <a href="https://cloud.google.com/blog/topics/threat-intelligence/chinese-vmware-exploitation-since-2021/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><strong>CVE-2023-34048</strong></a>, a high-severity <strong>VMware vCenter Server</strong> vulnerability, to expand their reach within compromised environments. Their ability to identify and exploit high-impact vulnerabilities before they are publicly disclosed underscores their advanced technical capabilities and suggests an organized and well-resourced effort to compromise key infrastructure.&nbsp;</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-krakenlabs-observed-highlights-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="krakenlabs-observed-highlights"></span>KrakenLabs observed highlights&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-vulnerability-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="vulnerability"></span>Vulnerability&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p><em>Critical PHP-CGI vulnerability:</em> GreyNoise reports widespread exploitation of <strong>CVE-2024-4577</strong>, a critical PHP-CGI vulnerability patched in June 2024. Initially observed in attacks on <strong>Japanese organizations</strong>, the threat has expanded globally, with <strong>Germany and China </strong>among the<strong> most affected regions</strong>. Over 1,000 unique IPs have been detected attempting exploitation, and at least 79 exploits are publicly available. <a href="https://www.greynoise.io/blog/mass-exploitation-critical-php-cgi-vulnerability-cve-2024-4577" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow">Learn more →</a> </p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-trend-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="trend"></span>Trend&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p><em>Trojan steals facial recognition:</em> <strong>GoldPickaxe.iOS </strong>is a new mobile <strong>iOS trojan</strong> capable of <strong>collecting facial recognition data, identity documents, and intercepting SMS</strong>. Threat actors would be feeding the stolen biometric data to AI-driver face-swapping services to create deepfakes and then combine those with the ID documents to intercept SMSs. This allows them ultimately to gain unauthorized access to the victim’s banking account. <a href="https://www.group-ib.com/blog/goldfactory-ios-trojan/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow">Learn more →</a> </p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-supply-chain-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="supply-chain"></span>Supply chain&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p><em>GitHub Action compromised:</em> A supply chain attack on the <strong>GitHub Action tj-actions/changed-files</strong> <strong>compromised 218 repositories</strong>, potentially <strong>exposing secrets</strong> such as <strong>GitHub tokens, DockerHub, npm, and AWS credentials</strong>. While only a small percentage of the 23,000 projects using the action were affected, some are highly popular, increasing the risk of further supply chain attacks. <a href="https://www.stepsecurity.io/blog/harden-runner-detection-tj-actions-changed-files-action-is-compromised" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow">Learn more →</a> </p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-emerging-threat-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="emerging-threat"></span>Emerging threat&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p><em>Fake document converters: </em>The FBI warns that cybercriminals are using <strong>fake online document converters</strong> to deploy malware, <strong>steal sensitive data</strong>, and even <strong>install ransomware</strong>. Researchers found malicious sites like docu-flex[.]com and pdfixers[.]com distributing malware-infected executables. Some scams use <strong>Google ads</strong> to spread threats like <strong>Gootloader</strong>, used to infiltrate corporate networks. <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/fbi-warnings-are-true-fake-file-converters-do-push-malware/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow">Learn more →</a> / <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/fbi-warnings-are-true-fake-file-converters-do-push-malware/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">and more →</a> </p>



<p><em>AI-Powered Fake GitHub Repos: </em>New malware campaigns are using <strong>AI-generated fake GitHub repositories</strong> to distribute <strong>SmartLoader</strong>, which acts as a loader for <strong>Lumma Stealer</strong> and other malicious payloads. Disguised as gaming cheats, cracked software, and system tools, these repositories <strong>deceive users</strong> into downloading ZIP files containing <strong>obfuscated Lua scripts</strong> that execute malware. <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/en_us/research/25/c/ai-assisted-fake-github-repositories.html" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow">Learn more →</a> </p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-ransomware-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="ransomware"></span>Ransomware&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p><em>Akira exploits webcam:</em> To circumvent an Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR) tool, the<strong> &#8220;<em>Akira</em>&#8221; ransomware group</strong> has been observed <strong>compromising its victim through a vulnerable IoT device</strong>. Through an internal network scan, they identified a vulnerable webcam running a lightweight Linux operating system that supported command execution. <em>Akira </em>then deployed their Linux-based ransomware and, as the device was not being monitored, the security team was unaware of the increase in malicious Server Message Block (SMB) traffic from the webcam to the impacted server. <a href="https://www.s-rminform.com/latest-thinking/camera-off-akira-deploys-ransomware-via-webcam" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow">Learn more →</a> </p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-what-s-new-in-threat-context-this-month"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="whats-new-in-threat-context-this-month%e2%80%af"></span>What’s new in Threat Context this month?  <span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>
