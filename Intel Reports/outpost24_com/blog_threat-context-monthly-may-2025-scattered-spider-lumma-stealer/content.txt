					<h1 class="entry-title">Threat Context Monthly May 2025: Scattered Spider &amp; Lumma Stealer  </h1>					<div class="post-info">
						<span class="post-category">

<p>Welcome to the Threat Context Monthly blog series where we provide a comprehensive roundup of the most relevant cybersecurity news and threat information from KrakenLabs, Outpost24’s cyber threat intelligence team. Here’s what you need to know from May about Scattered Spider, Lumma Stealer and more.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-threat-actor-of-the-month-scattered-spider"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="threat-actor-of-the-month-scattered-spider"></span>Threat actor of the month: Scattered Spider <span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<p>Three <strong>major UK retailers</strong>—<strong>Marks &amp; Spencer, Co-op and Harrods</strong>—have all reported <a href="https://specopssoft.com/blog/scattered-spider-service-desk-defense-tips/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">ransomware incidents</a> that began with seemingly routine <strong>help-desk interactions</strong>. In the M&amp;S case, attackers convinced support staff to <strong>reset an employee’s password</strong>, then allegedly deployed the <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/marks-and-spencer-breach-linked-to-scattered-spider-ransomware-attack/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">DragonForce encryptor</a> that locked down internal systems. In the following days, similar password-reset schemes and network infiltrations hit Co-op and Harrods, forcing each company to shut down affected services and prompting the <a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/news/cyber-attacks-are-wake-up-call-for-businesses-pat-mcfadden" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">National Cyber Security Centre</a> to remind organizations to treat any <strong>unexpected support requests </strong>with extreme caution. </p>



<p>A great deal of speculation has focused on a loose online community nicknamed “<strong><em>Scattered Spider</em></strong>”. Rather than a single gang, this label describes English-speaking cybercriminals—often in their early twenties—who share social-engineering know-how on platforms like <strong>Discord and Telegram</strong>. Between mid-2022 and mid-2024, individuals linked to these methods are thought to have worked as <strong>affiliates</strong> for <strong>ransomware</strong> services such as <strong><em>BlackCat, RansomHub </em>and <em>Qilin</em></strong>, using impersonation, <strong>SIM swapping and MFA-bypass </strong>tricks to win initial access. Although several suspects <a href="https://www.reuters.com/technology/cybersecurity/us-charges-five-scattered-spider-hacking-scheme-2024-11-20/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">were arrested</a> in 2024, the community’s full makeup remains unclear, and there could be copycat threat actors continuing to leverage the same playbook. </p>



<p>Adding another layer of uncertainty, multiple sources report that the <strong>encryptor</strong> used against M&amp;S—and allegedly in the Co-op and Harrods attacks—belonged to the <a href="https://specopssoft.com/blog/dragonforce-ransomware-as-a-service/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><strong><em>DragonForce</em> Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS)</strong> operation</a>. <strong>DragonForce’s own operators</strong> are said to have <a href="https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/crkx3vy54nzo" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">contacted the BBC</a>, providing screenshots and data samples as proof of their network breaches and large-scale data theft. While there’s no definitive evidence that <em>Scattered Spider</em> worked directly with<em> DragonForce </em>this time, the overlap of social-engineering techniques and the known history of these hackers partnering with RaaS providers makes the theory a strong—but still unconfirmed—<a href="https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/ckgnndrgxv3o" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">possibility</a>.  </p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-scattered-spider-ttps"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="scattered-spider-ttps"></span>Scattered Spider TTPs <span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p>Their core method <a href="https://specopssoft.com/blog/scattered-spider-service-desk-defense-tips/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">exploits the human element</a>: impersonating employees or contractors to <strong>persuade service-desk agents</strong> to reset passwords, <strong>disable MFA</strong> or <strong>provision privileged accounts</strong>. By mimicking internal processes, using <strong>spoofed numbers</strong> and demonstrating fluent, localized English, they slip past defenses in minutes without triggering alerts. </p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-spotlight-threat-international-operation-against-lumma-stealer"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="spotlight-threat-international-operation-against-lumma-stealer"></span>Spotlight threat: International operation against Lumma Stealer <span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<p>“<strong><em>Lumma Stealer</em></strong>” is an information stealer written in a combination of C++ and ASM and sold in <strong>underground forums</strong> and in Telegram since<strong> December 2022</strong> under a Malware-as-a-Service (MaaS) scheme.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<p>Lumma steals browser<strong> credentials and cookies</strong>, and autofill data from Chromium, Mozilla, and Gecko-based <strong>browsers</strong>, steals <strong>cryptocurrency</strong> wallets, collects <strong>VPN</strong> data, <strong>email</strong> clients, FTP clients, and Telegram applications. Furthermore, it harvests user documents and system data.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<p>The information stealer was developed and commercialized by the underground user “<strong><em>Shamel</em></strong>” a Russian-located threat actor. Since its inception <strong>Lumma </strong>stealer has become <strong>very popular</strong> within the cybercriminal community and the developer mentioned in an interview that it had more than <a href="https://g0njxa.medium.com/approaching-stealers-devs-a-brief-interview-with-lummac2-94111d4b1e11" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">400 active clients</a> in late November 2023. The malware’s popularity continued growing, and Lumma <strong>detections grew exponentially</strong> during the <a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/en/papers/threat-reports/eset-threat-report-h22024.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow">H2 of 2024</a> becoming one of the<strong> most prolific infostealers</strong>. </p>



<p>In late May 2025, the <strong>Microsoft</strong> <strong>Digital Crimes Unit</strong>, together with the <strong>US Department of Justice, EUROPOL, Japan’s Cybercrime Control Center</strong> and various cybersecurity <strong>vendors</strong>, performed<strong> a coordinated </strong><a href="https://blogs.microsoft.com/on-the-issues/2025/05/21/microsoft-leads-global-action-against-favored-cybercrime-tool/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><strong>disruption operation</strong></a><strong> against </strong><a href="https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2025/05/21/lumma-stealer-breaking-down-the-delivery-techniques-and-capabilities-of-a-prolific-infostealer/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><strong>Lumma infostealer</strong></a>. Following a US court order, Microsoft seized and facilitated the takedown, suspension, and blocking of approximately 2,300 Lumma infrastructure malicious domains.  </p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-threat-actors-using-lumma-stealer"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="threat-actors-using-lumma-stealer"></span>Threat actors using Lumma Stealer <span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p>Since its inception <strong>Lumma stealer</strong> has been <strong>used by multiple threat actors</strong>. Prolific <strong>ransomware groups</strong> such as “<em>Black Basta</em>” “<em>Scattered Spider”, “Interlock”, “Storm-1607”, “Storm-1113/Payk_34”, or “TA577/ Storm-1674</em>&#8221; have included it in their toolset. </p>



<p>Furthermore, within the <strong>traffers ecosystem </strong>relevant adversaries such as “<em>FatherOfCarders”, “LogsDiller”, “Moon Cloud” </em>or<em> “TA2727”, </em>among many others, also utilize Lumma to perform their malicious activities. </p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-krakenlabs-observed-highlights"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="krakenlabs-observed-highlights"></span>KrakenLabs observed highlights <span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-ransomware"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="ransomware"></span>Ransomware <span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p><em>LockBit leaks:</em> A <strong>LockBit ransomware administration panel</strong> was hacked, resulting in the leak of sensitive data, including <strong>private messages</strong> between affiliates and victims, <strong>Bitcoin wallet</strong> addresses, <strong>affiliate accounts</strong>, and information about <strong>malware and infrastructure</strong>. <em>LockBit </em>downplayed<strong> the incident</strong>, claiming no victim data or decryptors were compromised. <a href="https://www.securityweek.com/valuable-information-leaked-in-lockbit-ransomware-hack/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Learn more</a> / <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7326004945583792128/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">and more →</a>&nbsp;</p>



<p><em>Coinbase suffered extortion:</em> <strong>Cryptocurrency exchange Coinbase</strong> disclosed a <strong>data breach</strong> performed by <strong>insiders, who were bribed by threat actors.</strong> The breach affected 69,000 customers, and the malicious actors demanded a ransom of US$ 20 million. The company declined to pay and instead offered a reward of US$ 20 million in exchange for information about the authors. <a href="https://www.coinbase.com/en-gb/blog/protecting-our-customers-standing-up-to-extortionists" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Learn more →</a> </p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-supply-chain"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="supply-chain"></span>Supply chain <span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p><em>E-commerce stores backdoored:</em> A supply chain attack affecting <strong>500–1,000 e-stores</strong>, including a US$40 billion multinational, through 21 backdoored <strong>Magento extensions</strong> from vendors <strong>Tigren, Meetanshi, and Magesolution (MGS)</strong>. These backdoors, hidden for six years, were only recently abused, allowing remote control via a manipulated license check file. <a href="https://sansec.io/research/license-backdoor" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Learn more →</a> </p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-geopolitics"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="geopolitics"></span>Geopolitics <span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p><em>Hacktivist activity related to India-Pakistan conflict:</em> In the wake of recent kinetic escalations between India and Pakistan, at least <strong>45 hacktivist groups have become active</strong>, with <strong>10 aligned as pro-India</strong> and <strong>35 as pro-Pakistan</strong>. This situation highlights how geopolitical conflicts now consistently drive cyber activity. <strong>Defacement </strong>and <strong>DDoS attacks</strong> are the most common tactics observed. <a href="https://x.com/Cyberknow20/status/1920106581112336576" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Learn more →</a> </p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-vulnerabilities"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="vulnerabilities"></span>Vulnerabilities <span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p><em>New European Vulnerability Database (EUVD):</em> The European Union&#8217;s cybersecurity authority <strong>ENISA has announced the new European Vulnerability Database (EUVD)</strong> is operational. The information comes from multiple sources such as open-source databases, CSIRTs, or vendors advisories. EUVD offers three different dashboard views: <strong>critical vulnerabilities</strong>, <strong>exploited</strong> vulnerabilities, and <strong>EU coordinated</strong> ones. <a href="https://www.enisa.europa.eu/news/consult-the-european-vulnerability-database-to-enhance-your-digital-security" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Learn more →</a> / <a href="https://euvd.enisa.europa.eu/homepage" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">and more →</a> </p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-law-enforcement-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="law-enforcement"></span>Law enforcement&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p><em>Operation Endgame announced new targets</em>: International law enforcement coalition <strong>“Operation Endgame” </strong>launched an <strong>operation </strong>against the <strong>ransomware kill chain</strong> by targeting initial access malware strains: <strong>Bumblebee, Latrodectus, Qakbot, Hijackloader, DanaBot, Trickbot and Warmcookie</strong>. The operation coordinated by Europol took down <strong>300 servers </strong>worldwide, disrupted<strong> 650 domains</strong>, seized <strong>EUR 3.5 million</strong> in cryptocurrency and issued international arrest warrants against 20 targets.&nbsp; <a href="https://www.europol.europa.eu/media-press/newsroom/news/operation-endgame-strikes-again-ransomware-kill-chain-broken-its-source" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Learn more→</a>&nbsp;</p>



<p><em>Spyware vendor fined:</em> A US federal jury has ordered <strong>Israeli spyware vendor NSO Group</strong> to pay <strong>US$167 million</strong> in punitive damages and US$444,719 in <strong>compensatory damages to WhatsApp</strong> for a 2019 campaign that targeted 1,400 users with <strong>Pegasus spyware</strong>. This case marks the first time a spyware vendor has been held accountable in court, setting a precedent for the industry. <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/legal/nso-group-fined-167m-for-spyware-attacks-on-1-400-whatsapp-users/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Learn more →</a>&nbsp;</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-legislation-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="legislation"></span>Legislation&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p><em>New Japan’s cyberdefense law:</em> <strong>Japan enforced the new Active Cyberdefense Law </strong>which allows offensive cyber operations. The new bill explicitly <strong>allows</strong> <strong>law enforcement </strong>agencies to <strong>infiltrate and neutralize</strong> hostile servers before any malicious activity has taken place and provides government with the power to analyze foreign internet traffic entering or transiting through Japan networks. <a href="https://therecord.media/japan-enacts-new-law-allowing-offensive-cyber-operations" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Learn more →</a>&nbsp;</p>
