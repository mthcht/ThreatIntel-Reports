					<h1 class="entry-title">Unmasking EncryptHub: Help from ChatGPT &amp; OPSEC blunders  </h1>					<div class="post-info">
						<span class="post-category">
							<a href="https://outpost24.com/blog/category/research-and-threat-intel/">Research &amp; Threat Intel</a>							</span>
							<span class="post-date">Last updated: 03 Apr 2025</span>
						</div>
					</div>
											<div class="article-author">
							<span class="written-by">Written By</span>
																							<div class="author-container">
									<span class="author-name"><a href="https://outpost24.com/blog/authors/krakenlabs/">KrakenLabs</a></span>
									<span class="author-title">Threat Intelligence Team, Outpost24</span>
								</div>
													</div>
										
<p>This is the second part of Outpost24’s KrakenLabs investigation into <em>EncryptHub</em>, an up-and-coming cybercriminal who has been gaining popularity in recent months and is heavily expanding and evolving operations at the time of writing. We’ve already published one article <a href="https://outpost24.com/blog/unveiling-encrypthub-multi-stage-malware/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">explaining <em>EncryptHub’s</em> campaigns and TPPs, infrastructure, infection methods, and targets</a>.&nbsp;</p>



<p>This article will follow a different approach. We’ll explore <em>EncryptHub</em>’s&nbsp;last decade online with a particular focus on his one-year-old foray into cybercrime, the OPSEC mistakes he’s made along the way, and how he used ChatGPT as a faithful accomplice throughout. This way, we hope to give you a human image beyond the amorphous dark entity that the generic tag of ‘<em>Threat Actor</em>’ usually gives.&nbsp;</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-who-is-encrypthub-nbsp-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="who-is-encrypthub"></span>Who is EncryptHub?&nbsp;&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<p>When people think of cybercriminals, they tend to imagine high-tech, government-backed teams and elite hackers using cutting-edge technology. However, many hackers are normal people who at some point decided to follow a dark path. Due to OPSEC errors (detailed later) we were able to learn a lot about <em>Encrypthub</em>, most of which will remain private.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<p>And to be clear, the purpose of this section is not to humiliate or dox the individual. It’s simply to shed light on the fact most cybercriminals are ordinary humans that lead mostly ordinary lives.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img decoding="async" width="858" height="707" src="//outpost24.com/wp-content/plugins/a3-lazy-load/assets/images/lazy_placeholder.gif" data-lazy-type="image" data-src="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-1_blur.png" alt="EncryptHub Facebook Profile screenshot" class="lazy lazy-hidden wp-image-8152" srcset="" data-srcset="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-1_blur.png 858w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-1_blur-300x247.png 300w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-1_blur-768x633.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 858px) 100vw, 858px" /><noscript><img decoding="async" width="858" height="707" src="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-1_blur.png" alt="EncryptHub Facebook Profile screenshot" class="wp-image-8152" srcset="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-1_blur.png 858w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-1_blur-300x247.png 300w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-1_blur-768x633.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 858px) 100vw, 858px" /></noscript></figure>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-early-years"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="early-years"></span>Early years<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p>What follows is a brief account of <em>EncryptHub</em>’s life constructed from a cursory glance of his online activity. It is by no means exhaustive nor verified. There are also many details we have chosen not to share from his exposed information. However, it is not our job to pry into people’s private lives nor our intention to expose them.&nbsp;</p>



<p>About 10 years ago, <em>EncryptHub</em> fled his hometown in Ukraine due to an unknown incident, relocating to a new city, likely along the coast near Romania. There, he kept a low profile, probably working while self-studying computer science. He began seeking computer-related jobs, claiming experience in mobile repairs, system administration, call centre work, and junior-level programming. A year later, while still searching for work, he enrolled in online courses to improve his skills.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<p>However, by early 2022, all his activity suddenly stopped. While Russia’s invasion of Ukraine could have played a role, we’ve seen strong evidence suggesting he ended up in <strong>jail </strong>for a while. Once released, he resumed his job search, this time offering <strong>freelance </strong>web and app development services, which gained some traction.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<p>But the pay likely wasn’t enough, and after briefly trying <strong>bug bounty</strong> programs with little success, we believe he <strong>pivoted to cybercrime </strong>in the first half of 2024. Initially, he may have done some&nbsp; grunt work in campaigns related to vishing and ransomware. This likely fuelled his interest in vulnerability research and malware development, which led to the various campaigns that shone a light on upon him and have been thoroughly documented both by us and many other security researchers.&nbsp;</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-journey-into-cybercrime-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="journey-into-cybercrime"></span>Journey into cybercrime&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p>Despite his descent into a life of cybercrime, he didn’t abandon his aspirations as a legitimate security researcher and his efforts eventually paid off. While writing this report, <em>SkorikARI</em>, another username used by <em>EncryptHub</em>, was acknowledged by MSRC (Microsoft Security Response Center) for the findings of <strong>CVE-2025-24071</strong> and <strong>CVE-2025-24061</strong>, both very likely exploited in some of his campaigns <a href="https://outpost24.com/blog/unveiling-encrypthub-multi-stage-malware/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">[1]</a><a href="https://www.fortinet.com/blog/threat-research/fickle-stealer-distributed-via-multiple-attack-chain" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">[2]</a><em>.</em>&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<p>This brings us to the present, and to the picture of a conflicted man. Someone who has perhaps not had the easiest life, and has struggled to choose between a legitimate career and cybercrime and trying to play both roles at the same time. Whatever his motivations, he is clearly a <strong>driven</strong> individual with a desire to learn.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img decoding="async" width="1024" height="437" src="//outpost24.com/wp-content/plugins/a3-lazy-load/assets/images/lazy_placeholder.gif" data-lazy-type="image" data-src="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-2-1024x437.png" alt="Screenshot Windows Mark of the Web Security Feature Bypass Vulnerability" class="lazy lazy-hidden wp-image-8151" srcset="" data-srcset="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-2-1024x437.png 1024w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-2-300x128.png 300w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-2-768x328.png 768w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-2-1536x655.png 1536w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-2.png 1587w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /><noscript><img decoding="async" width="1024" height="437" src="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-2-1024x437.png" alt="Screenshot Windows Mark of the Web Security Feature Bypass Vulnerability" class="wp-image-8151" srcset="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-2-1024x437.png 1024w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-2-300x128.png 300w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-2-768x328.png 768w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-2-1536x655.png 1536w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-2.png 1587w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></noscript></figure>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-opsec-mistakes-exposing-your-crimes-for-the-world-to-see-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="opsec-mistakes-exposing-your-crimes-for-the-world-to-see"></span>OPSEC mistakes: Exposing your crimes for the world to see&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<p>As we outlined in our <a href="https://outpost24.com/blog/unveiling-encrypthub-multi-stage-malware/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">previous report</a> on <em>EncryptHub</em>, the actor made several fundamental operational mistakes that enabled us to collect a vast wealth of information, providing a pretty clear picture of his activities.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-password-management-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="password-management"></span>Password management&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p>One of the most glaring mistakes made by our subject was the <a href="https://specopssoft.com/blog/password-reuse-hidden-danger/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><strong>reuse of passwords</strong></a>. Just as an example, in a single file exfiltrated by his own malware, 82 out of 200 accounts shared nearly identical passwords, with only slight variations like a single character change or an extra letter at the end. To make matters worse, many of these passwords lacked complexity—short and composed solely of alphanumeric characters.&nbsp;</p>



<p>This is far from a trivial issue. With such prominent repetition, credentials for accounts that were not present in that file could have been easily guessed. And the accounts were not trivial either. There was repetition in accounts for EncryptRAT C2 domains, bulletproof hosting, registrars and SSL certificate providers, and cryptocurrency exchanges among others.&nbsp;</p>



<p>One more critical error was his failure to enable two-factor authentication (2FA) on nearly every account. On his Gmail account—arguably the most important—he did activate 2FA but stored the backup codes in a .txt file that was also exfiltrated by his own malware. These 10 single-use codes could have been easily used to bypass 2FA.&nbsp;</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-mixing-hacking-with-home-life-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="mixing-hacking-with-home-life"></span>Mixing hacking with home-life&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Mixing usernames and access credentials used for personal or private matters with those tied to criminal endeavours&nbsp;</li>



<li>Using his personal emails to manage and create accounts related to his cybercriminal activity&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>



<li>Loging into personal accounts from the same system he used to develop and test his malware&nbsp;</li>



<li>Reusing infrastructure and domains related to his <em>legitimate</em> jobs for his criminal endeavours&nbsp;</li>
</ul>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-server-hardening-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="server-hardening"></span>Server hardening&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p>The most notable ‘developer-related’ mistake was poor access management on his C2 server, leaving confidential parts of the server exposed with <strong>directory listing </strong>enabled, accessible to anyone without authentication. This mistake was actually what triggered the entire investigation, as it revealed a JSON file used to configure malware, which contained Telegram bot information.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<p>Over time, he began addressing this issue by restricting access to some critical paths, such as the directory where stolen credentials were stored. However, at the time of writing, the <em>init.json</em> file is still exposed if you know the path where it is located. His latest C2 server was also launched with the default XAMPP installation, thus <strong>revealing the real IP</strong> address despite using Cloudflare. He has also been reusing the same alias in his TLS certificates, making it easier to locate new C2 the instant they go live.&nbsp;</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img decoding="async" width="570" height="708" src="//outpost24.com/wp-content/plugins/a3-lazy-load/assets/images/lazy_placeholder.gif" data-lazy-type="image" data-src="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-3.png" alt="Screenshot index of /server/uploads/encrypthub" class="lazy lazy-hidden wp-image-8150" srcset="" data-srcset="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-3.png 570w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-3-242x300.png 242w" sizes="(max-width: 570px) 100vw, 570px" /><noscript><img decoding="async" width="570" height="708" src="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-3.png" alt="Screenshot index of /server/uploads/encrypthub" class="wp-image-8150" srcset="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-3.png 570w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-3-242x300.png 242w" sizes="(max-width: 570px) 100vw, 570px" /></noscript></figure>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-telegram-opsec-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="telegram-opsec"></span>Telegram OPSEC&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p>Now focusing on Telegram. Early versions of the bot used to notify about successful infections— the one exposed in the aforementioned<em> init.json</em>—had admin privileges in the groups it was a part of, allowing it to generate invite links. These links could be used to infiltrate the groups, monitor activities, and even obtain the actor’s credentials when he infected himself.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<p><em>EncryptHub </em>showed a lack of awareness in this regard. We were able to remain in those groups for weeks before we were ‘detected’ and kicked out, even though one of those groups should only have had two members, himself and the bot. Once he realized we were there, he banned us but continued using the same groups and bots for weeks without changing any configurations.&nbsp;</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-knowing-when-to-log-out-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="knowing-when-to-log-out"></span>Knowing when to log out&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p>A final note to wrap up this section. Anyone (criminal or otherwise) should know never to store passwords in their browser and to always log out of sensitive sessions. Our subject ignored these rules, testing his malware on the development server, exfiltrating hundreds of sessions and accounts, and exposing his personal information.&nbsp;</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-chatgpt-encrypthub-s-faithful-assistant-nbsp-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="chatgpt-encrypthubs-faithful-assistant"></span>ChatGPT: EncryptHub’s faithful assistant&nbsp;&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<p>AI chatbots are being used by millions on a daily basis. This includes cybercriminals—and our protagonist is no exception. In fact, he has used it so extensively that we could almost consider ChatGPT a key accomplice in <em>EncryptHub</em>’s campaigns.&nbsp;</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-development-assistant-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="development-assistant"></span>Development assistant&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p>We can’t stress this enough—<em>EncryptHub </em>used ChatGPT a lot. Almost every piece of code, every configuration, and even some procedures and ideas were suggested, evaluated, created, or improved by ChatGPT. If a piece of malware was used by him, chances are the LLM had been involved.&nbsp;</p>



<p>It has assisted him with:&nbsp;</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Creation and configuration of Telegram Bots, C2 servers, Phishing sites, Mail Servers, and .onion services&nbsp;</li>



<li>Learning how to create macOS apps, how to properly use a REST API, and how to call PowerShell scripts from Go&nbsp;</li>



<li>Write malware, such as clippers and cookie stealers&nbsp;</li>



<li>Understand and improve scripts and code from other sources and how to better integrate it with his current malware&nbsp;</li>



<li>Many other things!&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
</ul>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img decoding="async" width="792" height="710" src="//outpost24.com/wp-content/plugins/a3-lazy-load/assets/images/lazy_placeholder.gif" data-lazy-type="image" data-src="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-4.png" alt="screenshot how EnryptHub might have used ChatGPT - full code of the PowerShell clipper" class="lazy lazy-hidden wp-image-8149" srcset="" data-srcset="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-4.png 792w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-4-300x269.png 300w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-4-768x688.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 792px) 100vw, 792px" /><noscript><img decoding="async" width="792" height="710" src="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-4.png" alt="screenshot how EnryptHub might have used ChatGPT - full code of the PowerShell clipper" class="wp-image-8149" srcset="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-4.png 792w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-4-300x269.png 300w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-4-768x688.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 792px) 100vw, 792px" /></noscript></figure>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img decoding="async" width="784" height="695" src="//outpost24.com/wp-content/plugins/a3-lazy-load/assets/images/lazy_placeholder.gif" data-lazy-type="image" data-src="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-5.png" alt="screenshot researching the various CVEs in ChatGPT" class="lazy lazy-hidden wp-image-8156" srcset="" data-srcset="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-5.png 784w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-5-300x266.png 300w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-5-768x681.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 784px) 100vw, 784px" /><noscript><img decoding="async" width="784" height="695" src="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-5.png" alt="screenshot researching the various CVEs in ChatGPT" class="wp-image-8156" srcset="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-5.png 784w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-5-300x266.png 300w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Figure-5-768x681.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 784px) 100vw, 784px" /></noscript></figure>



<p>It’s highly probable that <em>EncryptHub </em>also used ChatGPT when researching the various CVEs he has been linked to. However, the first conversation he exposed dates back to January and begins with the translation of an email from MSRC confirming the reported behaviour. So it’s not possible to confirm the involvement of AI in his vulnerability research, although, from all the observed activity, it&#8217;s very likely.&nbsp;</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-writing-companion-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="writing-companion"></span>Writing companion&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p>Another more traditional<em> </em>task <em>EncryptHub </em>has given ChatGPT is as a writing assistant. As mentioned in the previous section, he has used it to translate emails and messages, as well as whole conversations and negotiations with other TAs and potential clients and employers. He has also used it to write forum posts and statements.&nbsp;</p>



<p>On March 11<sup>th</sup>, 2025, the same day that the MSRC released the two CVEs he was acknowledged with, <em>EncryptHub </em>decided to start selling some of his exploits on the Russian speaking forum xss[.]is. <p>These are not the same he had reported as <em>SkorikARI</em>, but one of them is pretty similar. Afterwards, he asked the chatbot to generate a couple of posts to put exploits that took advantage of it for sale and directly copied them onto the underground forum.&nbsp;</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-the-value-of-threat-intelligence-nbsp-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="the-value-of-threat-intelligence"></span>The value of threat intelligence&nbsp;&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<p>We hope that this report has been able to provide a characterization of the person behind criminal activities as well as to show an example of what the consequences can be when not following proper security procedures during the course of online activities.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<p><em>EncryptHub</em>’s<em> </em>case<em> </em>is a prime example of how it does not matter how good you are at what you do, you still need to know the basics. He has shown and proven a lot of talent finding vulnerabilities and will be a force to be reckoned with if he keeps improving and solving his most glaring weaknesses. That said, his malware, like most throughout history, is not invincible, and cautious users who follow basic security measures are unlikely to fall victim to it.&nbsp;</p>



<p>The most complex 0-day exploit is useless against a user that knows better than download a suspicious executable from a shady site. Conversely, the most hardened infrastructure with the latest up-to-date antivirus software will be useless if you expose your access credentials for the world to see.&nbsp;</p>
