bl">Introduction</h2><p id="5f67" class="pw-post-body-paragraph oy oz iv pa b pb pc pd pe pf pg ph pi gv pj pk pl gy pm pn po hb pp pq pr ps hw bl">The term evasion is derived from the Latin word “evadere” which means — “To escape, to get away.” The <a class="ah hi" href="https://www.notion.so/Bypasses-0d2d2c10b07e46b986f491089347e19b#16182368629e425d8e70fdba40283ba5" rel="noopener ugc nofollow" target="_blank"><strong class="pa iw">DOD</strong></a> defines evasion as — “The process whereby isolated personnel avoid capture with the goal of successfully returning to areas under friendly control.”</p><p id="6e91" class="pw-post-body-paragraph oy oz iv pa b pb pt pd pe pf pu ph pi gv pv pk pl gy pw pn po hb px pq pr ps hw bl">Fairly often I come across a post on Twitter or a new blog that talks about a new <strong class="pa iw"><em class="py">evasion</em></strong> or <strong class="pa iw"><em class="py">bypass</em></strong> technique. Whether explicitly stated or not. I’ve seen posts that discuss UAC Bypass, new methods of process injection, or LSASS dumping that will bypass certain sensors or even precise detections that are released (at first this doesn’t sound like it fits into an evasion technique, but this will be explained later within the blog).</p><p id="0201" class="pw-post-body-paragraph oy oz iv pa b pb pt pd pe pf pu ph pi gv pv pk pl gy pw pn po hb px pq pr ps hw bl">This made me think — what does <strong class="pa iw"><em class="py">evasion</em></strong> or <strong class="pa iw"><em class="py">bypass</em></strong> truly mean? Are there different categories that these evasion techniques fit into? Lastly, if these techniques are to fit into categories — how can detection engineers leverage these for engagements?</p><p id="6ed9" class="pw-post-body-paragraph oy oz iv pa b pb pt pd pe pf pu ph pi gv pv pk pl gy pw pn po hb px pq pr ps hw bl">Capturing the type of evasion that was executed would help identify the gaps within the environment, which in turn would help engineers understand if that gap is something they can fix or if it is something that is out of their control. Once these gaps are identified, prioritization can be created.</p><p id="50a0" class="pw-post-body-paragraph oy oz iv pa b pb pt pd pe pf pu ph pi gv pv pk pl gy pw pn po hb px pq pr ps hw bl">For example: say a red-team engagement is performed and the red-teamer <strong class="pa iw"><em class="py">evades</em></strong> the sensor, there are 3 scenarios that could be in effect:</p><ol class=""><li id="79de" class="oy oz iv pa b pb pt pd pe pf pu ph pi gv pv pk pl gy pw pn po hb px pq pr ps pz qa qb bl">The sensor doesn’t collect the correct data to correlate with the event</li><li id="f58d" class="oy oz iv pa b pb qc pd pe pf qd ph pi gv qe pk pl gy qf pn po hb qg pq pr ps pz qa qb bl">The sensor has a built-in detection that the red-teamer evaded (engineers don’t have insight into what the detection logic is)</li><li id="061d" class="oy oz iv pa b pb qc pd pe pf qd ph pi gv qe pk pl gy qf pn po hb qg pq pr ps pz qa qb bl">The sensor collects the correct telemetry to provide visibility into the activity, but the engineer now has to create a custom detection.</li></ol><p id="0e2b" class="pw-post-body-paragraph oy oz iv pa b pb pt pd pe pf pu ph pi gv pv pk pl gy pw pn po hb px pq pr ps hw bl">I discuss this in the next section, but a priority could be set once the correct scenario above is identified.</p><p id="f2a1" class="pw-post-body-paragraph oy oz iv pa b pb pt pd pe pf pu ph pi gv pv pk pl gy pw pn po hb px pq pr ps hw bl">For scenarios 2 &amp; 3, if the data is there then setting a priority for creating the detection should be high.</p><p id="6e65" class="pw-post-body-paragraph oy oz iv pa b pb pt pd pe pf pu ph pi gv pv pk pl gy pw pn po hb px pq pr ps hw bl">For scenario 1, if the sensor doesn’t collect data to correlate with the event then addressing this issue should be a priority.</p><p id="6bbb" class="pw-post-body-paragraph oy oz iv pa b pb pt pd pe pf pu ph pi gv pv pk pl gy pw pn po hb px pq pr ps hw bl">Throughout this blog post, I will use the term <strong class="pa iw"><em class="py">evasion</em></strong> to reference both <strong class="pa iw"><em class="py">evasion</em></strong> and <strong class="pa iw"><em class="py">bypass</em></strong> terms because their definitions are interchangeable.</p><h2 id="8cd2" class="oc od iv bg oe of og oh gs oi oj ok gu ol om on oo op oq or os ot ou ov ow ox bl">Types of Evasions</h2><p id="d52c" class="pw-post-body-paragraph oy oz iv pa b pb pc pd pe pf pg ph pi gv pj pk pl gy pm pn po hb pp pq pr ps hw bl"><strong class="pa iw">Note: </strong>Throughout this write-up, I will be referring to terminology that is applied to the <a class="ah hi" rel="noopener ugc nofollow" href="/introducing-the-funnel-of-fidelity-b1bb59b04036" target="_blank" data-discover="true"><strong class="pa iw">Funnel of Fidelity</strong></a>, a mapping created by Jared Atkinson. I would highly suggest reading his piece on that methodology before moving forward, as well as his talk at SO-CON — <a class="ah hi" href="https://youtu.be/CRtmeWCbRZQ" rel="noopener ugc nofollow" target="_blank"><strong class="pa iw">Rethinking Detection Engineering</strong> </a>which goes into how to classify False Positives/False Negatives by leveraging base conditions.</p><p id="3cb0" class="pw-post-body-paragraph oy oz iv pa b pb pt pd pe pf pu ph pi gv pv pk pl gy pw pn po hb px pq pr ps hw bl">Initially, when diving into this methodology I wanted to make sure I was applying these opportunities to a mapping that was already created to help with comprehension. To accomplish this I chose to tie each evasion opportunity to a piece of the Funnel of Fidelity. This allowed me to categorize these methods more easily while helping with how to prioritize each method. For example, if an environment is more focused on their detection processes, they would want to focus on the evasion opportunities that pertain to that piece of the funnel. However; regardless of which piece of the funnel an organization wants to focus on I would suggest focusing on the opportunities that pertain to the data collection piece first, as this flows through the whole funnel. This is where I chose to start my focus when it comes to this methodology.</p><p id="410a" class="pw-post-body-paragraph oy oz iv pa b pb pt pd pe pf pu ph pi gv pv pk pl gy pw pn po hb px pq pr ps hw bl">When setting my focus on the data collection portion of the funnel I decided to spend a lot of time on data/ sensor tools. Often times I have seen someone state “this method of this attack bypasses sensor x”, my question then becomes “does it bypass the sensor’s logging capabilities, the sensor’s/EDR’s (Endpoint Detection and Response tool) default detections, or the custom detections you have set into place?” While breaking those things out, 2 different evasion techniques are identified along with 2 sub-techniques:</p><p id="ef44" class="pw-post-body-paragraph oy oz iv pa b pb pt pd pe pf pu ph pi gv pv pk pl gy pw pn po hb px pq pr ps hw bl"><strong class="pa iw"><em class="py">Collection Evasion</em></strong> is broken up into two different sub-techniques: <strong class="pa iw"><em class="py">End-User</em></strong> and <strong class="pa iw"><em class="py">Product</em></strong><em class="py">.</em> There is value in separating these two sub-techniques even though they fall under the same evasion category. One method is controlled by the customer/analyst: <strong class="pa iw"><em class="py">End-User</em></strong>. The data is there for you to collect, enabling just needs to take place. The other method: <strong class="pa iw"><em class="py">Product</em></strong> is something the customer/analyst has zero control over. Neither one can control the sensor is capable of collecting, this falls on the developers of that tool. id="4ac6" class="pw-post-body-paragraph oy oz iv pa b pb pc pd pe pf pg ph pi gv pj pk pl gy pm pn po hb pp pq pr ps hw bl">I think as time progresses, certain terms start to lose their meaning due to redefinition or misuse. We see this with a lot of terminologies, even outside of InfoSec. Evasion and bypass have seemed to fall under that category. Maybe it’s not that they have lost their meaning, but more specific classification has to be applied when talking about the subject. This is what piqued my interest when it comes to this topic. Not only — how do we classify these evasion methods so that detection engineers can leverage them better, but also setting a common terminology that can be leveraged by the SOC and all sub-teams (threat research, red-team, detection engineers, etc).</p><p id="05c6" class="pw-post-body-paragraph oy oz iv pa b pb pt pd pe pf pu ph pi gv pv pk pl gy pw pn po hb px pq pr ps hw bl">It’s important to remember that regardless if someone is part of a red-team, detection engineer, threat hunting team, etc the goal should always be to output something to the SOC. If terminology is misconstrued and everyone has their disparate definitions of a word, then the expectation of the output potentially gets lost.</p><p id="57d8" class="pw-post-body-paragraph oy oz iv pa b pb pt pd pe pf pu ph pi gv pv pk pl gy pw pn po hb px pq pr ps hw bl">I’m sure as time moves forward there will be more to add to this, but that is the exciting part of this.
