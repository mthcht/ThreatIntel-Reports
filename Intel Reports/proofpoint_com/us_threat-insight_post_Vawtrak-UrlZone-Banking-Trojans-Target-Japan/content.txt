  </h1>

        </span>

    <div class="node-full__body blog-content__body">  <p><strong>Overview</strong></p>

<p>In January and February 2016, Proofpoint researchers observed threat actors spreading banking Trojans in Japan and other countries that had not recently experienced high volumes of this family of malware. These countries certainly have not been targeted previously in the same way as the UK, United States, and others. Instead, it appears that the new campaigns in Japan (and Spain) are continuations of the trend first observed with <a href="https://www.proofpoint.com/us/threat-insight/post/dridex-shifu-give-spam-bots-day-off">Shifu in October 2015</a>. The key takeaways are:</p>

<ul>
	<li>The UrlZone banking Trojan is spreading via email spam and targeting Japanese and Spanish banks</li>
	<li>The Vawtrak Trojan is spreading using Angler Exploit Kit and targeting Japanese banks</li>
	<li>Both Trojans are using the same dynamic injects system that allows them to manipulate a financial institution’s website content (likely sharing resources or renting from the same third party)</li>
	<li>The injects system appears to be written by a Russian author</li>
</ul>

<p>&nbsp;</p>

<p><strong>UrlZone Banking Trojan Campaigns</strong></p>

<p>On January 21st of this year, Proofpoint researchers observed a large spam campaign consisting of tens of thousands of emails targeting Japanese email accounts. Other researchers have also observed an uptick in <a href="https://securityintelligence.com/organized-cybercrime-big-in-japan-urlzone-now-on-the-scene">UrlZone activity</a> <a href="https://www.fireeye.com/blog/threat-research/2016/01/urlzone_zones_inon.html">in Japan</a> but there are additional details behind this emerging threat that are worth pointing out.</p>

<p>Emails with the subject “copy 3” from multiple Gmail accounts contained a zipped executables and an empty email body. Proofpoint observation of actors such as those spreading Dridex over the past year shows increasing use of very simple lure techniques like this throughout 2015. The simplicity and lack of apparent ruse does not appear to hinder this technique: it is simple to create, requires no imagination on the part of the actor, needs no localization, and may be sufficient to entice the user to click.</p>

<p><img alt="Email lure" src="https://www.proofpoint.com/sites/default/files/vawtrak-1.png?itok=0amp7xtK" style="height:214px; width:480px"></p>

<p><em>Figure 1: Email lure</em></p>

<p>The attachment utilized in this campaign is Andromeda. <a href="https://blog.fortinet.com/post/a-good-look-at-the-andromeda-botnet" style="line-height: 1.6;">Andromeda is multi-purpose </a><a href="https://blog.fortinet.com/post/a-good-look-at-the-andromeda-botnet" style="line-height: 1.6;">bot</a>, but in this case it is primarily used to download UrlZone. However, as is often the case in malware ecosystems, Andromeda was also observed loading a plethora of additional malware.</p>

<ul>
	<li><a href="http://www.arbornetworks.com/blog/asert/an-update-on-the-UrlZone-banker/"><em>UrlZone</em></a>: a man-in-the-browser banking Trojan that has been around for several years</li>
	<li><em>Pushdo Downloader</em>: aside from <a href="https://www.virusbtn.com/virusbulletin/archive/2013/04/vb201304-Pushdo">adding the infected computer to a spam botnet</a>, the loader also downloaded a Neutrino Bot</li>
	<li><a href="http://malware.dontneedcoffee.com/2014/06/neutrino-bot-aka-kasidet.html"><em>Neutrino Bot</em></a>: a multi-purpose bot capable of stealing passwords, DDoS, loading additional payloads, etc. In this instance, it downloaded Pony for additional stealing.</li>
	<li><a href="https://www.damballa.com/pony-loader-2-0-steals-credentials-bitcoin-wallets-source-code-sale/"><em>Pony</em></a>: primarily used for loading additional malware and stealing passwords and Bitcoin wallets. This instance was used for its stealing capabilities.</li>
</ul>

<p>It is also worth mentioning that Proofpoint observed a related large related Cryptowall campaign on January 27, 2016. The Cryptowall downloaded the same Neutrino Bot that was present in the UrlZone campaign. Also, on December 11, 2015, we observed an email campaign delivering the same Andromeda botnet found in the UrlZone campaign, but in the December campaign, Andromeda only downloaded Pushdo. The observations suggest that these campaigns are likely connected by shared affiliates and/or spamming partners.</p>

<p><img alt="UrlZone and related campaigns" src="https://www.proofpoint.com/sites/default/files/vawtrak-2.png?itok=eLfAOKfO" style="height:411px; width:480px"></p>

<p><em>Figure 2: UrlZone and related campaigns</em></p>

<p>&nbsp;</p>

<p><strong>Dynamic Injects Shared by Vawtrak and UrlZone</strong></p>

<p>After extracting the injects code from both Trojans we observed that there is an overlap in the targeted banks. Both banking Trojans are using the same dynamic injects system that allows them to manipulate a financial institution’s website content. This means that the two banking Trojans use the same JavaScript code for stealing login credentials, PINs, one-time-passwords, etc. This could also mean that the responsible actors are sharing resources or renting from the same third party. Additionally, the injects JavaScript code appears to be written by a Russian developer, as observed by code comments such as “Startuem nash interval na proverku statusa”, which translates to “Begin our interval for checking the status”.</p>

<p><img alt="Screenshot of part of inject code" src="https://www.proofpoint.com/sites/default/files/vawtrak-4.png?itok=SZwKPU07" style="height:79px; width:480px"></p>

<p><em>Figure 6: Screenshot of part of the inject code</em></p>

<p><strong>Conclusion</strong></p>

<p>As others have noted, the emergence of banking Trojans in Japan and Spain presents some particular challenges. While organizations in other countries like the UK and the United States have been targets for massive Dridex, Dyre, Vawtrak (and other banking Trojans) campaigns and businesses there have implemented a number of protections, countries with less experience combatting these threats may find themselves vulnerable to considerable losses. Unfortunately, as threat actors saturate targets in many geographies, it's only a matter of time until new geographies begin experiencing the same sorts of volumes and persistence that characterize recent campaigns with Dridex and other malware.</p>
