<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="key-points">Key Points</h2>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Check Point Research (CPR) is closely tracking the malicious execution of compiled Javascript files, which led to the discovery of JSCEAL, a campaign targeting crypto app users.</li>



<li>The campaign leverages malicious advertisements to lure victims to install fake applications which impersonate almost 50 common cryptocurrency trading apps. During the first half of 2025, threat actors promoted around 35,000 malicious advertisements, which led to a few million views in the EU alone.</li>



<li>The actors separate the installer’s functionality into different components and most notably move some functionality to the JavaScript files inside the infected websites.</li>



<li>A modular, multi-layered infection flow enables the attackers to adapt new tactics and payloads at every stage of the operation.</li>



<li>The actors leveraged Node.js to launch a compiled JavaScript malware, which we call JSCEAL. This malware steals cryptocurrency-related data such as credentials and wallets.</li>
</ul>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="introduction">Introduction</h2>



<p>Following our&nbsp;<a href="https://research.checkpoint.com/2024/exploring-compiled-v8-javascript-usage-in-malware/">VIEW8</a>&nbsp;publication, an open source tool for analysis of Compiled JavaScript files (JSC), we are continuously tracking the usage of such files by threat actors. Among the threats we identified, a significant campaign caught our attention, which we call JSCEAL. The campaign, which impersonates common crypto trading apps, has been active since at least March 2024 and has gradually evolved over time.</p>



<p>In the campaign’s latest iteration, the threat actors adopted unique anti-analysis mechanisms. These mechanisms are kicked off by MSI installers downloaded from fake websites which are promoted by malicious advertisements. Following the initial infection, an elaborate script-based fingerprinting chain kicks off, eventually leading to the final JSC payload.</p>



<p>The unique anti-evasion methods used in this campaign, in combination with the adoption of JSC to execute the final payload, has led to extremely low detection rates, especially considering its wide distribution. During our analysis, hundreds of samples associated with this activity were submitted to VirusTotal and were undetected for a prolonged period of time.</p>



<p>While some parts of this campaign were discussed&nbsp;<a href="https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2025/04/15/threat-actors-misuse-node-js-to-deliver-malware-and-other-malicious-payloads/">publicly</a>, in this blog we highlight the technical details of the latest iteration of the JSCEAL campaign, focusing on its deployment. During finalization of this research, we recently came across a whitepaper covering significant portions of this activity under the name “<a href="https://labs.withsecure.com/publications/weevilproxy">WeevilProxy</a>. The findings in this report are correlated with our understanding of the JSCEAL campaign.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="background">Background</h2>



<p>Compiled V8 JavaScript is a lesser-known feature in V8, Google’s JavaScript engine, that enables the compilation of JavaScript into low-level bytecode. This technique helps attackers to evade static detections and hide their original source code, making it almost impossible to analyze statically. Earlier, we published&nbsp;<a href="https://research.checkpoint.com/2024/exploring-compiled-v8-javascript-usage-in-malware/">VIEW8</a>, an open-source tool to decompile such files.</p>



<p>Among the various campaigns that utilize JSC files, the&nbsp;<strong>JSCEAL campaign</strong>&nbsp;stands out for its scale, reach, and technical sophistication. First observed in March 2024, JSCEAL has evolved considerably since its inception.</p>



<p>JSCEAL began with limited activity but has grown into a far more sophisticated operation. In the campaign’s latest phase, the threat actors acquired a large number of domains and adopted distinctive techniques to evade detection, including sometimes avoiding deploying the final payload. While it’s difficult to measure the full extent of its reach, several findings throughout our research suggest this campaign has had a significant impact.</p>



<p>Our analysis of the advertising campaign suggests it has generated millions of views, prompting users to download the malicious installers. Despite its broad deployment, the campaign continues its stealth operations. Recent variants of the installers remain undetected on VirusTotal despite being submitted more than a hundred(!) times. More recently, as parts of the campaign were exposed, some of its components were flagged as malicious.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="infection-chain">Infection Chain</h2>



<p>The deployment of the most recent version of JSCEAL is multi-layered and involves three main stages:&nbsp;<strong>Initial Deployment,</strong>&nbsp;<strong>Profiling Scripts</strong>, and the&nbsp;<strong>Final JSC Payload</strong>. We focus on the first two stages, which evolved significantly since we first encountered this campaign.</p>

<figure class="aligncenter size-full"><img fetchpriority="high" decoding="async" width="2560" height="1477" src="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/Infection_chain_abstract-scaled.png" alt="" class="wp-image-31706" srcset="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/Infection_chain_abstract-scaled.png 2560w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/Infection_chain_abstract-300x173.png 300w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/Infection_chain_abstract-1024x591.png 1024w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/Infection_chain_abstract-768x443.png 768w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/Infection_chain_abstract-1536x886.png 1536w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/Infection_chain_abstract-2048x1181.png 2048w" sizes="(max-width: 2560px) 100vw, 2560px" /><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Figure 01 &#8211; Abstract infection flow.</figcaption></figure></div>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="initial-deployment">Initial Deployment</h2>



<p>The initial stage of the campaign leverages malicious advertisements to lure users to download a malicious MSI installer from an attacker-controlled website. The threat actors implemented a unique mechanism that requires both the malicious site and the installer to run in parallel for successful execution, which significantly complicates analysis and detection efforts.</p>


<div class="wp-block-image">
<figure class="aligncenter size-full"><img decoding="async" width="2560" height="918" src="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/infection_chain_installer-scaled.png" alt="" class="wp-image-31708" srcset="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/infection_chain_installer-scaled.png 2560w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/infection_chain_installer-300x108.png 300w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/infection_chain_installer-1024x367.png 1024w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/infection_chain_installer-768x275.png 768w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/infection_chain_installer-1536x551.png 1536w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/infection_chain_installer-2048x734.png 2048w" sizes="(max-width: 2560px) 100vw, 2560px" /><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Figure 02 &#8211; The initial deployment infection flow.</figcaption></figure></div>


<p></p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="malicious-advertisements">Malicious Advertisements</h3>



<p>The infection chain starts with paid malvertising on social media. The actors use either stolen accounts or newly created ones to write malicious posts and increase their reach with paid promotions. The malicious advertisements are usually related to various cryptocurrencies, tokens, and financial institutions. We observed almost 50 different financial institutions who were impersonated in this campaign.</p>


<div class="wp-block-image">
<figure class="aligncenter"><img decoding="async" src="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/KW59ANDRGD-image4.png" alt="Figure 03 - Malicious advertisements on Facebook."/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Figure 03 &#8211; Malicious advertisements on Facebook.</figcaption></figure></div>


<p>To date, the actors impersonated dozens of different brands. The table in&nbsp;<em><strong>Appendix A</strong></em>&nbsp;lists the forms of impersonation we identified, whether through an existing subdomain, an installer file, or both. Any identified malicious advertisements or fake websites are listed in a separate column.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="redirection-chain">Redirection Chain</h3>



<p>Most of the advertisements do not contain direct links to the landing pages. Instead, there is typically one layer of redirection, which leads either to a landing page or a decoy page.</p>



<p>The domain names used in the redirection chain are not completely random but follow specific naming conventions:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>The top-level domain (TLD) ends with the extension&nbsp;<code>.com</code></li>



<li>Each domain consists of two or three words connected with dashes</li>



<li>The words are selected from the following set:&nbsp;<code>app</code>,&nbsp;<code>download</code>,&nbsp;<code>desktop</code>,&nbsp;<code>pc</code>,&nbsp;<code>window</code></li>



<li>Each word may appear in its singular or plural form (with an added&nbsp;<code>s</code>), but not both within the same domain name</li>
</ul>



<p>Each domain may include one or more subdomains. These subdomain names usually resemble the brands the actors attempt to impersonate.</p>



<p>Using&nbsp;<a href="https://mathigon.org/world/Combinatorics">combinatorics</a>, we estimate that 560 unique domain names comply with the four rules above. At the time of publication, only about 15% of these domains appear to be registered. While not all are linked to this specific campaign, most of them are. The redirection domains associated with this activity are listed in the&nbsp;<em><strong>IOC</strong></em>&nbsp;section.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="decoy-websites">Decoy Websites</h3>



<p>After the victim clicks the link, a first layer of filtering is applied—meaning not every target is redirected to the fake webpage. If the target’s IP address is not within the desired range, or the referrer is not Facebook, a decoy website is displayed instead.</p>


<div class="wp-block-image">
<figure class="aligncenter"><img decoding="async" src="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/KW59ANDRGD-image5.png" alt="Figure 04 - Decoy website."/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Figure 04 &#8211; Decoy website.</figcaption></figure></div>


<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="fake-landing-websites">Fake (landing) websites</h3>



<p>If the redirection criteria are met, the target is redirected to a fake website that appears legitimate and offers to download and install the application.</p>


<div class="wp-block-image">
<figure class="aligncenter"><img decoding="async" src="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/KW59ANDRGD-image6.png" alt="Figure 05 - Fake landing website."/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Figure 05 &#8211; Fake landing website.</figcaption></figure></div>


<p>After clicking the download button, the target is offered an MSI installer file. The installation is accompanied by a short three-page tutorial that encourages the target to complete the setup.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter"><img decoding="async" src="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/KW59ANDRGD-image7.png" alt="Figure 06 - Installation procedure as presented by the website to
lute victims to install the file immediately."/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Figure 06 &#8211; Installation procedure as presented by the website to lute victims to install the file immediately.</figcaption></figure>



<p>Interestingly, the website includes a JavaScript script that attempts to communicate with a localhost server on port 30303. At this stage, no server is listening on that port, so the request returns a 502 &#8211; Bad Gateway status code.</p>


<div class="wp-block-image">
<figure class="aligncenter"><img decoding="async" src="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/KW59ANDRGD-image8.png" alt="Figure 07 - Unsuccessful queries to localhost, port 30303."/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Figure 07 &#8211; Unsuccessful queries to localhost, port 30303.</figcaption></figure></div>


<p>The fake website is essential for the installer to function. The infection flow cannot proceed without it, and any static analysis of the installer alone will be inconclusive.</p>



<p>The website hosts two JavaScript files:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Primary script</strong> – Handles analytics, tracks the installation process, and initiates a worker script via a <a href="https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/API/SharedWorker">SharedWorker</a>.</li>



<li><strong>Worker script</strong>&nbsp;– Initiates POST requests that are handled by other components unpacked by the MSI installer.</li>
</ul>



<p>The tracking methods used by the primary script include&nbsp;<a href="https://posthog.com/">PostHog</a>,&nbsp;<a href="https://www.facebook.com/business/tools/meta-pixel">Meta Pixel</a>, and&nbsp;<a href="https://marketingplatform.google.com/about/analytics/">Google Analytics</a>. However, only the PostHog integration is actually initialized. All campaign metadata is stored in a&nbsp;<code>PARAMS</code>&nbsp;JSON object, which includes:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><code>site</code>: The name of the impersonated financial platform.</li>



<li><code>utm_campaign</code>: A value ending in&nbsp;<strong>1406</strong>, suggesting the installer launch date of&nbsp;<strong>June 14, 2025.</strong></li>
</ul>



<pre class="EnlighterJSRAW" data-enlighter-language="generic" data-enlighter-theme="" data-enlighter-highlight="" data-enlighter-linenumbers="" data-enlighter-lineoffset="" data-enlighter-title="" data-enlighter-group="">&lt;script> window.PARAMS = {
        ...
    "site": " #48 TradingView (New Version)",
    ...
    "utm_campaign": "TradingView1406",
        ...
}
&lt;/script></pre>



<p>The worker script is responsible for communicating with localhost. The script defines the localhost IP address (127.0.0.1) and port (30303) and contains an array of&nbsp;<a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/win32/wmisdk/wmi-start-page">WMI</a>&nbsp;commands that it later requests to execute.</p>



<pre class="EnlighterJSRAW" data-enlighter-language="generic" data-enlighter-theme="" data-enlighter-highlight="" data-enlighter-linenumbers="" data-enlighter-lineoffset="" data-enlighter-title="" data-enlighter-group="">const localhost = "hxxp://127.0.0.1:30303";
const WMIs = [
                                "SELECT Manufacturer, Name, Version FROM Win32_BIOS", 
                                "SELECT Name, Manufacturer, Domain, Model, NumberOfLogicalProcessors FROM Win32_ComputerSystem", 
                                "SELECT Name, Version, RegisteredUser FROM Win32_OperatingSystem"
];</pre>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="scope-of-the-malvertising-campaign">Scope of the malvertising campaign</h3>



<p>Determining the full scope of a malicious campaign is often not possible. Fortunately for us as researchers, the European Union adopted the&nbsp;<a href="https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/dsa-vlops">Digital Services Act</a>, which promotes “transparent advertising.” We leveraged Meta’s&nbsp;<a href="https://www.facebook.com/ads/library/">Ad Library</a>&nbsp;to estimate a lower bound on the scale of the malvertising campaign.</p>



<p>We searched for all known domains and subdomains discovered using the method described in the “Redirection Chain” section. Each search in the Ad Library provides an estimate of the number of ads containing the corresponding malicious domain. Many of these ads reuse the same image, video, or text, but vary in start dates, geographic targeting, or ad budgets. These ads are often short-lived and typically remain active for only a few hours.</p>


<div class="wp-block-image">
<figure class="aligncenter size-full"><img decoding="async" width="627" height="198" src="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/KW59ANDRGD-image9.png" alt="" class="wp-image-31711" srcset="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/KW59ANDRGD-image9.png 627w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/KW59ANDRGD-image9-300x95.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 627px) 100vw, 627px" /><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Figure 10 &#8211; Malicious domain appears in about 1100 malicious ads.</figcaption></figure></div>


<p>We limited our search to the first half of 2025 (January 1–June 30). Out of a few hundred queries, approximately 120 returned results which amounted to a total of around 35,000 ads.</p>



<p>For each advertisement, the Ad Library provides its&nbsp;<strong>reach</strong>, defined as “the number of Accounts Center accounts in the EU that saw this ad at least once.” It’s important to note that reach does not equal the number of victims. While we did not query every individual ad, we observed reach values ranging from as low as 1 to as high as several thousand.</p>


<div class="wp-block-image">

<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="msi-installers">MSI Installers</h3>



<p>After the user starts the installation, the MSI installer (created with&nbsp;<a href="https://github.com/wixtoolset">WIX Toolset</a>) invokes the CustomAction function. <h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="conclusion">Conclusion</h2>



<p>Cybercrime actors continue to weaponize legitimate applications and platforms to compromise unsuspecting users and exfiltrate sensitive data for financial gain. While such masquerading techniques are well established, the adoption of Node.js to load compiled JavaScript (JSC) payloads, as demonstrated by JSCEAL, represents a notable shift. Using JSC files allows attackers to simply and effectively conceal their code, helping it evade security mechanisms, and making it difficult to analyze.</p>



<p>The JSCEAL payload we observed is part of a larger trend, involving the usage JSC-based payloads. Although these kinds of payload have a low detection rate, they still depend on legitimate frameworks that can be monitored by security solutions. Detecting malicious executions of JSC application, as showcased in this article, will be very effective against such threats.</p>
