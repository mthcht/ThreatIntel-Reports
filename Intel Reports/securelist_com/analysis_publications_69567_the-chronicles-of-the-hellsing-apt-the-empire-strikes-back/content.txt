<h2 id="introduction">Introduction</h2>
<p>One of the most active APT groups in Asia, and especially around the South China Sea area is &#8220;Naikon&#8221;.   Naikon plays a key part in our story, but the focus of this report is on another threat actor entirely; one who came to our attention when they hit back at a Naikon attack. </p>
<p>Naikon is known for its custom backdoor, called <a href="https://blog.trendmicro.com/trendlabs-security-intelligence/bkdr_rarstone-new-rat-to-watch-out-for/">RARSTONE</a>, which our colleagues at Trend Micro have described in detail. The name Naikon comes from a custom user agent string, &#8220;NOKIAN95/WEB&#8221;, located within the backdoor: </p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062141/Hellsing_1.jpg" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" id="69624" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062141/Hellsing_1.jpg" alt="Hellsing_1" width="755" height="284" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-69624" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062141/Hellsing_1.jpg 755w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062141/Hellsing_1-300x113.jpg 300w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 755px) 100vw, 755px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align:center;font-weight:bold"><em>NOKIAN string in Naikon backdoor</em></p>
<p>The Naikon group is mostly active in countries such as the Philippines, Malaysia, Cambodia, Indonesia, Vietnam, Myanmar, Singapore, and Nepal, hitting a variety of targets in a very opportunistic way. What was perhaps one of the biggest operations of the Naikon group was launched in March 2014, in the wake of the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malaysia_Airlines_Flight_370">MH370 tragedy</a> that took place on March 8th.  By  March 11th, the Naikon group was actively hitting most of the nations involved in the search for MH370. The targets were extremely wide-ranging but included institutions with access to information related to the disappearance of MH370, such as:</p>
<ul>
<li>Office of the President</li>
<li>Armed Forces</li>
<li>Office of the Cabinet Secretary</li>
<li>National Security Council(s)</li>
<li>Office of the Solicitor General</li>
<li>National Intelligence Coordinating Agency</li>
<li>Civil Aviation Authority</li>
<li>Department of Justice</li>
<li>National Police</li>
<li>Presidential Management Staff</li>
</ul>
<p>The Naikon group used mostly spear-phished documents for the attacks, with CVE-2012-0158 exploits that dropped the group&#8217;s signature backdoor. </p>
<p><strong>While many of these attacks were successful, at least one of the targets didn&#8217;t seem to like being hit, and instead of opening the documents, decided on a very different course of action.</strong></p>
<h2 id="the-empire-strikes-back">The empire strikes back</h2>
<p>Here&#8217;s a question &#8211; what should you do when you receiving a suspicious document from somebody you don&#8217;t know, or know very little? Choose one:</p>
<ul>
<li>Open the document</li>
<li>Don&#8217;t open the document</li>
<li>Open the document on a Mac (everybody knows <a href="https://securelist.com/flashfake-mac-os-x-botnet-confirmed-25/32661/">Mac&#8217;s don&#8217;t get viruses</a>)</li>
<li>Open the document in a virtual machine with Linux</li>
</ul>
<p>Based on our experience, most people would say 2, 3 or 4. Very few would open the document and even fewer would actually decide to test the attacker and verify its story.</p>
<p>But this is exactly what happened when one of the Naikon spear-phishing targets received a suspicious email. Instead of opening the document or choosing to open it on an exotic platform, they decided to check the story with the sender:</p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062137/Hellsing_2.jpg" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" id="69625" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062137/Hellsing_2.jpg" alt="Hellsing_2" width="755" height="284" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-69625" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062137/Hellsing_2.jpg 755w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062137/Hellsing_2-300x113.jpg 300w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 755px) 100vw, 755px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align:center;font-weight:bold"><em>Naikon target asks for confirmation of the email</em></p>
<p>In the email above, we can see the target questioning the authenticity of the Naikon spear-phishing. They ask the sender if it was their intention to email this document.</p>
<p>The attacker was, of course, not confused in the slightest, and being very familiar with the internal structure of the target&#8217;s government agency, replied claiming that they work for the secretariat division and were instructed to send it by the organization&#8217;s management:</p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062132/Hellsing_3.jpg" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" id="69626" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062132/Hellsing_3.jpg" alt="Hellsing_3" width="815" height="328" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-69626" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062132/Hellsing_3.jpg 815w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062132/Hellsing_3-300x121.jpg 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062132/Hellsing_3-768x309.jpg 768w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 815px) 100vw, 815px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align:center;font-weight:bold"><em>Naikon attacker replies to the target</em></p>
<p>The reply is written in poor English and indicates that the attacker is probably not as proficient in the language as the intended victim. Seeing the reply, the target obviously decided not to open the document. Moreover, they decided to go a bit further and try to learn more about the attacker.</p>
<p>Not long after the first exchange, the following email was sent to the attacker by the target:</p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062129/Hellsing_4.jpg" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" id="69627" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062129/Hellsing_4.jpg" alt="Hellsing_4" width="871" height="368" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-69627" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062129/Hellsing_4.jpg 871w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062129/Hellsing_4-300x127.jpg 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062129/Hellsing_4-768x324.jpg 768w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 871px) 100vw, 871px" /></a></p>
<p>The attachment is a RAR archive with password, which allows it to safely bypass malware scanners associated with the free email account used by the attackers. Inside the archive we find two decode PDF files and one SCR file:</p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062124/Hellsing_5.jpg" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" id="69628" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062124/Hellsing_5.jpg" alt="Hellsing_5" width="490" height="151" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-69628" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062124/Hellsing_5.jpg 490w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062124/Hellsing_5-300x92.jpg 300w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 490px) 100vw, 490px" /></a></p>
<p>Much to our surprise, the &#8220;SCR&#8221; file turned out to be a backdoor prepared especially for the Naikon fraudsters. </p>
<p>The file &#8220;<strong>Directory of &#8230; Mar 31, 2014.scr</strong>&#8221; (md5: <strong>198fc1af5cd278091f36645a77c18ffa</strong>) drops a blank document containing the error message and a backdoor module (md5: <strong>588f41b1f34b29529bc117346355113f</strong>). The backdoor connects to the command server located at <strong>philippinenews[.]mooo[.]com</strong>.</p>
<p>The backdoor can perform the following actions:</p>
<ul>
<li>download files</li>
<li>upload files</li>
<li>update itself</li>
<li>uninstall itself</li>
</ul>
<p>We were amazed to see this course of action and decided to investigate the &#8220;Empire Strikes Back&#8221;-door further; naming the actor &#8220;Hellsing&#8221; (explained later).</p>
<p>The malware used by the intended victim appears to have the following geographical distribution, according to KSN data:</p>
<ul>
<li>Malaysia – government networks</li>
<li>Philippines – government networks</li>
<li>Indonesia – government networks</li>
<li>USA &#8211; diplomatic agencies</li>
<li>India (old versions of malware)</li>
</ul>
<p>In addition, we&#8217;ve observed the targeting of ASEAN-related entities.</p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062119/Hellsing_6.jpg" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" id="69629" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062119/Hellsing_6.jpg" alt="Hellsing_6" width="1142" height="827" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-69629" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062119/Hellsing_6.jpg 1142w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062119/Hellsing_6-300x217.jpg 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062119/Hellsing_6-1024x742.jpg 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062119/Hellsing_6-768x556.jpg 768w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 1142px) 100vw, 1142px" /></a></p>

<h2 id="artifacts-and-overlap-with-other-apts">Artifacts and overlap with other APTs</h2>
<p>Interestingly, some of the infrastructure used by the attackers appears to overlap (although around a year apart) with a group tracked internally at Kaspersky Lab as PlayfullDragon (also known as &#8220;GREF&#8221;); while other aspects of the infrastructure overlap with a group known as Mirage or Vixen Panda.</p>
<p>For instance, one of the PlayfullDragon&#8217;s <a href="https://www.computerworld.com/article/2694311/cyberespionage-group-starts-using-new-mac-os-x-backdoor-program.html">Xslcmd backdoors described by our colleagues from FireEye</a> (md5: 6c3be96b65a7db4662ccaae34d6e72cc) beams to <strong>cdi.indiadigest[.]in:53</strong>. One of the Hellsing samples we analysed (md5: 0cbefd8cd4b9a36c791d926f84f10b7b) connects to the C&amp;C server at <strong>webmm[.]indiadigest[.]in</strong>. Although the hostname is not the same, the top level domain suggests some kind of connection between the groups. Several other C&amp;C subdomains on &#8220;indiadigest[.]in&#8221; include:</p>
<ul>
<li>aac.indiadigest[.]in</li>
<li>ld.indiadigest[.]in</li>
<li>longc.indiadigest[.]in</li>
</ul>
<p>Another overlap we observed is with an APT known as Cycldek or Goblin Panda. Some of the Hellsing samples we analysed in this operation (e.g. md5: a91c9a2b1bc4020514c6c49c5ff84298) communicate with the server <strong>webb[.]huntingtomingalls[.]com</strong>, using a protocol specific to the Cycldek backdoors (binup.asp/textup.asp/online.asp).</p>
<p>It appears that the Hellsing developer started with the Cycldek sources and worked together with the operators from other APT groups. Nevertheless, it is sufficiently different to warrant classification as a stand-alone operation. </p>
<p>So, where does the Hellsing name come from? One of the samples we analysed (md5: 036e021e1b7f61cddfd294f791de7ea2) appears to have been compiled in a rush and the attacker forgot to remove the debug information. One can see the project name is Hellsing and the malware is called &#8220;msger&#8221;:</p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062116/Hellsing_7.jpg" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" id="69630" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062116/Hellsing_7.jpg" alt="Hellsing_7" width="759" height="154" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-69630" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062116/Hellsing_7.jpg 759w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062116/Hellsing_7-300x61.jpg 300w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 759px) 100vw, 759px" /></a></p>
<p>Of course, Hellsing can have many different meanings, including the famous doctor from Bram Stoker&#8217;s Dracula. However, according to Wikipedia, <em>&#8220;Hellsing (ヘルシング Herushingu) is also a Japanese <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manga">manga</a> series written and illustrated by <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kouta_Hirano">Kouta Hirano</a>. It first premiered in <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Young_King_OURs">Young King Ours</a> in 1997 and ended in September 2008&#8243;</em>.</p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062111/Hellsing_8.jpg" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" id="69631" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062111/Hellsing_8.jpg" alt="Hellsing_8" width="337" height="479" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-69631" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062111/Hellsing_8.jpg 337w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062111/Hellsing_8-211x300.jpg 211w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 337px) 100vw, 337px" /></a></p>
<p>The Hellsing series chronicles the efforts of the mysterious and secret Hellsing Organization, as it combats <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vampire">vampires</a>, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghoul">ghouls</a>, and other <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernatural">supernatural</a> foes; which makes it perhaps an appropriate name for our group.</p>
<p>In addition to the Hellsing/msger malware, we&#8217;ve identified a second generation of Trojan samples which appear to be called &#8220;xweber&#8221; by the attackers:</p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062106/Hellsing_9.jpg" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" id="69632" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062106/Hellsing_9.jpg" alt="Hellsing_9" width="759" height="149" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-69632" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062106/Hellsing_9.jpg 759w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062106/Hellsing_9-300x59.jpg 300w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 759px) 100vw, 759px" /></a></p>
<p>&#8220;Xweber&#8221; seems to be the more recent Trojan, taking into account compilation timestamps. All the &#8220;msger&#8221; samples we have seen appear to have been compiled in 2012.  The &#8220;Xweber&#8221; samples are from 2013 and from 2014, indicating that at some point during 2013 the &#8220;msger&#8221; malware project was renamed and/or integrated into &#8220;Xweber&#8221;.</p>
<p>During our investigation we&#8217;ve observed the Hellsing APT using both the &#8220;Xweber&#8221; and &#8220;msger&#8221; backdoors in their attacks, as well as other tools named &#8220;xrat&#8221;, &#8220;clare&#8221;, &#8220;irene&#8221; and &#8220;xKat&#8221;.</p>

<h2 id="attribution">Attribution</h2>
<p>In general, the attribution of APTs is a very tricky task which is why we prefer to publish technical details and allow others to draw their own conclusions.</p>
<p>The Hellsing-related samples appear to have been compiled around the following times:</p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062146/Hellsing_12.jpg" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" id="69623" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062146/Hellsing_12.jpg" alt="Hellsing_12" width="1037" height="739" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-69623" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062146/Hellsing_12.jpg 1037w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062146/Hellsing_12-300x214.jpg 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062146/Hellsing_12-1024x730.jpg 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2015/04/08062146/Hellsing_12-768x547.jpg 768w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 1037px) 100vw, 1037px" /></a></p>
<p>Assuming normal work starts at around 9 am, the attacker seems to be most active in a time-zone of GMT+8 or +9, considering a work program of 9/10 am to 6/7pm.</p>
<h2 id="conclusions">Conclusions</h2>
<p>The Hellsing APT group is currently active in the APAC region, hitting targets mainly in the South China Sea area, with a focus on Malaysia, the Philippines and Indonesia. The group has a relatively small footprint compared to massive operations such as &#8220;<a href="https://securelist.com/equation-the-death-star-of-malware-galaxy/68750/">Equation</a>&#8220;. Smaller groups can have the advantage of being able to stay under the radar for longer periods of time, which is what happened here.</p>
<p>The targeting of the Naikon group by the Hellsing APT is perhaps the most interesting part. In the past, we&#8217;ve seen APT groups accidentally hitting each other while stealing address books from victims and then mass-mailing everyone on each of these lists. But, considering the timing and origin of the attack, the current case seems more likely to be an APT-on-APT attack.</p>
<p>To protect against a Hellsing attack, we recommend that organisations follow basic security best practices:</p>
<ul>
<li>Don&#8217;t open attachments from people you don&#8217;t know</li>
<li>Beware of password-protected archives which contain SCR or other executable files inside</li>
<li>If you are unsure about the attachment, try to open it in a sandbox</li>
<li>Make sure you have a modern operating system with all patches installed</li>
<li>Update all third party applications such as Microsoft Office, Java, Adobe Flash Player and Adobe Reader</li>
</ul>
<p>Kaspersky Lab products detect the backdoors used by the Hellsing attacker as: <strong>HEUR:Trojan.Win32.Generic, Trojan-Dropper.Win32.Agent.kbuj, Trojan-Dropper.Win32.Agent.kzqq</strong>.</p>
