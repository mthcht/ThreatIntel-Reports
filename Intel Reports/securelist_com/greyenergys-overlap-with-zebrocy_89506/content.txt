			<a  href="https://securelist.com/author/icscert/" >
													<img src="https://securelist.com/wp-content/themes/securelist2020/assets/images/avatar-default/avatar_default_2.png">
										<span>Kaspersky ICS CERT</span></a>
		</li>
														</ul>
											</div>

											
											<div class="js-reading-content">
												<div class="c-wysiwyg">
													<p>In October 2018, ESET published a<a target="_blank" href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/10/ESET_GreyEnergy.pdf" rel="noopener noreferrer"> report</a> describing a set of activity they called GreyEnergy, which is believed to be a successor to BlackEnergy group. BlackEnergy (a.k.a. Sandworm) is best known, among other things, for having been involved in attacks against Ukrainian energy facilities in 2015, which led to power outages. Like its predecessor, GreyEnergy malware has been detected attacking industrial and ICS targets, mainly in Ukraine.</p>
<p><a target="_blank" href="https://ics-cert.kaspersky.com/" rel="noopener noreferrer">Kaspersky Lab ICS CERT</a> has identified an overlap between GreyEnergy and a Sofacy subset called <a target="_blank" href="https://securelist.com/a-slice-of-2017-sofacy-activity/83930/" rel="noopener noreferrer">&#8220;Zebrocy&#8221;</a>. The Zebrocy activity was named after malware that Sofacy group began to use since mid-November 2015 for the post-exploitation stage of attacks on its victims. Zebrocy&#8217;s targets are widely spread across the Middle East, Europe and Asia and the targets&#8217; profiles are mostly government-related.</p>
<p>Both sets of activity used the same servers at the same time and targeted the same organization.</p>
<h2 id="details">Details</h2>
<h3 id="servers">Servers</h3>
<p>In our private APT Intel report from July 2018 &#8220;Zebrocy implements new VBA anti-sandboxing tricks&#8221;, details were provided about different Zebrocy C2 servers, including <strong>193.23.181[.]151</strong>.</p>
<p>In the course of our research, the following Zebrocy samples were found to use the same server to download additional components (MD5):</p>
<p>7f20f7fbce9deee893dbce1a1b62827d<br />
170d2721b91482e5cabf3d2fec091151<br />
eae0b8997c82ebd93e999d4ce14dedf5<br />
a5cbf5a131e84cd2c0a11fca5ddaa50a<br />
c9e1b0628ac62e5cb01bf1fa30ac8317</p>
<p>The URL used to download additional data looks as follows:</p>
<p>hxxp://<strong>193.23.181</strong>[.]151/help-desk/remote-assistant-service/PostId.php?q={hex}</p>
<p>This same C2 server was also used in a spearphishing email attachment sent by GreyEnergy (aka FELIXROOT), as mentioned in a <a target="_blank" href="https://www.zdnet.com/article/hacking-campaign-uses-old-microsoft-office-flaws-to-create-backdoors-steal-files/" rel="noopener noreferrer">FireEye report</a>. Details on this attachment are as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li>The file (11227eca89cc053fb189fac3ebf27497) with the name &#8220;Seminar.rtf&#8221; exploited CVE-2017-0199</li>
<li>&#8220;Seminar.rtf&#8221; downloaded a second stage document from: hxxp://<strong>193.23.181[.]151</strong>/Seminar.rtf  (4de5adb865b5198b4f2593ad436fceff, exploiting CVE-2017-11882)</li>
<li>The original document (Seminar.rtf) was hosted on the same server and downloaded by victims from:  hxxp://<strong>193.23.181[.]151</strong>/ministerstvo-energetiki/seminars/2019/06/Seminar.rtf</li>
</ul>
<p>Another server we detected that was used both by Zebrocy and by GreyEnergy is <strong>185.217.0[.]124</strong>. Similarly, we detected a spearphishing GreyEnergy document (a541295eca38eaa4fde122468d633083, exploiting CVE-2017-11882), also named &#8220;Seminar.rtf&#8221;.</p>
<div style="width: 1010px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a target="_blank" href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2019/01/23125754/190123-GreyEnergy_overlap-1.png" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="magnificImage"><img decoding="async" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2019/01/23125754/190123-GreyEnergy_overlap-1.png" style="max-height:60vh" alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-81520" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text"><em>&#8220;Seminar.rtf&#8221;, a GreyEnergy decoy document</em></p></div>
<p>This document downloads a GreyEnergy sample (78734cd268e5c9ab4184e1bbe21a6eb9) from the following SMB link:</p>
<p>\\<strong>185.217.0[.]124</strong>\Doc\Seminar\Seminar_2018_1.AO-A</p>
<p>The following Zebrocy samples use this server as C2:</p>
<p>7f20f7fbce9deee893dbce1a1b62827d<br />
170d2721b91482e5cabf3d2fec091151<br />
3803af6700ff4f712cd698cee262d4ac<br />
e3100228f90692a19f88d9acb620960d</p>
<p>They retrieve additional data from the following URL:</p>
<p>hxxp://<strong>185.217.0[.]124</strong>/help-desk/remote-assistant-service/PostId.php?q={hex}</p>
<p>It is worth noting that at least two samples from the above list use both <strong>193.23.181[.]151</strong> and <strong>185.217.0[.]124</strong> as C2s.</p>
<div style="width: 1010px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a target="_blank" href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2019/01/23125807/190123-GreyEnergy_overlap-2.png" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="magnificImage"><img decoding="async" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2019/01/23125807/190123-GreyEnergy_overlap-2.png" style="max-height:60vh" alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-81520" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text"><em>Hosts associated with GreyEnergy and Zebrocy</em></p></div>
<h3 id="attacked-company">Attacked company</h3>
<p>Additionally, both GreyEnergy and Zebrocy spearphishing documents targeted a number of industrial companies in Kazakhstan. One of them was attacked in June 2018.</p>
<div style="width: 1010px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2019/01/24083359/190124-GreyEnergy_overlap-3.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="636" height="357" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2019/01/24083359/190124-GreyEnergy_overlap-3.png" alt="" style="max-height:60vh" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-89521" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2019/01/24083359/190124-GreyEnergy_overlap-3.png 636w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2019/01/24083359/190124-GreyEnergy_overlap-3-300x168.png 300w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 636px) 100vw, 636px" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text"><em>GreyEnergy and Zebrocy overlap</em></p></div>
<h3 id="attack-timeframe">Attack timeframe</h3>
<p>A spearphishing document entitled &#8216;Seminar.rtf&#8217;, which retrieved a GreyEnergy sample, was sent to the company approximately on June 21, 2018, followed by a Zebrocy spearphishing document sent approximately on June 28:</p>
<div style="width: 1010px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a target="_blank" href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2019/01/23125842/190123-GreyEnergy_overlap-4.png" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="magnificImage"><img decoding="async" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2019/01/23125842/190123-GreyEnergy_overlap-4.png" style="max-height:60vh" alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-81520" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text"><em>&#8216;(28.06.18) Izmeneniya v prikaz PK.doc&#8217; Zebrocy decoy document translation: </em><br />
<em>&#8216;Changes to order, Republic of Kazakhstan&#8217;</em></p></div>
<p>The two C2 servers discussed above were actively used by Zebrocy and GreyEnergy almost at the same time:</p>
<ul>
<li>193.23.181[.]151 was used by GreyEnergy and Zebrocy in June 2018</li>
<li>185.217.0[.]124 was used by GreyEnergy between May and June 2018 and by Zebrocy in June 2018</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="conclusions">Conclusions</h2>
<p>The GreyEnergy/BlackEnergy actor is an advanced group that possesses extensive knowledge on penetrating into their victim´s networks and exploiting any vulnerabilities it finds. This actor has demonstrated its ability to update its tools and infrastructure in order to avoid detection, tracking, and attribution.</p>
<p>Though no direct evidence exists on the origins of GreyEnergy, the links between a Sofacy subset known as Zebrocy and GreyEnergy suggest that these groups are related, as has been suggested before by some public analysis. In this paper, we detailed how both groups shared the same C2 server infrastructure during a certain period of time and how they both targeted the same organization almost at the same time, which seems to confirm the relationship&#8217;s existence.</p>
