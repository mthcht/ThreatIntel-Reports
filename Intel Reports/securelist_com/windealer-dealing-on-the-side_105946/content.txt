													<h2 id="introduction">Introduction</h2>
<p>LuoYu is a lesser-known threat actor that has been active since 2008. It primarily goes after targets located in China, such as foreign diplomatic organizations established in the country, members of the academic community, or companies from the defense, logistics and telecommunications sectors. In their initial disclosures on this threat actor, <a href="https://jsac.jpcert.or.jp/archive/2021/pdf/JSAC2021_301_shui-leon_en.pdf">TeamT5</a> identified three malware families: SpyDealer, Demsty and WinDealer. The actor behind these families is capable of targeting Windows, Linux and macOS machines, as well as Android devices.</p>
<p>In previous years, Kaspersky investigated LuoYu&#8217;s activities and was able to confirm the connection between Demsty and WinDealer. On January 27, we delivered a joint presentation with TeamT5 and ITOCHU Corporation at <a href="https://jsac.jpcert.or.jp/archive/2022/pdf/JSAC2022_7_leon-niwa-ishimaru_en.pdf">Japan Security Analyst Conference (JSAC)</a> to provide an update on the actor&#8217;s latest activities. In this article, we will focus on one of the most groundbreaking developments: the fact that LuoYu has the ability to perform <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Man-on-the-side_attack">man-on-the-side</a> attacks.</p>
<h2 id="delivery-method">Delivery method</h2>
<p>In the past, LuoYu used watering-hole attacks (for instance, on local news websites) to infect their targets. Seeing that some variants of their Android malware impersonate a popular messaging app in Asia, it is also likely that malicious APKs are distributed in a variety of ways, including social engineering to convince users to install fake updates for their applications.</p>
<p>In 2020, we discovered a whole new distribution method for the WinDealer malware that leverages the automatic update mechanism of select legitimate applications. In one case we investigated, we noticed that a signed executable qgametool.exe (MD5 <a href="https://opentip.kaspersky.com/f756083b62ba45dcc6a4d2d2727780e4/?utm_source=SL&amp;utm_medium=SL&amp;utm_campaign=SL">f756083b62ba45dcc6a4d2d2727780e4</a>), compiled in 2012, deployed WinDealer on a target machine. This program contains a hardcoded URL that it uses to check for updates, as shown in the following screenshot:</p>

<p>We also identified online <a href="https://bbs.kafan.cn/thread-2157062-1-1.html">message board posts</a> where Chinese-speaking users reported the discovery of malware under the same name – PPTV(pplive)_forap_1084_9993.exe – on their machine. The posted information was complete enough for us to confirm that they had indeed received a sample of WinDealer.<br />
Leaving the mystery of the delivery method aside for now, let&#8217;s look at the capabilities of the malware itself.</p>
<h2 id="windealers-technical-description">WinDealer&#8217;s technical description</h2>
<p>WinDealer is a modular malware platform. It starts execution by locating an embedded DLL file placed in its resources by looking for a hardcoded pattern, and proceeds to decode it using a 10-byte XOR key.</p>
<p style="text-align: center"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2022/02/28142608/WinDealer_dealing_on_the_side_03.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-105952" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2022/02/28142608/WinDealer_dealing_on_the_side_03-1024x382.png" alt="Layout of the encrypted data" width="1024" height="382" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2022/02/28142608/WinDealer_dealing_on_the_side_03-1024x382.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2022/02/28142608/WinDealer_dealing_on_the_side_03-300x112.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2022/02/28142608/WinDealer_dealing_on_the_side_03-768x286.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2022/02/28142608/WinDealer_dealing_on_the_side_03-469x175.png 469w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2022/02/28142608/WinDealer_dealing_on_the_side_03-370x138.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2022/02/28142608/WinDealer_dealing_on_the_side_03-751x280.png 751w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2022/02/28142608/WinDealer_dealing_on_the_side_03-800x298.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2022/02/28142608/WinDealer_dealing_on_the_side_03.png 1038w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>Layout of the encrypted data</em></strong></p>
<p>WinDealer&#8217;s logic is spread over the initial EXE and its companion DLL: the former contains the setup of the program as well as network communications, while the orders sent by the C2 are implemented in the latter. The malware possesses the following capabilities:</p>
<ul>
<li>File and file system manipulation: reading, writing and deleting files, listing directories, obtaining disk information;</li>
<li>Information gathering: collecting hardware details, network configuration and/or keyboard layout, listing running processes, installed applications and configuration files of popular messaging applications (Skype, QQ, WeChat and Wangwang);</li>
<li>Download and upload of arbitrary files;</li>
<li>Arbitrary command execution;</li>
<li>System-wide search across text files and Microsoft Word documents;</li>
<li>Screenshot capture;</li>
<li>Network discovery via ping scan;</li>
<li>Backdoor maintenance: set up or remove persistence (via the registry&#8217;s RUN key), configuration updates.</li>
</ul>
<p>A variant we discovered (MD5 <a href="https://opentip.kaspersky.com/26064e65a7e6ce620b0ff7b4951cf340/?utm_source=SL&amp;utm_medium=SL&amp;utm_campaign=SL">26064e65a7e6ce620b0ff7b4951cf340</a>) also featured the ability to list available Wi-Fi networks. Overall, WinDealer is able to collect an impressive amount of information, even when compared to other malware families. And yet, the most extraordinary aspect of WinDealer lies elsewhere.</p>
<h2 id="the-impossible-infrastructure">The impossible infrastructure</h2>
<p>The latest WinDealer sample we discovered in 2020 doesn&#8217;t contain a hardcoded C2 server but instead relies on a complex IP generation algorithm to determine which machine to contact. The details of this algorithm are left as an exercise to the reader, but the end result is that the IP address is selected<strong> at random</strong> from one of these two ranges:</p>
<ul>
<li>113.62.0.0/15 (AS4134, CHINANET XIZANG PROVINCE NETWORK)</li>
<li>111.120.0.0/14 (AS4134, CHINANET GUIZHOU PROVINCE NETWORK)</li>
</ul>
<p>Once the IP address has been selected, communications take place either over UDP port 6999 or TCP port 55556. In an even weirder twist, a research partner shared with us an additional WinDealer sample (MD5 <a href="https://opentip.kaspersky.com/d9a6725b6a2b38f96974518ec9e361ab/?utm_source=SL&amp;utm_medium=SL&amp;utm_campaign=SL">d9a6725b6a2b38f96974518ec9e361ab</a>) that communicates with the hardcoded URL &#8220;http://www[.]microsoftcom/status/getsign.asp&#8221;. This domain is obviously invalid and cannot resolve to anything in normal circumstances – yet the malware expects a response in a predetermined format (&#8220;\x11\x22\x??\x33\x44&#8221;).</p>
<p>Packets exchanged with the C2 server contain a header (described in the next table) followed by AES-encrypted data. They leverage a homemade binary protocol containing magic numbers and flags, making it easy to recognize and filter packets on a large scale.</p>

<h2 id="the-man-on-the-side-attack">The man-on-the-side attack</h2>
<p>Putting all the pieces together, WinDealer&#8217;s infrastructure is nothing short of extraordinary:</p>
<ul>
<li>It appears to be distributed via plain HTTP requests that normally return legitimate executables.</li>
<li>It communicates with IP addresses selected randomly inside a specific AS.</li>
<li>It can interact with non-existent domain names.</li>
</ul>
<p>It is very hard to believe that an attacker would be able to control the 48,000 IP addresses of the aforementioned IP ranges, or even a significant portion of them. The only way to explain these seemingly impossible network behaviors is by assuming the existence of a man-on-the-side attacker who is able to intercept all network traffic and even modify it if needed.</p>
<p>Such capabilities are not unheard of: the <a href="https://blog.fox-it.com/2015/04/20/deep-dive-into-quantum-insert/">QUANTUM</a> program revealed in 2014 was the first known instance. The general idea is that when the attacker sees a request for a specific resource on the network, it tries to reply to the target faster than the legitimate server. If the attacker wins the &#8220;race&#8221;, the target machine will use the attacker-supplied data instead of the normal data. This is consistent with the scenario described earlier in this article, where the target receives an infected executable instead of the normal one. Automatic updaters are prime targets for such attacks as they perform frequent requests – it doesn&#8217;t matter if the attackers don&#8217;t win most races, as they can try again until they succeed, guaranteeing that they will infect their targets eventually. This class of attack is particularly devastating because there is nothing users can do to protect themselves, apart from routing traffic through another network. This can be done with the use of a VPN, but these may be illegal depending on the jurisdiction and would typically not be available to Chinese-speaking targets.</p>
<p>Confirming our assessment, we later discovered a downloader utility (MD5 <a href="https://opentip.kaspersky.com/4e07a477039b37790f7a8e976024eb66/?utm_source=SL&amp;utm_medium=SL&amp;utm_campaign=SL">4e07a477039b37790f7a8e976024eb66</a>) that uses the same unique user-agent as WinDealer samples we analyzed (&#8220;BBB&#8221;), tying it weakly to LuoYu.</p>
<p style="text-align: center"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2022/02/28142657/WinDealer_dealing_on_the_side_04.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-105953" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2022/02/28142657/WinDealer_dealing_on_the_side_04-1024x304.png" width="1024" height="304" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2022/02/28142657/WinDealer_dealing_on_the_side_04-1024x304.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2022/02/28142657/WinDealer_dealing_on_the_side_04-300x89.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2022/02/28142657/WinDealer_dealing_on_the_side_04-768x228.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2022/02/28142657/WinDealer_dealing_on_the_side_04-1536x456.png 1536w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2022/02/28142657/WinDealer_dealing_on_the_side_04-590x175.png 590w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2022/02/28142657/WinDealer_dealing_on_the_side_04-370x110.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2022/02/28142657/WinDealer_dealing_on_the_side_04-944x280.png 944w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2022/02/28142657/WinDealer_dealing_on_the_side_04-800x237.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2022/02/28142657/WinDealer_dealing_on_the_side_04.png 1568w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>A downloader utility and WinDealer of 2021 use the unique user-agent &#8220;BBB&#8221;</em></strong></p>
<p>The downloader periodically retrieves and runs an executable from hxxp://www.baidu[.]com/status/windowsupdatedmq.exe. This URL normally returns a 404 error and we consider it extremely unlikely that the attackers have control over this domain.</p>
<p>Based on all the evidence laid out above, we speculate that the attackers may have the following capabilities over AS4134:</p>
<ul>
<li>Intercepting all network traffic, which allows them to receive backdoor responses to random IP addresses without having to deploy actual C2 servers.</li>
<li>Injecting arbitrary TCP and UDP packets on the network, a capability through which they can send orders to WinDealer.</li>
<li>Full control over the DNS, meaning they can provide responses for non-existent domains.</li>
<li>Either QUANTUMINSERT capabilities or the ability to modify the contents of HTTP packets on the fly, thanks to which they can achieve remote, zero-click malware installation by abusing auto-update mechanisms. One noteworthy observation is that the attackers specifically target plain HTTP sessions, indicating that they may not have the ability to break or downgrade HTTPS.</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="windealers-targets">WinDealer&#8217;s targets</h2>
<p>Our analysis of WinDealer reveals that it specifically looks for popular applications in Asia, such as QQ, WeChat and WangWang. It also contains references to registry keys created by Sogou programs. This indicates to us that the LuoYu APT is predominantly focused on Chinese-speaking targets and organizations related to China. Our telemetry confirms that the vast majority of LuoYu targets are located in China, with occasional infections in other countries such as Germany, Austria, the United States, Czech Republic, Russia and India.</p>
<p>In recent months, LuoYu has started to widen its scope to companies and users in East Asia and their branches located in China.</p>

<h2 id="conclusion">Conclusion</h2>
<p>With this report, we recognize LuoYu as an extremely sophisticated threat actor able to leverage capabilities available only to the most mature attackers. We can only speculate as to how they were able to obtain such capabilities. They could have compromised routers on the route to (or inside) AS4134. Alternatively, they may use signals intelligence methods unknown to the general public. They may even have access (legitimate or fraudulent) to law enforcement tools set up at the ISP level and are abusing them to perform offensive operations. Overall, a threat actor is leveraging capabilities that could be compared (but are distinct) from the QUANTUMINSERT program in order to infect targets located in China.</p>
<p>Man-on-the-side attacks are devastating because they do not require any interaction with the target to lead to a successful infection: simply having a machine connected to the internet is enough. They can only be detected through careful network monitoring, which is outside of the realm of everyday users, or if an endpoint security program catches the payload when it is deployed on the attacked computer.</p>
<p>Whatever the case, the only way for potential targets to defend against such intrusions is to remain extremely vigilant and have robust security procedures involving regular antivirus scans, analysis of outbound network traffic and extensive logging to detect anomalies.</p>
