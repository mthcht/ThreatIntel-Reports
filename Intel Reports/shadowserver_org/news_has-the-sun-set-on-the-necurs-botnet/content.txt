<div class="content" data-shadowserver-name="ss:content" id="content-9ydEBME9zgY4"><p>&#xA0;</p>
<p><img fetchpriority="high" decoding="async" class="alignnone wp-image-2273 size-large" src="/wp-content/uploads/2020/03/Necurs-Title-Logo-1024x364.png" alt="" width="1024" height="364"></p>
<p>On <strong>March 10th 2020</strong>, while many people around the world were increasingly focused on the spreading COVID-19 pandemic, <strong>Microsoft&#x2019;s Digital Crime Unit (DCU)</strong> <a href="https://blogs.microsoft.com/on-the-issues/2020/03/10/necurs-botnet-cyber-crime-disrupt/">announced</a> a disruption action against a long-lived and very damaging virtual threat &#x2013; the <strong>Necurs</strong> botnet. Microsoft DCU described Necurs as &#x201C;<em>the world&#x2019;s largest online criminal network</em>&#x201D; responsible for &#x201C;<em>infecting over 9 million users globally since 2012</em>&#x201D;. The Necurs botnet has historically been used to deliver a torrent of other high profile cyber threats to the world, including the <a href="/news/gameover-zeus-cryptolocker/">GameOver Zeus</a> and <a href="/news/dridex-update-the-wheels-of-international-law-enforcement-keep-on-turning/">Dridex</a> banking trojans, <strong>Locky</strong> ransomware and, more recently, the banking trojan turned all purpose cybercrime-as-a-service, <strong>Trickbot</strong>. It has also been used to promote pump-and-dump stock scams, fake pharmaceutical spam emails and &#x201C;Russian dating&#x201D; scams.</p>
<p><strong>Shadowserver</strong> has previously collaborated with Microsoft DCU on successful botnet takedowns, such as the <a href="https://www.pcworld.com/article/190234/article.html">Waledec</a> spambot and the <a href="https://www.shadowserver.org/news/avalanche-year-two-this-time-with-andromeda/">Andromeda</a> malware dropper (part of <a href="https://www.shadowserver.org/news/avalanche-123/">Avalanche</a>). It has also assisted with the take down of other historic spambots by collaborating with private partners (<strong>Grum</strong>) and with Law Enforcement, such as against various versions of <a href="https://www.shadowserver.org/news/kelihos-e/">Kelihos</a> with the FBI, DoJ and other private sector partners. <a href="https://blogs.microsoft.com/on-the-issues/2020/03/10/necurs-botnet-cyber-crime-disrupt/">Microsoft DCU</a> and <a href="https://www.bitsight.com/blog/joint-effort-with-microsoft-to-takedown-massive-criminal-botnet-necurs">Bitsight</a> had conducted a multi-year investigation into Necurs, and Shadowserver was asked to support their disruption efforts once an action plan had been agreed.</p>
<p>We would usually provide in-depth details here about the technical functionality of the Necurs malware, reverse engineering information, the use of multiple layers of Domain Generation Algorithms (DGAs) &#x2013; including .bit for blockchain based name to IP address resolution, the tiered command and control (C2) infrastructure, the structure of the multiple sub-botnets or the disruption strategies employed, but we recommend you read <a href="https://www.bitsight.com/blog/joint-effort-with-microsoft-to-takedown-massive-criminal-botnet-necurs">Bitsight&#x2019;s already published series of excellent Necurs articles</a>. We will instead focus on some other interesting aspects of the Necurs disruption operation.</p>
<h2>Registry Actions</h2>
<p>Some botnet takedowns are purely criminal cases, performed by Law Enforcement Agencies using criminal laws &#x2013; although, not every country has developed such legislation. In some countries, particularly the USA, LEAs can use civil legal orders in the fight against cybercrime. This avenue is also open to private sector organizations; indeed, Microsoft has <a href="https://botnetlegalnotice.com">previously used civil court orders to tackle large botnets</a>. Where the name of a suspect behind the botnet is <strong>not</strong> known, but damages can be demonstrated, it is still possible for a plaintiff (in this case Microsoft) to make an &#x201C;ex parte&#x201D; (meaning in the interests of one side, when the other side is not present) &#x201C;John Doe&#x201D; complaint through a civil court. If successful, a judge can issue a Temporary Restraining Order (TRO) that grants injunctive relief to the plaintiff. This is the legal approach that was adopted by Microsoft in regards to Necurs. You can view the Eastern District of New York court documents <a href="https://noticeofpleadings.com/NECURS/">here</a> and read the wording of its TRO application <a href="https://noticeofpleadings.com/NECURS/files/Application for TRO/Proposed Order.pdf">here</a>.</p>
<div id="attachment_2242" style="width: 1034px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><a href="https://noticeofpleadings.com/NECURS/files/Application for TRO/Proposed Order.pdf"><img decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-2242" class="wp-image-2242 size-large" src="/wp-content/uploads/2020/03/Microsoft_Necurs_TRO-1024x627.png" alt="" width="1024" height="627"></a><p id="caption-attachment-2242" class="wp-caption-text">Microsoft Necurs Ex Parte Temporary Restraining Order (TRO)</p></div>
<p>Shadowserver were on site with Bitsight and Microsoft at its Digital Crime Center in Redmond, WA, USA to support the Necurs disruption effort. Our contribution to the operation was primarily in two areas:</p>
<ol>
<li>Coordinating with our extensive, international network of country-code, top-level, domain (ccTLD) Registry operators who trust us to report malware to them that uses millions of unregistered DGA domains, as in previous large botnet takedown operations such as <a href="https://www.shadowserver.org/news/avalanche-123/">Avalanche</a>.</li>
<li>Using Shadowserver&#x2019;s proven <a href="https://www.shadowserver.org/what-we-do/network-reporting/">victim remediation network</a>, of <a href="https://www.shadowserver.org/news/celebrating-milestones-european-cert-csirt-report-coverage/">107 National CERTs/CSIRTs in 136 countries</a> and over 4,600 vetted network owners (covering 90% of the Internet by IP space/ASN/CIDR), to ensure that the collected sinkhole data was quickly distributed to as many <a href="https://www.shadowserver.org/who-we-serve/">constituents</a> as possible, in order to maximize world-wide victim remediation. Data is also available through Microsoft CTIP and Bitsight&#x2019;s commercial services.</li>
</ol>
<p>Our special purpose <strong>The Registrar of Last Resort Foundation</strong> (RoLR), another Dutch Stichting non-profit, public-benefit organization, created specifically by Shadowserver to quarantine toxic domain names, provided crucial support to the Necurs disruption activities. Voluntary action was successfully secured across <strong>22 ccTLD registries</strong>, complementing Microsoft&#x2019;s US civil court orders, which had been executed on 5 US-based registries.</p>
<p>The Necurs botnet makes use of hardcoded domains and multiple layers of DGAs to attempt to guarantee reliable command and control (C2) capabilities. Since the Necurs DGAs and seed values had been reverse engineered by security researchers and reimplemented in code, 25 months of future domains could be forward calculated (a total of <strong>6.1 million</strong> potential botnet C2 domains across <strong>42 TLDs</strong>).</p>
<p>Under the wording of Microsoft&#x2019;s <a href="https://noticeofpleadings.com/NECURS/files/Application for TRO/Proposed Order.pdf">civil court ordered TRO</a>, all existing Necurs botnet C2 domain names (except those determined to belong to legitimate security researchers) within US-operated TLDs should be seized and sinkholed by the Registry. Any unregistered DGA domain names would be blocked so that they could not be registered in the future &#x2013; except by &#x201C;<em>Microsoft or its security industry partners Stichting The Registrar of Last Resort Foundation and The Shadowserver Foundation for the purposes of analyzing the botnet</em>&#x201D;.</p>
<div id="attachment_2243" style="width: 1034px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><a href="https://noticeofpleadings.com/NECURS/files/Application for TRO/Proposed Order.pdf"><img decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-2243" class="wp-image-2243 size-large" src="/wp-content/uploads/2020/03/Microsoft_Necurs_TRO_wording-1024x337.png" alt="" width="1024" height="337"></a><p id="caption-attachment-2243" class="wp-caption-text">Microsoft Necurs Ex Parte Temporary Restraining Order (TRO) Wording</p></div>
<p>Shortly before the operation took place, a subset of the pre-calculated domains for all of the <strong>Necurs 5,7,9,11,13 and 15 sub-botnets</strong> was registered and pointed to Microsoft&#x2019;s and Bitsight&#x2019;s sinkhole servers, to ensure that all infected victims would attempt to resolve at least one sinkholed botnet C2 domain and communicate with at least one sinkhole server every day; hopefully, without being able to communicate with the criminals operating the botnet. This approach allows the victim population to be identified and hopefully remediated, while minimizing the number of potential DGA C2 domains that actually have to be registered (around a thousand C2 domains per year, rather than the potentially millions of C2 domains).</p>
<p><strong>RoLR</strong> was used to register Necurs C2 domains for sinkholing purposes and to manage the ongoing maintenance and renewal of the domains. The actions of breaking the DNS resolution of criminal controlled C2 domains, and instead delivering infected victim computers to the Microsoft and Bitsight sinkhole servers, attempts to protect victims from further criminal abuse, and allows National CERTs/CSIRTs and responsible network owners to be notified to remediate those infections. The Necurs disruption sinkhole data is available through Microsoft&#x2019;s CTIP service, Bitsight&#x2019;s commercial service and Shadowserver&#x2019;s <a href="https://www.shadowserver.org/what-we-do/network-reporting/">free daily network reports</a>. If you or your organization do not already subscribe to this <strong>free public benefit service</strong>, then <a href="https://www.shadowserver.org/what-we-do/network-reporting/get-reports/">please do sign up</a>.</p>

<p><strong>gTLDs</strong> / <strong>nTLDs</strong>: .biz, .com, .net, .org, .pro, .xxx</p>
<p><strong>Other</strong>: .bit</p>

<h2>Sinkhole Data Analysis</h2>
<p>Although Necurs may have been eclipsed by <strong>Emotet</strong> as the main dropper of current top threats such as <strong>Trickbot</strong> and <strong>Dridex,</strong> now that we have <a href="https://www.shadowserver.org/what-we-do/network-reporting/">reported out</a> the initial few days of sinkhole data, we can see that around <strong>155,000 Necurs infected unique IP addresses per day</strong> are being observed by the sinkholes. In operations where we have accurate bot IDs, we typically see about a one-to-one ratio of bots to IP addresses when taking into account DHCP lease churn and multiple bots behind a single NAT gateway. That number aligns well with Bitsight&#x2019;s own published historical analysis, which shows a gradual decline in the number of detected unique Necurs victim IP address per day since the peak botnet populations that were observed in 2016 and 2017.</p>

<h2>Analaysis</h2>
<p>When blogging about specific botnet takedowns, we try to provide analysis that is unique to our own perspective on that operation. Ideally, we also present that analysis in the context of the wider and historical threat ecosystem, using Shadowserver&#x2019;s own unique and extensive datasets.</p>
<p>For Necurs, we thought that it would be interesting to compare the IP-geolocated country level victim distributions of victims during the peak 24 hours after sinkholing data collection began.</p>
<p>Selected spam botnet subjects are:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Grum</strong> (2012/2013)</li>
<li><strong>Pushdo/Cutwail</strong> (2013/2014), multiple datasets</li>
<li><strong>Kelihos.C</strong> spambot (2014/2015)</li>
<li><strong>Kelihos.E</strong> spambot (2017-2018)</li>
<li><strong>Necurs</strong> spambot (2020)</li>
<li><strong>Andromeda</strong> malware dropper, for comparison (2017 to 2020)</li>
</ol>

<p>From our range of historical and current sinkhole datasets, if we take the peak 24 hours of each botnet&#x2019;s observed victim population, there are a number of countries where the largest victim distributions are typically found. This pattern has repeated in many of the spamming botnet networks built up and then disrupted or taken down over the past decade. Similar distributions occur in the still very large Andromeda malware dropper victim pools, which have experienced very slow remediation rates over the past three years, despite significant attention being drawn to this at industry conferences, in blog posts, through events in victim remediation feeds such as Shadowserver&#x2019;s <a href="https://www.shadowserver.org/what-we-do/network-reporting/">free daily network reports</a>, etc.</p>

<p>The purpose of this comparison analysis is not to disparage any particular country, or to blame anyone for this situation (other than the cyber criminals who have been victimizing these populations for years). We have deliberately chosen to focus at a country level, rather than the individual ASN or IP level (although we do have that data available). The Shadowserver Foundation is a <strong>public benefit</strong> non-profit organization which gives data away for <strong>free</strong> each day to any vetted network owner and National CERT/CSIRT, and we have strived to help remediate these victim populations for many years now. We merely want to highlight the observed trends across a decade of spam and malware dropping botnets, to better assist defenders in protecting <strong>all</strong> networks, in every country.</p>
<p>From a global Internet security hygiene perspective, you might ask the question &#x201C;<strong>After the next big takedown or disruption of the next big spamming botnet (perhaps Emotet?) &#x2013; where would the victim population likely be located?</strong>&#x201D; Every botnet is obviously different, as are the owner&#x2019;s goals and the circumstances on the Internet at that time. But 15 years of historic sinkhole data would suggest that it would likely be many of the same areas again.</p>
<p>These repeated botnet population patterns could be due to limited infosec budgets in certain countries, ignorance of the available (free) remediation data sources (such as Shadowserver), a lack of public awareness and good security practice guidance, larger populations of older, more vulnerable operating systems and computer hardware, perhaps a higher chance of software licensing compliance problems, or it could just be simple economic realities. Given that spamming botnets and malware droppers have proven to be highly effective, are often long lived, and are one of the primary attack vectors against the entire online world, it seems likely that countries with fewer infections will continue to be bombarded with spam and malware from similar locations in the future.</p>
<p>This is a obviously a complex topic, requiring more than a single blog post to do the subject justice. At a time when the physical world is dealing with the current COVID-19 pandemic and isolation is being considered, at both national and personal level, it is obviously difficult for many people to worry too much about malware and Internet-borne viruses. That is understandable, and protecting human lives needs to come first. However, we should all also think about some of the difficult challenges in maintaining good global internet hygiene, and how best the various members of the National CERT/CSIRT, network owner, Law Enforcement Agency, private sector business and ordinary members of the public can work together to help minimize the risk of another major spambot outbreak soon.</p>
<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>It has been good to work again with Microsoft and Bitsight on another major cybercrime disruption operation. We appreciate being offered the opportunity to support the final phase of their Necurs disruption effort, and to assist in what will hopefully be a rapid, effective, global remediation effort.</p>
<p>Thanks to the way Microsoft have managed this investigation and the civil court orders that they obtained, along with the use of RoLR for non-US TLDs, hundreds of thousands of unique daily victim IP addresses observed by the Necurs sinkholes will not only be made available to Microsoft and Bitsight&#x2019;s customers, but will also be available through Shadowserver&#x2019;s <a href="https://www.shadowserver.org/what-we-do/network-reporting/">free daily network reports</a> (in the Shadowserver drone feed, tagged as <strong>type=necurs</strong>). Some ISPs will also be blocking outbound connections to the known C2 infrastructure to further protect their end users.</p>
<p>This is a truly laudable effort from two private sector companies collectively trying to clean up this persistent, highly effective and impactful botnet. Regardless of whether the Necurs botnet will eventually be rebuilt, notifying &#x2013; and hopefully remediating &#x2013; so many victims of cybercrime is always a positive result for the good guys, and hopefully a bad day for the criminals.</p>
<p>If you receive an infection notification from your ISP, network owner or National CERT/CSIRT, please use anti-virus software to disinfect your computer and reduce the risk to yourself and others who might be impacted as a consequence of further malware attacks launched from your infected system. At this time in particular, we should all be aware that not only do we need to look after our own health, but we also have to think about our social responsibility towards others.</p>
