      <h1 style="position:absolute;width:1px;height:1px;padding:0;margin:-1px;overflow:hidden;clip:rect(0,0,0,0);white-space:nowrap;border-width:0">FIN7 Tools Resurface in the Field – Splinter or Copycat?</h1>
      <div>
        <p>T <em>his blog is part 1 and covers FIN7, a highly-skilled group, and the two tools. To find a walkthrough of Remcos executed via Splunk's Attack Range Local, check out part 2, <strong><a href="/en_us/blog/security/splunk-fin7-tool-detections-remcos.html">Detecting Remcos Tool Used by FIN7 with Splunk.</a></strong></em></p>
        <p>FIN7 is a well-organized criminal group composed of highly-skilled individuals that target financial institutions, hospitality, restaurant, and gambling industries. Until recently, it was known that high-level individuals of this criminal enterprise were arrested — specifically <a href="https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/three-members-notorious-international-cybercrime-group-fin7-custody-role-attacking-over-100">3 of them</a> — and extradited to the United States.</p>
        <p>This criminal group performed highly technical malicious campaigns which included effective compromise, exfiltration and fraud using stolen payment cards. Another heist related to the history of this group and actors includes withdrawing money from ATMs, bypassing all controls as seen in the video linked below.</p>

Without delving deeper into the assumptions of being two different groups, however, we can take a look at their tools which is what we can measure via payload samples and research from the community.</p>

        <p>Due to the notoriety, extent and sophistication of this group and the tools they use, we are going to particularly focus on FIN7 tools, techniques and procedures. Recently, a specific tool which is a signature of this group known as the JSS loader has apparently resurfaced, indicated by reports from some <a href="https://www.proofpoint.com/us/blog/threat-insight/jssloader-recoded-and-reloaded">security research sites</a> and mentioned in <a href="https://threatpost.com/fin7-windows-11-release/169206/">some security publications</a>.</p>
        <p>Based on previous arrests of what was thought to be some of the main characters of this organization, we need to ask ourselves: is this a splinter from a former group trying to get business back online, or is this a copycat using the former tools, rewriting them and even attempting to reuse former infrastructure from past campaigns? Or basically, the group was indeed not affected by arrests and decided to lay low and then reappear as reported recently by <a href="https://geminiadvisory.io/fin7-ransomware-bastion-secure/?__cf_chl_jschl_tk__=wfMw07NrLQx2J4GEcAgOHxr4m48a0HQgImZ5fUKCZcE-1636316392-0-gaNycGzNCOU">Recorded Future</a>.</p>
        <p>We do not have enough information to respond to the above questions, however, we can prepare ourselves to defend against this group by looking at their tools.</p>
        <p>In this two-part blog we are going to address two tools used by this group — JSS Loader and Remcos.</p>
        <h2 id="fin7-javascript">FIN7 Javascript</h2>
        <p>FIN7 is well known to use a spear-phishing campaign to compromise a machine by downloading or executing an obfuscated javascript as the first stage. We analyze old and the latest script found in the wild to summarize all possible behavior it may execute in the targeted machine.</p>
        <h3 id="javascript-execution-using-xsl-file">Javascript Execution Using .XSL File</h3>
        <p>One interesting behavior we saw in one of these variants is how it executes the malicious javascript.         <pre><code>| tstats `security_content_summariesonly` count min(_time) as firstTime max(_time) as lastTime from datamodel=Endpoint.Processes 
  where  (Processes.process_name = "ipconfig.exe" OR Processes.process_name = "systeminfo.exe") 
  AND NOT (Processes.parent_process_name = "cmd.exe" OR Processes.parent_process_name = "powershell*" OR Processes.parent_process_name = "explorer.exe") 
  by Processes.parent_process_name Processes.parent_process Processes.process_name Processes.process_id Processes.process Processes.dest Processes.user 
  | `drop_dm_object_name(Processes)` 
  | `security_content_ctime(firstTime)`
  | `security_content_ctime(lastTime)` 
</code></pre>
