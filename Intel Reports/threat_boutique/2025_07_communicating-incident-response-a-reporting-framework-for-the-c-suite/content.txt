                            <h1 class="title-post" itemprop="name">Communicating Incident Response: A Reporting Framework for the C-Suite</h1>
                    <div class="date">
            <span itemprop="datePublished"><a href="https://threat.boutique/archive/2025-07" title="Show all posts made on this month">22 July 2025</a></span>
            - Posted in            <span itemprop="articleSection"><a href="https://threat.boutique/category/incident-response">Incident Response</a></span> by            <span itemprop="author"><a href="https://threat.boutique/author/b3vSclAuunBMsdt">Jok3r</a></span> -
            <span><a href="https://threat.boutique/2025/07/communicating-incident-response-a-reporting-framework-for-the-c-suite" rel="permalink">Permalink</a></span>
        </div>
                    <div class="featured-image">
                <a href="https://threat.boutique/2025/07/communicating-incident-response-a-reporting-framework-for-the-c-suite"><img src="https://threat.boutique/content/images/20250720130024-cyberattack_Anucha_Cheechang_shutterstock.webp" alt="Communicating Incident Response: A Reporting Framework for the C-Suite"/></a>
            </div>
                                        <div class="post-body post-1753175001" itemprop="articleBody">
            <p>Background:
Incident response is divided into two parts: hands-on activities and management activities. We have discussed hands-on activities extensively. However, if we want to present the activity timeline—including all its components—to the C-suite team, it should be structured in a way that enables effective decision-making and facilitates lessons learned.</p>

<p>Chapter 1 Incident metadata:
It should contain information about the incident, including the incident name, by whom it created, incident severity, and incident commander name,and the incident document version .</p>

<p>Chapter 2 Incident legal information:
It should contain information from the legal team describing the process of document ownership changes and related procedures.</p>

<p>Chapter 3 Executive summary:
Include a brief incident description and a high-level overview of related details.</p>

<p>Chapter 3 Incident timeline:
Describe the actions taken by the Incident Response (IR) team, the decision-making team, and the threat actor during the incident.</p>

<p>Chapter 4 Impacted Systems:
Describe the list of impacted and non impacted assets</p>

<p>Chapter 5 Stakeholders:
Describes the stakeholders lists and systems owners</p>

<p>Chapter 6 Legal and Compliance:
Should be filled by  legal team related to legal consequences</p>

<p>Chapter 7 Action Items and Tasks:
Describes the the tasks which need to be done during the incident</p>

<p>Chapter 8 Evidences and Findings:
Describe the finding related to incident</p>

<p>Chapter 9 Root cause:
Describes root of cause of the incident</p>

<p>Chapter 10 Lesson learn:
Describe lesson learn</p>

<p>Chapter 11 Conclusion:
Describe conclusion related to noted incident .</p>

<p>Conclusion:
The template should always be delivered to the C-suite as a post-mortem report to help the organization avoid legal and other issues in daily operations.</p>
