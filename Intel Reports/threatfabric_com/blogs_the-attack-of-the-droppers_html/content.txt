						<span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><h2 id="another-130000-installations-of-malicious-droppers-from-official-store">Another 130.000+ installations of malicious droppers from official store</h2>
<p>A year ago, we<span>&nbsp;</span><a href="https://www.threatfabric.com/blogs/deceive-the-heavens-to-cross-the-sea.html">highlighted a trend</a><span>&nbsp;</span>of malicious droppers in Google Play store used to distribute banking Trojans. We also predicted further efforts of cybercriminals to reduce the malicious footprint of their malware in order to stay undetected. Distribution through droppers on official stores remains one of the most efficient ways for threat actors to reach a wide and unsuspecting audience. Although other distribution methods are also used depending on cybercriminals targets, resources, and motivation, droppers remain one of the best option on price-efforts-quality ratio, competing with SMiShing.</p>
<!--more--><p><img loading="lazy" src="https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/distribution.png?width=1920&amp;height=1080&amp;name=distribution.png" alt="distribution" width="1920" height="1080" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1920px;" srcset="https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/distribution.png?width=960&amp;height=540&amp;name=distribution.png 960w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/distribution.png?width=1920&amp;height=1080&amp;name=distribution.png 1920w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/distribution.png?width=2880&amp;height=1620&amp;name=distribution.png 2880w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/distribution.png?width=3840&amp;height=2160&amp;name=distribution.png 3840w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/distribution.png?width=4800&amp;height=2700&amp;name=distribution.png 4800w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/distribution.png?width=5760&amp;height=3240&amp;name=distribution.png 5760w" sizes="(max-width: 1920px) 100vw, 1920px"></p>
<p><span>The history of competition between malware authors and seсurity mechanisms knows several twists when new measures are introduced. Droppers on Google Play went from using AccessibilityService to auto-allow installation from unknown sources to using legitimate sources to control them and store malicious payloads. Following the updates to the “Developer Program Policy” and system updates, actors immediately introduce new ways to sneak to the official store, overcoming limitations or adjusting droppers to follow the guidelines and not arouse suspicion. A brief story of that battle is presented on the graph below.</span></p>
<p><span><img loading="lazy" src="https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/evolution-1.png?width=1920&amp;height=1080&amp;name=evolution-1.png" alt="evolution-1" width="1920" height="1080" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1920px;" srcset="https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/evolution-1.png?width=960&amp;height=540&amp;name=evolution-1.png 960w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/evolution-1.png?width=1920&amp;height=1080&amp;name=evolution-1.png 1920w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/evolution-1.png?width=2880&amp;height=1620&amp;name=evolution-1.png 2880w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/evolution-1.png?width=3840&amp;height=2160&amp;name=evolution-1.png 3840w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/evolution-1.png?width=4800&amp;height=2700&amp;name=evolution-1.png 4800w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/evolution-1.png?width=5760&amp;height=3240&amp;name=evolution-1.png 5760w" sizes="(max-width: 1920px) 100vw, 1920px"></span></p>
<p>In this blog we uncover additional tactics cybercriminals use in new Google Play droppers discovered by ThreatFabric analysts. These droppers have cumulative number of<span>&nbsp;</span><strong>130k+</strong><span>&nbsp;</span>installations distributing Sharkbot and Vultur banking Trojans.</p>
<h2 id="sharkbot-the-less-you-see-the-more-they-get">Sharkbot: the less you see, the more they get</h2>
<p>In the beginning of October 2022 ThreatFabric analysts spotted a new campaign of banking Trojan Sharkbot, targeting Italian banking users. This campaign involved Sharkbot<span>&nbsp;</span><strong>version 2.29 – 2.32</strong>. Following the research path, our analysts were able to identify the dropper app located on Google Play with 10k+ installations and disguised as an app to calculate tax code in Italy (“Codice Fiscale”) targeting Italian users.</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" src="https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/sharkbot_dropper.png?width=1920&amp;height=1080&amp;name=sharkbot_dropper.png" alt="sharkbot_dropper" width="1920" height="1080" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1920px;" srcset="https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/sharkbot_dropper.png?width=960&amp;height=540&amp;name=sharkbot_dropper.png 960w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/sharkbot_dropper.png?width=1920&amp;height=1080&amp;name=sharkbot_dropper.png 1920w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/sharkbot_dropper.png?width=2880&amp;height=1620&amp;name=sharkbot_dropper.png 2880w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/sharkbot_dropper.png?width=3840&amp;height=2160&amp;name=sharkbot_dropper.png 3840w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/sharkbot_dropper.png?width=4800&amp;height=2700&amp;name=sharkbot_dropper.png 4800w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/sharkbot_dropper.png?width=5760&amp;height=3240&amp;name=sharkbot_dropper.png 5760w" sizes="(max-width: 1920px) 100vw, 1920px"></p>
<p>This is not the first time that a Sharkbot dropper sneaks into the official Google store, but this time authors did their best to hide the malicious intents of the dropper. Previous versions of Sharkbot droppers as well as other droppers (including those we highlight below in this blog) include ability to download, install and launch the malicious payload. Obviously, such behaviour is quite suspicious and already made Google to introduce changes to the<span>&nbsp;</span><a href="https://support.google.com/googleplay/android-developer/answer/12766072?hl=en&amp;ref_topic=9877065">Developer Program Policy</a><span>&nbsp;</span>where usage of<span>&nbsp;</span><strong>REQUEST_INSTALL_PACKAGES</strong><span>&nbsp;</span>permission was limited to apps that have it as core functionality.</p>
<p>However, in this new iteration, Sharkbot dropper authors tried their best to not include suspicious permissions at all, thus maintaining an extremely low profile. The new dropper has only 3 permissions that are quite common.</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" src="https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/permissions.png?width=1920&amp;height=1080&amp;name=permissions.png" alt="permissions" width="1920" height="1080" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1920px;" srcset="https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/permissions.png?width=960&amp;height=540&amp;name=permissions.png 960w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/permissions.png?width=1920&amp;height=1080&amp;name=permissions.png 1920w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/permissions.png?width=2880&amp;height=1620&amp;name=permissions.png 2880w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/permissions.png?width=3840&amp;height=2160&amp;name=permissions.png 3840w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/permissions.png?width=4800&amp;height=2700&amp;name=permissions.png 4800w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/permissions.png?width=5760&amp;height=3240&amp;name=permissions.png 5760w" sizes="(max-width: 1920px) 100vw, 1920px"></p>
<p><span>To ensure that the dropper is launched on a real targeted device, the app obtains the SIM coutry and compares it to “</span><strong>it</strong><span>” (Italy): if not matched, no malicious activity will be performed. Besides, additional checks are made on the C2 side to ensure that the dropper is running on the targeted device: if C2 is reached from a non-Italian IP address, the C2 will respond with a default “exit” message. Otherwise, it will receive a configuration data with the URL containing the payload.</span></p>
<p><span><img loading="lazy" src="https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/requests.png?width=1920&amp;height=1080&amp;name=requests.png" alt="requests" width="1920" height="1080" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1920px;" srcset="https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/requests.png?width=960&amp;height=540&amp;name=requests.png 960w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/requests.png?width=1920&amp;height=1080&amp;name=requests.png 1920w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/requests.png?width=2880&amp;height=1620&amp;name=requests.png 2880w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/requests.png?width=3840&amp;height=2160&amp;name=requests.png 3840w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/requests.png?width=4800&amp;height=2700&amp;name=requests.png 4800w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/requests.png?width=5760&amp;height=3240&amp;name=requests.png 5760w" sizes="(max-width: 1920px) 100vw, 1920px"></span></p>
<p><span>Here the interesting part starts: in order to avoid using REQUEST_INSTALL_PACKAGES permission, the dropper opens a fake Google Play store page impersonating Codice Fiscale app page. It contains fake information about the number of installations and reviews, and urges the victim to perform an update. Shortly after the page is opened, the automatic download starts. Thus, the dropper outsources the download and installation procedure to the browser, avoiding suspicious permissions.</span></p>
<p><span><img loading="lazy" src="https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/fake_update.png?width=1920&amp;height=1080&amp;name=fake_update.png" alt="fake_update" width="1920" height="1080" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1920px;" srcset="https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/fake_update.png?width=960&amp;height=540&amp;name=fake_update.png 960w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/fake_update.png?width=1920&amp;height=1080&amp;name=fake_update.png 1920w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/fake_update.png?width=2880&amp;height=1620&amp;name=fake_update.png 2880w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/fake_update.png?width=3840&amp;height=2160&amp;name=fake_update.png 3840w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/fake_update.png?width=4800&amp;height=2700&amp;name=fake_update.png 4800w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/fake_update.png?width=5760&amp;height=3240&amp;name=fake_update.png 5760w" sizes="(max-width: 1920px) 100vw, 1920px"></span></p>
<p>Obviously, such approach requires more actions from the victim, as the browser will show several messages about the downloaded file. However, since victims are sure about the origin of the application, they will highly likely install and run the downloaded Sharkbot payload.</p>
<p>During our investigation, we also found another Sharkbot dropper available on Google Play. It had zero installations at the time of discovery and was quickly removed from the website. The interesting detail about this dropper was that it had the REQUEST_INSTALL_PACKAGES permission in place and operated more in line with usual dropper’s behaviour. Actors were also following the updated policy of Google, as this dropper was masqueraded as file manager, a category that is allowed to have this permission due to it being a core functionality.</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" src="https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/sharkbot_dropper_2.png?width=1920&amp;height=1080&amp;name=sharkbot_dropper_2.png" alt="sharkbot_dropper_2" width="1920" height="1080" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1920px;" srcset="https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/sharkbot_dropper_2.png?width=960&amp;height=540&amp;name=sharkbot_dropper_2.png 960w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/sharkbot_dropper_2.png?width=1920&amp;height=1080&amp;name=sharkbot_dropper_2.png 1920w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/sharkbot_dropper_2.png?width=2880&amp;height=1620&amp;name=sharkbot_dropper_2.png 2880w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/sharkbot_dropper_2.png?width=3840&amp;height=2160&amp;name=sharkbot_dropper_2.png 3840w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/sharkbot_dropper_2.png?width=4800&amp;height=2700&amp;name=sharkbot_dropper_2.png 4800w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/sharkbot_dropper_2.png?width=5760&amp;height=3240&amp;name=sharkbot_dropper_2.png 5760w" sizes="(max-width: 1920px) 100vw, 1920px"></p>
<p>Similarities in code, C2 communication, and encryption used lead us to the same author behind the older versions of Sharkbot dropper, trying to reduce footprints and stay undetected.</p>
<h3 id="targets">Targets</h3>
<p>The new “Codice Fiscale” dropper discovered by ThreatFabric is configured to distribute Sharkbot payload to Italian users only, while the other “File Manager” dropper has Italy and UK in its configuration. At the same time, the payload delivered still has banks from Italy, UK, Germany, Spain, Poland, Austria, US, and Australia in its target list. Please, find the full target list in<span>&nbsp;</span><a href="https://www.threatfabric.com/blogs/the-attack-of-the-droppers.html#sharkbot-targets">Appendix</a>.</p>
<h2 id="vultur-brunhilda-is-back">Vultur: Brunhilda is back</h2>
<p>Another malware family that has been very active in the last year has been Vultur.<span>&nbsp;</span><a href="https://www.threatfabric.com/blogs/vultur-v-for-vnc.html">First discovered by ThreatFabric in July 2021</a>, Vultur is an Android banking trojan which specializes in stealing PII from infected devices using its screen-streaming capabilities. It is also able to create a remote session on the device using VNC technology to perform actions on the victim’s device, effectively leading to On-Device Fraud (ODF).</p>
<p>Upon discovery, ThreatFabric first reported the strong connection between this malware family, and the<span>&nbsp;</span><strong>“Brunhilda Project”</strong><span>&nbsp;</span>crew. This Threat Actor was known for its central role in the distribution sector of the Android Banking malware landscape thanks to its dropper applications, which managed often to pass Google security checks and be approved on the Google Play Store. Initially, the Brunhilda droppers were deploying a variety of Android malware applications, like for example samples of the malware family Alien. However, after the first discovery of Vultur, every dropper found on the Google Play Store only installed samples belonging to the Vultur malware family.</p>
<p>Recently, ThreatFabric discovered 3 new Droppers on the Google Play store, ranging from 1.000 to 100.000 installations reported by Google. As previous campaigns observed throughout 2022, these droppers pose as applications like security authenticators, or file recovery tools.</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" src="https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/droppers.png?width=1920&amp;height=1080&amp;name=droppers.png" alt="droppers" width="1920" height="1080" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1920px;" srcset="https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/droppers.png?width=960&amp;height=540&amp;name=droppers.png 960w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/droppers.png?width=1920&amp;height=1080&amp;name=droppers.png 1920w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/droppers.png?width=2880&amp;height=1620&amp;name=droppers.png 2880w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/droppers.png?width=3840&amp;height=2160&amp;name=droppers.png 3840w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/droppers.png?width=4800&amp;height=2700&amp;name=droppers.png 4800w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/droppers.png?width=5760&amp;height=3240&amp;name=droppers.png 5760w" sizes="(max-width: 1920px) 100vw, 1920px"></p>
<h3 id="the-dropper">The Dropper</h3>
<p>The dropper applications are consistent with the droppers that we reported in our first blog about Vultur, and the modus operandi has not changed much with respect to older variants. As usual, the dropper is made of a trojanized application, which provides the advertised functionality, as well as the hidden dropper functionality. As previously reported, the dropper initially sends a registration message to its C2 server. As a response, the server sends back an appToken, which is then used in the following requests to identify the device. At this point, the dropper prompts the victim with a screen asking to download an<span>&nbsp;</span><strong>update</strong><span>&nbsp;</span>for the current application. If the user accepts the displayed request, the dropper proceeds to install Vultur.</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" src="https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/installation.jpg?width=1920&amp;height=1080&amp;name=installation.jpg" alt="installation" width="1920" height="1080" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1920px;" srcset="https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/installation.jpg?width=960&amp;height=540&amp;name=installation.jpg 960w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/installation.jpg?width=1920&amp;height=1080&amp;name=installation.jpg 1920w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/installation.jpg?width=2880&amp;height=1620&amp;name=installation.jpg 2880w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/installation.jpg?width=3840&amp;height=2160&amp;name=installation.jpg 3840w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/installation.jpg?width=4800&amp;height=2700&amp;name=installation.jpg 4800w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/installation.jpg?width=5760&amp;height=3240&amp;name=installation.jpg 5760w" sizes="(max-width: 1920px) 100vw, 1920px"></p>
<p>In terms of execution of the installation, there are no real updates from previous versions of Brunhilda. However, the dropper currently implements a few obfuscation techniques which were not present in initial versions of the dropper. Firstly, in these new version, the installation logic is not contained in the main DEX file, but in a additional dex file which is loaded dynamically. This additional step can complicate the life of researchers, making it harder to identify the code responsible for malicious activity.</p>
<p>The latest version of Brunhilda also implemented a new layer of obfuscation, which encrypts strings by using AES with a varying key, which is included in the byte array that is given as input to the decryption method, as explained in the picture below:</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" src="https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/strings.png?width=1920&amp;height=1080&amp;name=strings.png" alt="strings" width="1920" height="1080" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1920px;" srcset="https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/strings.png?width=960&amp;height=540&amp;name=strings.png 960w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/strings.png?width=1920&amp;height=1080&amp;name=strings.png 1920w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/strings.png?width=2880&amp;height=1620&amp;name=strings.png 2880w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/strings.png?width=3840&amp;height=2160&amp;name=strings.png 3840w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/strings.png?width=4800&amp;height=2700&amp;name=strings.png 4800w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/strings.png?width=5760&amp;height=3240&amp;name=strings.png 5760w" sizes="(max-width: 1920px) 100vw, 1920px"></p>
<p>Once the payload is installed, the Brunhilda dropper launches the malware, and then continues existing on the device, acting as the application it is posing as.</p>
<h3 id="vultur">Vultur</h3>
<p>These Brunhilda droppers all deploy samples belonging to a<span>&nbsp;</span><strong>novel variant</strong><span>&nbsp;</span>of Vultur Android Banking malware family.</p>
<p>This new variant maintains the Modus Operandi that characterized the original samples from 2021: once installed, the malware initiates a connection with its C2, and after registering it obtains its configuration containing its targets. Vultur features two separate target lists: one for screen-streaming targets and one for keylogging targets. Similarly to its older variant, the keylogging targets are social and messaging applications, while the screen-streaming targets are applications for online banking and cryptocurrency exchange. However, in this new variant, this second set of targets is also the list of applications for which extensive accessibility logging is performed.</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" src="https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/capabilities%20(1).png?width=1920&amp;height=1080&amp;name=capabilities%20(1).png" alt="capabilities (1)" width="1920" height="1080" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1920px;" srcset="https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/capabilities%20(1).png?width=960&amp;height=540&amp;name=capabilities%20(1).png 960w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/capabilities%20(1).png?width=1920&amp;height=1080&amp;name=capabilities%20(1).png 1920w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/capabilities%20(1).png?width=2880&amp;height=1620&amp;name=capabilities%20(1).png 2880w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/capabilities%20(1).png?width=3840&amp;height=2160&amp;name=capabilities%20(1).png 3840w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/capabilities%20(1).png?width=4800&amp;height=2700&amp;name=capabilities%20(1).png 4800w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/capabilities%20(1).png?width=5760&amp;height=3240&amp;name=capabilities%20(1).png 5760w" sizes="(max-width: 1920px) 100vw, 1920px"></p>
<p><span>By accessibility logging we mean the extensive logging of all UI elements and all the events associated with them (like for example clicks, gestures, etc). This is not a novel technique, but it is the first time we see it implemented in Vultur. This might be a solution to the issue created by a security flag often used in these banking applications: Android offers a way to tag the content of the window as secure, by using the&nbsp;</span><strong>“FLAG_SECURE”</strong><span>, which prevents it “from appearing in screenshots or from being viewed on non-secure displays”. ThreatFabric tested this and is able to confirm that windows with this flag enabled only show a&nbsp;</span><strong>black screen</strong><span>&nbsp;during screen-streaming. However, if the&nbsp;</span><strong>keyboard</strong><span>&nbsp;is opened during interaction with the secured app, it&nbsp;</span><strong>will be visible</strong><span>&nbsp;on the recording as well as all the keys pressed by victim leading to potential theft of input data. In this case, it is possible to obtain enough information to steal credentials even with a black screen, when all the UI events are logged and sent to the C2.</span></p>
<p><span><img loading="lazy" src="https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/a11y.png?width=1920&amp;height=1080&amp;name=a11y.png" alt="a11y" width="1920" height="1080" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1920px;" srcset="https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/a11y.png?width=960&amp;height=540&amp;name=a11y.png 960w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/a11y.png?width=1920&amp;height=1080&amp;name=a11y.png 1920w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/a11y.png?width=2880&amp;height=1620&amp;name=a11y.png 2880w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/a11y.png?width=3840&amp;height=2160&amp;name=a11y.png 3840w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/a11y.png?width=4800&amp;height=2700&amp;name=a11y.png 4800w, https://www.threatfabric.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Threatfabric/images/a11y.png?width=5760&amp;height=3240&amp;name=a11y.png 5760w" sizes="(max-width: 1920px) 100vw, 1920px"></span></p>
<p>In our previous blog we documented through our research why we believe that Vultur, the malware downladed by these droppers, is a malware family that is not only distributed, but also created by the criminals behind the Brunhilda Project. In addition to the reasons discussed in our previous blog, which focus on the networking protocol used, new variants of Vultur also adopted the very same string obfuscation algorithm discussed in the previous section about the Brunhilda Dropper, further confirming our beliefs about the connection between these two malware families.</p>
<h3 id="targets-1">Targets</h3>
<p>These new Brunhilda-Vultur campaigns have been very active and successful in the last few months, reaching more than 100.000 potential fraud victims. Based on our research and investigation, in addition to cryptowallets which have always been in the target list, the largest campaign we observed focused on UK and Netherlands, while the two smaller and more recent ones switched to Germany, France, and Italy. Full target list of Vultur is provided in<span>&nbsp;</span><a href="https://www.threatfabric.com/blogs/the-attack-of-the-droppers.html#vultur-targets">Appendix</a>.</p>
<h2 id="conclusion">Conclusion</h2>
<p>Another trend predicted by ThreatFabric’s experts comes true and looks like it is here to stay. Malicious dropper applications still find their way to sneak in the official store despite the changes made to the policy and security mechanisms. Distibution through droppers on Google Play still remains the most “affordable” and scalable way of reaching victims for most of the actors of different level. While sophisticated tactics like<span>&nbsp;</span><a href="https://www.threatfabric.com/blogs/toad-fraud.html">telephone-oriented attack delivery</a><span>&nbsp;</span>require more resources and are hard to scale, droppers on official and third-party stores allow threat actors to reach wide unsuspecting audience with reasonable efforts. Such way of distribution of Android banking Trojans is very dangerous as victims may stay unsuspecting for a long time and may not alert their bank about suspicious transactions made without them knowlege. Thus it is very important to take actions on the organisation side to detect such malicous apps and their payloads as well as suspicious behaviour happenning on customer’s device.</p>
