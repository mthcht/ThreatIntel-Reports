	<h1 class="article-details__title">An Analysis of the Nefilim Ransomware</h1>
	<p class="article-details__description">Nefilim is known for its double extortion capabilities and notable attacks in 2020. We give an overview of its techniques and tools in this entry.</p>
	<p class="article-details__author-by">By: Janus Agcaoili, Byron Gelera
		
			<time class="article-details__date">February 23, 2021</time>
		
		
		<span>Read time:&nbsp;</span><span class="eta"></span> (<span class="words"></span> words)
	</p>

		<div class="responsive-table-wrap">
			<p>Nefilim is among the notable <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/tmr/?/us/security/definition/Ransomware" target="_blank" style="font-weight: normal;">ransomware</a> variants that use double extortion tactics in their campaigns. First discovered in <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/tmr/?/us/security/news/cybercrime-and-digital-threats/nefilim-ransomware-threatens-to-expose-stolen-data" style="font-weight: normal;">March 2020</a>, Nefilim threatens to release victims’ stolen data to coerce them into paying the ransom. Aside from its use of this tactic, another notable characteristic of Nefilim is its similarity to Nemty; in fact, it is believed to be an evolved version of the older ransomware.</p>
<p>We provide a brief analysis of this active ransomware and how to defend systems against it.</p>
<p><span class="body-subhead-title">Technical Details</span></p>
<p><i>Initial access</i></p>
<p>For its initial access, threat actors behind Nefilim make use of various affiliates to spread their malware. These affiliates use various methods. Based on previous attacks, Nefilim has been largely known to reach systems via exposed RDPs. Some affiliates also use other known vulnerabilities for initial access. This is supported by various reports, from which we found the use of the Citrix vulnerability (<a href="https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2019-19781" style="font-weight: normal;">CVE-2019-19781</a>), an unsecure and brute-force RDP, to enter a system. </p>
<p>Nefilim has also been seen using party tools to gather credentials that include Mimikatz, LaZagne, and NirSoft’s NetPass. The stolen credentials are used to reach high-value machines like servers.</p>
<p>Once inside a victim system, the ransomware begins to drop and execute its components such as anti-antivirus, exfiltration tools, and finally Nefilim itself.</p>
<p><i>Lateral movement on the network</i></p>
<p>The attackers make use of several legitimate tools for lateral movement. For example, it uses PsExec or Windows Management Instrumentation (WMI) for lateral movement, dropping and executing other components including the ransomware itself. Nefilim has been observed to use a batch file for terminating certain processes and services. It even uses third-party tools like PC Hunter, Process Hacker, and Revo Uninstaller to terminate antivirus-related processes, services, and applications. It also uses AdFind, BloodHound, or SMBTool to identify active directories and/or machines that are connected to the domain.</p>
<p><i>Data exfiltration</i></p>
<p>A notable aspect of recent ransomware variants are their data exfiltration capabilities. As for Nefilim, it has been observed to copy data from servers or shared directories to a local directory and to archive these using 7-Zip. It then uses MEGAsync to exfiltrate this data.</p>
<p><span class="body-subhead-title">Defending systems against ransomware</span></p>
<p>Campaigns that are similar to Nefilim spend a lot of time between the initial breach and the start of serious lateral movement. However, as soon as lateral movement begins, threat actors work quickly. They prioritize moving between hosts and exfiltrating data. Therefore, organizations can consider limiting the number of computers that can be leveraged during a lateral movement phase. This involves solutions such as utilizing two-factor authentication (2FA) wherever they can, implementing application safelisting, and practicing least privilege security.  </p>
<p>With regard to defending systems against the threat of Nefilim, best practices still apply. It is best to work on defenses that prevent the lateral movement of similar attacks. Organizations should consider the use of canary file-based monitoring, encryption monitoring, and process killing. Other best practices to review include the following:</p>
<ul>
<li> </li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet"><span style="font-weight: normal;">Avoid opening unverified emails or clicking on their embedded links, as these can start the ransomware installation process. </span></span></li>
<li> </li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet"><span style="font-weight: normal;">Back up your important files using the 3-2-1 rule: Create three backup copies on two different file formats, with one of the backups in a separate location. </span></span></li>
<li> </li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet"><span style="font-weight: normal;">Regularly update software, programs, and applications to ensure that your apps are current, with the latest protections from new vulnerabilities.</span></span></li>
</ul>
<p>If you believe that your organization has been affected by this campaign, visit <a href="https://success.trendmicro.com/solution/000285717" target="_blank" style="font-weight: normal;">this page</a> for the available Trend Micro solutions that can help detect and mitigate any risks from this campaign.</p>
