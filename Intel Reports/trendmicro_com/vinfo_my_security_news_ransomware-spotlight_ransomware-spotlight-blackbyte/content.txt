Like its contemporaries, it has gone after critical infrastructure for a higher chance of a getting a payout. What techniques set it apart? </p></div><p class="right downloadbox"><a href="https://documents.trendmicro.com/images/TEx/articles/RS-BlackByte-Infographic-EHGnsy5w1.jpg" target="_blank" rel="noopener" title="Ransomware Spotlight: BlackByte Infographic" data-mce-href="https://documents.trendmicro.com/images/TEx/articles/RS-BlackByte-Infographic-EHGnsy5w1.jpg"><img src="https://documents.trendmicro.com/images/TEx/articles/RS-BlackByte-Infographic-thumb-kYmsJqA.jpg" alt="Ransomware Spotlight: BlackByte Infographic" caption="false" be_marvel="1" onerror="this.onerror=null;this.src='https://documents.trendmicro.com/images/TEx/articles/Conti-infographic-thumbAR8mNYk.jpg'" width="178" height="285" data-mce-src="https://documents.trendmicro.com/images/TEx/articles/RS-BlackByte-Infographic-thumb-kYmsJqA.jpg"> </a>View infographic of "Ransomware Spotlight: BlackByte"</p><p>BlackByte debuted in July 2021. Its first year of activity garnered the attention of the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) and the US Secret Service (USS). According to a <a href="https://www.ic3.gov/Media/News/2022/220211.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-mce-href="https://www.ic3.gov/Media/News/2022/220211.pdf">joint advisory</a> by these two government agencies, BlackByte had already gone after at least three US critical infrastructure sectors (government facilities, financial, and food and agriculture) by November 2021.</p><p>This advisory shows just how BlackByte was actively establishing itself as a new noteworthy <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/my/security/definition/ransomware" data-mce-href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/my/security/definition/ransomware">ransomware</a> variant. This however did not stop BlackByte as developers released newer versions that used multiple keys and <a href="https://www.theregister.com/2022/05/19/blackbyte-ransomware-attacks/" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-mce-href="https://www.theregister.com/2022/05/19/blackbyte-ransomware-attacks/">ramped up</a> operations, going as far as to warn their victims against using the available decrypter on their website.</p><p>BlackByte’s emergence could be part of a larger scheme. With the purported shut down of <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/my/security/news/ransomware-spotlight/ransomware-spotlight-conti" data-mce-href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/my/security/news/ransomware-spotlight/ransomware-spotlight-conti">Conti</a>, researchers from AdvIntel surmise that BlackByte is one of the chief new ransomware variants <a href="https://www.advintel.io/post/hydra-with-three-heads-blackbyte-the-future-of-ransomware-subsidiary-groups" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-mce-href="https://www.advintel.io/post/hydra-with-three-heads-blackbyte-the-future-of-ransomware-subsidiary-groups">part of its rebranding</a>.</p><p>At present, BlackByte continues to target organizations from all over the world. However, like <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/my/security/news/ransomware-spotlight/ransomware-spotlight-lockbit" data-mce-href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/my/security/news/ransomware-spotlight/ransomware-spotlight-lockbit">LockBit</a>, <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/my/security/news/ransomware-spotlight/ransomware-spotlight-ransomexx" data-mce-href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/my/security/news/ransomware-spotlight/ransomware-spotlight-ransomexx">RansomEXX</a>, and many other ransomware families, BlackByte avoids attacking Russia-based entities.</p><h2>What do organizations need to know about BlackByte?</h2><div><br></div><p>While BlackByte operators use their piece of ransomware in attacks for their own gain, they also run on a ransomware-as-a-service (RaaS) model for their affiliates. We have listed down the key highlights of BlackByte here:</p><ul><li><b>Initial versions used symmetric keys. </b>The earlier variant of BlackByte used the same key in each campaign to encrypt files. It also used AES, a symmetric key algorithm. This allowed researchers to create a decrypter to help BlackByte victims, thus forcing the group to change their encryption method in newer variants.</li><li><b>It has multiple variants. </b>The first known version of BlackByte was written in C#. Operators then released two Go-based variants. The more recent Go-variant was introduced around February 2022 and sported modifications particularly in its encryption algorithm.</li><li><b>Archives files using WinRAR. </b>In BlackByte campaigns data exfiltration is done before the ransomware is deployed. This is because the BlackByte ransomware is incapable of exfiltrating data, instead it archives files using WinRAR then uploads the file to sharing sites. </li><li><b>Uses trojanized <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/my/security/news/cybercrime-and-digital-threats/locked-loaded-and-in-the-wrong-hands-legitimate-tools-weaponized-for-ransomware-in-2021" data-mce-href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/my/security/news/cybercrime-and-digital-threats/locked-loaded-and-in-the-wrong-hands-legitimate-tools-weaponized-for-ransomware-in-2021">legitimate tools</a>. </b>Like most modern ransomware variants, BlackByte uses living-off-the-land binaries. For example, it uses the remote tool AnyDesk to gain further control over a system and for lateral movement.</li><li><strong>Involves phishing emails or a known ProxyShell vulnerability for initial access.</strong> BlackByte has been known to use phishing emails or exploit unpatched ProxyShell vulnerability in Microsoft Exchange Servers to gain initial access into a system.</li></ul><p>BlackByte trajectory seems to point to continuing activity. In fact, reports indicate that BlackByte is among the ransomware operations that have set their sights on <a href="https://www.recordedfuture.com/latin-american-governments-targeted-by-ransomware" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-mce-href="https://www.recordedfuture.com/latin-american-governments-targeted-by-ransomware">Latin American governments</a> in May 2022. However, underneath it all could be a more intricate scheme of threat groups dispersing under new monikers. </p><p>As with the case of BlackByte, knowing its notable tactics, while also staying knowledgeable of bigger trends can help organizations create an effective strategy for ransomware attacks.
