We review this ransomware group’s constantly changing schemes and discuss how companies can shore up defenses against this threat.</p></div><p class="right downloadbox"><a href="https://documents.trendmicro.com/images/TEx/articles/rs-Clop-Infographic-C1jGKfj.jpg" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-mce-href="https://documents.trendmicro.com/images/TEx/articles/rs-Clop-Infographic-C1jGKfj.jpg"><img src="https://documents.trendmicro.com/images/TEx/articles/clop-infographic-thumbnail-F2hGRmt.jpg" alt="Ransomware Spotlight: Clop Infographic" caption="false" width="400" height="641" data-mce-src="https://documents.trendmicro.com/images/TEx/articles/clop-infographic-thumbnail-F2hGRmt.jpg"> </a>View infographic of "Ransomware Spotlight: Clop"</p><p>(Last update: August 31, 2023) Clop (sometimes stylized as “Cl0p”) has been one of the most prolific <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/tr/security/definition/ransomware" data-mce-href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/tr/security/definition/ransomware">ransomware</a> families in the past three years. It has gained infamy for compromising high-profile organizations in various industries worldwide using multilevel extortion techniques that resulted in <a href="https://ipdefenseforum.com/2021/08/south-korean-police-help-take-down-cyber-threat-in-ukraine/" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-mce-href="https://ipdefenseforum.com/2021/08/south-korean-police-help-take-down-cyber-threat-in-ukraine/">huge payouts estimated at US$500 million as of November 2021</a>. In concerted efforts to dismantle ransomware cartels, a global coalition across five continents that involved law enforcement and private partners led to the <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/en_us/research/21/k/global-operations-lead-to-arrests-of-alleged-members-of-gandcrab.html" data-mce-href="https://www.trendmicro.com/en_us/research/21/k/global-operations-lead-to-arrests-of-alleged-members-of-gandcrab.html">arrests in Ukraine of six suspected Clop members</a> in June 2021.</p><p>While the arrests in Ukraine might have dealt a big blow to Clop’s operations, the group’s criminal activities went unabated from 2021 to 2022. Data in 2023 show, however, that there has been a slow down in deployments, seemingly as the group pivots to data stealing and extortion. <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/clop-ransomware-is-back-in-business-after-recent-arrests/" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-mce-href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/clop-ransomware-is-back-in-business-after-recent-arrests/">Reports</a> mentioned that only parts of the ransomware’s operations, such as the server infrastructure used by affiliates to disseminate the malware and the channels used to launder cryptocurrency ransom payments that were illegally obtained, were seized and taken down, respectively.</p><p>As enterprises ponder on ways to bolster their security defenses in the post-pandemic era, learning more about potential threats is essential to adopting a proactive cybersecurity approach. In this report, we focus the spotlight on the notorious Clop ransomware’s operations.</p><h2>History of Clop</h2><p>Clop evolved as a variant of the <a href="https://www.webroot.com/blog/2016/07/22/about-cryptomix-ransomware/" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-mce-href="https://www.webroot.com/blog/2016/07/22/about-cryptomix-ransomware/">CryptoMix</a> ransomware family. In February 2019, security researchers discovered the use of Clop by the threat group known as TA505 when it launched a large-scale <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/tr/security/definition/spear-phishing" data-mce-href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/tr/security/definition/spear-phishing">spear-phishing</a> email campaign. Clop is an example of <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/tr/security/definition/ransomware-as-a-service-raas" data-mce-href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/tr/security/definition/ransomware-as-a-service-raas">ransomware as a service (RaaS)</a> that is operated by <a href="https://www.cybereason.com/blog/cl0p-ransomware-gang-tries-to-topple-the-house-of-cards" aria-invalid="true" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-mce-href="https://www.cybereason.com/blog/cl0p-ransomware-gang-tries-to-topple-the-house-of-cards">a Russian-speaking group</a>. Additionally, this ransomware used a verified and digitally signed binary, which made it look like a legitimate executable file that could evade security detection.</p><p>In 2020, it was reported that FIN11 — a financially motivated hacking group — deployed Clop ransomware and threatened their victims to publish exfiltrated data. FIN11 exploited <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/tr/security/definition/zero-day-vulnerability" data-mce-href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/tr/security/definition/zero-day-vulnerability">zero-day vulnerabilities</a> in the legacy file transfer appliance (FTA) of Kiteworks (formerly known as Accellion) to infiltrate the network of the victims. It then aimed to deliver the Clop ransomware as its payload and steal data as well. Researchers also discovered that the group used a specific <a href="https://success.trendmicro.com/solution/000283514" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-mce-href="https://success.trendmicro.com/solution/000283514">web shell</a> that was referred to as “DEWMODE” to exfiltrate stolen information from its victims.</p><p>Researchers found two groups of malicious actors that have known connections to FIN11 and identified them as UNCA2546 and UNCA2582. These were also the groups responsible for the massive <a href="https://www.kiteworks.com/sites/default/files/resources/accellion-company-overview.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-mce-href="https://www.kiteworks.com/sites/default/files/resources/accellion-company-overview.pdf">attacks on Kiteworks</a> users.</p><p>The operators behind Clop made their first attempt at using the <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/tr/security/news/cybercrime-and-digital-threats/ransomware-double-extortion-and-beyond-revil-clop-and-conti" data-mce-href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/tr/security/news/cybercrime-and-digital-threats/ransomware-double-extortion-and-beyond-revil-clop-and-conti">double extortion</a> scheme in April 2020 when they publicized the data of a <a href="https://techcrunch.com/2020/04/27/execupharm-clop-ransomware/" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-mce-href="https://techcrunch.com/2020/04/27/execupharm-clop-ransomware/">pharmaceutical company</a> on their leak site. Clop’s dedicated leak site hosts its list of victims, which has markedly grown since its launch. Over time, the gang’s extortion tactics have become more sophisticated and thus more destructive.</p><p>In November 2021, security researchers detected malicious activity by Clop operators that exploited a <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/clop-gang-exploiting-solarwinds-serv-u-flaw-in-ransomware-attacks/" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-mce-href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/clop-gang-exploiting-solarwinds-serv-u-flaw-in-ransomware-attacks/">SolarWinds</a> Serv-U vulnerability to breach corporate networks and deliver the Clop ransomware as a payload. The Serv-U Managed File Transfer and Serv-U Secure FTP remote code execution (RCE) vulnerability, tracked as <a href="https://cve.mitre.org/cgi-bin/cvename.cgi?name=CVE-2021-35211#:~:text=Microsoft%20discovered%20a%20remote%20code,machine%20hosting%20Serv%2DU%20Only." target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-mce-href="https://cve.mitre.org/cgi-bin/cvename.cgi?name=CVE-2021-35211#:~:text=Microsoft%20discovered%20a%20remote%20code,machine%20hosting%20Serv%2DU%20Only.">CVE-2021-35211</a>, allowed RCE on the vulnerable server with elevated privileges. </p><p>A maritime services giant with headquarters in Singapore also fell prey to Clop. In November 2021, it was reported that <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/marine-services-provider-swire-pacific-offshore-hit-by-ransomware/" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-mce-href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/marine-services-provider-swire-pacific-offshore-hit-by-ransomware/">Clop breached its IT systems</a> to steal classified proprietary commercial information and employee data that included bank account details, payroll information, passports, email addresses, and internal correspondence, among others.</p><p>In October 2022, Microsoft Security <span><a href="https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2022/10/27/raspberry-robin-worm-part-of-larger-ecosystem-facilitating-pre-ransomware-activity/" data-mce-href="https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2022/10/27/raspberry-robin-worm-part-of-larger-ecosystem-facilitating-pre-ransomware-activity/">reported</a></span> that DEV-0950, a ransomware group that appears to be associated with the cybercriminal group <span><a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/actor/fin11" data-mce-href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/actor/fin11">FIN11 (aka TA505),</a></span> distributed Clop in a campaign that targeted one of the victims of the Raspberry Robin worm.</p><div><br></div></div></div></div></div></div><div class="full-width-block normalStyleBlock"><h2 class="centeredBlock margin-topAdjust"><span style="font-size: 39.1px;" data-mce-style="font-size: 39.1px;">An overview of Clop operations</span></h2><div class="width-90-wide grid-1-col-layout"><div class="grid-col"><p>The Clop ransomware appends the “.ClOP” (“Clop” spelled with a small “L”) extension to the files it encrypts. Researchers also discovered that Clop targets a victim’s entire network instead of just individual computers. This is made possible by hacking into the Active Directory (AD) server before the ransomware infection to determine the system’s Group Policy. This allows the ransomware to persist in the endpoints even after incident responders have already cleaned them up.</p><p>Previous attacks by the TA505 group saw the <a href="https://www.hornetsecurity.com/en/security-information/clop-clop-ta505-html-malspam-analysis/?_adin=11551547647" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-mce-href="https://www.hornetsecurity.com/en/security-information/clop-clop-ta505-html-malspam-analysis/?_adin=11551547647">delivery of the Clop malware as the final stage of its payload</a> in massive phishing campaigns. The malicious actors would send spam emails with HTML attachments that would redirect recipients to a macro-enabled document such as an XLS file used to drop a loader named Get2. This loader facilitates the download of various tools such as <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/us/threat-encyclopedia/malware/SDBOT/" data-mce-href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/us/threat-encyclopedia/malware/SDBOT/">SDBOT</a>, <a href="https://success.trendmicro.com/solution/1123301-flawedammyy-malware-information#:~:text=FlawedAmmyy%20is%20a%20remote%20access,on%20leaked%20Ammyy%20Admin%20software.&text=Upon%20infection%2C%20the%20RAT%20can,Capture%20screenshots" data-mce-href="https://success.trendmicro.com/solution/1123301-flawedammyy-malware-information#:~:text=FlawedAmmyy%20is%20a%20remote%20access,on%20leaked%20Ammyy%20Admin%20software.&text=Upon%20infection%2C%20the%20RAT%20can,Capture%20screenshots">FlawedAmmyy</a>, and <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/en_us/research/21/g/tracking_cobalt_strike_a_vision_one_investigation.html#:~:text=Cobalt%20Strike%20is%20a%20well,and%20remove%20a%20malware%20infection." data-mce-href="https://www.trendmicro.com/en_us/research/21/g/tracking_cobalt_strike_a_vision_one_investigation.html#:~:text=Cobalt%20Strike%20is%20a%20well,and%20remove%20a%20malware%20infection.">Cobalt Strike</a>. Once the malicious actors intrude into the system, they proceed to reconnaissance, lateral movement, and exfiltration to set the stage for deployment of the Clop ransomware.</p><p>The operators behind Clop coerce their victims by sending out emails in a bid for negotiations. They also resort to more severe threats such as publicizing and auctioning off the stolen information on their data leak site “Cl0p^_-Leaks” if their messages are ignored. They have also gone to the extent of using <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/us/security/news/cybercrime-and-digital-threats/ransomware-double-extortion-and-beyond-revil-clop-and-conti" data-mce-href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/us/security/news/cybercrime-and-digital-threats/ransomware-double-extortion-and-beyond-revil-clop-and-conti">quadruple extortion techniques</a>, which have involved going after <a href="https://www.zdnet.com/article/some-ransomware-gangs-are-going-after-top-execs-to-pressure-companies-into-paying/" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-mce-href="https://www.zdnet.com/article/some-ransomware-gangs-are-going-after-top-execs-to-pressure-companies-into-paying/">top executives</a> and <a href="https://securityaffairs.co/wordpress/116029/cyber-crime/clop-ransomware-extortion.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-mce-href="https://securityaffairs.co/wordpress/116029/cyber-crime/clop-ransomware-extortion.html">customers</a> to pressure companies into settling the ransom.</p><p>Having established itself well in the world of cybercrime, the Clop ransomware gang is deemed as a trendsetter for its ever-changing tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs). Indeed, the group’s Kiteworks FTA exploits set a new trend as these significantly <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/accellion-data-breaches-drive-up-average-ransom-price/" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-mce-href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/accellion-data-breaches-drive-up-average-ransom-price/">pulled up the average ransom payments for the first quarter of 2021</a>. A <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/accellion-data-breaches-drive-up-average-ransom-price/" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-mce-href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/accellion-data-breaches-drive-up-average-ransom-price/">report</a> that cited Coveware’s findings revealed that the average ransomware payments significantly went up to US$220,298, which is an increase of 43%. It also said that the median ransom payment increased sharply to US$78,398 from US$49,459, which translates to a 60% hike.</p><h2 class="centeredBlock margin-topAdjust">Recent Clop activities</h2><p>The Clop ransomware gang also claimed to have targeted 130 organizations who were victims of the <span><a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/clop-ransomware-gang-begins-extorting-goanywhere-zero-day-victims/" data-mce-href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/clop-ransomware-gang-begins-extorting-goanywhere-zero-day-victims/">Fortra GoAnywhere MFT vulnerability</a></span> over a month-long period in March 2023. class="grid-1-col-layout"><p>Despite arrests of alleged members of the Clop ransomware cartel in Ukraine in 2021, our detections of this ransomware indicate that the group is still a potential threat and might strike anytime. Moreover, the operators behind Clop are known to regularly change their TTPs, which means that expecting them to sharpen the proverbial saw is par for the course. It is therefore best to stay vigilant and armed with the knowledge that ransomware operators are always waiting for a chance to pounce on their next victim.</p><p>To protect systems against similar threats, organizations can establish security frameworks that allocate resources systematically for establishing a strong defense strategy against ransomware.</p><p>Here are some best practices that organizations can consider:</p></div><div class="grid-3-col-layout darkGrid margin-topAdjust"><div class="grid-col-layout"><div class="iconContainerHolder inventory-icon"><br></div><h4>Audit and inventory</h4><ul><li>Take an inventory of assets and data.</li><li>Identify authorized and unauthorized devices and software.</li><li>Make an audit of event and incident logs.</li></ul></div><div class="grid-col-layout"><div class="iconContainerHolder configure-icon"><br></div><h4>Configure and monitor</h4><ul><li>Manage hardware and software configurations.</li><li>Grant admin privileges and access only when necessary to an employee’s role.</li><li>Monitor network ports, protocols, and services.</li><li>Activate security configurations on network infrastructure devices such as firewalls and routers.</li><li>Establish a software allowlist that only executes legitimate applications.</li></ul></div><div class="grid-col-layout"><div class="iconContainerHolder patch-icon"><br></div><h4>Patch and update</h4><ul><li>Conduct regular vulnerability assessments.</li><li>Perform patching or virtual patching for operating systems and applications. </li><li>Update software and applications to their latest versions. </li><li>To prevent attacks like the Kiteworks FTA exploits, update to and patch the latest version of the FTA to clear the zero-day vulnerabilities that were released by the malicious actors and dedicated to the attack signatures.</li></ul></div><div class="grid-col-layout"><div class="iconContainerHolder protect-icon"><br></div><h4>Protect and recover</h4><ul><li>Implement data protection, backup, and recovery measures.</li><li>Enable multifactor authentication (MFA). </li></ul></div><div class="grid-col-layout"><div class="iconContainerHolder secure-icon"><br></div><h4>Secure and defend</h4><ul><li>Employ sandbox analysis to block malicious emails.</li><li>Deploy the latest versions of security solutions to all layers of the system, including email, endpoint, web, and network.</li><li>Detect early signs of an attack such as the presence of suspicious tools in the system.</li><li>Use advanced detection technologies such as those powered by AI and machine learning.</li></ul></div><div class="grid-col-layout"><div class="iconContainerHolder test-icon"><br></div><h4>Train and test</h4><ul><li>Regularly train and assess employees on security skills.</li><li>Conduct red-team exercises and penetration tests.</li></ul></div></div><div class="grid-1-col-layout"><p>A multilayered approach can help organizations guard the possible entry points into the system (endpoint, email, web, and network).
