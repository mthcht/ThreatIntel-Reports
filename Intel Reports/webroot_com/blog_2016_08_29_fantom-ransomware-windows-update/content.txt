<p><a href="https://blog-en.webroot.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/Fantom_Properties.png"><img decoding="async" class="alignright wp-image-19661" src="https://blog-en.webroot.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/Fantom_Properties.png" alt="Fantom_Properties" width="265" height="368" srcset="https://blog-en.webroot.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/Fantom_Properties.png 371w, https://blog-en.webroot.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/Fantom_Properties-768x1064.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 265px) 100vw, 265px" /></a><a href="https://www.webroot.com/us/en/home/products/antivirus-for-windows">Windows 10</a> has been notorious about automatically installing updates on users’ machines and now there is a ransomware that aims to capitalize on it. The new ransomware, Fantom, is based on the EDA2 open-source ransomware project on GitHub called <a href="https://github.com/utkusen/hidden-tear">hidden tear</a> that’s recently been abandoned.</p>
<h2>Fantom behind the scenes</h2>
<p>In an attempt to conceal malicious intention, the authors of this ransomware modified the file properties to show copyright and legal trademarks mimicking a Windows update.</p>
<p>Once this dropper is executed, the payload “WindowsUpdate.exe” is dropped in AppData\Local\Temp displaying the fake Windows Update screen as shown below. This screen locks you out of doing anything else on your computer, keeping in line with the scam that Windows 10 doing its normal interrupt of updates.</p>
<p>The percentage counter does work and will go up at about a percent per minute. However, it’s fake and doesn’t represent anything other than to communicate to you that this “Windows update” will take a while and that you shouldn’t be alarmed of CPU usage and hard drive activity. You can close this fake update overlay by ending the process “WindowsUpdate.exe” using task manager, but the encryption of your files is unaffected.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2></h2>
<h2><a href="https://blog-en.webroot.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/Fantom_DECRYPT_YOUR_FILES.HTML.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="size-full wp-image-19659 alignleft" src="https://blog-en.webroot.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/Fantom_DECRYPT_YOUR_FILES.HTML.png" alt="Fantom_DECRYPT_YOUR_FILES.HTML" width="249" height="204" srcset="https://blog-en.webroot.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/Fantom_DECRYPT_YOUR_FILES.HTML.png 249w, https://blog-en.webroot.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/Fantom_DECRYPT_YOUR_FILES.HTML-768x629.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 249px) 100vw, 249px" /></a>DECRYPT_YOUR_FILES.HTML ransom note</h2>
<p>Encryption is done using AES-128 encryption and when a file is encrypted it will append “.fantom” to the extension of the file. Also in every directory that a file is encrypted, a standard ransom note “DECRYPT_YOUR_FILES.HTML” is created.</p>
<p>The ransom note doesn’t have an onion link as your payment portal for your files – a standard for most encrypting ransomware. Instead, you&#8217;re asked to email the cyber criminals and await response. This tactic is meant to target less savvy computer users who would be intimidated by creating a bitcoin wallet address and using a tor browser to connect to the darknet for ransom payment. To increase odds of gaining trust, two &#8220;freebie&#8221; files for decryption are allowed.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>However, it’s clear that these cyber criminals have a very loose grip on the English language so we don’t anticipate much traction with their scams through email. We also reached out as a test and have yet to hear back in over 24 hours.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><a href="https://blog-en.webroot.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/Fantom_Attention.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="aligncenter wp-image-19655" src="https://blog-en.webroot.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/Fantom_Attention.png" alt="Fantom_Attention" width="893" height="370" srcset="https://blog-en.webroot.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/Fantom_Attention.png 1153w, https://blog-en.webroot.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/Fantom_Attention-768x318.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 893px) 100vw, 893px" /></a></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2>Employ a backup solution</h2>
<p>Webroot will catch this specific variant in real time before any encryption takes place. We’re always on the lookout for new threats, but just in case of new zero-day variants, remember that with encrypting ransomware, the best protection is going to be a good backup solution. This can be either through the cloud or offline external storage. Keeping it up to date is key so as not to lose productivity. Webroot has backup features built into our consumer product that allow you to have directories constantly synced to the cloud. If you were to get infected by a zero-day variant of encrypting ransomware, you can just restore your files back as we save a snapshot history for each of your files up to ten previous copies. Please see our community <a href="https://community.webroot.com/t5/Webroot-Education/Best-practices-for-securing-your-environment-against/ta-p/191172">post</a> on best practices for securing your environment against encrypting ransomware.</p>
