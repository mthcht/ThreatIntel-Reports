    <div class="article-tags mb-2 dark big"><p class="article-tag text-capitalize">Cybercrime</p></div>    <h1 class="page-headline">Carbanak gang is back and packing new guns</h1>    <p class="sub-title">A few days ago, CSIS published details about new Carbanak samples found in the wild. In this blog we examine the latest developments in the Carbanak story.</p>
    
    <div class="article-authors d-flex flex-wrap"><div class="article-author d-flex"><a href="/en/our-experts/anton-cherepanov/" title="Anton Cherepanov"><picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2020/03/Anton_Cherepanov.jpg" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><img class="author-image me-3"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2020/03/Anton_Cherepanov.jpg" alt="Anton Cherepanov"  /></picture></a><div class="author-text"><p><a href="/en/our-experts/anton-cherepanov/" title="Anton Cherepanov"><b>Anton Cherepanov</b></a></p></div></div></div>

                    <div class="article-body">
    <p>The Carbanak <em>financial APT </em>group made the headlines when Group-IB and Fox-IT <a href="https://www.fox-it.com/en/press-releases/anunak/" target="_blank">broke the news</a> in December 2014, followed by the Kaspersky <a href="https://securelist.com/blog/research/68732/the-great-bank-robbery-the-carbanak-apt/" target="_blank">report</a> in February 2015. The two reports describe the same cybercriminal gang which stole up to several hundreds of millions of dollars from various financial institutions.</p>
<p>However, the story is interesting not only because of the large amount of money stolen but also from a technical point of view. The Carbanak team does not just blindly compromise large numbers of computers and try to ‘milk the cow’ as other actors do, instead they act like a mature APT-group. They only compromise specific high-value targets and once inside the company networks, move laterally to hosts that can be monetized.</p>
<p>A few days ago CSIS <a href="https://www.csis.dk/en/csis/blog/4710/" target="_blank">published details</a> about new Carbanak samples found in the wild.</p>
<p>In this blog we will describe the latest developments in the Carbanak story.</p>
<h1>Casino hotel hack</h1>
<p>At the end of August, we detected an attempt to compromise the network of a casino hotel in the USA. The infection vector used in this attack may have been a spearphishing e-mail with a malicious attachment using an RTF-exploit or .SCR file. The attackers' aim was to compromise PoS servers used in payment processing.</p>
<p>The main backdoor used by attackers was the open-source Tiny Meterpreter. In this case, however, the source was modified – the process injection to svchost.exe was added to its functionality.</p>
<p>This Tiny Meterpreter backdoor dropped two different malware families:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="http://virusradar.com/en/Win32_Spy.Sekur/detail" target="_blank">Win32/Spy.Sekur</a> – well known malware used by the Carbanak gang</li>
<li><a href="http://virusradar.com/en/Win32_Wemosis/detail" target="_blank">Win32/Wemosis</a> – a PoS RAM Scraper backdoor</li>
</ul>
<p>As mentioned <a href="http://blog.trendmicro.com/trendlabs-security-intelligence/signed-pos-malware-used-in-pre-holiday-attacks-linked-to-targeted-attacks/" target="_blank">here</a> by our colleagues from TrendMicro, Carbanak malware is capable of targeting Epicor/NSB PoS systems, while Win32/Wemosis is a general-purpose PoS RAM Scraper which targets any PoS that stores card data in the memory. The Wemosis backdoor is written in Delphi and allows the attacker to control an infected computer remotely.</p>
<p>Both executables were digitally signed with the same certificate:</p>
<p><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2015/09/image1.png"><img class="aligncenter  wp-image-68640" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2015/09/image1.png" alt="image1" width="642" height="398" /></a></p>
<p>The certificate details:</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new,courier,monospace;">Company name: Blik</span><br />
<span style="font-family: courier new,courier,monospace;">Validity: from 02 October 2014 to 03 October 2015</span><br />
<span style="font-family: courier new,courier,monospace;">Thumbprint: ‎0d0971b6735265b28f39c1f015518768e375e2a3</span><br />
<span style="font-family: courier new,courier,monospace;">Serial number: ‎00d95d2caa093bf43a029f7e2916eae7fb</span><br />
<span style="font-family: courier new,courier,monospace;">Subject: CN = Blik</span><br />
<span style="font-family: courier new,courier,monospace;">O = Blik</span><br />
<span style="font-family: courier new,courier,monospace;">STREET = Berzarina, 7, 1</span><br />
<span style="font-family: courier new,courier,monospace;">L = Moscow</span><br />
<span style="font-family: courier new,courier,monospace;">S = Moscow</span><br />
<span style="font-family: courier new,courier,monospace;">PostalCode = 123298</span><br />
<span style="font-family: courier new,courier,monospace;">C = RU</span></p>
<p>This certificate was also used in the digital signature of a third malware family used by the same gang: <a href="http://virusradar.com/en/Win32_Spy.Agent.ORM/detail" target="_blank">Win32/Spy.Agent.ORM</a>.</p>
<p><strong>Win32/Spy.Agent.ORM - overview</strong></p>
<p><em>Win32/Spy.Agent.ORM</em> (also known as <em>Win32/Toshliph</em>) is a trojan used as one of their first-stage payloads by the Carbanak gang. The binary of the testing version was signed with a <strong>Blik</strong> certificate: moreover, <em>Spy.Agent.ORM</em> shares some similarities in the code with “the regular” Carbanak malware.</p>
<p>The <em>Win32/Spy.Agent.ORM</em> malware family is already known in the industry because of two blogposts. In July 2015 security company Cyphort <a href="http://www.cyphort.com/unicredit-compromised/" target="_blank">reported</a> the compromise of a news portal and a banking site – rbc.ua and unicredit.ua. It <a href="http://www.cyphort.com/unicredit-compromise-continued/" target="_blank">turns out</a> that the compromised sites served <em>Win32/Spy.Agent.ORM</em>. After that, Blue Coat <a href="https://www.bluecoat.com/security-blog/2015-08-21/tinted-cve-decoy-spearphising-attempt-central-bank-armenia-employees" target="_blank">reported</a> a spearphishing attempt targeting Central Bank of Armenia employees, the payload being the same.</p>
<p>This malware appeared on our radar at the beginning of summer 2015, and afterwards we started to track it.</p>
<p>We have seen attempts to attack various companies in Russia and Ukraine using spearphishing e-mails that have malicious attachments consisting of .SCR files or .RTF exploits.</p>
<p>Here is an example of a spearphishing email sent to one of the biggest Forex-trading companies:</p>
<p><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2015/09/image2.png"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-68641" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2015/09/image2.png" alt="image2" width="666" height="434" /></a></p>
<p>Roughly translated from Russian to English, it says:</p>
<p><em>"Due to the high volatility of the ruble exchange rate the Bank of Russia sends rules of trading on the currency market. Password the attached document: cbr"</em></p>
<p>Here is another example of a spear phishing attempt. Email with this text was sent to the largest electronic payment service in Russia:</p>

<h1>Win32/Spy.Agent.ORM - Technical details</h1>
<p>Win32/Spy.Agent.ORM is a small and simple backdoor that enables the attackers to assess the victim. When executed the trojan connects to a C&amp;C server and receives commands to grab screenshots, enumerate running processes and get information about the system and campaign ID. Based on that information malware operator decides whether the infected computer is useful: that is, whether it's the intended target or just a system that was accidentally infected.</p>

<p>Also, the latest sample is able to gain system privileges via an exploit and install itself as a system service. The trojan attempts to exploit a vulnerability – CVE-2015-2426 in the OpenType manager module (ATMFD.dll) – which was patched by Microsoft in <a href="https://technet.microsoft.com/en-us/library/security/ms15-078.aspx" target="_blank">MS15-078</a>. The exploit for this vulnerability was leaked in a <a href="/2015/07/06/400gb-info-leaked-hacking-team/" target="_blank">Hacking Team dump</a>.</p>
<p><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2015/09/image6.png"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-68645" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2015/09/image6.png" alt="image6" width="513" height="182" /></a></p>
<p>The digital certificate for <strong>Blik </strong>used in this case is not the only link between Win32/Spy.Agent.ORM and Win32/Spy.Sekur (Carbanak malware). They share similarities in code – take a look at the function that generates the BOTID-value, for example:</p>
<p><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2015/09/image7.png"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-68646" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2015/09/image7.png" alt="image7" width="628" height="988" /></a></p>
<p>The BOTID-value is a unique value generated on the basis of the hardware parameters of infected computer, and it’s used by attackers for computer identification. In both cases generation is based on the MAC-address and computer name and the resulting value is formatted using the <strong>wsprintf</strong> –function.</p>

<p>Our sinkhole of some C&amp;C domains used by the Win32/Wemosis has resulted in hits from bots in the following countries.</p>
<p><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2015/09/Screen-Shot-2015-09-08-at-09.50.14.png"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-68649" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2015/09/Screen-Shot-2015-09-08-at-09.50.14.png" alt="Statistics" width="664" height="472" /></a></p>

<h1>Conclusions</h1>
<p>Even after it has reportedly stolen hundreds of millions of dollars, the infamous Carbanak APT group isn’t resting on its laurels. On the contrary, it is very active and keeps attacking specific targets related to the finance industry, including banks, Forex-trading companies, and even an American casino hotel. Recently, we have detected malware used by the Carbanak group in the following countries, among others:</p>
<ul>
<li>United States of America</li>
<li>Germany</li>
<li>United Arab Emirates</li>
</ul>
<p>As described in this blog post, the gang doesn’t use just one malware family to carry out its operations but several. While the code in the different families – Carbanak (Win32/Spy.Sekur), Win32/Spy.Agent.ORM, and Win32/Wemosis – is different it does contain similar traits, including the same digital certificate.</p>
<p>Furthermore, the attackers are updating their arsenal with the latest exploits, such as the Microsoft Office remote code execution vulnerability, CVE-2015-1770, or the zero-day exploit leaked in the Hacking Team dumps, CVE-2015-2426.</p>
<p>We continue to monitor the Carbanak threats. For any enquiries or sample submissions related to the subject, contact as at: <a href="mailto:threatintel@eset.com">threatintel@eset.com</a>.</p>
