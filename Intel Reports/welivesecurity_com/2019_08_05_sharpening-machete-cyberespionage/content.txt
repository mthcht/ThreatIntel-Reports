    <div class="article-tags mb-2 dark big"><p class="article-tag text-capitalize">ESET Research</p></div>    <h1 class="page-headline">Sharpening the Machete</h1>    <p class="sub-title">ESET research uncovers a cyberespionage operation targeting Venezuelan government institutions</p>

                    <div class="article-body">
    <p>Latin America is often overlooked when it comes to persistent threats and groups with politically motivated targets. There is, however, an ongoing case of cyberespionage against high-profile organizations that has managed to stay under the radar. The group behind these attacks has stolen gigabytes of confidential documents, mostly from Venezuelan government organizations. It is still very active at the time of this publication, regularly introducing changes to its malware, infrastructure and spearphishing campaigns.</p>
<p>ESET has been tracking a new version of Machete (the group&rsquo;s Python-based toolset) that was first seen in April&nbsp;2018. While the main functionality of the backdoor remains the same as in previous versions, it has been extended with new features over the course of a year.</p>
<p><div class="download-widget" id="download-widget-195">

<h2>Targets</h2>
<p>From the end of March up until the end of May 2019, ESET researchers observed that there were more than 50&nbsp;victimized computers actively communicating with the C&amp;C server. This amounts to gigabytes of data being uploaded every week. More than 75% of the compromised computers were part of Venezuelan government organizations, including the military forces, education, police, and foreign affairs sectors. This extends to other countries in Latin America, with the Ecuadorean military being another organization highly targeted with the Machete malware. The distribution of this malware in these countries is shown in Figure 1.</p>
<div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2019/08/Figure-1.png"><img class="wp-image-128370" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2019/08/Figure-1.png" alt="" width="779" height="614" /></a>
<p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 1. Countries with Machete victims in 2019</em></p>
</div>
<h2>Malware operators</h2>
<p>Machete&rsquo;s operators use effective spearphishing techniques. Their long run of attacks, focused on Latin American countries, has allowed them to collect intelligence and refine their tactics over the years. They know their targets, how to blend into regular communications, and which documents are of the most value to steal. Not only does Machete exfiltrate common office suite documents, but also specialized file types used by geographic information systems (GIS) software. The group is interested in files that describe navigation routes and positioning using military grids.</p>
<p>The Machete group sends very specific emails directly to its victims, and these change from target to target. These emails contain either a link to, or an attachment of, a compressed self-extracting archive that runs the malware and opens a document that serves as a decoy.</p>
<p>Figure 2 is a typical PDF file displayed to a potential victim during compromise. To trick unsuspecting targets, Machete operators use real documents they have previously stolen; Figure 2 is a classified official document that is dated May 21<sup>st</sup>, 2019, the same day the related .zip file was first sent to targets. ESET has seen more cases like this where stolen documents dated on one particular day were bundled with malware and used on the same day as lures to compromise new victims.</p>
<div class="caption center"><img class="wp-image-128771" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2019/08/Figure-2-2.png" alt="" width="779" height="348" />
<p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 2. Decoy (PDF file) in one of the Machete downloaders (blurred)</em></p>
</div>
<p>The kind of documents used as decoys are sent and received legitimately several times a day by the group&rsquo;s targets. For example, <em>Radiogramas </em>are documents used for communication in the Venezuelan military forces. Attackers take advantage of that, along with their knowledge of military jargon and etiquette, to craft very convincing phishing emails.</p>
<h2>Main characteristics</h2>
<p>The Machete group is very active and has introduced several changes to its malware since a new version was released in April 2018. Previous versions were described by <a href="https://securelist.com/el-machete/66108/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Kaspersky in 2014</a> and <a href="https://threatvector.cylance.com/en_us/home/el-machete-malware-attacks-cut-through-latam.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Cylance in 2017</a>. In Figure 3 we show the components for the new version of the Machete malware.</p>
<div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2019/08/Figure-3.png"><img class="wp-image-128372" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2019/08/Figure-3.png" alt="" width="779" height="291" /></a>
<p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 3. Components of Machete</em></p>
</div>
<p>The first part of the attack consists of a downloader that comes as a self-extracting archive, made with <a href="https://sourceforge.net/projects/s-zipsfxbuilder/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">7z&nbsp;SFX&nbsp;Builder</a>. Once the archive is unpacked by the self-extraction code, the extractor opens a PDF or Microsoft Office file that serves as a decoy, and then runs the downloader executable from the archive. That executable is another self-extracting file that contains the actual downloader binary (a <a href="http://www.py2exe.org/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">py2exe</a> component) and a configuration file with the downloader&rsquo;s target URL as an encrypted string.</p>
<p>All download URLs we have seen are at either Dropbox or Google Docs. The files at these URLs have all been self-extracting (RAR SFX) archives containing encrypted configuration and py2exe backdoor components. Since May 2019, however, the Machete operators stopped using downloaders and started to include the decoy file and backdoor components in the same archive.</p>
<p>The py2exe binaries can be decompiled to obtain Python code. All of the components &ndash; downloaders and backdoors &ndash; are obfuscated with <a href="https://github.com/astrand/pyobfuscate" target="_blank" rel="noopener">pyobfuscate</a>. This has been used in previous versions of the malware as well. Figure 4 shows part of one of these obfuscated scripts.</p>
<div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2019/08/Figure-4.png"><img class="wp-image-128373 size-full" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2019/08/Figure-4.png" alt="" width="659" height="526" /></a>
<p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 4. Script obfuscated with pyobfuscate</em></p>
</div>
<p>Since August 2018, the Machete components have been delivered with an extra layer of obfuscation. The scripts now contain a block of zlib-compressed, base64-encoded text which, after being decoded, produces a script like the one in Figure 4. This first layer of obfuscation is produced using <a href="https://github.com/liftoff/pyminifier" target="_blank" rel="noopener">pyminifier</a> with the<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;"> -gzip</span> parameter.</p>
<h2>Backdoor components</h2>
<p>Machete&rsquo;s dropper is a RAR SFX executable. Three py2exe components are dropped: <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">GoogleCrash.exe</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Chrome.exe</span> and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">GoogleUpdate.exe</span>. A single configuration file, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">jer.dll</span>, is dropped, and it contains base64‑encoded text that corresponds to AES‑encrypted strings. A schema summarizing the components is shown in Figure 5.</p>
<div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2019/08/Figure-5.png"><img class="wp-image-128374" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2019/08/Figure-5.png" alt="" width="779" height="403" /></a>
<p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 5. Backdoor py2exe components of Machete</em></p>
</div>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">GoogleCrash.exe</span> is the main component of the malware. It schedules execution of the other two components and creates Windows Task Scheduler tasks to achieve persistence.</p>
<p>The <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Chrome.exe</span> component is responsible for collection of data from the victimized computer. It can:</p>
<ul>
<li>Take screenshots</li>
<li>Log keystrokes</li>
<li>Access the clipboard</li>
<li>AES-encrypt and exfiltrate documents</li>
<li>Detect newly inserted drives and copy files</li>
<li>Execute other binaries downloaded from the C&amp;C server</li>
<li>Retrieve specific files from the system</li>
<li>Retrieve user profile data from several browsers</li>
<li>Collect geolocation of victims and information about nearby Wi-Fi networks</li>
<li>Perform physical exfiltration to removable drives</li>
</ul>
<p>The Machete operators are interested in obtaining specific file types from their targets. Apart from Microsoft Office documents, drives are searched for:</p>
<ul>
<li>Backup files</li>
<li>Database files</li>
<li>Cryptographic keys (PGP)</li>
<li>OpenOffice documents</li>
<li>Vector images</li>
<li>Files for geographic information systems (topographic maps, navigation routes, etc.)</li>
</ul>
<p>Regarding the geolocation of victims, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Chrome.exe</span> collects data about nearby Wi-Fi networks and sends it to the <a href="https://location.services.mozilla.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Mozilla Location Service API</a>. In short, this application provides geolocation coordinates when it&rsquo;s given other sources of data such as Bluetooth beacons, cell towers or Wi-Fi access points. Then the malware takes latitude and longitude coordinates to build a Google Maps URL. Part of the code is shown in Figure 6.</p>
<div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2019/08/Figure-6.png"><img class="wp-image-128375" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2019/08/Figure-6.png" alt="" width="779" height="278" /></a>
<p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 6. Code for geolocation</em></p>
</div>
<p>The advantage of using Mozilla Location Service is that it permits geolocation without an actual GPS and can be more accurate than other methods. For example, an IP address can be used to obtain an approximate location, but it is not so accurate. On the other hand, if there is available data for the area, Mozilla Location Service can provide information such as in which building the target is located.</p>
<p>The <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">GoogleUpdate.exe</span> component is responsible for communicating with the remote C&amp;C server. The configuration to set the connection is read from the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">jer.dll</span> file: domain name, username and password. The principal means of communication for Machete is via FTP, although HTTP communication was implemented as a fallback in 2019.</p>
<p>This component uploads encrypted files to different subdirectories on the C&amp;C server, but it also retrieves specific files that have been put on the server by the Machete operators. This way, the malware can have its configuration, malicious binaries and file listings updated, but can also download and execute other binaries.</p>
<h2>In conclusion</h2>
<p>The Machete group is operating more strongly than ever, even after researchers have published technical descriptions and indicators of compromise for this malware. ESET has been tracking this threat for months and has observed several changes, sometimes within weeks.</p>
<p>At the time of this publication, the latest change introduced six backdoor components, which are no longer py2exe executables. Python scripts for malicious components, an original executable for Python&nbsp;2.7, and all libraries used are packed into a self-extracting file.</p>
<p>Various artifacts that we have seen in Machete&rsquo;s code and the underlying infrastructure lead us to think that this is a Spanish-speaking group. The presence of code to exfiltrate data to removable drives when there is physical access to a compromised computer may indicate that Machete operators could have a presence in one of the targeted countries, although we cannot be certain.</p>
<p>A full and comprehensive list of Indicators of Compromise (IoCs) can be found in the full white paper and on <a href="https://github.com/eset/malware-ioc/tree/master/machete" target="_blank" rel="noopener">GitHub</a>. ESET detects this threat as a variant of Python/Machete.</p>
<p>For a detailed analysis of the backdoor, refer to our white paper <a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2019/08/ESET_Machete.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Machete just got sharper: Venezuelan government institutions under attack</a>.</p>
