    <div class="article-tags mb-2 dark big"><p class="article-tag text-capitalize">ESET Research</p></div>    <h1 class="page-headline">No “Game over” for the Winnti Group</h1>    <p class="sub-title">The notorious APT group continues to play the video game industry with yet another backdoor</p>
    
    <div class="article-authors d-flex flex-wrap"><div class="article-author d-flex"><a href="/en/our-experts/mathieu-tartare/" title="Mathieu Tartare"><picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/mtartare.jpeg" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><img class="author-image me-3"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/mtartare.jpeg" alt="Mathieu Tartare"  /></picture></a><div class="author-text"><p><a href="/en/our-experts/mathieu-tartare/" title="Mathieu Tartare"><b>Mathieu Tartare</b></a></p></div></div><div class="article-author d-flex"><a href="/en/our-experts/martin-smolar/" title="Martin Smolár"><picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2020/05/martin.smolar-1.jpg" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><img class="author-image me-3"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2020/05/martin.smolar-1.jpg" alt="Martin Smolár"  /></picture></a><div class="author-text"><p><a href="/en/our-experts/martin-smolar/" title="Martin Smolár"><b>Martin Smolár</b></a></p></div></div></div>

        <picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x266/wls/2020/05/no-game-over-winnti-group.jpg" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x425/wls/2020/05/no-game-over-winnti-group.jpg" media="(max-width: 1120px)" /><img class="hero-image"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x700/wls/2020/05/no-game-over-winnti-group.jpg" alt="No “Game over” for the Winnti Group"  /></picture>    </div>

                    <div class="article-body">
    <p>In February 2020, we discovered a new, modular backdoor, which we named PipeMon. Persisting as a Print Processor, it was used by the Winnti Group against several video gaming companies that are based in South Korea and Taiwan and develop MMO (Massively Multiplayer Online) games. Video games developed by these companies are available on popular gaming platforms and have thousands of simultaneous players.</p>
<p>In at least one case, the malware operators compromised a victim’s build system, which could have led to a supply-chain attack, allowing the attackers to trojanize game executables. In another case, the game servers were compromised, which could have allowed the attackers to, for example, manipulate in-game currencies for financial gain.</p>
<p>The Winnti Group, active since at least 2012, is responsible for high-profile supply-chain attacks against the software industry, leading to the distribution of trojanized software (such as <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2017/09/21/cconsiderations-on-ccleaner-incident/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">CCleaner</a>, <a href="https://securelist.com/operation-shadowhammer-a-high-profile-supply-chain-attack/90380/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">ASUS LiveUpdate</a> and <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2019/03/11/gaming-industry-scope-attackers-asia/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">multiple video games</a>) that is then used to compromise more victims. Recently, ESET researchers also discovered a <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2020/01/31/winnti-group-targeting-universities-hong-kong/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">campaign of the Winnti Group targeting several Hong Kong universities</a> with ShadowPad and Winnti malware.</p>
<span class="update-block"><p>
<br /><em>About the “Winnti Group” naming: </em>
<br /><em>We have chosen to keep the name “Winnti Group” since it’s the name first used to identify it, in 2013, by Kaspersky. Since Winnti is also a malware family, we always write "Winnti Group" when we refer to the malefactors behind the attacks. Since 2013, it has been demonstrated that Winnti is only one of the many malware families used by the Winnti Group.</em>
<br /></p></span><h2>Attribution to the Winnti Group</h2>
<p>Multiple indicators led us to attribute this campaign to the Winnti Group. Some of the C&amp;C domains used by PipeMon were used by Winnti malware in previous campaigns mentioned in <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2019/10/14/connecting-dots-exposing-arsenal-methods-winnti/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">our white paper on the Winnti Group arsenal</a>. Besides, Winnti malware was also found in 2019 at some of the companies that were later compromised with PipeMon.</p>
<p>In addition to Winnti malware, a custom AceHash (a credential harvester) binary found at other victims of the Winnti Group, and signed with a well-known stolen certificate used by the group (Wemade IO), was also used during this campaign.</p>
<p>The certificate used to sign the PipeMon installer, modules and additional tools is linked to a video game company that was compromised in a <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2019/03/11/gaming-industry-scope-attackers-asia/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">supply-chain attack in late 2018 by the Winnti Group</a> and was likely stolen at that time.</p>
<p>Interestingly, PipeMon modules are installed in <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">%SYSTEM32%\spool\prtprocs\x64\</span>; this path was also used in the past to drop the <a href="https://www.crowdstrike.com/blog/in-depth-analysis-of-the-ccleaner-backdoor-stage-2-dropper-and-its-payload/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">second stage of the trojanized CCleaner</a>.</p>
<p>Additionally, compromising a software developer's build environment to subsequently compromise legitimate application is a known modus operandi of the Winnti Group.</p>
<h2>Targeted companies</h2>
<p>Companies targeted in this campaign are video game developers, producing MMO games and based in South Korea and Taiwan. In at least one case, the attackers were able to compromise the company's build orchestration server, allowing them to take control of the automated build systems. This could have allowed the attackers to include arbitrary code of their choice in the video game executables.</p>
<p>ESET contacted the affected companies and provided the necessary information to remediate the compromise.</p>
<h2>Technical analysis</h2>
<p>Two different variants of PipeMon were found at the targeted companies. Only for the more recent variant were we able to identify the first stage which is responsible for installing and persisting PipeMon.</p>
<h3>First stage</h3>
<p>PipeMon's first stage consists of a password-protected RARSFX executable embedded in the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">.rsrc</span> section of its launcher. The launcher writes the RARSFX to<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;"> setup0.exe</span> in a directory named with a randomly generated, eight-character, ASCII string located in the directory returned by <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">GetTempPath</span>. Once written to disk, the RARSFX is executed with <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">CreateProcess</span> by providing the decryption password in an argument, as follows:</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">setup0.exe -p*|T/PMR{|T2^LWJ*</span></p>
<p>Note that the password is different with each sample.</p>
<p>The content of the RARSFX is then extracted into <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">%TMP%\RarSFX0</span> and consists of the following files:</p>
<ul>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">CrLnc.dat</span> – Encrypted payload</li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Duser.dll</span> – Used for UAC bypass</li>

Further, not all these files are necessarily present in the archive, depending on the installer.</p>
<p>Once extracted,<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;"> setup.exe</span> is executed without arguments. Its sole purpose is to load <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">setup.dll</span> using <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">LoadLibraryA</span>. Once loaded, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">setup.dll</span> checks whether an argument in the format <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">–x:n</span> (where <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">n</span> is an integer) was provided; the mode of operation will be different depending on the presence of <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">n</span>. Supported arguments and their corresponding behavior are shown in Table 1. <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">setup.exe</span> is executed without arguments by the RARSFX, and checks whether it's running with elevated privileges. If not, it will attempt to obtain such privileges using <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1134/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">token impersonation</a> if the version of Windows is below Windows 7 build 7601; otherwise it will attempt different <a href="https://github.com/hfiref0x/UACME" target="_blank" rel="noopener">UAC bypass techniques</a>, allowing installation of the payload loader into one of:</p>

<h3>Persistence using Windows Print Processors</h3>
<p>The location where the malicious DLL is dropped was not chosen randomly. This is the path where <a href="https://docs.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/win32/printdocs/print-processor" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Windows Print Processors</a> are located and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">setup.dll</span> registers the malicious DLL loader as an alternative Print Processor by setting one of the following registry values:</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">HKLM\SYSTEM\ControlSet001\Control\Print\Environments\Windows x64\Print Processors\PrintFiiterPipelineSvc\Driver = “DEment.dll”</span></p>
<p>or</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">HKLM\SYSTEM\CurrentControlSet\Control\Print\Environments\Windows x64\Print Processors\lltdsvc1\Driver = “EntAppsvc.dll”</span></p>
<p>depending on the variant. Note the typo in <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">PrintFiiterPipelineSvc</span> (which has no impact on the Print Processor installation since any name can be used).</p>
<p>After having registered the Print Processor, PipeMon restarts the print spooler service (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">spoolsv.exe</span>). As a result, the malicious print process is loaded when the spooler service starts. Note that the Print Spooler service starts at each PC startup, which ensures persistence across system resets.</p>
<p>This technique is really similar to the <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1013/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Print Monitor persistence technique </a>(being used by <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2019/11/21/deprimon-default-print-monitor-malicious-downloader/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">DePriMon</a>, for example) and, to our knowledge, has not been documented previously.</p>
<p>Additionally, the encrypted payload,<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;"> CrLnc.dat</span>, extracted from the RARSFX is written to the registry at the following location, depending on the installer:</p>
<ul>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">HKLM\SOFTWARE\Microsoft\Print\Components\DC20FD7E-4B1B-4B88-8172-61F0BED7D9E8</span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">HKLM\SOFTWARE\Microsoft\Print\Components\A66F35-4164-45FF-9CB4-69ACAA10E52D</span></li>
</ul>
<p>This encrypted registry payload is then loaded, decrypted and executed by the previously registered Print Processor library. The whole PipeMon staging and persistence is shown in Figure 1.</p>

<h2>PipeMon</h2>
<p>We named this new implant PipeMon because it uses multiple named pipes for inter-module communication and according to its PDB path, the name of the Visual Studio project used by its developer is “Monitor”.</p>
<p>As mentioned previously, two different PipeMon variants were found. Considering the first variant, we couldn't retrieve the installer; thus, we don't know for sure the persistence technique that was used. But considering that this first variant of PipeMon was also located in the Print Processor directory, it's likely that the same persistence mechanism was used.</p>
<h3>Original variant</h3>
<p>PipeMon is a modular backdoor where each module is a single DLL exporting a function called <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">IntelLoader</span> and is loaded using a <a href="https://github.com/stephenfewer/ReflectiveDLLInjection/blob/master/dll/src/ReflectiveLoader.c" target="_blank" rel="noopener">reflective loading</a> technique. Each module exhibits different functionalities that are shown in Table 2.</p>
<p>The loader, responsible for loading the main modules (ManagerMain and GuardClient) is <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Win32CmdDll.dll</span> and is located in the Print Processors directory. The modules are stored encrypted on disk at the same location with inoffensive-looking names such as:</p>
<ul>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">banner.bmp</span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">certificate.cert </span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">License.hwp </span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">JSONDIU7c9djE</span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">D8JNCKS0DJE </span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">B0SDFUWEkNCj.logN</span></li>
</ul>
<p>Note that <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">.hwp</span> is the extension used by Hangul Word Processor from Hangul Office, which is very popular in South Korea.</p>
<p>The modules are RC4 encrypted and the decryption key <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Com!123Qasdz</span> is hardcoded into each module. <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Win32CmDll.dll</span> decrypts and injects the ManagerMain and GuardClient modules. The ManagerMain module is responsible for decrypting and injecting the Communication module, while the GuardClient module will ensure that the Communication module is running and reload it if necessary. An overview of how PipeMon operates is shown in Figure 2.</p>
<p>Win32CmDll.dll first tries to inject the ManagerMain and GuardClient modules into a process with one of the following names: <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">lsass.exe</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">wininit.exe</span> or <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">lsm.exe</span>. If that fails, it tries to inject into one of the registered windows services processes, excluding processes named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">spoolsv.exe</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">ekrn.exe</span> (ESET), <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">avp.exe</span> (Kaspersky) or <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">dllhost.exe</span>. As a last option, if everything else failed, it tries to use the processes <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">taskhost.exe</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">taskhostw.exe</span> or <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">explorer.exe</span>.</p>
<p>The process candidates for Communication module injection must be in the TCP connection table with either <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0.0.0.0</span> as the local address, or an ESTABLISHED connection and owning a LOCAL SERVICE token. These conditions are likely used to hide the Communication module into a process that is already communicating over the network so that the traffic from the Communication module would seem inconspicuous and possibly also whitelisted in the firewall. If no process meets the previous requirements, the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">ManagerMain</span> module tries to inject the Communication module into<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;"> explorer.exe</span>. Processes belonging to the Windows Store Apps and processes named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">egui.exe</span> (ESET) and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">avpui.exe</span> (Kaspersky) are ignored from the selection.</p>

<h4><strong>C&amp;C communication</strong></h4>
<p>The Communication module is responsible for managing communications between the C&amp;C server and the other modules via named pipes, similar to the PortReuse backdoor documented in <a href="https://github.com/stephenfewer/ReflectiveDLLInjection/blob/master/dll/src/ReflectiveLoader.c" target="_blank" rel="noopener">our white paper on the Winnti arsenal</a>.</p>
<p>Its C&amp;C address is hardcoded in the ManagerMain module and encrypted using RC4 with the hardcoded key <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Com!123Qasdz</span>. It is sent to the Communication module through a named pipe.</p>

The communication is handled with the <a href="https://github.com/ldcsaa/HP-Socket" target="_blank" rel="noopener">HP-Socket library</a>. All the messages are RC4 encrypted using the hardcoded key. If the size of the message to be transferred is greater than or equal to 4KB, it is first compressed using zlib’s Deflate implementation.</p>

<h3>Updated variant</h3>
<p>As mentioned earlier, the attackers also use an updated version of PipeMon for which we were able to retrieve the first stage described above. While exhibiting an architecture highly similar to the original variant, its code was likely rewritten from scratch.</p>
<p>The RC4 code used to decrypt the modules and strings was replaced by a simple XOR with <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0x75E8EEAF</span> as the key and all the hardcoded strings were removed. <p>Here, only the main loader (i.e. the malicious DLL installed as a Print Processor) is stored as a file on disk; the modules are stored in the registry by the installer (from the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">CrLnc.dat</span> file) and are described in Table 6.</p>

<h2>Stolen code-signing certificate</h2>
<p>PipeMon modules and installers are all signed with the same valid code-signing certificate that was likely stolen during a previous campaign of the Winnti Group. The certificate's owner revoked it as soon as they were notified of the issue.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2020/05/Figure-6-4.png"><img class="wp-image-138769 size-full" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2020/05/Figure-6-4.png" alt="" width="795" height="958" /></a><p class="caption-text"><i>Figure 6. Code-signing certificate used to sign PipeMon first stage and modules before (top) and after (bottom) revocation.</i></p></div></p>
<p>We found on a sample sharing platform other tools signed with this certificate, such as <u><a href="https://github.com/HiwinCN/HTran" target="_blank" rel="noopener">HTRan</a></u>, a connection bouncer, the <u><a href="https://github.com/kingron/s" target="_blank" rel="noopener">WinEggDrop</a></u> port scanner, Netcat, and Mimikatz which may have been used by the attackers as well.</p>
<p>Furthermore, a custom AceHash build signed with a Wemade IO stolen certificate already mentioned in <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2019/10/14/connecting-dots-exposing-arsenal-methods-winnti/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">our previous white paper</a> and usually used by the Winnti Group was found on some machines compromised with PipeMon.</p>
<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>Once again, the Winnti Group has targeted video game developers in Asia with a new modular backdoor signed with a code-signing certificate likely stolen during a previous campaign and sharing some similarities with the PortReuse backdoor. This new implant shows that the Winnti Group is still actively developing new tools using multiple open source projects; they don't rely solely on their flagship backdoors, ShadowPad and the Winnti malware.</p>
<p>We will continue to monitor new activities of the Winnti Group and will publish relevant information on our blog. For any inquiries, contact us at <em><a href="mailto:threatintel@eset.com">threatintel@eset.com</a></em>. The IoCs are also available at <a href="https://github.com/eset/malware-ioc/tree/master/winnti_group" target="_blank" rel="noopener">our GitHub repository</a>.</p>
