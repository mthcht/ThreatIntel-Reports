    <div class="article-tags mb-2 dark big"><p class="article-tag text-capitalize">ESET Research</p></div>    <h1 class="page-headline">I see what you did there: A&nbsp;look at the CloudMensis macOS spyware</h1>    <p class="sub-title">Previously unknown macOS malware uses cloud storage as its C&C channel and to exfiltrate documents, keystrokes, and screen captures from compromised Macs</p>

                    <div class="article-body">
    <p>In April 2022, ESET researchers discovered a previously unknown macOS backdoor that spies on users of the compromised Mac and exclusively uses public cloud storage services to communicate back and forth with its operators. Following analysis, we named it CloudMensis. Its capabilities clearly show that the intent of its operators is to gather information from the victims’ Macs by exfiltrating documents, keystrokes, and screen captures.</p>
<p>Apple has recently acknowledged the presence of spyware targeting users of its products and is <a href="https://www.apple.com/newsroom/2022/07/apple-expands-commitment-to-protect-users-from-mercenary-spyware/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">previewing Lockdown Mode</a> on iOS, iPadOS and macOS, which disables features frequently exploited to gain code execution and deploy malware. Although not the most advanced malware, CloudMensis may be one of the reasons some users would want to enable this additional defense. Disabling entry points, at the expense of a less fluid user experience, sounds like a reasonable way to reduce the attack surface.</p>
<p>This blogpost describes the different components of CloudMensis and their inner workings.</p>
<h2>CloudMensis overview</h2>
<p>CloudMensis is malware for macOS developed in Objective-C. Samples we analyzed are compiled for both Intel and Apple silicon architectures. We still do not know how victims are initially compromised by this threat. However, we understand that when code execution and administrative privileges are gained, what follows is a two-stage process (see Figure 1), where the first stage downloads and executes the more featureful second stage. Interestingly, this first-stage malware retrieves its next stage from a cloud storage provider. It doesn’t use a publicly accessible link; it includes an access token to download the <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">MyExecute</span> file from the drive. In the sample we analyzed, <a href="https://www.pcloud.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">pCloud</a> was used to store and deliver the second stage.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-1.-Outline-of-how-CloudMensis-uses-cloud-storage-services.png"><img class="wp-image-163706" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-1.-Outline-of-how-CloudMensis-uses-cloud-storage-services.png" alt="" width="800" height="521" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 1. Outline of how CloudMensis uses cloud storage services</em></p></div></p>
<p>Artifacts left in both components suggest they are called <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">execute</span> and <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">Client</span> by their authors, the former being the downloader and the latter the spy agent. Those names are found both in the objects’ absolute paths and ad hoc signatures.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-2.-Partial-strings-and-code-signature-from-the-downloader-component-execute.png"><img class="wp-image-163707" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-2.-Partial-strings-and-code-signature-from-the-downloader-component-execute.png" alt="" width="800" height="480" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 2. Partial strings and code signature from the downloader component, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">execute</span></em></p></div></p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-3.-Partial-strings-and-code-signature-from-the-spy-agent-component-Client.png"><img class="wp-image-163708" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-3.-Partial-strings-and-code-signature-from-the-spy-agent-component-Client.png" alt="" width="800" height="541" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 3. Partial strings and code signature from the spy agent component, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Client</span></em></p></div></p>
<p>Figures 2 and 3 also show what appear to be internal names of the components of this malware: the project seems to be called <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">BaD</span> and interestingly resides in a subdirectory named <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">LeonWork</span>. Further, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">v29</span> suggests this sample is version 29, or perhaps 2.9. This version number is also found in the configuration filename.</p>
<h2>The downloader component</h2>
<p>The first-stage malware downloads and installs the second-stage malware as a system-wide daemon. As seen in Figure 4, two files are written to disk:</p>
<ol>
<li><span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">/Library/WebServer/share/httpd/manual/WindowServer</span>: the second-stage Mach-O executable, obtained from the pCloud drive</li>
<li><span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">/Library/LaunchDaemons/.com.apple.WindowServer.plist</span>: a property list file to make the malware persist as a system-wide daemon</li>
</ol>
<p>At this stage, the attackers must already have administrative privileges because both directories can only be modified by the root user.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-4.-CloudMensis-downloader-installing-the-second-stage.png"><img class="wp-image-163709" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-4.-CloudMensis-downloader-installing-the-second-stage.png" alt="" width="800" height="259" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 4. CloudMensis downloader installing the second stage</em></p></div></p>
<h3>Cleaning up after usage of a Safari exploit</h3>
<p>The first-stage component includes an interesting method called <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">removeRegistration</span> that seems to be present to clean up after a successful Safari sandbox escape exploit. A first glance at this method is a bit puzzling considering that the things it does seem unrelated: it deletes a file called <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">root</span> from the EFI system partition (Figure 5), sends an XPC message to <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">speechsynthesisd</span> (Figure 6), and deletes files from the Safari cache directory. We initially thought the purpose of <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">removeRegistration</span> was to uninstall previous versions of CloudMensis, but further research showed that these files are used to launch sandbox and privilege escalation exploits from Safari while abusing four vulnerabilities. These vulnerabilities were discovered and <a href="https://phoenhex.re/2017-07-06/pwn2own-sandbox-escape" target="_blank" rel="noopener">well documented</a> by Niklas Baumstark and Samuel Groß in 2017. All four were patched by Apple the same year, so this distribution technique is probably not used to install CloudMensis anymore. This could explain why this code is no longer called. It also suggests that CloudMensis may have been around for many years.</p>

Decompiled code showing CloudMensis mounting the EFI partition</em></p></div></p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-6.-Sending-an-XPC-message-to-speechsynthesisd.png"><img class="wp-image-163711" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-6.-Sending-an-XPC-message-to-speechsynthesisd.png" alt="" width="800" height="244" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 6. <h2>The spy agent component</h2>
<p>The second stage of CloudMensis is a much larger component, packed with a number of features to collect information from the compromised Mac. The intention of the attackers here is clearly to exfiltrate documents, screenshots, email attachments, and other sensitive data.</p>
<p>CloudMensis uses cloud storage both for receiving commands from its operators and for exfiltrating files. It supports three different providers: pCloud, Yandex Disk, and Dropbox. The configuration included in the analyzed sample contains authentication tokens for pCloud and Yandex Disk.</p>
<h3>Configuration</h3>
<p>One of the first things the CloudMensis spy agent does is load its configuration. This is a binary structure that is 14,972 bytes long. It is stored on disk at <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">~/Library/Preferences/com.apple.iTunesInfo29.plist</span>, encrypted using a simple XOR with a generated key (see the <em>Custom encryption</em> section).</p>
<p>If this file does not already exist, the configuration is populated with default values hardcoded in the malware sample. Additionally, it also tries to import values from what seem to be previous versions of the CloudMensis configuration at:</p>
<ul>
<li><span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">~/Library/Preferences/com.apple.iTunesInfo28.plist</span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">~/Library/Preferences/com.apple.iTunesInfo.plist</span></li>
</ul>
<p>The configuration contains the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Which cloud storage providers to use and authentication tokens</li>
<li>A randomly generated bot identifier</li>
<li>Information about the Mac</li>
<li>Paths to various directories used by CloudMensis</li>
<li>File extensions that are of interest to the operators</li>
</ul>
<p>The default list of file extensions found in the analyzed sample, pictured in Figure 7, shows that operators are interested in documents, spreadsheets, audio recordings, pictures, and email messages from the victims’ Macs. The most uncommon format is perhaps audio recordings using the Adaptive Multi-Rate codec (using the <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">.amr</span> and <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">.3ga</span> extensions), which is specifically designed for speech compression. Other interesting file extensions in this list are <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">.hwp</span> and<span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;"> .hwpx</span> files, which are documents for <a href="https://office.hancom.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Hangul Office</a> (now Hancom Office), a popular word processor among Korean speakers.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-7.-File-extensions-found-in-the-default-configuration-of-CloudMensis.png"><img class="wp-image-163712" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-7.-File-extensions-found-in-the-default-configuration-of-CloudMensis.png" alt="" width="800" height="63" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 7. File extensions found in the default configuration of CloudMensis</em></p></div></p>
<h3>Custom encryption</h3>
<p>CloudMensis implements its own encryption function that its authors call <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">FlowEncrypt</span>. Figure 8 shows the disassembled function. It takes a single byte as a seed and generates the rest of the key by performing a series of operations on the most recently generated byte.  The input is XORed with this keystream. Ultimately the current byte’s value will be the same as one of its previous values, so the keystream will loop. This means that even though the cipher seems complex, it can be simplified to an XOR with a static key (except for the first few bytes of the keystream, before it starts looping).</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-8.-Disassembled-FlowEncrypt-method.png"><img class="wp-image-163713 size-full" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-8.-Disassembled-FlowEncrypt-method.png" alt="" width="417" height="652" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 8. Disassembled FlowEncrypt method</em></p></div></p>
<h3>Bypassing TCC</h3>
<p>Since the release of macOS Mojave (10.14) in 2018, access to some sensitive inputs, such as screen captures, cameras, microphones and keyboard events, are protected by a system called TCC, which stands for Transparency, Consent, and Control. When an application tries to access certain functions, macOS prompts the user whether the request from the application is legitimate, who can grant or refuse access. Ultimately, TCC rules are saved into a database on the Mac. This database is protected by System Integrity Protection (SIP) to ensure that only the TCC daemon can make any changes.</p>
<p>CloudMensis uses two techniques to bypass TCC (thus avoiding prompting the user), thereby gaining access to the screen, being able to scan removable storage for documents of interest, and being able to log keyboard events. If SIP is disabled, the TCC database (<span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">TCC.db</span>) is no longer protected against tampering. Thus, in this case CloudMensis add entries to grant itself permissions before using sensitive inputs. If SIP is enabled but the Mac is running any version of macOS Catalina earlier than 10.15.6, CloudMensis will exploit a vulnerability to make the TCC daemon (<span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">tccd</span>) load a database CloudMensis can write to. This vulnerability is known as <a href="https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2020-9934" target="_blank" rel="noopener">CVE-2020–9934</a> and was <a href="https://medium.com/@mattshockl/cve-2020-9934-bypassing-the-os-x-transparency-consent-and-control-tcc-framework-for-4e14806f1de8" target="_blank" rel="noopener">reported and described by Matt Shockley</a> in 2020.</p>
<p>The exploit first creates a new database under <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">~/Library/Application Support/com.apple.spotlight/Library/Application Support/com.apple.TCC/</span> unless it was already created, as shown in Figure 9.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-9.-Checking-it-the-illegitimate-TCC-database-file-already-exists.png"><img class="wp-image-163714" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-9.-Checking-it-the-illegitimate-TCC-database-file-already-exists.png" alt="" width="800" height="162" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 9. Checking it the illegitimate TCC database file already exists</em></p></div></p>
<p>Then, it sets the <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">HOME</span> environment variable to <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">~/Library/Application Support/com.apple.spotlight</span> using<span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;"> launchctl setenv</span>, so that the TCC daemon loads the alternate database instead of the legitimate one. Figure 10 shows how it is done using <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">NSTask</span>.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-10.-Mangling-the-HOME-environment-variable-used-by-launchd-with-launchctl-and-restarting-tccd.png"><img class="wp-image-163715" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-10.-Mangling-the-HOME-environment-variable-used-by-launchd-with-launchctl-and-restarting-tccd.png" alt="" width="800" height="411" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 10. Mangling the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">HOME</span> environment variable used by <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">launchd</span> with <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">launchctl</span> and restarting <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">tccd</span></em></p></div></p>
<h3>Communication with the C&amp;C server</h3>
<p>To communicate back and forth with its operators, the CloudMensis configuration contains authentication tokens to multiple cloud service providers. Each entry in the configuration is used for a different purpose. All of them can use any provider supported by CloudMensis. In the analyzed sample, Dropbox, pCloud, and Yandex Disk are supported.</p>
<p>The first store, called <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">CloudCmd</span> by the malware authors according to the global variable name, is used to hold commands transmitted to bots and their results. Another, which they call <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">CloudData</span>, is used to exfiltrate information from the compromised Mac. A third one, which they call <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">CloudShell</span>, is used for storing shell command output. However, this last one uses the same settings as <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">CloudCmd</span>.</p>
<p>Before it tries fetching remote files, CloudMensis first uploads an RSA-encrypted report about the compromised Mac to <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">/January/</span> on <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">CloudCmd</span>. This report includes shared secrets such as a bot identifier and a password to decrypt to-be-exfiltrated data.</p>
<p>Then, to receive commands, CloudMensis fetches files under the following directory in the <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">CloudCmd</span> storage:<span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;"> /Febrary/&lt;bot_id&gt;/May/</span>. Each file is downloaded, decrypted, and dispatched to the <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">AnalizeCMDFileName</span> method. Notice how both <em>February</em> and <em>Analyze</em> are spelled incorrectly by the malware authors.</p>
<p>The <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">CloudData</span> storage is used to upload larger files requested by the operators. Before the upload, most files are added to a password-protected ZIP archive. Generated when CloudMensis is first launched, the password is kept in the configuration, and transferred to the operators in the initial report.</p>
<h3>Commands</h3>
<p>There are 39 commands implemented in the analyzed CloudMensis sample. They are identified by a number between 49 and 93 inclusive, excluding 57, 78, 87, and 90 to 92. Some commands require additional arguments. Commands allow the operators to perform actions such as:</p>
<ul>
<li>Change values in the CloudMensis configuration: cloud storage providers and authentication tokens, file extensions deemed interesting, polling frequency of cloud storage, etc.</li>
<li>List running processes</li>
<li>Start a screen capture</li>
<li>List email messages and attachments</li>
<li>List files from removable storage</li>
<li>Run shell commands and upload output to cloud storage</li>
<li>Download and execute arbitrary files</li>
</ul>
<p>Figure 11 shows command with identifier 84, which lists all jobs loaded by <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">launchd</span> and uploads the results now or later, depending on the value of its argument.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-11.-Command-84-runs-launchctl-list-to-get-launchd-jobs.png"><img class="wp-image-163716 size-full" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-11.-Command-84-runs-launchctl-list-to-get-launchd-jobs.png" alt="" width="764" height="647" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 11. Command 84 runs <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">launchctl list</span> to get <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">launchd</span> jobs</em></p></div></p>
<p>Figure 12 shows a more complex example. Command with identifier 60 is used to launch a screen capture. If the first argument is 1, the second argument is a URL to a file that will be downloaded, stored, and executed by <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">startScreenCapture</span>. This external executable file will be saved as <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">windowserver</span> in the <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">Library</span> folder of FaceTime’s sandbox container. If the first argument is zero, it will launch the existing file previously dropped. We could not find samples of this screen capture agent.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-12.-Command-60-Start-a-screen-capture.png"><img class="wp-image-163717" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-12.-Command-60-Start-a-screen-capture.png" alt="" width="800" height="612" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 12. Command 60: Start a screen capture</em></p></div></p>
<p>It’s interesting to note that property list files to make <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">launchd</span> start new processes, such as <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">com.apple.windowServer.plist</span>, are not persistent: they are deleted from disk after they are loaded by <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">launchd</span>.</p>
<h2>Metadata from cloud storage</h2>
<p>Metadata from the cloud storages used by CloudMensis reveals interesting details about the operation. Figure 13 shows the tree view of the storage used by CloudMensis to send the initial report and to transmit commands to the bots as of April 22<sup>nd</sup>, 2022.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-13.-Tree-view-of-the-directory-listing-from-the-CloudCmd-storage.png"><img class="wp-image-163718" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-13.-Tree-view-of-the-directory-listing-from-the-CloudCmd-storage.png" alt="" width="800" height="849" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 13. <p>This metadata gave partial insight into the operation and helped draw a timeline. First, the pCloud accounts were created on January 19<sup>th</sup>, 2022. The directory listing from April 22<sup>nd</sup>  shows that 51 unique bot identifiers created subdirectories in the cloud storage to receive commands. Because these directories are created when the malware is first launched, we can use their creation date to determine the date of the initial compromise, as seen in Figure 14.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-14.-Subdirectory-creation-dates-under-Febrary-sic.png"><img class="wp-image-163719" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/07/Figure-14.-Subdirectory-creation-dates-under-Febrary-sic.png" alt="" width="800" height="365" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 14. <p>This chart shows a spike of compromises in early March 2022, with the first being on February 4<sup>th</sup>. The last spike may be explained by sandboxes running CloudMensis, once it was uploaded to VirusTotal.</p>
<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>CloudMensis is a threat to Mac users, but its very limited distribution suggests that it is used as part of a targeted operation. From what we have seen, operators of this malware family deploy CloudMensis to specific targets that are of interest to them. Usage of vulnerabilities to work around macOS mitigations shows that the malware operators are actively trying to maximize the success of their spying operations. At the same time, no undisclosed vulnerabilities (zero-days) were found to be used by this group during our research. Thus, running an up-to-date Mac is recommended to avoid, at least, the mitigation bypasses.</p>
<p>We still do not know how CloudMensis is initially distributed and who the targets are. The general quality of the code and lack of obfuscation shows the authors may not be very familiar with Mac development and are not so advanced. Nonetheless a lot of resources were put into making CloudMensis a powerful spying tool and a menace to potential targets.</p>
