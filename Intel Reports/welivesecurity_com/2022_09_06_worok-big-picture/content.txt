    <div class="article-tags mb-2 dark big"><p class="article-tag text-capitalize">ESET Research</p></div>    <h1 class="page-headline">Worok: The big picture</h1>    <p class="sub-title">Focused mostly on Asia, this new cyberespionage group uses undocumented tools, including steganographically extracting PowerShell payloads from PNG files</p>
    
    <div class="article-authors d-flex flex-wrap"><div class="article-author d-flex"><a href="/en/our-experts/thibaut-passilly/" title="Thibaut Passilly"><picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2021/08/Thibaut-Passilly.jpeg" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><img class="author-image me-3"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2021/08/Thibaut-Passilly.jpeg" alt="Thibaut Passilly"  /></picture></a><div class="author-text"><p><a href="/en/our-experts/thibaut-passilly/" title="Thibaut Passilly"><b>Thibaut Passilly</b></a></p></div></div></div>

        <picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x266/wls/2022/09/worok-apt-asia-malware.jpg" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x425/wls/2022/09/worok-apt-asia-malware.jpg" media="(max-width: 1120px)" /><img class="hero-image"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x700/wls/2022/09/worok-apt-asia-malware.jpg" alt="Worok: The big picture"  /></picture>    </div>

                    <div class="article-body">
    <p>ESET researchers recently found targeted attacks that used undocumented tools against various high-profile companies and local governments mostly in Asia. These attacks were conducted by a previously unknown espionage group that we have named Worok and that has been active since at least 2020. Worok’s toolset includes a C++ loader CLRLoad, a PowerShell backdoor PowHeartBeat, and a C# loader PNGLoad that uses steganography to extract hidden malicious payloads from PNG files.</p>
<h2>Who is Worok?</h2>
<p>During the ProxyShell (<a href="https://cve.mitre.org/cgi-bin/cvename.cgi?name=CVE-2021-34523" target="_blank" rel="noopener">CVE-2021-34523</a>) vulnerability disclosure in early 2021, we observed <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2021/03/10/exchange-servers-under-siege-10-apt-groups/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">activity from various APT groups</a>. One exhibited characteristics common with <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2020/12/10/luckymouse-ta428-compromise-able-desktop/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">TA428</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li>Activity times</li>
<li>Targeted verticals</li>
<li>Usage of ShadowPad</li>
</ul>
<p>The rest of the toolset is very different: for example, TA428 took part in the <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2020/12/10/luckymouse-ta428-compromise-able-desktop/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Able Desktop compromise</a> in 2020. We consider that the links are not strong enough to consider Worok to be the same group as TA428, but the two groups might share tools and have common interests. We decided to create a cluster and named it Worok. The name was chosen after a mutex in a loader used by the group. Further activity with variants of the same tools was then linked to this group. According to ESET’s telemetry, Worok has been active since late 2020 and continues to be active as of this writing.</p>
<p>Back in late 2020, Worok was targeting governments and companies in multiple countries, specifically:</p>
<ul>
<li>A telecommunications company in East Asia</li>
<li>A bank in Central Asia</li>
<li>A maritime industry company in Southeast Asia</li>
<li>A government entity in The Middle East</li>
<li>A private company in southern Africa</li>
</ul>
<p>There was a significant break in observed operations from 2021-05 to 2022-01, but Worok activity returned in 2022-02, targeting:</p>
<ul>
<li>An energy company in Central Asia</li>
<li>A public sector entity in Southeast Asia</li>
</ul>
<p>Figure 1 presents a visual heatmap of the targeted regions and verticals.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/08/Figure-1.-Map-of-the-targeted-regions-and-verticals.png"><img class="wp-image-164842" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/08/Figure-1.-Map-of-the-targeted-regions-and-verticals.png" alt="" width="800" height="407" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 1. Map of the targeted regions and verticals</em></p></div></p>
<p>Considering the targets’ profiles and the tools we’ve seen deployed against these victims, we think Worok’s main objective is to steal information.</p>
<h2>Technical analysis</h2>
<p>While the majority of initial accesses are unknown, in some cases through 2021 and 2022 we have seen exploits used against the ProxyShell vulnerabilities. In such cases, typically webshells have been uploaded after exploiting these vulnerabilities, in order to provide persistence in the victim’s network. Then the operators used various implants to gain further capabilities.</p>
<p>Once access had been acquired, the operators deployed multiple, publicly available tools for reconnaissance, including <a href="https://blog.gentilkiwi.com/mimikatz" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Mimikatz</a>, <a href="https://rootkiter.com/EarthWorm/en/index.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">EarthWorm</a>, <a href="https://github.com/sensepost/reGeorg" target="_blank" rel="noopener">ReGeorg</a>, and <a href="https://github.com/charlesroelli/nbtscan" target="_blank" rel="noopener">NBTscan</a>, and then deployed their custom implants: a first-stage loader, followed by a second stage .NET loader (PNGLoad). Unfortunately, we have not been able to retrieve any of the final payloads. In 2021, the first-stage loader was a CLR assembly (CLRLoad), while in 2022 it has been replaced, in most cases, by a full-featured PowerShell backdoor (PowHeartBeat) – both execution chains are depicted in Figure 2. These three tools are described in detail in the following subsections.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/08/Figure-2.-Worok-compromise-chains.png"><img class="wp-image-164843" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/08/Figure-2.-Worok-compromise-chains.png" alt="" width="800" height="403" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 2. Worok compromise chains</em></p></div></p>
<h3>CLRLoad: CLR assembly loader</h3>
<p>CLRLoad is a generic Windows PE that we have seen in both 32-and 64-bit versions. It is a loader written in C++ that loads the next stage (PNGLoad), which must be a <a href="https://docs.microsoft.com/en-us/dotnet/standard/clr" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Common Language Runtime (CLR) assembly</a> DLL file. That code is loaded from a file located on disk in a legitimate directory, presumably to mislead victims or incident responders into thinking it is legitimate software.</p>
<p>Some CLRLoad samples start by decoding the full path of the file whose content they will load as the next stage. These file paths are encoded with a single-byte XOR, with a different key in every sample. Decoded or cleartext, these file paths are absolute, with the following being those we have encountered:</p>
<ul>
<li><span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">C:\Program Files\VMware\VMware Tools\VMware VGAuth\xsec_1_5.dll</span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">C:\Program Files\UltraViewer\msvbvm80.dll</span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">C:\Program Files\Internet Explorer\Jsprofile.dll</span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">C:\Program Files\WinRar\RarExtMgt.dll</span></li>

<p>Next, a mutex is created and we’ve seen a different name in every sample. The loader checks for this mutex; if found, it exits, because the loader is already running. In one of the samples, the mutex <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">Wo0r0KGWhYGO</span> was encountered, which gave the group its name of Worok.</p>
<p>CLRLoad then loads a CLR assembly from the possibly decoded file path. As unmanaged code, CLRLoad achieves this via <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">CorBindToRuntimeEx</span> Windows API calls in 32-bit variants, or <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">CLRCreateInstance </span>calls in 64-bit variants.</p>
<h3>PowHeartBeat: PowerShell backdoor</h3>
<p>PowHeartBeat is a full-featured backdoor written in PowerShell, obfuscated using various techniques such as  compression, encoding, and encryption. Based on ESET telemetry, we believe PowHeartBeat replaced CLRLoad in more recent Worok campaigns as the tool used to launch PNGLoad.</p>
<p>The first layer of the backdoor code consists of multiple chunks of base64-encoded PowerShell code. Once the payload is reconstructed, it is executed via <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">IEX</span>. Once decoded, another layer of obfuscated code is executed, which we can see in Figure 3.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/08/Figure-3.-Excerpt-of-the-decoded-main-function-of-the-second-layer-of-PowHeartBeat.png"><img class="wp-image-164844" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/08/Figure-3.-Excerpt-of-the-decoded-main-function-of-the-second-layer-of-PowHeartBeat.png" alt="" width="800" height="352" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 3. Excerpt of the decoded main function of the second layer of PowHeartBeat</em></p></div></p>
<p>The second layer of the backdoor first base64 decodes the next layer of its code, which is then decrypted with <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple_DES" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Triple DES</a> (CBC mode). After decryption, this code is decompressed using the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gzip" target="_blank" rel="noopener">gzip</a> algorithm, thus giving the third layer of PowerShell code, which is the actual backdoor. It is divided into two main parts: configuration, and handling backdoor commands.</p>
<p>The main layer of backdoor code is also written in PowerShell and uses HTTP or ICMP to communicate with the C&amp;C server. It works as depicted in Figure 4.</p>

<h4><strong>Data encryption</strong></h4>
<p>PowHeartBeat encrypts logs and additional configuration file content.</p>
<p>Log file content is encrypted though multiple-byte XOR with a key specified in cleartext in the sample. Interestingly, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">clientId</span> is used as a salt for the index into the key array. The key is a 256-byte array, which was identical in every sample that we encountered. Additional configuration file content is encrypted through multiple-byte XOR with the value from <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">SecretKey</span> as its key.</p>
<h4><strong>C&amp;C communications</strong></h4>
<p>PowHeartBeat used HTTP for C&amp;C communications until version 2.4, and then switched to ICMP. In both case the communication is not encrypted.</p>
<h4><em>HTTP</em></h4>
<p>In an infinite loop, the backdoor sends a GET request to the C&amp;C server, asking for a command to issue. The encrypted answer is decrypted by the backdoor, which processes the command, and writes the command output to a file whose content is then sent to the C&amp;C server via a POST request.</p>
<p>The format of the GET requests is the following:</p>
<pre class="language-bash" title=""><code>GET &lt;UrlSendHeartBeat&gt; HTTP/1.1
User-Agent: &lt;UserAgent&gt;
Referer: &lt;Referer&gt;
Host: &lt;Domain&gt;
Cookie: &lt;CookieClientId&gt;=&lt;ClientId&gt;
Connection: close</code></pre>
<p>Note that the request is constructed using the eponymous configuration fields.</p>
<p>In the response from the C&amp;C server, the third byte of the content is the command identifier that indicates the command to be processed by the backdoor. We’ll call it <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">command_id</span>. The remaining content of the response will be passed as an argument to the command that is processed. This content is encrypted with the algorithm shown in Figure 6, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">taskId</span> being the value of the cookie named after <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">CookieTaskId</span>‘s value from the configuration.</p>
<pre class="language-bash" title=""><code>o[int] $pos = $taskId % 256;
for ($i = 0; $i -lt $tmpBytes.Value.Length; $i++)
{
	   $pos = $pos + $clientId;
	   if ($pos -ge 256)
	   {
			  $pos = $pos % 256;
	   }
	   $tmpBytes.Value[$i] = [byte]($tmpBytes.Value[$i] -bxor $hexEnc[$pos]);
}</code></pre>
<p><em>Figure 6. Requests content data encryption algorithm</em></p>
<p>The response from the C&amp;C server also contains another cookie, whose name is specified by the backdoor’s <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">CookieTerminalId</span> configuration variable. The value of this cookie is repeated in the POST request from the backdoor, and it must not be empty. After executing the backdoor command, PowHeartBeat sends the result as a POST request to the C&amp;C server. The result is sent as a file whose name is <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">&lt;command_id&gt;.png</span>.</p>
<h4><strong>ICMP</strong></h4>
<p>Starting from version 2.4 of PowHeartBeat, HTTP was replaced by ICMP, sent packets having a timeout of six seconds and being <a href="https://docs.microsoft.com/en-us/dotnet/api/system.net.networkinformation.pingoptions.dontfragment?view=net-6.0" target="_blank" rel="noopener">unfragmented</a>. Communication through ICMP is most likely a way to evade detection.</p>
<p>There is no major change in versions 2.4 and later, but we noticed some modifications in the code:</p>
<ul>
<li>PowHeartBeat sends a heartbeat packet at each loop that contains the string <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">abcdefghijklmnopqrstuvwxyz</span>, before requesting a command. This informs the C&amp;C server that the backdoor is ready to receive commands.</li>
<li>Requests to get commands performed by the backdoor contain the string <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">abcdefghijklmnop</span>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Heartbeat packets have the format described in Figure 7.</p>

<h3>PNGLoad: Steganographic loader</h3>
<p>PNGLoad is the second-stage payload deployed by Worok on compromised systems and, according to ESET telemetry, loaded either by CLRLoad or PowHeartBeat. While we don’t see any code in PowHeartBeat that directly loads PNGLoad, the backdoor has the capabilities to download and execute additional payloads from the C&amp;C server, which is likely how the attackers have deployed PNGLoad on systems compromised with PowHeartBeat. PNGLoad is a loader that uses bytes from PNG files to create a payload to execute. It is a 64-bit .NET executable – obfuscated with <a href="https://www.eziriz.com/dotnet_reactor.htm" target="_blank" rel="noopener">.NET Reactor</a> – that masquerades as legitimate software. For example, Figure 11 shows the CLR headers of a sample masquerading as a WinRAR DLL.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/08/Figure-11.-Example-of-a-fake-WinRAR-DLL.png"><img class="wp-image-164852" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/08/Figure-11.-Example-of-a-fake-WinRAR-DLL.png" alt="" width="750" height="321" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 11. Example of a fake WinRAR DLL</em></p></div></p>
<p>Once deobfuscated, only one class is present. In this class, there is a <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">MainPath</span> attribute containing the directory path the backdoor searches, including its subdirectories, for files with a <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">.png</span> extension, as shown in Figure 12.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/08/Figure-12.-.png-file-listing.png"><img class="wp-image-164853" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/08/Figure-12.-.png-file-listing.png" alt="" width="750" height="319" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 12. <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">.png</span> file listing</em></p></div></p>
<p>Each <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">.png</span> file located by this search of <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">MainPath</span> is then checked for steganographically embedded content. First, the least-significant bit of each pixel’s R (red), G (green), B (blue), and A (alpha) values are fetched and assembled into a buffer. Should the first eight bytes of that buffer match the magic number seen in Figure 13 and the next eight-byte value, control, be non-null, the file passes PNGLoad’s steganographic content check. For such files, processing continues with the remainder of the buffer decrypted with a multiple-byte XOR, using the key stored in PNGLoad’s <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">SecretKeyBytes</span> attribute, and then the decrypted buffer is gzip-decompressed. The result is expected to be a PowerShell script, which is run immediately.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/08/Figure-13.-Format-of-buffer-PNGLoad-creates-from-processing-.png-files.png"><img class="wp-image-164854" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/08/Figure-13.-Format-of-buffer-PNGLoad-creates-from-processing-.png-files.png" alt="" width="750" height="188" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 13. Format of buffer PNGLoad creates from processing <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">.png</span> files</em></p></div></p>
<p>Interestingly, operations performed by PNGLoad are logged in a file whose path is stored in the variable <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">LogFilePath</span>. Operations are only logged if a file is present whose path is specified by the internal variable <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">IfLogFilePath</span>.</p>
<p>We have not been able to obtain a sample <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">.png</span> file used along with PNGLoad, but the way PNGLoad operates suggests that it should work with valid PNG files. To hide the malicious payload, Worok uses Bitmap objects in C#, which only take pixel information from files, not the file metadata. This means that Worok can hide its malicious payloads in valid, innocuous-looking PNG images and thus hide in plain sight.</p>
<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>Worok is a cyberespionage group that develops its own tools, as well as leveraging existing tools, to compromise its targets. Stealing information from their victims is what we believe the operators are after because they focus on high-profile entities in Asia and Africa, targeting various sectors, both private and public, but with a specific emphasis on government entities. Activity times and toolset indicate possible ties with TA428, but we make this assessment with low confidence. Their custom toolset includes two loaders – one in C++ and one in C# .NET – and one PowerShell backdoor. While our visibility is limited, we hope that shedding light on this group will encourage other researchers to share information about this group.</p>
