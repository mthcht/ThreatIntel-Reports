    <div class="article-tags mb-2 dark big"><p class="article-tag text-capitalize">ESET Research</p></div>    <h1 class="page-headline">Unmasking MirrorFace: Operation LiberalFace targeting Japanese political entities</h1>    <p class="sub-title">ESET researchers discovered a spearphishing campaign targeting Japanese political entities a few weeks before the House of Councillors elections, and in the process uncovered a previously undescribed MirrorFace credential stealer</p>

        <picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x266/wls/2022/12/mirrorface-liberalface-japan-political-entities-malware-cyberattacks.jpg" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x425/wls/2022/12/mirrorface-liberalface-japan-political-entities-malware-cyberattacks.jpg" media="(max-width: 1120px)" /><img class="hero-image"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x700/wls/2022/12/mirrorface-liberalface-japan-political-entities-malware-cyberattacks.jpg" alt="Unmasking MirrorFace: Operation LiberalFace targeting Japanese political entities"  /></picture>    </div>

                    <div class="article-body">
    <p>ESET researchers discovered a spearphishing campaign, launched in the weeks leading up to the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_Japanese_House_of_Councillors_election" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Japanese House of Councillors election</a> in July 2022, by the APT group that ESET Research tracks as MirrorFace. The campaign, which we have named Operation LiberalFace, targeted Japanese political entities; our investigation revealed that the members of a specific political party were of particular focus in this campaign. ESET Research unmasked details about this campaign and the APT group behind it at the <a href="https://aavar.org/cybersecurity-conference/index.php/behind-the-mirrorface-mask-lodeinfo-malware-interfering-with-japanese-elections/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">AVAR 2022 conference</a> at the beginning of this month.</p>
<span class="update-block"><p><strong>Key points of the blogpost:</strong>
<ul>
<li>At the end of June 2022, MirrorFace launched a campaign, which we have named Operation LiberalFace, that targeted Japanese political entities.</li>
<li>Spearphishing email messages containing the group’s flagship backdoor LODEINFO were sent to the targets.</li>
<li>LODEINFO was used to deliver additional malware, exfiltrate the victim’s credentials, and steal the victim’s documents and emails.</li>
<li>A previously undescribed credential stealer we have named MirrorStealer was used in Operation LiberalFace.</li>
<li>ESET Research performed an analysis of the post-compromise activities, which suggests that the observed actions were carried out in a manual or semi-manual manner.</li>
<li>Details about this campaign were shared at the <a href="https://aavar.org/cybersecurity-conference/index.php/behind-the-mirrorface-mask-lodeinfo-malware-interfering-with-japanese-elections/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">AVAR 2022 conference</a>.</li>
</ul>
<br /></p></span><p>MirrorFace is a Chinese-speaking threat actor targeting companies and organizations based in Japan. While there is some speculation that this threat actor might be related to APT10 (<a href="https://www.macnica.co.jp/business/security/manufacturers/files/mpressioncss_ta_report_2019_4_en.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Macnica</a>, <a href="https://securelist.com/apt10-tracking-down-lodeinfo-2022-part-i/107742/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Kaspersky</a>), ESET is unable to attribute it to any known APT group. Therefore, we are tracking it as a separate entity that we've named MirrorFace. In particular, MirrorFace and LODEINFO, its proprietary malware used exclusively against targets in Japan, have been <a href="https://www.macnica.co.jp/business/security/manufacturers/files/mpressioncss_ta_report_2019_4_en.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">reported</a> as targeting media, defense-related companies, think tanks, diplomatic organizations, and academic institutions. The goal of MirrorFace is espionage and exfiltration of files of interest.</p>
<p>We attribute Operation LiberalFace to MirrorFace based on these indicators:</p>
<ul>
<li>To the best of our knowledge, LODEINFO malware is exclusively used by MirrorFace.</li>
<li>The targets of Operation LiberalFace align with traditional MirrorFace targeting.</li>
<li>A second-stage LODEINFO malware sample contacted a C&amp;C server that we track internally as part of MirrorFace infrastructure.</li>
</ul>
<p>One of the spearphishing emails sent in Operation LiberalFace posed as an official communication from the PR department of a specific Japanese political party, containing a request related to the House of Councillors elections, and was purportedly sent on behalf of a prominent politician. All spearphishing emails contained a malicious attachment that upon execution deployed LODEINFO on the compromised machine.</p>
<p>Additionally, we discovered that MirrorFace has used previously undocumented malware, which we have named MirrorStealer, to steal its target’s credentials. We believe this is the first time this malware has been publicly described.</p>
<p>In this blogpost, we cover the observed post-compromise activities, including the C&amp;C commands sent to LODEINFO to carry out the actions. Based on certain activities performed on the affected machine, we think that the MirrorFace operator issued commands to LODEINFO in a manual or semi-manual manner.</p>
<h2>Initial access</h2>
<p>MirrorFace started the attack on June 29<sup>th</sup>, 2022, distributing spearphishing emails with a malicious attachment to the targets. The subject of the email was &lt;redacted&gt;<span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">SNS</span>用動画 拡散のお願い (translation from Google Translate:<span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;"> [Important] &lt;redacted&gt; Request for spreading videos for SNS)</span>. Figure 1 and Figure 2 show its content.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/12/Figure-1.-Original-text-of-the-email.png"><img class="wp-image-167773 size-full" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/12/Figure-1.-Original-text-of-the-email.png" alt="" width="1468" height="341" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 1. Original text of the email</em></p></div></p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/12/Figure-2.-Translated-version.png"><img class="wp-image-167774 size-full" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/12/Figure-2.-Translated-version.png" alt="" width="1087" height="450" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 2. Translated version</em></p></div></p>
<p>Purporting to be a Japanese political party’s PR department, MirrorFace asked the recipients to distribute the attached videos on their own social media profiles (SNS – Social Network Service) to further strengthen the party’s PR and to secure victory in the House of Councillors. Furthermore, the email provides clear instructions on the videos’ publication strategy.</p>
<p>Since the House of Councillors election was held on July 10<sup>th</sup>, 2022, this email clearly indicates that MirrorFace sought the opportunity to attack political entities. Also, specific content in the email indicates that members of a particular political party were targeted.</p>
<p>MirrorFace also used another spearphishing email in the campaign, where the attachment was titled <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">【参考】220628</span>&lt;redacted&gt;発・&lt;redacted&gt;選挙管理委員会宛文書（添書分）<span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">.exe</span> (translation from Google Translate:<span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;"> [Reference] 220628 Documents from the Ministry of &lt;redacted&gt; to &lt;redacted&gt; election administration committee (appendix).exe)</span>. The attached decoy document (shown in Figure 3) references the House of Councillors election as well.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/12/Figure-3.-Decoy-document-shown-to-the-target.png"><img class="wp-image-167775 size-full" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/12/Figure-3.-Decoy-document-shown-to-the-target.png" alt="" width="909" height="1276" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 3. Decoy document shown to the target</em></p></div></p>
<p>In both cases, the emails contained malicious attachments in the form of self-extracting WinRAR archives with deceptive names &lt;redacted&gt;SNS用動画 拡散のお願い<span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">.exe</span> (translation from Google Translate: &lt;redacted&gt; <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">Request for spreading videos for SNS.exe</span>) and 【参考】<span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">220628</span>&lt;redacted&gt;発・&lt;redacted&gt;選挙管理委員会宛文書（添書分）<span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">.exe</span> (translation from Google Translate: <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">[Reference] 220628 Documents from the Ministry of &lt;redacted&gt; to &lt;redacted&gt; election administration committee (appendix).exe)</span> respectively.</p>
<p>These EXEs extract their archived content into the <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">%TEMP%</span> folder. In particular, four files are extracted:</p>
<ul>
<li><span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">K7SysMon.exe</span>, a benign application developed by K7 Computing Pvt Ltd vulnerable to DLL search order hijacking</li>
<li><span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">K7SysMn1.dll</span>, a malicious loader</li>
<li><span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">K7SysMon.Exe.db</span>, encrypted LODEINFO malware</li>
<li>A decoy document</li>
</ul>
<p>Then, the decoy document is opened to deceive the target and to appear benign. As the last step, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">K7SysMon.exe</span> is executed which loads the malicious loader <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">K7SysMn1.dll</span> dropped alongside it. Finally, the loader reads the content of <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">K7SysMon.Exe.db</span>, decrypts it, and then executes it. Note this approach was also observed by Kaspersky and described in their <a href="https://securelist.com/apt10-tracking-down-lodeinfo-2022-part-i/107742/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">report</a>.</p>
<h2>Toolset</h2>
<p>In this section, we describe the malware MirrorFace utilized in Operation LiberalFace.</p>
<h3>LODEINFO</h3>
<p>LODEINFO is a MirrorFace backdoor that is under continual development. JPCERT <a href="https://blogs.jpcert.or.jp/en/2020/02/malware-lodeinfo-targeting-japan.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">reported about the first version</a> of LODEINFO (v0.1.2), which appeared around December 2019; its functionality allows capturing screenshots, keylogging, killing processes, exfiltrating files, and executing additional files and commands. Since then, we have observed several changes introduced to each of its versions. For instance, version 0.3.8 (which we first detected in June 2020) added the command ransom (which encrypts defined files and folders), and version 0.5.6 (which we detected in July 2021) added the command <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">config</span>, which allows operators to modify its configuration stored in the registry. Besides the JPCERT reporting mentioned above, a detailed analysis of the LODEINFO backdoor was also published earlier this year by <a href="https://securelist.com/apt10-tracking-down-lodeinfo-2022-part-ii/107745/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Kaspersky</a>.</p>
<p>In Operation LiberalFace, we observed MirrorFace operators utilizing both the regular LODEINFO and what we call the second-stage LODEINFO malware. The second-stage LODEINFO can be distinguished from the regular LODEINFO by looking at the overall functionality. In particular, the second-stage LODEINFO accepts and runs PE binaries and shellcode outside of the implemented commands. Furthermore, the second-stage LODEINFO can process the C&amp;C command <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">config</span>, but the functionality for the command <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">ransom</span> is missing.</p>
<p>Finally, the data received from the C&amp;C server differs between the regular LODEINFO and the second-stage one. For the second-stage LODEINFO, the C&amp;C server prepends random web page content to the actual data. See Figure 4, Figure 5, and Figure 6 depicting the received data difference. Notice the prepended code snippet differs for every received data stream from the second-stage C&amp;C.</p>

<h3>MirrorStealer</h3>
<p>MirrorStealer, internally named <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">31558_n.dll</span> by MirrorFace, is a credential stealer. To the best of our knowledge, this malware has not been publicly described. In general, MirrorStealer steals credentials from various applications such as browsers and email clients. Interestingly, one of the targeted applications is <a href="https://www.rimarts.co.jp/becky.htm" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Becky!</a>, an email client that is currently only available in Japan. All the stolen credentials are stored in <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">%TEMP%\31558.txt</span> and since MirrorStealer doesn’t have the capability to exfiltrate the stolen data, it depends on other malware to do it.</p>
<h2>Post-compromise activities</h2>
<p>During our research, we were able to observe some of the commands that were issued to compromised computers.</p>
<h3>Initial environment observation</h3>
<p>Once LODEINFO was launched on the compromised machines and they had successfully connected to the C&amp;C server, an operator started issuing commands (see Figure 7).</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/12/Figure-7.-Initial-environment-observation-by-the-MirrorFace-operator-via-LODEINFO.png"><img class="wp-image-167779" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/12/Figure-7.-Initial-environment-observation-by-the-MirrorFace-operator-via-LODEINFO.png" alt="" width="679" height="550" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 7. Initial environment observation by the MirrorFace operator via LODEINFO</em></p></div></p>
<p>First, the operator issued one of the LODEINFO commands, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">print</span>, to capture the screen of the compromised machine. This was followed by another command, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">ls</span>, to see the content of the current folder in which LODEINFO resided (i.e., <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">%TEMP%</span>). Right after that, the operator utilized LODEINFO to obtain network information by running <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">net view</span> and <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">net view /domain</span>. The first command returns the list of computers connected to the network, while the second returns the list of available domains.</p>
<h3>Credential and browser cookie stealing</h3>
<p>Having collected this basic information, the operator moved to the next phase (see Figure 8).</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/12/Figure-8.-Flow-of-instructions-sent-to-LODEINFO-to-deploy-credential-stealer-collect-credentials-and-browser-cookies-and-exfiltrate-them-to-the-CC-server.png"><img class="wp-image-167780" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/12/Figure-8.-Flow-of-instructions-sent-to-LODEINFO-to-deploy-credential-stealer-collect-credentials-and-browser-cookies-and-exfiltrate-them-to-the-CC-server.png" alt="" width="679" height="619" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 8. Flow of instructions sent to LODEINFO to deploy credential stealer, collect credentials and browser cookies, and exfiltrate them to the C&amp;C server</em></p></div></p>
<p>The operator issued the LODEINFO command send with the subcommand <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">-memory</span> to deliver <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">MirrorStealer</span> malware to the compromised machine. The subcommand <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">-memory</span> was used to indicate to LODEINFO to keep MirrorStealer in its memory, meaning the MirrorStealer binary was never dropped on disk. Subsequently, the command <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">memory</span> was issued. This command instructed LODEINFO to take MirrorStealer, inject it into the spawned <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">cmd.exe</span> process, and run it.</p>
<p>Once MirrorStealer had collected the credentials and stored them in <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">%temp%\31558.txt</span>, the operator used LODEINFO to exfiltrate the credentials.</p>
<p>The operator was interested in the victim’s browser cookies as well. However, MirrorStealer doesn’t possess the capability to collect those. Therefore, the operator exfiltrated the cookies manually via LODEINFO. First, the operator used the LODEINFO command <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">dir</span> to list the contents of the folders <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">%LocalAppData%\Google\Chrome\User Data\</span> and <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">%LocalAppData%\Microsoft\Edge\User Data\</span>. Then, the operator copied all the identified cookie files into the <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">%TEMP%</span> folder. Next, the operator exfiltrated all the collected cookie files using the LODEINFO command <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">recv</span>. Finally, the operator deleted the copied cookie files from the <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">%TEMP%</span> folder in an attempt to remove the traces.</p>
<h3>Document and email stealing</h3>
<p>In the next step, the operator exfiltrated documents of various kinds as well as stored emails (see Figure 9).</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/12/Figure-9.-Flow-of-the-instructions-sent-to-LODEINFO-to-exfiltrate-files-of-interest.png"><img class="wp-image-167781" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/12/Figure-9.-Flow-of-the-instructions-sent-to-LODEINFO-to-exfiltrate-files-of-interest.png" alt="" width="771" height="463" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 9. Flow of the instructions sent to LODEINFO to exfiltrate files of interest</em></p></div></p>
<p>For that, the operator first utilized LODEINFO to deliver the WinRAR archiver (<span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">rar.exe</span>). Using <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">rar.exe</span>, the operator collected and archived files of interest that were modified after 2022-01-01 from the folders <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">%USERPROFILE%\ and C:\$Recycle.Bin\</span>. The operator was interested in all such files with the extensions .<span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">doc*</span>, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">.ppt*</span>, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">.xls*</span>, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">.jtd</span>, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">.eml</span>, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">.*xps</span>, and <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">.pdf</span>.</p>
<p>Notice that besides the common document types, MirrorFace was also interested in files with the <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">.jtd</span> extension. This represents documents of the Japanese word processor <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ichitaro_(word_processor)" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Ichitaro</a> developed by JustSystems.</p>
<p>Once the archive was created, the operator delivered the Secure Copy Protocol (SCP) client from the <a href="https://www.chiark.greenend.org.uk/~sgtatham/putty/latest.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">PuTTY</a> suite (<span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">pscp.exe</span>) and then used it to exfiltrate the just-created RAR archive to the server at <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">45.32.13[.]180</span>. This IP address had not been observed in previous MirrorFace activity and had not been used as a C&amp;C server in any LODEINFO malware that we have observed. Right after the archive was exfiltrated, the operator deleted <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">rar.exe</span>, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">pscp.exe</span>, and the RAR archive to clean up the traces of the activity.</p>
<h3>Deployment of second-stage LODEINFO</h3>
<p>The last step we observed was delivering the second-stage LODEINFO (see Figure 10).</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/12/Figure-10.-Flow-of-instructions-sent-to-LODEINFO-to-deploy-second-stage-LODEINFO.png"><img class="wp-image-167782" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/12/Figure-10.-Flow-of-instructions-sent-to-LODEINFO-to-deploy-second-stage-LODEINFO.png" alt="" width="771" height="550" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 10. Flow of instructions sent to LODEINFO to deploy second-stage LODEINFO</em></p></div></p>
<p>The operator delivered the following binaries: <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">JSESPR.dll</span>, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">JsSchHlp.exe</span>, and <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">vcruntime140.dll</span> to the compromised machine. The original <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">JsSchHlp.exe</span> is a benign application signed by JUSTSYSTEMS CORPORATION (makers of the previously mentioned Japanese word processor, Ichitaro). However, in this case the MirrorFace operator abused a known Microsoft digital signature verification <a href="https://docs.microsoft.com/en-us/security-updates/SecurityAdvisories/2014/2915720" target="_blank" rel="noopener">issue</a> and appended RC4 encrypted data to the <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">JsSchHlp.exe</span> digital signature. Because of the mentioned issue, Windows still considers the modified <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">JsSchHlp.exe</span> to be validly signed.</p>
<p><span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">JsSchHlp.exe</span> is also susceptible to DLL side-loading. Therefore, upon execution, the planted <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">JSESPR.dll</span> is loaded (see Figure 11).</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/12/Figure-11.-Execution-flow-of-second-stage-LODEINFO.png"><img class="wp-image-167783" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/12/Figure-11.-Execution-flow-of-second-stage-LODEINFO.png" alt="" width="772" height="404" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 11. Execution flow of second-stage LODEINFO</em></p></div></p>
<p><span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">JSESPR.dll</span> is a malicious loader that reads the appended payload from <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">JsSchHlp.exe</span>, decrypts it, and runs it. The payload is the second-stage LODEINFO, and once running, the operator utilized the regular LODEINFO to set the persistence for the second-stage one. In particular, the operator ran the <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">reg.exe</span> utility to add a value named <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">JsSchHlp</span> to the <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">Run</span> registry key holding the path to <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">JsSchHlp.exe</span>.</p>
<p>However, it appears to us the operator didn’t manage to make the second-stage LODEINFO communicate properly with the C&amp;C server. Therefore, any further steps of the operator utilizing the second-stage LODEINFO remain unknown to us.</p>
<h2>Interesting observations</h2>
<p>During the investigation, we made a few interesting observations. One of them is that the operator made a few errors and typos when issuing commands to LODEINFO. For example, the operator sent the string <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">cmd /c dir "c:\use\"</span> to LODEINFO, which most likely was supposed to be <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">cmd /c dir "c:\users\"</span>.</p>
<p>This suggests the operator is issuing commands to LODEINFO in a manual or semi-manual manner.</p>
<p>Our next observation is that even though the operator performed a few cleanups to remove traces of the compromise, the operator forgot to delete <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">%temp%\31558.txt</span> – the log containing the stolen credentials. Thus, at least this trace remained on the compromised machine and it shows us that the operator was not thorough in the cleanup process.</p>
<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>MirrorFace continues to aim for high-value targets in Japan. In Operation LiberalFace, it specifically targeted political entities using the then-upcoming House of Councillors election to its advantage. More interestingly, our findings indicate MirrorFace particularly focused on the members of a specific political party.</p>
<p>During the Operation LiberalFace investigation, we managed to uncover further MirrorFace TTPs, such as the deployment and utilization of additional malware and tools to collect and exfiltrate valuable data from victims. Moreover, our investigation revealed that the MirrorFace operators are somewhat careless, leaving traces and making various mistakes.</p>
