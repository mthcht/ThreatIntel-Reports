February-March 2026, Bitdefender Labs identified and mapped a sprawling global scam infrastructure and scalable disinformation-for-profit network that uses trusted news brands, real personalities, fabricated media narratives, emotional hooks, and advanced evasion techniques to drive victims into investment fraud funnels.</p><p>On <strong>February 9-March 5, 2026</strong>, we analyzed <strong>310 malvertising campaigns </strong>distributed through paid advertising on Meta platforms.</p><h2 id="key-findings">Key findings:</h2><ul><li>This is a global, coordinated investment scam ecosystem spanning at least 25 countries across &nbsp;Europe, North America, South America, Asia, Oceania, and Africa.The narratives vary, but the financial objective is consistent: drive users into deposit-based investment fraud funnels.</li><li>Bitdefender Labs researchers Alecsandru Daj and Alexandra Dinulica uncovered <strong>310 coordinated scam campaigns </strong>documenting<strong> over 26,000 ad sightings </strong>with <strong>localized content in 15+ languages. </strong>The campaigns are best described<strong> as three distinct but structurally identical scam sub-campaigns</strong>&nbsp;operated by what appears to be at least&nbsp;<strong>two to three separate threat actor groups</strong>&nbsp;using the same scam playbook, combined with a smaller&nbsp;<strong>fourth independent sub-campaign</strong>.</li><li>Most of the documented variants ultimately pivot to investment scams.<br>Whether the entry point is a fake broadcast scandal, a celebrity will revelation, or a “national investment platform,” the goal is lead generation by harvesting user data (name, email, phone) for fraudulent purposes.</li><li>Advanced moderation evasion techniques are embedded in the infrastructure.<br>Observed tactics include:<ul><li>Whitelisted domain preview abuse (e.g., legitimate news and google.com)</li><li>Fake media domain farms</li><li>Cyrillic homoglyph substitution to bypass filters</li></ul></li><li>Russian-language operational signals appear across multiple European scam campaigns. Internal campaign metadata and shared buyer identifiers indicate a Russian-speaking affiliate or management layer coordinating parts of the infrastructure. No state-sponsored attribution evidence was observed.</li><li>The structure strongly suggests a modular affiliate or franchise model.<br>A shared toolkit and playbook appear to be distributed to region-specific operators, allowing localized deployment while maintaining consistent monetization funnels.</li><li>This infrastructure is active and adaptable. Creative churn, domain rotation, and cross-regional technique migration indicate the ecosystem is evolving rather than static.</li></ul><p><strong>&nbsp;</strong></p><h2 id="an-investment-scam-network-dressed-as-news">An Investment Scam Network Dressed as News</h2><p>In at least <strong>25 countries on 6 continents</strong>, we have documented coordinated scam ad campaigns that:</p><ul><li>Impersonate major media outlets</li><li>Use the names of real public figures</li><li>Fabricate “exclusive scandals”</li><li>Pivot to investment opportunities</li><li>Collect financial deposits</li><li>Promise fast returns</li></ul><p>These fake narratives are used as bait. The real objective is <strong>investment fraud</strong>, through high-risk trading platforms, binary options type schemes, crypto schemes, and direct deposit funnels. Many campaigns share <strong>UTM and pixel signatures</strong>, overlapping infrastructure, and coordinated launch timing, showing this is a single, scalable architecture with regional variants. The campaigns are <strong>not</strong> a single scam campaign but multiple sub-campaigns that appear as various “offers” built from the same components, likely run by <strong>two or three operator groups</strong> using a shared playbook.</p><p>Across these sub-campaigns, the end destination is consistent: <strong>lead-generation pages</strong> that collect details for follow-on contact and pressure tactics typical of investment fraud funnels.</p><h2 id="the-scam-funnel-explained">The scam funnel explained</h2><p>Most variants follow the exact pattern:</p><ol><li><strong>You see a sponsored post on Facebook</strong> that looks like a scandal clip, an exposé, or a “deleted interview.” (All campaigns in the global analysis use <strong>Facebook paid ads</strong>).</li><li><strong>The ad appears to point to a trusted site</strong> (sometimes a real one, sometimes a convincing clone).</li><li><strong>A redirect chain silently moves you</strong> from that “safe-looking” preview to a suspicious destination.</li><li><strong>A fake news article or dramatic narrative “warms you up,”</strong> then pushes you to “register,” “unlock access,” or “start earning.”</li><li><strong>You submit details</strong>, such as name, phone, email, and sometimes more, and the investment pressure begins. Once a victim hands over their details, they typically become a <strong>lead in a call-center-driven investment scam</strong>. From that point, several things can happen:</li></ol><ul><li>A “broker” calls within minutes or hours.</li><li>The caller claims to represent a trading platform.</li><li>The victim is encouraged to deposit a minimum amount.</li><li>A fake dashboard shows fabricated early “profits.”</li><li>The victim is pressured to increase deposits.</li><li>Withdrawal becomes difficult or impossible.</li></ul><p>&nbsp;</p><p><strong>Important Note: </strong>Some ad variants redirect users to cloned websites or fraudulent online shops, potentially enabling data harvesting, extortion, or other malicious activities. Additionally, threat actors may pre-stage or deliberately prepare certain websites to support future malicious campaigns, designing this infrastructure for reuse in follow-on attacks or broader fraudulent operations.</p><h2 id="global-narrative-templates-used-in-the-scam">Global Narrative Templates Used in the Scam</h2><p>The scam operation uses several narrative archetypes, each tailored to a regional context but all with the same monetization funnel:</p><ol><li><strong>Live TV Scandal Confrontations</strong></li><li><strong>Celebrity Wills &amp; Final Revelations</strong></li><li><strong>National Investment Platform Scams</strong></li><li><strong>Other Emotional, Urgent, ‘Watch Before It’s Taken Down’ Hooks</strong></li></ol><p>Each narrative is localizable, reusable, and emotionally compelling – precisely what makes them effective on social platforms.</p><h2 id="three-primary-scam-campaign-archetypes">Three Primary Scam Campaign Archetypes </h2><p><strong>(Across 310 Scam Campaigns)</strong></p>

The actors did not simply post fake content, but designed infrastructure specifically to survive automated moderation, including:</p><p><strong>1. Whitelisted Domain ‘Sandwiching’</strong></p><p>Ads displayed trusted preview domains of major news outlets, retail brands, and even google.com while the actual click routed users through redirect chains to investment scam landing pages.</p><p>To both users and moderation systems, the ad looked legitimate at first. The trusted domain acted as a credibility shield, masking the true destination.</p><p><strong>2. Media Brand Spoofing &amp; Domain Squatting</strong></p><p>Operators registered convincing lookalike domains mimicking national media brands. Examples included multiple fake Swiss Blick domains, Belgian Le Soir lookalikes, Polish news typosquats and Spanish El Mundo clone networks.</p><p>These domains were visually credible and designed to withstand superficial inspection while routing traffic to financial lead-generation pages.</p><p><strong>Italy’s Restaurant Cloaking Technique</strong></p><p>The scam operation targeting Italy introduced a different evasion layer. Instead of spoofing media brands, the campaign operators used legitimate restaurant websites from Florence as preview URLs. To automated systems, the URL pointed to a real restaurant page. But when a user clicked, traffic was silently redirected to investment scam infrastructure. While other countries forged media identities, the Italian operation borrowed real businesses as camouflage.</p><p><strong>3. Homoglyph (Character Substitution) Obfuscation</strong></p><p>In several malvertising campaigns, Cyrillic characters were substituted for Latin letters in ad copy.</p><p>To the human eye, the text looked normal. To automated keyword filters, the string was technically different, allowing flagged terms to bypass detection. This also signals a deliberate moderation awareness.</p><p><strong>4. Rotating Facebook Page Entities</strong></p><p>Instead of using a single page, operators rotated multiple low-credibility or generic Facebook pages to distribute ad spend and reduce enforcement exposure. This fragmentation complicates attribution and slows takedown efforts.</p><h2 id="how-to-stay-safe">How to stay safe</h2><p><strong>Our investigation </strong>shows that modern investment scams no longer look like obvious fraud. Instead, they may start with a breaking news story, live TV scandal, or fake official national investment programs. If you see a shocking confrontation involving a banker, politician, journalist, or celebrity, especially in a sponsored social media ad — pause before clicking.</p><p><strong>1. Don’t trust ‘breaking news’ inside social media ads</strong></p><p>If a public figure truly stormed off live TV, it would be covered widely by multiple legitimate outlets.</p><p>Search directly for the story on:</p><ul><li>The broadcaster’s official website</li><li>Verified social media accounts</li><li>Multiple established news organizations</li></ul><p>If it only exists in a sponsored post, it’s almost certainly a scam.</p><p><strong>2. Be suspicious of ‘deleted interview’ or ‘watch before it’s removed’ claims</strong></p><p>Be cautious of ads using psychological triggers like:</p><ul><li>“Watch before it’s taken down”</li><li>“They tried to silence this”</li><li>“You won’t see this on TV again”</li></ul><p><strong>3. Look closely at the website address</strong></p><p>Even if the preview looks legitimate:</p><ul><li>Check the full URL in the address bar</li><li>Watch for slight spelling changes (e.g., extra letters, swapped characters)</li><li>Be cautious of unfamiliar domain endings (.pro, .top, .info, .xyz)</li></ul><p>In this campaign, trusted news brands were impersonated through lookalike domains</p><p><strong>4. Never deposit money because of a news story</strong></p><p>Stop immediately if a “news article” suddenly shifts into:</p><ul><li>“Here’s how to start earning”</li><li>“Minimum deposit required”</li><li>“AI trading program”</li><li>“Government-backed investment opportunity”</li></ul><p>Legitimate banks, journalists, and public officials do not promote private investment platforms through viral TV confrontations.</p><p><strong>5. Treat celebrity and political endorsements with caution</strong></p><p>If a public figure appears to endorse a trading platform:</p><ul><li>Verify the endorsement independently.</li><li>Check the official website or verified social media of that person.</li><li>Be aware that deepfakes and fabricated interviews are increasingly common.</li></ul><p><strong>6. Remember: Sponsored does not mean verified</strong></p><p>Paid ads on major platforms can still be fraudulent.</p><p>The presence of a sponsored label does not guarantee legitimacy.</p><p>Scammers use advertising tools to amplify reach and target specific regions, just like legitimate marketers.</p><p><strong>7. Protect yourself against investment fraud pressure tactics</strong></p><p>After submitting contact details, victims often receive:</p><ul><li>Repeated calls from “account managers”</li><li>Pressure to increase deposits</li><li>Claims of guaranteed returns</li><li>Fake dashboards showing fabricated profits</li></ul><p>If someone pressures you to invest quickly, it’s a red flag.</p><h2 id="add-an-extra-layer-of-protection">Add an Extra Layer of Protection</h2><ul><li><strong>Check suspicious investment pitches with </strong><a href="https://www.bitdefender.com/en-us/consumer/scamio"><strong>Bitdefender Scamio</strong></a><strong> for free</strong><br>If you see a “shocking” news story tied to a trading platform, paste the link, message, or screenshot into Scamio.
