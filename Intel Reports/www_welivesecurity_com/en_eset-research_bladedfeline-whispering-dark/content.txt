                    <div class="article-body">
    <p>In 2024, ESET researchers discovered several malicious tools in the systems used by Kurdish and Iraqi government officials. The APT group behind the attacks is BladedFeline, an Iranian threat actor that has been active since at least 2017, when it compromised officials within the Kurdistan Regional Government (KRG). This group develops malware for maintaining and expanding access within organizations in Iraq and the KRG. While this is our first blogpost covering BladedFeline, we discovered the group in 2023, after it targeted Kurdish diplomatic officials with the Shahmaran backdoor, and previously reported on its activities in ESET APT Activity reports <a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/en/papers/threat-reports/eset-apt-activity-report-q4-2023-q1-2024.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Q4 2023-Q1 2024</a> and <a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/en/papers/threat-reports/eset-apt-activity-report-q2-2024-q3-2024.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Q2 2024-Q3 2024</a>.</p>
<p>The array of tools utilized in the recent campaign shows that since deploying Shahmaran, BladedFeline has continued to develop its arsenal. We found two reverse tunnels, a variety of supplementary tools, and most notably, a backdoor that we named Whisper and a malicious IIS module we dubbed PrimeCache. Whisper is a backdoor that logs into a compromised webmail account on a Microsoft Exchange server and uses it to communicate with the attackers via email attachments. PrimeCache also serves as a backdoor: it is a malicious IIS module related to what we referred to as Group 2 in our 2021 paper <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2021/08/06/anatomy-native-iis-malware/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Anatomy of native IIS malware</a>. Significantly, PrimeCache also bears similarities to the RDAT backdoor used by the Iran-aligned OilRig APT group.</p>
<p>Based on these code similarities, as well as on further evidence presented in this blogpost, we assess with medium confidence that BladedFeline is a subgroup of OilRig, an Iran-aligned APT group going after governments and businesses in the Middle East. We have previously <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/en/eset-research/oilrigs-outer-space-juicy-mix-same-ol-rig-new-drill-pipes/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">reported</a> on other <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/en/eset-research/oilrig-persistent-attacks-cloud-service-powered-downloaders/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">activity</a> linked to OilRig. To avoid confusion, we have since refined our OilRig tracking, and we now track both of those operations under a separate subgroup &ndash; Lyceum &ndash; within OilRig.</p>
<p>BladedFeline has worked consistently to maintain illicit access to Kurdish diplomatic officials, while simultaneously exploiting a regional telecommunications provider in Uzbekistan, and developing and maintaining access to officials in the government of Iraq. This blogpost details the technical aspects of the initial implants delivered to BladedFeline&rsquo;s targets, the links between the victims, and lays the groundwork for associating this subgroup with OilRig.</p>

<h2>BladedFeline overview</h2>
<p>BladedFeline is an Iran-aligned cyberespionage group, active since at least 2017 according to ESET telemetry. We discovered the group in 2023 when it deployed its Shahmaran backdoor against Kurdish diplomatic officials. Shahmaran, named after a mythical half-snake, half-woman creature from Iranian folklore, is a 64-bit portable executable that we found in the target&rsquo;s Startup directory. This simple backdoor doesn&rsquo;t use any compression or encryption for network communications. After checking in with the C&amp;C server, the backdoor executes any operator commands provided, which include uploading and downloading additional files, requesting specific file attributes and providing file and directory manipulation API.</p>
<p>As evidenced by the campaign toolset we describe in this blogpost; since deploying Shahmaran, BladedFeline has continued to develop its malware in order to retain and even further extend its access to the KRG and to high levels within the government of Iraq (GOI). We uncovered the campaign in 2024 after finding BladedFeline&rsquo;s Whisper backdoor, PrimeCache IIS backdoor, and a set of post-compromise tools in the networks of Kurdish diplomatic officials, Iraqi government officials, and a regional telecommunications provider in Uzbekistan.</p>
<p>We detected and collected one version of Whisper and found another on VirusTotal, uploaded by a user in Iraq. They are virtually identical, and we were able to determine the likely identity of the VirusTotal uploader, based on data in the Whisper sample and other samples uploaded under the same submitter ID. PrimeCache, Flog (a webshell), and Hawking Listener (an early-stage implant that listens on a specified port) were all uploaded to VirusTotal by the same submitter ID who uploaded the Whisper samples. Based on the Whisper link and the close timeframe (both were uploaded within a matter of minutes) we believe it was deployed by BladedFeline to a victim in Iraq&rsquo;s government. Some of the tools mentioned below in the <em>Timeline</em> are discussed later in the report (e.g., Slippery Snakelet).</p>

<h3>Attribution</h3>
<p>Our attribution of this campaign to BladedFeline is based on the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>The campaign targets members of the KRG, as have previous attacks conducted by BladedFeline.</li>
<li>The original attack activity targeting the KRG organization allowed us to identify successive malware, as BladedFeline has attempted to maintain and expand access to the organization.</li>
<li>Further analysis of the attacks led us to identify the telecommunications victim in Uzbekistan.</li>
<li>At the same time, looking into the Whisper backdoor helped us identify the GOI victim.</li>
</ul>
<p>We assess that BladedFeline is targeting the KRG and the GOI for cyberespionage purposes, with an eye toward maintaining strategic access to high-ranking officials in both governmental entities. The KRG&rsquo;s diplomatic relationship with Western nations, coupled with the oil reserves in the Kurdistan region, makes it an enticing target for Iran-aligned threat actors to spy on and potentially manipulate. In Iraq, these threat actors are most probably trying to counter the influence of Western governments following the US invasion and occupation of the country.</p>
<p>We believe with medium confidence that BladedFeline is a subgroup of OilRig:</p>
<ul>
<li>As does OilRig, BladedFeline targets organizations in the Middle East with the purpose of cyberespionage.</li>
<li>We have found OilRig tools (VideoSRV and RDAT) in a compromised KRG system.</li>
<li>BladedFeline&rsquo;s malicious IIS module PrimeCache shares code similarities with OilRig&rsquo;s RDAT.</li>
</ul>
<p>BladedFeline is not the only subgroup of OilRig that we are monitoring: we have already been tracking Lyceum, also known as HEXANE or Storm-0133, as another OilRig subgroup. Lyceum focuses on targeting various Israeli organizations, including governmental and local governmental entities and organizations in healthcare. Major tools we attribute to Lyceum include <a href="https://vblocalhost.com/uploads/VB2021-Kayal-etal.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">DanBot</a>, the <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20230129145433/https:/www.prevailion.com/latest-targets-of-cyber-group-lyceum/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Shark, Milan</a>, and Marlin backdoors, <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/en/eset-research/oilrigs-outer-space-juicy-mix-same-ol-rig-new-drill-pipes/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Solar and Mango</a>, <a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/en/papers/threat-reports/eset-apt-activity-report-q2-2023-q3-2023.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">OilForceGTX</a>, and a <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/en/eset-research/oilrig-persistent-attacks-cloud-service-powered-downloaders/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">variety of downloaders</a> using legitimate cloud services for C&amp;C communication.</p>
<p>We will continue to use the name OilRig to refer to the parent group, also known as APT34 or Hazel Sandstorm (formerly EUROPIUM). OilRig is a cyberespionage group that has been active since at least 2014 and <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/groups/G0049/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">is commonly believed</a> to be based in Iran. The group targets Middle Eastern governments and a variety of business verticals, including chemical, energy, finance, and telecommunications. Notable OilRig campaigns include the <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/2018/11/dnspionage-campaign-targets-middle-east.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">2018</a> and <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/2019/04/dnspionage-brings-out-karkoff.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">2019</a> DNSpionage campaign, targeting victims in Lebanon and the United Arab Emirates; the 2019&ndash;2020 <a href="https://www.mandiant.com/resources/hard-pass-declining-apt34-invite-to-join-their-professional-network" target="_blank" rel="noopener">HardPass</a> campaign, using LinkedIn to target Middle Eastern victims in the energy and government sectors; <a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/oilrig-novel-c2-channel-steganography/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">the 2020 attack</a> against a telecommunications organization in the Middle East using the RDAT backdoor; and the 2023 attacks targeting organizations in the Middle East with the <a href="https://symantec-enterprise-blogs.security.com/blogs/threat-intelligence/crambus-middle-east-government" target="_blank" rel="noopener">PowerExchange</a> and <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/en_us/research/23/b/new-apt34-malware-targets-the-middle-east.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">MrPerfectionManager</a> backdoors.</p>
<h4>OilRig tools used by BladedFeline</h4>
<p>We have found two OilRig tools on the KRG machines compromised by BladedFeline.</p>
<h5>RDAT<a id="RDAT"></a></h5>
<p>We discovered a previously unreported version of the OilRig backdoor RDAT on two KRG victim systems. Analyzing RDAT, we found that the operational flow (see <a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/oilrig-novel-c2-channel-steganography/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Unit 42&rsquo;s report</a> for specifics), compilation timestamp (2017-12-26 10:49:35), and file write time (2018-01-30) align with OilRig activity and targeting, particularly with regard to the group&rsquo;s 2017 activity. We observed a file with an SHA-1 of <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">562E1678EC8FDC1D83A3F73EB511A6DDA08F3B3D</span> and a path of <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">C:\Windows\System32\LogonUl.exe</span> on both systems. The PDB path also corroborates that this binary is RDAT: <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">C:\Users\Void\Desktop\RDAT\client\x64\Release\client.pdb</span>. To date, we have only ever observed RDAT in use by OilRig. Moreover, we have not seen any custom implant sharing between OilRig and other Middle Eastern groups, and it seldom occurs between Iran-aligned threat actors.</p>
<p>Further bolstering the case that BladedFeline is an OilRig subgroup, as with Lyceum, is the analysis linking RDAT with PrimeCache, a malicious IIS module that was uploaded to VirusTotal presumably by the GOI victim. This link is explored in more depth in the <em><a href="#Links with OilRig">Links with OilRig</a></em> section of the blogpost.</p>
<h5>VideoSRV</h5>
<p>One additional data point on the OilRig and BladedFeline connection is a reverse shell deployed to one of the KRG victims (September 21<sup>st</sup>, 2017) prior to RDAT getting dropped on the same system (January 30<sup>th</sup>, 2018). VideoSRV (SHA-1: <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">BE0AD25B7B48347984908175404996531CFD74B7</span>), so named for its filename videosrv.exe, has the PDB string <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">C:\Users\v0id\Desktop\reverseShell\clientProxy\x64\Release\ConsoleApplication1.pdb</span>, which bears some similarities to the RDAT PDB string <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">C:\Users\Void\Desktop\RDAT\client\x64\Release\client.pdb</span>.</p>
<h2>Technical analysis</h2>
<h3>Initial access</h3>
<p>It is still unclear how BladedFeline is developing access to its victims. What we know is that in the case of the KRG victims, the threat actors obtained access at least as far back as 2017 and have maintained it ever since. As for the GOI victims, we suspect that the group exploited a vulnerability in an application on an internet-facing web server, which allowed them to deploy the Flog webshell.</p>
<h3>Toolset</h3>
<h4>PrimeCache &ndash; malicious IIS module</h4>
<p>PrimeCache, whose name we derived from the <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/cpp/cpp/run-time-type-information?view=msvc-170" target="_blank" rel="noopener">RTTI</a> <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">AVRSAPrimeSelector</span> and its filename (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">cachehttp.dll</span>), is a passive backdoor implemented as a native IIS module with an internal name of <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">HttpModule.dll</span>. It was uploaded to VirusTotal by the same user who uploaded one of the Whisper backdoor samples. It is a 64-bit C++ DLL with a compilation timestamp of 2023-05-14 06:55:52 and has a minimized PDB string of just <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">HttpModule.pdb</span>. It has a single export: <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">RegisterModule</span>.</p>
<p>PrimeCache is a successor to a collection of unattributed IIS backdoors that we have previously reported as Group 2 (simple IIS backdoors) in our 2021 blogpost, <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2021/08/06/anatomy-native-iis-malware/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Anatomy of native IIS malware</a>. We obtained those original samples from VirusTotal where they were uploaded by users from Bahrain, Israel, and Pakistan, between 2018 and 2020. Based solely on the location of the presumed victims, it is possible that those cases were also related to BladedFeline &ndash; or, more broadly, OilRig &ndash; activities.</p>
<h5>Main functionality</h5>
<p>PrimeCache&rsquo;s main functionality is implemented in the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">CGlobalModule::OnGlobalPreBeginRequest</span> handler. This is a unique implementation, differing from its predecessors, which used the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">CHttpModule::OnBeginRequest</span> handler. PrimeCache filters incoming HTTP requests, only processing those from the BladedFeline operators, which are recognized by having a <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">cookie</span> header with the structure:</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">F=&lt;command_ID&gt;,&lt;param&gt;;</span></p>
<p>Note that this value can be standalone or embedded into a longer cookie, surrounded by semicolon (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">;</span>) characters.</p>
<p>The backdoor works in an unusual way (new with this version as compared with our 2021 analysis). Rather than accepting a backdoor command and all its parameters within a single HTTP request, each action is split into multiple requests. First, the BladedFeline operator sends an individual request for each single parameter; these parameters are stored in a global structure. Then the operator sends another request to trigger the backdoor command. Finally, PrimeCache uses the previously received parameters to execute the specified action, and then clears the cached parameters.</p>

<h5>Encryption<a id="Encryption"></a></h5>
<p>Similar to its predecessors, PrimeCache uses both RSA and AES-CBC for its C&amp;C communication. The parameters and the return values are always AES-CBC encrypted using the session key, then base64 encoded. The session key is RSA encrypted; the backdoor has a hardcoded private and public RSA key (not a pair) to handle both directions of the communication.</p>
<p>A statically linked Crypto++ library is used to handle the encryption and decryption operations.</p>
<h5>C&amp;C communications</h5>
<p>Operator commands are transmitted in the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">cookie</span> header (another deviation from earlier versions, which used the URL or the HTTP request body). PrimeCache responses are added to the HTTP response body. If a file is being exfiltrated, the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Content-Type</span> header is set to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">attachment</span>, matching the functionality of the previous versions.</p>
<p>The PrimeCache predecessors also used the same encryption scheme, and similar parameter names (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">a</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">c</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">f</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">k</span>), but all were sent to the backdoor in a single request. The only supported commands were <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">r</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">u</span>, and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">d</span>.</p>
<h5>Links with OilRig<a id="Links with OilRig"></a></h5>
<p>When we compare PrimeCache with RDAT, as described in the <em><a href="#RDAT">RDAT</a></em> attribution subsection, we see several similarities that support our supposition that BladedFeline is a subgroup of OilRig.</p>
<ul>
<li>Both RDAT and PrimeCache use the Crypto++ library, and both parse the backdoor commands using the regular expression <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">[^,]+</span>.
<ul>
<li>The payload attempts to parse the decrypted cleartext using the regular expression <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">[^,]+</span> to get the command value and the command arguments that are split with a comma.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Both share a function, shown in Figure 1, that executes a shell command and reads the output, which, across our corpus, is found only in these two pieces of malware.</li>
</ul>

<h4>Whisper backdoor</h4>
<p>Whisper is a 32-bit Windows binary written in C#/.NET, named after its PDB strings <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">G:\csharp\Whisper_Trojan_winform\Whisper_Trojan_winform\Whisper_Trojan_winform\obj\Release\Veaty.pdb</span> and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Z:\csharp\Whisper_Trojan_winform_for_release\Whisper_Trojan_winform\Whisper_Trojan_winform\obj\Release\Veaty.pdb</span>. It uses a Microsoft Exchange server to communicate with the attackers by sending email attachments via a compromised webmail account. We have seen two versions of the backdoor: we detected and collected one version, and was uploaded to VirusTotal from Iraq. These samples are virtually identical, but we were able to determine the likely identity of the VirusTotal uploader based on data in the Whisper sample and other samples uploaded by that user.</p>
<p>Both these versions of Whisper have timestomped compilation timestamps (2090-04-11 23:38:14 and 2080-12-11 03:50:47). They are built using <a href="https://github.com/Fody/Costura" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Costura</a>, presumably to ensure that the victim&rsquo;s system uses the DLLs packaged with the binary and not DLLs in the <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/dotnet/framework/app-domains/gac" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Global Assembly Cache</a>.</p>
<p>Whisper&rsquo;s operation is not the first time we have observed an OilRig subgroup using cloud services for its C&amp;C protocol. While, unlike with Whisper, there were no emails actually being sent, Lyceum used email drafts for communication between its malware and operators throughout 2022, as we described in a <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/en/eset-research/oilrig-persistent-attacks-cloud-service-powered-downloaders/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">previous blogpost</a>.</p>
<h5>Operational workflow</h5>
<p>Whisper does not require or accept any arguments. Instead, its dropper &ndash; which we dubbed Whisper Protocol after its filename, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Protocol.pdf.exe</span> &ndash; writes its configuration file to disk alongside it (see the <em><a href="#Whisper Protocol">Whisper Protocol</a> </em>section). The config file, shown in Figure 2, is in XML format with its key and value strings base64 encoded. It is called by the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Specs</span> class of Whisper, which uses a function &ndash; <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">DelockItems</span> &ndash; to base64 decode the config variables.</p>

Whisper configuration file with its base64-encoded elements (left) and decoded (right)" width="" height="" />

<p>Figure 3 shows the operational flow of Whisper, which we detail in the following paragraphs.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 3. Basic operational flow of Whisper" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2025/05-25/bladedfeline/figure-3.png" alt="Figure 3. Basic operational flow of Whisper" width="" height="" />

<p>Whisper&rsquo;s operational flow can be broken down into seven steps:</p>
<p>In Step 1, Whisper uses the credentials from the config file (line 15 in Figure 2) and the <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/dotnet/api/microsoft.exchange.webservices.data.exchangeservice?view=exchange-ews-api" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Microsoft Exchange Web Services</a> class <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">ExchangeService</span> to attempt to log into compromised webmail accounts. Once Whisper successfully logs into an account, it saves the credentials in memory and writes the following to the log file <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">c:\Windows\Temp\WindowsEventLogs.txt</span>:</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">------------ ItemContext is set: username [&lt;username&gt;] , use_defaultCred: [credentials&gt;]</span></p>
<p>If no credentials in the config file are valid, Whisper logs the following error messages to the log file:</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">---------------------------------- there was No Way to access any MailBox.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">__________ Extraction function is called.</span></p>
<p>If an unexpected error is caught, Whisper writes the following to the log file (note the misspelling of the word <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">happened</span>, indicative of a non-native English speaker) and exits using the <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/dotnet/api/system.environment.exit?view=net-8.0" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Environment.Exit(Int32)</a> method. Strangely, the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">exitCode</span> used, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0</span>, indicates that the process completed successfully.</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">----------------------------------__ an unknown Exception happend. program turned off</span></p>
<p>Next, in Step 2, Whisper uses the credentials from the previous step to check for inbox rules using the <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/dotnet/api/microsoft.exchange.webservices.data.exchangeservice.getinboxrules?view=exchange-ews-api" target="_blank" rel="noopener">ExchangeService.GetInboxRules</a> method (which <em>[r]etrieves a collection of Inbox rules that are associated with the specified user</em>). Using the value in line 13 of the configuration file (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">key="receive_sign", value="PMO"</span>), Whisper iterates over the inbox rules looking for that value to be specified in one of three places: <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">subject</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">body</span>, or <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">subjectorbody</span> and for emails matching that value to be sent to a specified location (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">deleteditems</span> or <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">inbox</span>, depending on the version of Whisper). If the inbox has such a rule, Whisper goes to the next step; otherwise, Whisper creates a rule with the given parameters:</p>
<ul>
<li>Rule name: <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">MicosoftDefaultRules</span>.</li>
<li>Move to folder: <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">deleteditems</span> or <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">inbox</span>.
<ul>
<li>One version of Whisper specifies the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">deleteditems</span> folder; the other points to the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">inbox</span>. Both are hardcoded in the separate binaries.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Mark as read: <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">true</span>.</li>
<li>Condition: <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">subject</span> contains <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">PMO</span>.
<ul>
<li>The location to look for the string, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">subject</span>, is hardcoded in both versions of Whisper. The string to look for, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">PMO</span>, is in the configuration file used by Whisper; we were unable to collect the other configuration file.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>In Step 3, Whisper initiates a never-ending do loop that sends a check-in email message from the compromised email account in Step 1 to an email address specified in the configuration file (line 16, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">key="alive_mail"</span>). The check-in message is sent every 10 hours (line 10 in the configuration file, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">key="al_time"</span>; in minutes), the subject (line 17, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">key="alive_msg_subj"</span>) is <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Content</span>, and the message body contains the string defined below:</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">"Content ID: "</span> + base64_encode(<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">"COMPUTERNAME:USERDNSDOMAIN:USERNAME"</span>)</p>
<p>Next, in Step 4, Whisper fetches operator commands. It does so by searching the inbox identified in Step 1 for files in a given folder (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">deleteditems</span> or <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">inbox</span>, depending on the version of Whisper) with attachments where the subject matches a string (supplied in the configuration file; <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">PMO</span> in the only configuration file we collected). For matching emails with attachments, Whisper scrapes the attachment body (which should contain encrypted commands) and stores the sender&rsquo;s email address for use later as the C&amp;C server to which operator command results are uploaded.</p>
<p>In Step 5, Whisper decrypts the operator commands. It does so by first base64 decoding the string containing the command and then decrypting the result using the <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/dotnet/api/system.security.cryptography.aes?view=net-8.0" target="_blank" rel="noopener">.NET AES class</a> with a 16-byte initialization vector and the encryption key found in the configuration file (line 18, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">key="enc_key" value="cXdlcmFzZHp4Y3ZmZ2d0aGhsZGZvZ2g/bHZtZ2xrZyE="</span>). Decrypted commands are in the form of <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;cmd_id&gt;;&lt;command_to_execute&gt;</span>. The command ID, commands, and command output are saved in the following format:</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">base64-encoded(&lt;command_id&gt;: &lt;cmd_id&gt;\n&lt;cmd_output&gt;\n)</span></p>
<p>Then, in Step 6, Whisper executes the backdoor commands and records the results. Possible commands include:</p>
<ul>
<li style="font-size: 1em;">Write a file to disk</li>
</ul>
<p>The data written to disk is:</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">this is my file content</span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;filepath&gt;</span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;filename&gt;</span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;nbytes-to-write&gt;</span></p>
<p>The bytes to write are base64 encoded (and decoded before writing to disk). Successful execution returns:</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">file received properly. wrote to: &lt;filepath&gt;\&lt;filename&gt;</span></p>
<ul>
<li style="font-size: 1em;">Send a file to the C&amp;C server</li>
</ul>
<p>This command is prefixed with <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">this is my required file path</span> followed by <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">\n&lt;unknown_variable&gt;\n&lt;filepath&gt;\&lt;filename&gt;</span>. Whisper reads the contents of the file into memory, base64 encodes them, and returns:</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">this is my required file &lt;path&gt;\n&lt;unknown_variable&gt;\n&lt;filename&gt;\n&lt;base64_encoded_file_contents&gt;</span></p>
<ul>
<li style="font-size: 1em;">Execute a PowerShell script</li>
</ul>
<p>This command does not have a prefix and instead only contains a plaintext command that PowerShell is capable of executing, postfixed with a pipe after which Whisper appends <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace; white-space: nowrap;">Out-String</span>. Output is saved in this form:</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">base64-encoded(&lt;command_id&gt;: &lt;cmd_id&gt;\n&lt;cmd_output&gt;\n)</span></p>
<p>Finally, in Step 7, Whisper sends the command output in an email message to the C&amp;C inbox found in Step 4. The email is formatted with these particulars:</p>
<ul>
<li>sending email address: inbox from Step 1,</li>
<li>recipient: email address from Step 4,</li>
<li>subject: <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Email</span> (from the configuration file, line 14, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">key="send_sign"</span>),</li>
<li>message body: <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Hey There! find your results in the attachment</span> (hardcoded in the binary), and</li>
<li>attachment: output from the commands in Step 6, encrypted with the same encryption key in Step 5 (configuration file line 18, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">key="enc_key" value="cXdlcmFzZHp4Y3ZmZ2d0aGhsZGZvZ2g/bHZtZ2xrZyE="</span>).</li>
</ul>
<p>Steps 4&ndash;7 continue in a loop using the same check-in schedule from Step 3 until the credentials hardcoded in the configuration file are changed.</p>
<h4>Shahmaran backdoor</h4>
<p>The Shahmaran backdoor, named after a mythical half-snake, half-woman creature from <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shahmaran" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Iranian folklore</a>, is a 64-bit PE that was found in the startup folder as:</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">%ROAMINGAPPDATA%\Microsoft\Windows\Start Menu\Programs\Startup\adobeupdater.exe</span></p>
<p>At system startup, Shahmaran creates a Windows <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/win32/api/synchapi/nf-synchapi-createeventw" target="_blank" rel="noopener">event object</a>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">SysPrep</span>. It is possible that the Shahmaran developers chose <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">SysPrep</span> as the event name to blend into the background noise, as <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows-hardware/manufacture/desktop/sysprep--system-preparation--overview?view=windows-11" target="_blank" rel="noopener">SysPrep</a> is part of the Windows imaging process. Windows admins use it to create a standard Windows image (often referred to as a Gold or Golden image) before deployment to enterprise systems. <h4>Slippery Snakelet backdoor</h4>
<p>Slippery Snakelet is a small Python-based backdoor with limited capabilities:</p>
<p style="mso-list: l10 level1 lfo3;">1. executes a command via <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">cmd.exe</span>,</p>
<p style="mso-list: l10 level1 lfo3;">2. downloads a file from a URL, and</p>
<p style="mso-list: l10 level1 lfo3;">3. upload a file to the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">/newfile/</span> URI path.</p>
<p>Slippery Snakelet has a hardcoded C&amp;C server, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">zaincell[.]store</span>, and communicates with it via URLs of the form <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">https://zaincell[.]store/request/&lt;UID&gt;</span>, where the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;UID&gt;</span> is the victim&rsquo;s login domain and the compromised computer&rsquo;s name separated by a period then base64 encoded (e.g., <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">victim_domain.computer_name = dmljdGltX2RvbWFpbi5jb21wdXRlcl9uYW1l</span>).</p>
<p>Slippery Snakelet also has this hardcoded User-Agent:</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Mozilla/5.0 (Windows NT 10.0; Win64; x64) AppleWebKit/537.36 (KHTML, like Gecko) Chrome/88.0.4324.104 Safari/537.36</span></p>

<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">error in creaate ssh client</span></li>
</ul>
<p>Interestingly, at another point in the reverse tunnels, the developers correctly spelled the word elapsed (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">time elapsed!</span>), which is indicative of poor coding and lax code review, if any is performed (e.g., there is a lot of command result text output to the command line, as if the reverse tunnels were shipped immediately after successful testing was completed).</p>
<p>The actual function and flow of Laret and Pinar after collecting the parameters from the configuration file is quite banal, but that is probably an intentional effort to blend in. Both look for a filename in the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">process_file</span> parameter and, if a file matching the supplied name is present, execute it and start two threads:</p>
<ol>
<li>Sets up an SSH connection to the C&amp;C IP in the configuration file using the <a href="https://www.nuget.org/packages/Core.Renci.SshNet/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Core.Renci.SshNet</a> DLL included within the binary. Port <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">22</span> is hardcoded as the C&amp;C port and port forwarding is also enabled, using the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">remote_port</span> variable from the configuration file.</li>
<li>Sets up a listener on the port specified in the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">local_port</span> parameter of the configuration file. Note that any data sent to the listener is done in the clear (i.e., no encryption or obfuscation is used beyond extra <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">\0</span> characters that are removed at the time of receipt by Laret and Pinar).</li>
</ol>
<p>If no file is specified in <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">process_file</span>, both Laret and Pinar skip setting up a listener port.</p>
<p>Laret and Pinar only differ significantly in that Pinar sets up a service, called <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Service1</span>, for persistence prior to executing the two threads. Laret has no means of persistence beyond its process running indefinitely.</p>
<h4>Supplementary tools</h4>
<h5>Flog webshell</h5>
<p>Flog is a webshell found uploaded to VirusTotal from Iraq by the same submitter who uploaded one of the versions of Whisper. Based on that and the close timeframe (both were uploaded within a matter of minutes) we believe it was deployed by BladedFeline to the victim in the Iraq government.</p>
<p>Flog, so named for its filename &ndash; <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">flogon.aspx</span> &ndash; looks for specific input from the BladedFeline operators of the form <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;password&gt;=&lt;(a|b|c|d)&gt;#&lt;path&gt;</span></p>
<p>Flog hashes the password, which must match the MD5 checksum <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">4CC88CE123B0DA8D75C0FE66A39339F6</span>.</p>
<p>Variables (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">a|b|c|d</span>) are command options:</p>
<ul>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">a</span> returns, for the path provided, a directory listing and the byte length of each file,</li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">b</span> creates a file on disk, using the path provided,</li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">c</span> splits the path variable on a pipe and writes a file to disk where the first part of the path is the filename and the second part is the data to write, and</li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">d</span> deletes a specified file given in the supplied path.</li>
</ul>
<h5>Hawking Listener</h5>
<p>Hawking Listener, so named for its PDB string &ndash; <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">C:\Users\g18u04\source\repos\Hawking\Hawking\obj\Release\listner.pdb</span> &ndash; is a 32-bit .NET/C# Windows binary with a timestomped compilation time of 2057-11-14 16:59:12. It was also uploaded to VirusTotal by the same user who uploaded Flog and is probably a BladedFeline tool. It implements the <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/dotnet/api/system.net.httplistener?view=net-8.0" target="_blank" rel="noopener">.NET HTTPListener class</a> to set up a listener with a hardcoded URL (which we cannot disclose in this case without revealing the identity of the victim). Alternatively, Hawking can be provided at runtime with URLs for the listener socket to monitor.</p>
<p>Hawking listens for a provided <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">QueryString</span> (from a BladedFeline operator) with <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">snmflwkejrhgsey</span> as the key in the key-value pair. Once received, Hawking executes the value in <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">cmd.exe</span> and returns the output. To stop Hawking, operators need only send <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">stop</span> as the key in the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">QueryString</span> with a non-null variable in the value.</p>
<p>Hawking logs all interactions, runtime arguments, and command output to the file <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">log.txt</span> in its working directory.</p>
<h5>P.S. Olala</h5>
<p>P.S. Olala is a 32-bit .NET binary named for its intended function (executing PowerShell scripts) and its PDB path <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">G:\csharp\psExecuterService\ewsService\obj\Release\Olala.pdb</span>. It does not accept any runtime arguments. Rather, at runtime, P.S. Olala uses the <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/dotnet/api/system.serviceprocess.servicebase.run?view=dotnet-plat-ext-8.0#system-serviceprocess-servicebase-run(system-serviceprocess-servicebase())" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Run(ServiceBase[])</a> method of the .NET <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/dotnet/api/system.serviceprocess.servicebase?view=dotnet-plat-ext-8.0" target="_blank" rel="noopener">ServiceBase</a> class to register itself as a service with the <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/win32/services/service-control-manager" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Service Control Manager</a> (for persistence).</p>
<p>When the P.S. Olala service is called, it spawns a thread and executes the function <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">mainLoop</span>, shown in Figure 6. Essentially, P.S. Olala is an executor of the PowerShell script stored in <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">%APPDATA%\Local\Microsoft\InputPersonalization\TrainedDataStore.ps1</span>.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 6. The main function of P.S. Olala" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2025/05-25/bladedfeline/figure-6.png" alt="Figure 6. The main function of P.S. Olala" width="" height="" />

</figure>
<p>Unfortunately, we were unable to collect any of the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">TrainedDataStore.ps1</span> scripts. However, contextual information indicates it is likely an executor of the Whisper backdoor, or one of the reverse tunnels (Laret or Pinar). The entire flow (P.S. Olala &rarr; TrainedDataStore &rarr; Whisper/Laret/Pinar) is probably an elongated persistence chain aiming to maintain access.</p>
<h5>Sheep Tunneler</h5>
<p>Sheep Tunneler, a custom tunneling application that we named based on the PDB string <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">C:\Users\sheep\source\repos\MP\MP\obj\Release\MP.pdb</span>), has been observed in the two following locations:</p>
<ul>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">%APPDATA%\Local\Microsoft\Windows\Ringtones\RingService.exe</span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">%APPDATA%\Local\Microsoft\Windows\Shell\mspsrv.exe</span></li>
</ul>
<p>Sheep Tunneler can be executed in two modes: network tunneling (by using the runtime argument <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">middle</span>) or connect back (by using the arguments <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">cb &lt;ip&gt;:&lt;port&gt;</span>).</p>
<h5>Whisper Protocol<a id="Whisper Protocol"></a></h5>
<p>Whisper Protocol, so named for its filename (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Protocol.pdf.exe</span>) is a 64-bit Python-compiled Windows binary with a compilation timestamp of 2024-03-11 09:01:20. It creates a folder in <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">C:\ProgramData\VeeamUpdate</span> and writes both Whisper and its configuration file to that folder. Whisper Protocol also copies itself to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">%APPDATA%\Roaming\Microsoft\Windows\Start Menu\Programs\Startup\VeeamUpdate.lnk</span> for persistence. Finally, it executes Whisper and exits gracefully.</p>
<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>BladedFeline is an advanced threat group that specializes in targeting Iraqi and Kurdish victims, specifically governmental officials and organizations. We assess that the group is likely a subgroup of OilRig. We expect to find that BladedFeline will persist with implant development in order to maintain and expand access within its compromised victim set, likely for cyberespionage.</p>
