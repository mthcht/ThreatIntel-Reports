    <div class="article-tags mb-2 dark big"><p class="article-tag text-capitalize">ESET Research</p></div>    <h1 class="page-headline">You will always remember this as the day you finally caught FamousSparrow</h1>    <p class="sub-title">ESET researchers uncover the toolset used by the FamousSparrow APT group, including two undocumented versions of the group’s signature backdoor, SparrowDoor</p>

                    <div class="article-body">
    <p>In July 2024, ESET Research noticed suspicious activity on the system of a trade group in the United States that operates in the financial sector. While helping the affected entity remediate the compromise, we made an unexpected discovery in the victim&rsquo;s network: malicious tools belonging to FamousSparrow, a China-aligned APT group. There had been no publicly documented FamousSparrow activity since 2022, so the group was thought to be inactive. Not only was FamousSparrow still active during this period, it must have also been hard at work developing its toolset, since the compromised network revealed not one, but two previously undocumented versions of SparrowDoor, FamousSparrow&rsquo;s flagship backdoor.</p>
<p>Both of these versions of SparrowDoor constitute marked progress over earlier ones, especially in terms of code quality and architecture. One of them resembles the backdoor that researchers at Trend Micro called <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/en_sg/research/24/k/earth-estries.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">CrowDoor</a> and attributed to the Earth Estries APT group in November 2024. The other is modular and significantly different from all previous versions. This campaign is also the first documented time FamousSparrow used ShadowPad, a <a href="https://assets.sentinelone.com/c/Shadowpad?x=P42eqA" target="_blank" rel="noopener">privately sold backdoor</a>, known to only be supplied to China-aligned threat actors.</p>
<p>We further discovered that, as part of this campaign, the threat actor managed to breach a research institute in Mexico just a couple of days prior to the compromise in the US.</p>
<p>While setting up tracking based on what we discovered in these attacks, we uncovered additional activity by the group between 2022 and 2024, which we&rsquo;re still investigating. Among others, it targeted a governmental institution in Honduras.</p>
<p>This blogpost provides an overview of the toolset used in the July 2024 campaign, focusing on the undocumented versions of the SparrowDoor backdoor that we discovered at the US victim.</p>
<blockquote>
<div><strong>Key points of this blogpost:</strong></div>
<ul>
<li>ESET researchers discovered that FamousSparrow compromised a trade group for the financial sector in the United States and a research institute in Mexico.</li>
<li>FamousSparrow deployed two previously undocumented versions of the SparrowDoor backdoor, one of them modular.</li>
<li>Both versions constitute considerable progress over previous ones and implement parallelization of commands.</li>
<li>The APT group was also observed using the ShadowPad backdoor for the first time.</li>
<li>We discuss Microsoft Threat Intelligence&rsquo;s attribution claims linking FamousSparrow to Salt Typhoon.</li>
</ul>
</blockquote>
<p>FamousSparrow is a cyberespionage group with ties to China, active since at least 2019. We first publicly documented the group in a <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2021/09/23/famoussparrow-suspicious-hotel-guest/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">2021 blogpost</a> when we observed it exploiting the ProxyLogon vulnerability. The group was initially known for targeting hotels around the world, but has also targeted governments, international organizations, engineering companies, and law firms. FamousSparrow is the only known user of the SparrowDoor backdoor.</p>
<p>Even though FamousSparrow seemed inactive at the time of our discovery, we attribute this activity to the group with high confidence. The deployed payloads are new versions of SparrowDoor, a backdoor that appears to be exclusive to this group. While these new versions exhibit significant upgrades in code quality and architecture, they can still be traced back directly to earlier, publicly documented versions. The loaders used in these attacks also present substantial code overlaps with samples previously attributed to FamousSparrow. Notably, they use the same reflective loader shellcode as the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">libhost.dll</span> loader sample described in a <a href="https://www.ncsc.gov.uk/report/mar-sparrowdoor" target="_blank" rel="noopener">report</a> from February 2022 published by the UK National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC). Its configuration also shares the same specific format, except for the encryption key which is instead hardcoded in the loader and backdoor. XOR encryption has also been replaced with RC4.</p>
<p>Additionally, C&amp;C server communications use a format very similar to that used in previous SparrowDoor versions.</p>
<p>In 2021, Kaspersky researchers wrote about a threat actor they track as GhostEmperor. Despite some infrastructure overlap with FamousSparrow, we track them as separate groups. In August 2023, Trend Micro <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/en_us/research/23/h/earth-estries-targets-government-tech-for-cyberespionage.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">noted</a> that some FamousSparrow TTPs overlap with those of Earth Estries. We have also observed code overlaps between SparrowDoor and that group&rsquo;s HemiGate. These are discussed in more detail in the <em><a href="#Plugins">Plugins</a></em> section. We believe that the two groups overlap at least partially, but we do not have enough data to fully assess the nature and extent of the link between the two groups.</p>
<h2>FamousSparrow and Salt Typhoon</h2>
<p>Before we dive into the analysis of FamousSparrow&rsquo;s toolset, we want to discuss our position on the links between FamousSparrow and Salt Typhoon made by Microsoft Threat Intelligence.</p>
<p>In September 2024, the Wall Street Journal published <a href="https://www.wsj.com/tech/cybersecurity/u-s-wiretap-systems-targeted-in-china-linked-hack-327fc63b">an article</a> (the article is behind a paywall) reporting that internet service providers in the United States had been compromised by a threat actor named Salt Typhoon. The article relays claims by Microsoft that this threat actor is the same as FamousSparrow and GhostEmperor. It is the first public report that conflates the latter two groups. However, as we already stated, we see GhostEmperor and FamousSparrow as two distinct groups. There are few overlaps between the two but many discrepancies. Both used <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">27.102.113[.]240</span> as a download server in 2021. Both groups were also early exploiters of the ProxyLogon vulnerability (<a href="https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2021-26855">CVE-2021-26855</a>) and have used some of the same publicly available tools. However, besides these publicly available tools, each threat actor has its own custom toolset.</p>
<p>Since that initial publication, researchers at Trend Micro have added <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/en_us/research/24/k/earth-estries.html">Earth Estries</a> to the list of groups that are linked to Salt Typhoon. As of this writing, Microsoft, who created the Salt Typhoon cluster, has not published any technical indicators or details about TTPs used by the threat actor, nor provided an explanation for this attribution. To avoid further muddying the waters, we will keep tracking the cluster of activity we see as directly linked to SparrowDoor as FamousSparrow until we have information necessary to reliably assess these attribution claims.</p>
<p>Based on our data and analysis of the publicly available reports, FamousSparrow appears to be its own distinct cluster with loose links to the others mentioned in this section. We believe those links are better explained by positing the existence of a shared third party, such as a digital quartermaster, than by conflating all of these disparate clusters of activity into one.</p>
<h2>Technical Analysis</h2>
<p>In order to gain initial access to the affected network, FamousSparrow deployed a webshell on an IIS server. While we were unable to determine the exact exploit used to deploy the webshells, both victims were running outdated versions of Windows Server and Microsoft Exchange, for which there are several publicly available exploits.</p>
<p>As for the toolset used in the campaign, the threat actor employed a mix of custom tools and malware along with those shared by China-aligned APT groups, as well as from publicly available sources. The final payloads were SparrowDoor and ShadowPad. Figure 1 provides an overview of the compromise chain deployed in the attacks.</p>

Overview of the compromise chain used in this FamousSparrow campaign" width="" height="" />

<p>The threat actor initially downloaded a batch script over HTTP from a download server, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">43.254.216[.]195</span>. This script contains a base64-encoded .NET webshell that it writes to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace; white-space: nowrap;">C:\users\public\s.txt</span>. It then decodes it using certutil.exe and saves the decoded output to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace; white-space: nowrap;">C:\users\public\s.ashx</span>. An <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/previous-versions/aspnet/bb398986(v=vs.100)#built-in-http-handlers-in-aspnet" target="_blank" rel="noopener">ASHX module</a> is a type of HTTP handler for ASP.NET. Although similar to ASPX modules, ASHX modules do not include any user interface components. The script then walks through drives <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">C:</span> to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">I:</span>, and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">P:</span>, to find the installation directory of <a href="https://dnncommunity.org/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">DotNetNuke</a>; it then copies the ASHX webshell to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;DotNetNuke_directory&gt;\DesktopModules\DotNetNuke.ashx</span>.</p>
<p>The webshell itself is fairly generic and doesn&rsquo;t use anything specific to DotNetNuke. All the data it receives, and returns, is AES encrypted with the hardcoded key <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">e2c99096bcecd1b5</span>. On first request, it expects a .NET PE file. This executable file is loaded into memory and saved in a session variable. On subsequent requests, an instance of the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">LY</span> class contained within that .NET assembly is created and the data received is passed to its <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Equals</span> method. We did not collect any payload sent to this webshell, but it&rsquo;s obvious that the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Equals</span> <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/dotnet/csharp/programming-guide/classes-and-structs/methods">method</a> does not follow the typical <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Design_by_contract">contract</a>.</p>
<p>In the cases we observed, this was used to spawn an interactive remote PowerShell session. Once this session was established, attackers used legitimate Windows tools to obtain information about the host and the Active Directory domains to which it was joined. They then downloaded <a href="https://github.com/AdrianVollmer/PowerHub">PowerHub</a>, an open-source post-exploitation framework, from an attacker-controlled server and used the<em> </em><a href="https://github.com/BeichenDream/BadPotato" target="_blank" rel="noopener">BadPotato</a> privilege-escalation technique to gain SYSTEM privileges. This exploit is not present in the framework, but it appears that the group added the open-source <a href="https://github.com/S3cur3Th1sSh1t/PowerSharpPack/blob/master/PowerSharpBinaries/Invoke-BadPotato.ps1" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Invoke-BadPotato</a> module to its deployment of PowerHub. Finally, the attacker used PowerShell&rsquo;s built-in Invoke-WebRequest to download three files from the same server that comprise SparrowDoor&rsquo;s <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2020/12/10/luckymouse-ta428-compromise-able-desktop/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">trident loader</a>.</p>
<p>In a process very similar to the one <a href="https://www.ncsc.gov.uk/files/NCSC-MAR-SparrowDoor.pdf">described in 2022</a> by the UK NCSC, the aforementioned files use a trident loading scheme to execute SparrowDoor. In this instance, the executable used for DLL side-loading is a legitimate version of K7AntiVirus Messenger Scanner named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace; white-space: nowrap;">K7AVMScn.exe</span>, while the malicious DLL and encrypted payload files are named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace; white-space: nowrap;">K7AVWScn.dll</span> and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace; white-space: nowrap;">K7AVWScn.doc</span>, respectively. The payload file is encrypted using an RC4 key that is hardcoded in both the loader and the resulting decrypted payload, but which varies across samples.</p>
<p>The decrypted payload consists of a custom configuration and reflective loader shellcode almost identical to that described by the UK NCSC, with the only difference being that the first field, which contained the four-byte XOR key, has been removed. The last 202 bytes of the file are encrypted separately, but using the same RC4 key, and contain the C&amp;C server configuration.</p>
<h3>SparrowDoor</h3>
<p>As stated, we observed two new versions of SparrowDoor used in these attacks. The first one is very similar to what was called CrowDoor by researchers at Trend Micro, in an article published in November 2024 <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/en_sg/research/24/k/earth-estries.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">about Earth Estries</a>. This malware was first documented by researchers at ITOCHU and Macnica in <a href="https://www.virusbulletin.com/conference/vb2023/abstracts/unveiling-activities-tropic-trooper-2023-deep-analysis-xiangoop-loader-and-entryshell-payload" target="_blank" rel="noopener">a presentation at VirusBulletin</a> in 2023. From our perspective, these are part of the continued development effort on SparrowDoor rather than a different <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">family.</span> We can follow the evolution from the first version we described in 2021, through the ones referred to as CrowDoor, to the modular version we analyze in the later part of this blogpost.</p>
<p>Both versions of SparrowDoor used in this campaign constitute onsiderable advances in code quality and architecture compared to older ones. The most significant change is the parallelization of time-consuming commands, such as file I/O and the interactive shell. This allows the backdoor to continue handling new commands while those tasks are performed. We will explain the procedure later in the blogpost when we discuss the commands in detail.</p>

<p>When executed without any arguments, the malware establishes persistence. It first tries to do so by creating a service named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">K7Soft</span> that is set to run automatically on startup. If this fails, a registry Run key with the same name is used instead. In both cases, the persistence mechanism is set to execute the backdoor with a command line argument of <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">11</span>. It is also launched immediately with that same argument using the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">StartServiceA</span> or <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">ShellExecuteA</span> API.</p>
<p>When executed with the argument <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">11</span>, the backdoor launches the Windows color management tool (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">colorcpl.exe</span>) with a command line argument of <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">22</span> and injects its loader into the newly created process.</p>
<p>It is only when the command line argument is set to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">22</span> that the backdoor actually executes its main payload.</p>
<p>After SparrowDoor is executed in this backdoor mode, it terminates, in a roundabout way, any other already running instances. The backdoor uses the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">K32EnumProcesses</span> API to iterate through the process IDs (PIDs) of all running processes and tries to create a mutex named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Global\ID(&lt;PID&gt;)</span>. PIDs of <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">15</span> or less are skipped, likely as a way to exclude killing some essential system processes. If the mutex already exists, the process is terminated. Otherwise, the mutex is closed immediately. When SparrowDoor is done iterating through the PIDs, it creates a new mutex using the same name format and its own PID.</p>
<p>The backdoor then reads the last 202 bytes from the encrypted payload file and decrypts them using the same RC4 key used by the loader. The resulting plaintext is the C&amp;C server configuration, which consists of three pairs of addresses and ports, followed by four numeric values that, respectively, represent the number of days, hours, minutes, and seconds the backdoor should wait after all configured C&amp;C servers have been tried. This is related to the functionality we describe later while talking about the command the backdoor uses for changing the C&amp;C configuration.</p>
<p>After loading this configuration, the backdoor will try to connect to the first server. If it is unable to connect or if the C&amp;C server issues a command that causes execution to exit the main command loop, SparrowDoor will try to connect to the next server, and so on. Once the last server in the configuration has been tried, the backdoor will sleep for the defined time (six minutes in the sample we analyzed), reload the configuration, and then repeat the process. Note that, during this time, SparrowDoor does not respond to commands. However, the parallelized commands that were already running will keep doing so until they complete, encounter an error, or are terminated by the server.</p>
<p>The backdoor uses two <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Class_(computer_programming)">classes</a> to manage its connections: the abstract <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">CBaseSocket</span> and its child class <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">CTcpSocket</span>. These are essentially wrappers around <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/win32/winsock/windows-sockets-start-page-2">Winsock</a> TCP sockets. While the class names are generic and follow the same naming convention used in the <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/cpp/mfc/reference/mfc-classes">Microsoft Foundation Class Library</a> (MFC), the code they contain appears to be custom.</p>
<p>SparrowDoor uses an integer value as a victim or session identifier. This is sent to the C&amp;C server when it requests information about the host and whenever a new socket is created. The value is read from the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">HKLM\Software\CLASSES\CLSID\ID</span> registry key, falling back to the same path in the HKCU hive if there&rsquo;s an issue. If it is not present, the identifier is derived from the machine&rsquo;s performance counter and written to the aforementioned registry key. Although the value itself is benign, the use of this nonstandard registry key presents a detection opportunity. Indeed, the name of any registry key under <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Software\Classes\CLSID\</span> should be a valid <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/win32/com/clsid-key-hklm">CLSID</a>, which are represented as a GUID surrounded by curly brackets. While it is not necessarily an indicator of maliciousness, the presence of keys with nonstandard names under <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">CLSID</span> is unusual.</p>

We have not had access to that sample, so we cannot tell whether the additional commands were absent or simply not publicly documented by the authors.</p>

<h5>Interactive shell<a id="Interactive shell"></a></h5>
<p>Upon receiving the interactive shell command, SparrowDoor spawns a new thread and socket as previously described, and performs all the following actions within this thread using the new socket. First, the backdoor sends back an acknowledgment message with command ID <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0x32341125</span> and the unique victim ID in the data field. It then spawns a <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">cmd.exe</span> process and uses a pair of threads and named pipes to relay commands and their output between the C&amp;C server and the shell. The named pipe <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">\\.\pipe\id2&lt;address&gt;</span> is used to pass commands received from the C&amp;C server to the shell and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">\\.\pipe\id1&lt;address&gt;</span> is used for the resulting output on <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">STDOUT</span> and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">STDERR</span>. In both instances, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;address&gt;</span> is the memory address, in decimal form, of the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">CTcpSocket</span> instance. These commands use the ID <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0x32341126</span> and the data is, respectively, the command line to be executed and the raw output. If the backdoor receives a message with the command ID set to any other value, the interactive shell session is terminated.</p>
<h5>Changing the C&amp;C configuration</h5>
<p>The C&amp;C configuration is kept in the encrypted payload file. If the backdoor receives the command to change this configuration (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0x3234112A</span>), the received structure is RC4 encrypted and then the last 202 bytes of the encrypted file are overwritten with the result. Interestingly, the configuration is not automatically reloaded. As we explained previously, the configuration is only reloaded when all three configured servers have been tried. To forcibly reload the configuration, the server can issue the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0x32341127</span> command or an invalid command, both of which will cause SparrowDoor to exit the command loop and move to the next server. The configuration is also reloaded if the backdoor is relaunched, such as by using the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0x3234112B</span> command.</p>
<h5>File operations<a id="File operations"></a></h5>
<p>As with other commands processed in parallel, everything here is performed in a new thread using a new socket. SparrowDoor sends an acknowledgment message with the same ID as the original command. The body of this message contains the unique ID of the victim and the operation ID sent by the C&amp;C server. This operation ID does not appear to have any meaning, and is probably only used by the server to link the connection to the file operation command if multiple such commands are performed in parallel. Command IDs <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0x3234112E</span> and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0x3234112F</span> are used, respectively, for file reads and writes.</p>
<p>For a file read, the message body contains the starting offset, the size to be read, and the path to the file. If the requested read goes past the end of the file, it causes an error and no response is sent. Otherwise, the malware reads the file in chunks of 4 kB, each of which is sent in the body of a message with the command ID <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0x32341130</span>.</p>
<p>The process is similar for a file write. The initial message from the C&amp;C contains the total size of the data to be written followed by the target file path. Interestingly, the write is only performed if this size is greater than the current size of the target file. The data is then sent by the C&amp;C server in chunks of 4 kB, using the same command ID of <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0x32341130</span>.</p>
<h5>File listing<a id="File listing"></a></h5>
<p>When the file listing command is received, the backdoor first sends back an acknowledgment message with the command ID <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0x32341133</span>. It then uses the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">FindFirstFileW</span> and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">FindNextFileW</span> API functions to iterate, non-recursively, through files in the target directory. For each file, SparrowDoor sends one message, with the same command ID as the list file command (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0x32341132</span>) and the information described in Figure 3. Note that, even though the length of the filename isn&rsquo;t specified directly, it can be obtained by subtracting the size of the rest of the fields (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0x16</span>) from the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">data_length</span> value in the header.</p>

<figcaption><em>Figure 3. Format of the information sent for each listed file</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>Once the iteration is done, a message with command <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">ID 0x32341134</span> and no data is sent to indicate that the file listing operation has completed successfully.</p>
<h5>Proxy<a id="Proxy"></a></h5>
<p>This functionality allows the backdoor to act as a TCP proxy between the C&amp;C server and an arbitrary machine. As with other commands processed in parallel, the following is done in a new thread using its own socket. SparrowDoor sends an acknowledgment message with the same ID as the original command; the body of this message contains the unique ID of the victim. Command ID <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0x32341139</span> is then sent by the server to actually initiate the proxy. The proxy functionality is achieved by creating two new sockets, one connected to the C&amp;C server and another connected to an address and port provided by the server on that new connection. SparrowDoor then uses a pair of <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/win32/api/winsock2/ns-winsock2-wsaoverlapped">Winsock structures and events</a> to keep track of incoming packets and relay them between the two parties. The addition of proxy functionality to SparrowDoor may be a hint that the group is following the trend of China-aligned threat actors building and using <a href="https://cloud.google.com/blog/topics/threat-intelligence/china-nexus-espionage-orb-networks">operational relay box (ORB) networks</a>.</p>
<h4>Modular SparrowDoor</h4>
<p>The modular version of SparrowDoor is significantly different from the previous ones. On the network communication side, the command header is sent separately from the body and that data is RC4 encrypted with the hardcoded key <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">iotrh^%4CFGTj</span>. The custom classes used for network communication in this version still use Winsock TCP sockets and are very similar to those we mentioned previously &ndash; the most notable difference being that the child class is deceptively named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">CShttps</span> instead of <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">CTcpSocket</span>. As seen in Table 3, of the commands present in previous versions of SparrowDoor, this one only implements the commands that relate to managing the C&amp;C configuration and uninstalling the backdoor. Information about the host machine is sent automatically after the initial connection message and includes a list of installed security products in addition to what was sent in previous versions.</p>
<p>All of the other commands are related to the handling of plugins. We believe that the removed functionality has simply been moved to one or more modules. While we have yet to observe any such plugin, we can share insights based on our analysis of the code that implements this functionality.</p>

<h5>Plugins<a id="Plugins"></a></h5>
<p>Installed plugins are referenced via a standard C++ list; each entry consists of a bitmask and a handler function address. The bitmask is used to determine which command IDs are handled by the plugin and corresponds to the low nibble of the third byte of the command ID (i.e., <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">CommandID &amp; 0xF0000</span>).</p>
<p>This version of SparrowDoor can use five different command IDs to invoke plugin commands. Of those, three (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0x3A76</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0x3A77</span>, and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0x3A7B</span>) follow almost exactly the same path in the code &ndash; the only difference being the response ID of the acknowledgment message. There are some very minor differences in the handshake process between this set of commands and the other two. However, in all cases, the command is parallelized using the same method we described in the <em><a href="#Commands">Commands</a></em> section. On the new socket, the backdoor sends the corresponding response ID, the unique host ID, and the data it initially received from the C&amp;C server. This data appears to function like the operation ID mentioned in the <em><a href="#File operations">File operations</a></em> section. After this handshake is completed, all five commands call the same function to actually handle the plugin command. This function receives the command ID and data from the C&amp;C server, then iterates through installed plugins to dispatch the command to the correct handler. The process is repeated until the backdoor receives an incorrectly formatted command message.</p>
<p>By default, only one plugin, with a bitmask of <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0x10000</span>, is installed. This plugin handles the installation of new plugins sent by the C&amp;C server. Plugins are sent by the server as PE files and are never stored on disk. Coupled with the reduced function set present in the base backdoor, this is probably meant to evade detection. After such a plugin is received, it is manually mapped in memory and its <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">fmain</span> export is called. This function returns a pointer to a structure containing the address of a function that returns the bitmask for the plugin and the address of the handler function. If no installed plugin has the same bitmask, the newly received plugin is added to the list.</p>
<h5>Links to previous versions</h5>
<p>We have also identified older samples that present significant code overlaps with this modular version, including similar code to handle plugins. These samples correspond to the backdoor that Trend Micro named HemiGate in <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/en_ca/research/23/h/earth-estries-targets-government-tech-for-cyberespionage.html">an August 2023 article</a>. Some of the samples even use the same RC4 key mentioned in that article. Rather than being sent by the C&amp;C, plugins are implemented as C++ classes inheriting from an abstract class named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">PluginInterface</span>. These plugins follow the same pattern described in the previous paragraph: they have a method that returns a bitmask, used to dispatch commands, and a second method to handle commands. We believe that HemiGate represents an earlier step in the evolution of the modular backdoor. Thus, it is likely that the plugins contained therein are representative of those used in the more recent modular version. Table 4 presents an overview of the plugins and their functionality.</p>

<h3>ShadowPad</h3>
<p>After SparrowDoor was detected in the US victim&rsquo;s network, it was used to execute an <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/cpp/mfc/mfc-desktop-applications?view=msvc-170">MFC</a>-based loader bearing similarities to the ShadowPad loaders <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/chinese-hacking-group-apt41-compromised-taiwanese-government-affiliated-research-institute-with-shadowpad-and-cobaltstrike-2/">previously documented by Cisco Talos</a>.</p>
<p>This ShadowPad loader is a DLL named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">imjpp14.dll</span>, meant to be loaded via DLL side-loading by the more-than-14-year-old, legitimate, outdated version of the <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/security-updates/securitybulletins/2013/ms13-075">Microsoft Office IME executable</a>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">imecmnt.exe</span>, renamed to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">imjp14k.exe</span>. The loader first checks whether its current process is the expected side-loading host by performing pattern matching at offset <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0xE367</span> in-memory. Once this validation succeeds, the malicious DLL decrypts the file named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">imjp14k.dll.dat</span> that is located in the same directory as the DLL and its side-loading host. Finally, the decrypted payload is injected into a wmplayer.exe process (Windows Media Player).</p>
<p>Even though we did not retrieve the encrypted payload, an in-memory ShadowPad detection occurred in a <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">wmplayer.exe</span> process, with <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">impjp14k.exe</span> as its parent process. Furthermore, it connected to a ShadowPad C&amp;C server (IP: <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">216.238.106[.]150</span>). While we didn&rsquo;t observe any ShadowPad sample using it, one of the SparrowDoor C&amp;C servers had a TLS certificate matching a known ShadowPad fingerprint.</p>
<p>Additionally, we detected ShadowPad loaders and the ShadowPad backdoor in memory on several machines in the victim&rsquo;s network.</p>
<p>Note that this is the first time we have observed FamousSparrow making use of the ShadowPad backdoor.</p>
<h3>Other tools</h3>
<p>During the compromise, in addition to the various malware mentioned above, we also observed the following being used by the threat actor:</p>
<ul>
<li>A basic batch script that dumps the registry with the following commands:
<ul>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">reg save HKLM\SYSTEM C:\users\public\sys.hiv</span>,</li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">reg save HKLM\SAM C:\users\public\sam.hiv</span>, and</li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">reg save hklm\security C:\users\public\security.hiv</span>.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a href="https://github.com/fortra/impacket" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Impacket</a> or <a href="https://github.com/Pennyw0rth/NetExec" target="_blank" rel="noopener">NetExec</a>, detected by our firewall, but we have not collected any of the commands.</li>
<li>A loader for a version of the open-source <a href="https://github.com/XZB-1248/Spark">Spark</a> RAT that was modified to include code from an open-source <a href="https://github.com/HZzz2/go-shellcode-loader" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Go shellcode loader</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>We also noticed the use of a tool to dump <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/win32/secauthn/lsa-authentication" target="_blank" rel="noopener">LSASS</a> memory with the undocumented <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">MiniDumpW</span> API function. This tool is split into two DLLs stored on disk as <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">%HOME%\dph.dll</span> and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace; white-space: nowrap;">%WINDIR%\SysWOW64\msvc.dll</span>. The latter is probably meant to blend in with the legitimate libraries for Microsoft Visual C++ (MSVC) that are stored in the same directory. The former is loaded via a legitimate version of VLC&rsquo;s Cache Generator (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">vlc-gen-cache.exe</span>), renamed to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">dph.exe</span>, and imports functions from the latter. Since VLC plugins can be native DLLs, its cache generator naturally contains code to load and execute such libraries.</p>
<h2>Network infrastructure</h2>
<p>The ShadowPad C&amp;C server uses a self-signed TLS certificate, with a SHA-1 fingerprint of <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">BAED2895C80EB6E827A6D47C3DD7B8EFB61ED70B</span>, that attempts to spoof those used by Dell. This follows the format that was described by Hunt Intelligence in <a href="https://hunt.io/blog/tracking-shadowpad-infrastructure-via-non-standard-certificates">an article</a> from February 2024. While this pattern can be used to track ShadowPad servers, it is not linked to any specific threat actor. One of the C&amp;C servers used by SparrowDoor (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace; white-space: nowrap;">45.131.179[.]24:80</span>) had a TLS certificate, on port 443, with the same Common Name (CN) as the certificate used by the aforementioned ShadowPad C&amp;C server. This server is also the only one that was present in both versions of SparrowDoor.</p>
<p>We observed three unique SparrowDoor C&amp;C servers in this campaign, all of which used port 80. The modular sample was configured with amelicen[.]com as its third C&amp;C server. When the sample was first detected, this domain pointed to the IP address mentioned in the previous paragraph. One of the C&amp;C servers configured in the modular sample (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">43.254.216[.]195:80</span>) was also used by the SparrowDoor loader. This is strange, since SparrowDoor uses plain TCP and the files were downloaded over HTTP. However, there is a gap of almost two weeks between the downloads, on June 30, 2024, and the compilation of the modular SparrowDoor, on July 12, 2024. We do not know whether the service listening on that port was changed between those two occurrences or whether the SparrowDoor C&amp;C server includes functionality to serve files over HTTP.</p>
<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>Due to the lack of activity and public reporting between 2022 and 2024, FamousSparrow was presumed to be inactive. However, our analysis of the US network compromised in July 2024 revealed two new versions of SparrowDoor, showing that FamousSparrow is still developing its flagship backdoor. One of these new versions was also found on a machine in Mexico. As we were setting up tracking based on what is covered in this blogpost, we uncovered additional activity by the group during this period, including the targeting of a governmental institution in Honduras. This newly found activity indicates that not only is the group still operating, but it was also actively developing new versions of SparrowDoor during this time.</p>
<p>We will continue to monitor and report on activity by FamousSparrow, and will also continue to follow the discussion surrounding potential links between FamousSparrow and Salt Typhoon.</p>
